### Meta Compute

From Mark Zuckerberg on [Threads](<https://www.threads.com/@zuck/post/DTa3-B1EbTp>):

> Today we’re establishing a new top-level initiative called Meta Compute. Meta is planning to build tens of gigawatts this decade, and hundreds of gigawatts or more over time. How we engineer, invest, and partner to build this infrastructure will become a strategic advantage. This effort will be led by Santosh Janardhan and Daniel Gross.
>
> Santosh will continue to lead our technical architecture, software stack, silicon program, developer productivity, and building and operating our global datacenter fleet and network. Daniel will lead a new group responsible for long-term capacity strategy, supplier partnerships, industry analysis, planning, and business modeling.
>
> They will work closely with Dina Powell McCormick, who just joined Meta as President and Vice Chairman to work on partnering with governments and sovereigns to build, deploy, invest in, and finance Meta’s infrastructure. I’m looking forward to working closely with Daniel, Santosh, Dina and their teams to scale Meta Compute and deliver personal superintelligence to billions of people around the world.

A narrative seemed to quickly spin up that this signaled that Meta is finally going to get into cloud computing, competing with Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, and, well, I really don’t think that is going to happen. What exactly would be the point? Amazon invented the space and invested heavily to have a structural cost advantage in commodity compute; Microsoft had a natural customer base with enterprise; Google has differentiated infrastructure and an outlet to fully leverage their AI investments without fear of cannibalization. What exactly would Meta bring to the table that the other hyperscalers do not have, such that they would have confidence that they could be anything other than a 2nd choice competing on nothing but price?

### The Meta-OpenAI Battle

OpenAI was founded by Sam Altman and Elon Musk with the express purpose of countering Google; then, when ChatGPT took the world by storm, the immediate assumption by everyone — including yours truly — is that Google was the Big Tech company most threatened. In January 2023’s [AI and the Big Five](<https://stratechery.com/2023/ai-and-the-big-five/>) I wrote:

> That, though, ought only increase the concern for Google’s management that generative AI may, in the specific context of search, represent a disruptive innovation instead of a sustaining one. Disruptive innovation is, at least in the beginning, not as good as what already exists; that’s why it is easily dismissed by managers who can avoid thinking about the business model challenges by (correctly!) telling themselves that their current product is better. The problem, of course, is that the disruptive product gets better, even as the incumbent’s product becomes ever more bloated and hard to use — and that certainly sounds a lot like Google Search’s current trajectory.

Meanwhile, I was already optimistic about the impact on Meta:

> What will be interesting to see is how things like image and text generation impact Meta in the long run: [Sam Lessin has posited](<https://twitter.com/lessin/status/1551931628305502208>) that [the end-game for algorithmic timelines](<https://stratechery.com/2022/instagram-tiktok-and-the-three-trends/>) is AI content; I’ve made the same argument [when it comes to the Metaverse](<https://stratechery.com/2022/dall-e-the-metaverse-and-zero-marginal-content/>). In other words, while Meta is investing in AI to give personalized recommendations, that idea, combined with 2022’s breakthroughs, is personalized content, delivered through Meta’s channels.
>
> For now it will be interesting to see how Meta’s advertising tools develop: the entire process of both generating and A/B testing copy and images can be done by AI, and no company is better than Meta at making these sort of capabilities available at scale. Keep in mind that Meta’s advertising is primarily about the top of the funnel: the goal is to catch consumers’ eyes for a product or service or app they did not know previously existed; this means that there will be a lot of misses — the vast majority of ads do not convert — but that also means there is a lot of latitude for experimentation and iteration. This seems very well suited to AI: yes, generation may have marginal costs, but those marginal costs are drastically lower than a human.

I expanded on my Meta optimism in 2024’s [Meta’s AI Abundance](<https://stratechery.com/2024/metas-ai-abundance/>), but by 2025 things were looking dicey. First there was the Llama 4 fiasco, and then [that weird interview I did with Mark Zuckerberg](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-meta-ceo-mark-zuckerberg-about-ai-and-the-evolution-of-social-media/>) where I felt like I had a clearer vision of Meta’s AI opportunity than he did; a few months later and Zuckerberg overhauled Meta’s entire AI team, bringing in talent like Gross and many others, and declaring that [Personal Superintelligence was the company’s new mission](<https://www.meta.com/superintelligence/>):

> Meta’s vision is to bring personal superintelligence to everyone. We believe in putting this power in people’s hands to direct it towards what they value in their own lives. This is distinct from others in the industry who believe superintelligence should be directed centrally towards automating all valuable work, and then humanity will live on a dole of its output. At Meta, we believe that people pursuing their individual aspirations is how we have always made progress expanding prosperity, science, health, and culture. This will be increasingly important in the future as well. The intersection of technology and how people live is Meta’s focus, and this will only become more important in the future…
>
> Meta believes strongly in building personal superintelligence that empowers everyone. We have the resources and the expertise to build the massive infrastructure required, and the capability and will to deliver new technology to billions of people across our products. I’m excited to focus Meta’s efforts towards building this future.

That vision is all well and good; what has been much more concerning to me is the prospect of ChatGPT actually being much more of a threat to Meta than to Google. First off, there is simply the matter of time; time is the only scarce resource on the Internet, and every minute spent in ChatGPT is a minute that is no longer available to Meta. Second, a product like Sora was [much more of an assault on Meta’s core use case than Google’s](<https://stratechery.com/2025/sora-ai-bicycles-and-meta-disruption/>).

Sora has, in the end, not amounted to much; it’s 77th in the App Store, even behind Meta AI at 68 (and, for the record, I still find Vibes more interesting, although I rarely use it). OpenAI [did make that deal with Disney](<https://stratechery.com/2025/disney-and-openai-totems-in-an-ai-world-google-versus-the-world/>), so they haven’t given up on Sora, but I think it’s enough to note that just because Meta dominates time spent — and may have seen off this threat — it’s not guaranteed they will dominate time spent forever. There are potential weak points.

To that end, the best defense is often a good offense, and OpenAI has weaknesses of its own. Specifically, [the accidental consumer tech company](<https://stratechery.com/2023/the-accidental-consumer-tech-company-chatgpt-meta-and-product-market-fit-aggregation-and-apis/>) is, by all accounts, still starved for compute, and has announced audacious infrastructure plans. Those plans, however, have boomeranged on the company, as the primary response has been questions about how exactly OpenAI will pay for everything, particularly given the fact the company still doesn’t have a scalable advertising model.

A scalable advertising model is exactly what Meta has, however, and the free cash flow that comes with it. The question for OpenAI is if they can not just raise money but make money; the question for Meta is what Mark Zuckerberg is willing to commit to. And, well, he’s committing to building more compute than OpenAI, and critically, has a credible way to pay for it.

To put it another way, I view the Meta Compute initiative as a bet that the limiting factor in AI will be infrastructure. Meta may not have state of the art models today, but the company is betting that that can be overcome, as long as you have enough compute to leverage them, which, by extension, means a business model to fund them. That’s exactly what Meta has — and remember, Zuckerberg has claimed that the company already [sees positive ROI from more compute](<https://stratechery.com/2025/google-earnings-meta-earnings-the-cost-of-reality-labs/>) (and I believe him).

### The Reality Labs Sacrifice

However, Wall Street doesn’t necessarily believe Zuckerberg, if the reaction to last quarter’s earnings is any indication; [I wrote at the time](<https://stratechery.com/2025/google-earnings-meta-earnings-the-cost-of-reality-labs/>) that this lack of faith was the true cost of Reality Labs.

> What I also think is happening is that Meta is finally paying the price for the billions of dollars it has spent on Reality Labs with precious little financial return to show for it. Yes, investors have long grumbled at how much money Meta has spent on AR and VR, but ultimately I think Zuckerberg has gotten a pass; even the stock market freakout in 2022 was more about TikTok and ATT than about the Metaverse (the Metaverse spending was taken as an indication that Meta’s core business was faltering).
>
> This time, however, Zuckerberg once again seems to be asking for a blank check, and given that the last blank check is growing, not cashing, I think that investors are hesitant about writing another one. Again, I’m not sure this is necessarily fair — Meta is clearly leveraging more compute effectively in its ad business, and AI is not the Metaverse — but unless Zuckerberg wants to become the world’s biggest ad business salesman he’s going to need to show some tangible evidence sooner or later that Meta can in fact win on product.

Given this, I’m not at all surprised by [this news from Bloomberg](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-13/meta-begins-jobs-cuts-after-shifting-focus-from-metaverse-to-phones>):

> Meta Platforms Inc. is beginning to cut more than 1,000 jobs from the company’s Reality Labs division, part of a plan to redirect resources from virtual reality and metaverse products toward AI wearables and phone features. Affected employees will be notified of the layoffs starting Tuesday morning, according to an internal post from Chief Technology Officer Andrew Bosworth that was reviewed by Bloomberg News. The cuts are expected to hit roughly 10% of employees within the Reality Labs group, which has about 15,000 workers, Bloomberg reported earlier this week. As part of the reduction, Meta is pivoting its metaverse efforts to focus on mobile devices, according to Bosworth’s memo. The company is also planning to cut back on its virtual reality investments to make the business “more sustainable,” Bosworth wrote.

I wouldn’t say this is an admission of failure when it comes to the Metaverse; there will still be somewhere around 13–14,000 employees working on the initiative, albeit with much more of a focus on augmented reality and mobile, and a shift in focus away from VR and gaming.

Rather, I interpret this as an acceptance of reality and the fact that while Zuckerberg may have total control of Meta, he still does, at the end of the day, answer to shareholders. Trimming Reality Labs spending has always been a card that Meta could play to allay investor concerns; that the company announced it is doing just that on the same day that Zuckerberg announced Meta Compute is surely not a coincidence.
