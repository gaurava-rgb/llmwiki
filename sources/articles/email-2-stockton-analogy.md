he program created to stabilize the banking system in the wake of the **Silicon Valley Bank (SVB)** collapse was called the **Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP)** .

Here’s how it worked and what made it distinct:

* * *

###  🏦 **Bank Term Funding Program (BTFP)** — _March 2023_

**Created by:**
The **Federal Reserve** , with support from the **Treasury Department** and the **FDIC** , on **March 12, 2023** , two days after SVB’s failure.

**Purpose:**
To prevent a nationwide bank run by giving banks **instant liquidity** so they wouldn’t have to sell government bonds or mortgage-backed securities at massive losses.

* * *

###  💡 **Mechanism**

  * Banks could **borrow from the Fed** for up to **one year** , using **U.S. Treasuries, agency debt, or mortgage-backed securities** as collateral.
  * Crucially, the Fed **valued that collateral at par (face value)** , **not** at the depressed market value — effectively neutralizing the unrealized losses that triggered SVB’s collapse.
  * The Treasury set aside **$25 billion** from the Exchange Stabilization Fund as a backstop, though actual usage was far larger (peaking above $160 billion).



* * *

###  🧾 **Separate but Related: Deposit Guarantee**

  * Simultaneously, the **FDIC** , **Federal Reserve** , and **Treasury** invoked the **“systemic risk exception”** under the Federal Deposit Insurance Act.
  * This allowed the government to **guarantee _all_ deposits ** at Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank — **including those above the $250,000 FDIC limit** — to prevent contagion across regional banks.

##  ⚖️ **Systemic Risk Exception (SRE): What It Is**

  * **Created under:** The **Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation Improvement Act (FDICIA) of 1991** , passed after the **Savings and Loan crisis** of the 1980s.
  * **Purpose:** To _override_ the normal rule that limits the FDIC to protecting only insured deposits (up to the FDIC cap, now $250,000).
  * **Trigger condition:** It can only be invoked if the failure of an institution **“poses systemic risk”** to the financial system — i.e., threatens contagion or collapse beyond the single bank.



To activate it, three key entities must agree:

  1. **Two-thirds of the FDIC Board of Directors** ,
  2. **Two-thirds of the Federal Reserve Board** , and
  3. The **Treasury Secretary** , in consultation with the President.



* * *

##  📜 **How Often Has It Been Used?**

**Only twice before Silicon Valley Bank.**

###  1️⃣ **2008 – IndyMac and Wachovia (initial discussions, not fully used)**

  * The _potential_ use was debated but not formally invoked until later that year.



###  2️⃣ **2008 – Citigroup and Bank of America**

  * The **SRE was officially invoked in 2008** to allow **guarantees of non-deposit liabilities** (like senior debt and interbank funding) during the **Global Financial Crisis** .
  * It authorized programs such as:
    * The **Temporary Liquidity Guarantee Program (TLGP)** , which guaranteed bank debt and full coverage of non-interest-bearing transaction accounts.
    * This was a massive step — it effectively **expanded government backing beyond insured deposits** .



* * *

##  💡 **Then Silence for 15 Years**

After 2009, the SRE went unused for over a decade — regulators were wary of moral hazard and the political backlash from “bailouts.”

Then, suddenly:

###  3️⃣ **2023 – Silicon Valley Bank & Signature Bank **

  * Invoked on **March 12, 2023** — the first use since the 2008 crisis.
  * Allowed the FDIC to **guarantee _all_ deposits ** , including the 90% that were uninsured at SVB (many held by startups and payroll accounts).
  * The explicit goal: **prevent a digital-era bank run** that could have cascaded across dozens of regional banks within days.

In short:

> The **Systemic Risk Exception** had been used _only once before_ (2008) — and only for the largest banks.
>  Its **2023 use for mid-sized banks (SVB and Signature)** was historic, signaling that in the digital age, _“systemic”_ now means _speed_ , not size.
