Earlier this year, [I interviewed Manna founder and CEO Bobby Healy about his startup’s foray into drone delivery](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-manna-founder-and-ceo-bobby-healy-about-drone-delivery/>); Jon at [Asianometry](<https://asianometry.passport.online/>) went to Ireland to visit Manna and [made a video about his experience](<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BBDnOgFpgI4>).

On to the Update:

### Meta Vibes

From [The Verge](<https://www.theverge.com/news/786499/meta-ai-vibes-feed-discover-videos>):

> On Thursday, Meta AI launched a new feed of short-form, AI-generated videos called Vibes. The feed is designed to encourage users to remix AI-generated videos that come from “creators and communities,” the company said. Users can post their own AI-generated videos to Vibes or cross-post the videos across Meta’s platforms for friends and followers to see. While AI-generated content appears, well, everywhere, the Vibes feed dedicated to AI-generated videos appears only on the Meta AI app.

After recording [Thursday’s Sharp Tech](<https://sharptech.fm/member/episode/videos-ongoing-victory-over-text-google-and-you-tubes-abundance-of-opportunity-six-questions-about-an-ai-bubble>), in which I was pretty critical of Meta’s inability to ship AI-related features (as compared to YouTube), Andrew and I decided to record [an emergency podcast](<https://sharptech.fm/member/episode/emergency-pod-instant-reactions-to-meta-ai-vibes>) about the Vibes launch, and I’m glad we did, as it ensured my reaction was genuine and not tainted by the almost universal disdain that was poured on Meta over the weekend.

That’s because I really like Vibes; more than that, I’m really intrigued by where it points to in the future.

### Good Vibes

The Verge article I linked to played it by the book; [TechCrunch](<https://techcrunch.com/2025/09/25/meta-launches-vibes-a-short-form-video-feed-of-ai-slop/>) was a bit more opinionated:

> In a move no one asked for, Meta is introducing “Vibes,” a new feed in the Meta AI app and on meta.ai for sharing and creating short-form, AI-generated videos. Think TikTok or Instagram Reels, but every single video you come across is essentially AI slop…
>
> The new feed likely won’t be welcomed by users, especially since the rise of AI technology has caused social media platforms to become flooded with AI slop. The problem has become so widespread that companies like YouTube are now looking to crack down on the issue. This makes Meta’s move particularly puzzling, given that the company said earlier this year that it was tackling “unoriginal” Facebook content and advised creators that they should focus on “authentic storytelling,” not short videos offering little value.

I get the sentiment here — again, this is pretty representative of almost all of the reactions I saw — but I think it misses three important things.

First, this is obviously a matter of subjective opinion, but I think the “slop” in Vibes is like a Chipotle burrito bowl: sure it’s slop, but it’s really good slop, and I wanted to consume more, not less. Chief AI Officer Alexandr Wang was honest about where the aesthetic came from:

> For this early version, we’ve partnered with Midjourney and Black Forest Labs while we continue developing our own models behind the scenes.
>
> — Alexandr Wang (@alexandr_wang) [September 25, 2025](<https://twitter.com/alexandr_wang/status/1971295159427145941?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw>)

I’m not super familiar with [Black Forest Labs](<https://bfl.ai/>), but the overall aesthetic of Vibes absolutely is downstream from MidJourney’s opinionated aesthetics. More than that, however, is what seems to be a clear focus — particularly in the videos Vibes was seeded with — on images that are some combination of beautiful and aspirationally futuristic, with a dash of cuteness interspersed. This very much gibes with what MidJourney founder and CEO David Holz told me was his goal; from [a 2022 Stratechery Interview](<https://stratechery.com/2022/an-interview-with-midjourney-founder-david-holz-about-generative-ai-vr-and-silicon-valley/>):

> I had this goal, which was we needed to somehow create a more imaginative world. I mean, one of the biggest risks in the world I think is a collapse in belief, a belief in ourselves, a belief in the future. And part of that I think comes from a lack of imagination, a lack of imagination of what we can be, lack of imagination of what the future can be. And so this imagination thing I think is an important pillar of something that we need in the world. And I was thinking about this and I saw this, I’m like, “I can turn this into a force that can expand the imagination of the human species.” It was what we put on our company thing now. And that felt realistic. So that was really exciting.

MidJourney has been succeeding at this for a while, but has been buried on Discord; Meta is more than happy to take the concept and bring it to the world, and I think they succeeded. There is not currently a way to see your “likes” or easily export the videos, so to get a taste for the app you’ll have to rely on this collection of screenshots I sent to a group chat Thursday night:

![A collection of screenshots from MetaAI Vibes](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/meta-vibes-1.png?resize=1330%2C708&ssl=1)

What you don’t get from these screenshots is what a difference movement and music makes. Video really is so much more compelling than anything else, and Meta married the videos to pop anthems (you can choose a music clip when you make your own); it makes a big difference in the experience. I did — and still do — find the app very enjoyable to use.

Second, yes, AI content is increasingly infiltrating other feeds, including short-form video, but not only do I hate AI content in those feeds, the Vibes feed actually made clear to me why it is so off-putting. Go back to a point I made [a few years ago](<https://stratechery.com/2021/twitter-has-a-new-ceo-what-about-a-new-business-model/>) about Instagram versus Twitter:

> It’s more than just the medium though; it’s about the user’s mental state as well. Instagram is leisurely and an escape, something you do when you’re procrastinating; Twitter is intense and combative, and far more likely to be tied to something happening in the physical world, whether that be watching sports or politics or doing work:
>
> ![Instagram is a lean-back experience; on Twitter you lean forward](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/11/twitter-1.png?resize=857%2C500&ssl=1)
>
> This matters for advertising, particularly advertising that depends on a direct response: when you are leaning back and relaxed why not click through to that Shopify site to buy that knick-knack you didn’t even know you needed, or try out that mobile game? When you are leaning forward, though, you don’t have either the time or the inclination.

One of the reasons why AI slop is so annoying is — paradoxically — the fact that a lot of it has gotten quite good. That means that when consuming content you have to continually be ascertaining if what you see is real or AI-generated; to put in the terms of the Article I just quoted, you might want to lean back, but if you don’t want to be taken in or make a fool of yourself then you have to constantly be leaning forward to figure out what is or isn’t AI.

What this means for Vibes is the fact it is unapologetically and explicitly all AI is quite profound: it’s a true lean-back experience, where the fact none of it is real is a point of interest and — if Holz is right — inspiration and imagination. I find it quite relaxing to consume, in a way I don’t find almost any other feed on my phone. It’s also notable that to the extent that AI is most compelling as a new feed — as opposed to extra content for existing feeds — is the extent to which Meta can profit; should Vibes become popular (which is very much to be determined) that means an entirely new source of inventory (for AI-generated ads, natch).

Third — and this is the part that got me very excited — my immediate reaction upon using Vibes the first time is that this was Meta’s first VR product. Part of this goes back to point 2: the fact that Vibes isn’t asking me to distinguish between what is real and what is AI is another way of saying that Vibes is unapologetically virtual. More than that, however, Vibes felt like the first taste of what I wrote about in 2022’s [DALL-E, the Metaverse, and Zero Marginal Cost Content](<https://stratechery.com/2022/dall-e-the-metaverse-and-zero-marginal-content/>):

> What is fascinating about DALL-E is that it points to a future where these three trends can be combined. DALL-E, at the end of the day, is ultimately a product of human-generated content, just like its GPT-3 cousin. The latter, of course, is about text, while DALL-E is about images. Notice, though, that progression from text to images; it follows that machine learning-generated video is next. This will likely take several years, of course; video is a much more difficult problem, and responsive 3D environments more difficult yet, but this is a path the industry has trod before:
>
>   * Game developers pushed the limits on text, then images, then video, then 3D
>   * Social media drives content creation costs to zero first on text, then images, then video
>   * Machine learning models can now create text and images for zero marginal cost
>

>
> In the very long run this points to a metaverse vision that is much less deterministic than your typical video game, yet much richer than what is generated on social media. Imagine environments that are not drawn by artists but rather created by AI: this not only increases the possibilities, but crucially, decreases the costs.

The snippets of new worlds in Vibes left me wanting more: I want to visit these fantastical worlds, and wander around. They are exciting precisely because they are unreal, literally.

More generally, it occurs to me what a mistake it has been to devolve our definition of virtual reality down to the device something is displayed on; the truth about most VR applications, including games, is that they are little different than applications we might as well use on a flat screen. Vibes, on the other hand, isn’t available in any headset, but the experience on the flat screen of my phone feels like something new; I don’t know any way to explain it other than what I just said: it’s the first VR application from Meta in a way that all of my other Quest experiences just don’t compare to.

### Vibes Vision

There are, needless to say, reasons to be concerned about Vibes. While other AI products can point to productivity gains or explicit positive impacts on the real world, the virtual nature of Vibes is its own condemnation: if you thought that short form video of other humans was addicting, what if we remove the last vestiges of the real world and just make a closed loop?

Indeed, this is part of why Vibes seems like a risky move: it’s possible that, as the TechCrunch article contends, no one wants this; on the other hand, what seemed to underly a lot of the criticism of Vibes is the concern that people will want it too much! What does seem true is that this fundamental paradox underlying critiques of this sort speaks to a fundamental unease and even hostility that a lot of folks have to AI, and they may have a point.

Meta, however, more than any other company, seeks to serve people’s revealed preferences, not their stated ones, for better or for worse — and is fairly impervious to criticism along the way; I have already explicitly contrasted this quality with [the wobbliness of OpenAI when GPT-5 launched](<https://stratechery.com/2025/chatgpt-5-product-trade-offs-personality-and-model-upgrades/>):

> However, that doesn’t mean that OpenAI was wrong to make the changes. That depends! Specifically, I mentioned above that one of the most important lessons for a consumer app developer to learn is to trust the data. The canonical example here was Facebook’s introduction of the News Feed, which led to massive protests, including outside their offices in Palo Alto; there was a huge amount of pressure on CEO Mark Zuckerberg to undo the change, which people felt violated their privacy. What Zuckerberg and Facebook saw, however, was that the data showed that people actually loved the News Feed, so they stuck with it, and it became the foundation of their entire business model and the default way that information is presented across the entire web.
>
> To that end, the big question I have for OpenAI is what drove this change? The company had a clear vision for the way that ChatGPT should be organized going forward, one that, as I wrote above, increased the quality of the user experience for most people. Moreover, Altman shared at least one piece of data — the increase in reasoning model usage — that showed that the change worked. Now, however, within a matter of days, and in the face of very loud complainers on social media, the company has backtracked. Sure, Auto is still the default, but everything is more confusing than it should be — and 4o, which is a pretty dumb model comparatively speaking, is back by default. Did the company have clear usage data that people were fleeing ChatGPT en masse? Were subscriptions being canceled at an elevated rate (although, as noted above, a certain class of Plus subscribers did have a valid complaint)? Or was it just the loudest of the complainers?

A common question that has come up on Twitter over the weekend is why anyone who wanted to work on AI would want to work for a company that launched Vibes, and this excerpt — and the quality of the product — might help answer the question.

First, Meta can still build a good product. Vibes may not end up being for everyone — I hesitate to generalize too broadly from what I like, given my rather esoteric media consumption habits — but I think it is executed very well, with a strong and unique point of view. That sort of product sensibility is something that has generally been missing in AI.

Second, Meta has the courage and conviction to lean into what AI can uniquely do. There are a lot of things to wring one’s hands about when it comes to Vibes, but Meta is leaving that hand-wringing to the peanut gallery, and actually shipping. There’s something to be said for that.

Third, the VR point might be the answer to a question raised in a scathing memo from a departing AI researcher at Meta that was reported on in [The Information in July](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/meta-ai-researcher-warns-metastatic-cancer-afflicting-company-culture>):

> Another problem with Meta’s AI efforts, according to Blankevoort, is a lack of vision. Over the last two years, the company’s generative AI group has accumulated a list of sometimes competing responsibilities, including Llama, its Meta AI assistant and other AI products and features across its social media apps and devices. In contrast, much of OpenAI’s attention is on its hit ChatGPT chatbot, and Anthropic has been hyperfocused on developing the best AI for coding, resulting in an explosion of revenue growth this year.

Like I said, it’s fair to debate whether creating actual virtual reality is a societal good; it is, however, a distinct thing to focus on, and something that is broadly in line with the rest of Meta. I still don’t know exactly what Zuckerberg is referring to when he says he wants to build [Personal Superintelligence](<https://www.meta.com/superintelligence/>), and how exactly that is distinct from anyone else; Vibes, however, is distinct, and, for better or worse, a product that I think only Meta could make. It’s a start of a vision, in more ways than one.
