The rise of artificial intelligence has turned the software industry into a minefield for investors. To address the risks, some are relying more on a metric known as net revenue retention to pick winners and avoid losers.

In its simplest form, NRR, also known as net dollar retention, compares annual revenue from a year ago to now. The number can be useful, with one big caveat. There is no set definition of NRR, and a survey of high-scoring companies shows that they calculate the metric in different ways, often to flatter their own numbers. High NRRs are often cited as evidence that a company is succeeding with AI.

Software company Figma’s stunning initial public offering last month was driven in part by its NRR, which at 132% for the March quarter was the highest for any publicly listed U.S. enterprise software company that reports the figure. That number, though, isn’t quite what it seems.

Calculating NRR starts with revenue, which should be simple but isn’t. In practice, what many companies count as annual revenue is really an annual run rate, which multiplies the amount of revenue generated in a given month by 12. Other companies simply use actual annual revenue, while still others use an annualized value of all customer contracts. An NRR rate over 100% suggests that a company’s customers who have stuck around since the start of the measurement period spent more on its products in the aggregate.

NRR is important to investors because in the long run it’s more efficient for a company to grow by selling more to its existing customers than by finding new ones, said Rishi Jaluria, a software equity analyst at RBC Capital Markets. “If customers find a lot of value in you, they’re willing to spend more year after year,” he said, adding that signing up new customers can get increasingly expensive as the pool of prospective clients shrinks.

Figma’s NRR got investors excited, but comparing the company to other software makers is impossible because of the differences in how they define the metric. Here are some ways companies with high scores have gussied up their numbers.

**Cherry-Picking Customers**

Figma gets the cherry-picking prize for using just a small number of its customers to calculate its NRR. In its IPO filing, the company says it only includes customers in its revenue retention calculation that have agreements with Figma worth over $10,000 in annual recurring revenue.

That means the NRR calculation is based on spending for just 11,000 or so of Figma’s 450,000 paying customers. That 11,000 did account for 64% of Figma’s total annualized revenue for the quarter ending in March, but most other companies use all of their customers, making comparisons impossible. For example, MongoDB and Datadog, two software companies with high NRRs, include all of their paying customers in the calculation.

Figma’s NRR looks like a “questionable disclosure,” D.A. Davidson analyst Gil Luria said. “It’s possible that they do get a lot of revenue from customers under $10,000 [in annual recurring revenue], and they’re trying to steer away the conversation from how much churn there is in that cohort” by excluding them from the calculation.

A spokesperson for Figma said the company thought reporting the metric only for customers over $10,000 in annual recurring revenue “would be a more informative guidepost” for investors looking to build financial models.

Even if companies don’t try to polish their NRRs by excluding customers, some don’t disclose the data in a useful way. GitLab, which sells tools for software developers, went public in October 2021. In its IPO prospectus, it reported an exact NRR figure—148%. But in subsequent filings, it changed its approach, saying it would only share the exact number if it came in below 130%. That means investors won’t necessarily know how the metric changes in each period. GitLab did not respond to requests for comment.

**Numbers Game**

Sometimes the difference between companies comes down to simple math. Rubrik, for example, calculates its one-year NRR by averaging the numbers for each of the four most recent quarters. It doesn’t disclose the quarterly numbers. Rubrik reports its NRR in a similar way to GitLab, sharing a range or ballpark rather than precise numbers.

Datadog has a different take. Determining the NRR it shares with investors every quarter starts by calculating point-in-time NRRs each month, which it doesn’t disclose. Then, according to its head of investor relations, Yuka Broderick, it takes a weighted average of those figures, assigning more weight to better months. In a similar vein to GitLab, Datadog typically only shares a range or threshold for its reported NRR rather than a precise figure.

Data management company Snowflake measures its NRR over a trailing two-year period instead of the more standard one year. That means shifts in Snowflake’s business, good or bad, could take longer to show up in the numbers.

Snowflake’s NRR hovered around or above 170% for a couple years after it went public in 2020. In the fiscal year ending January 2023, that metric dropped sharply from 178% to 158% as higher interest rates slammed the software sector. The decline might have been more severe if Snowflake was reporting its numbers on an annual basis.

A Snowflake spokesperson said the company chose the two-year time frame because it charges customers based on usage, and its customers typically take 8 to 9 months to ramp up spending. Other companies that charge based on usage, including MongoDB and Fastly, report NRR on a 12-month basis.

Shares of software companies are down broadly as investors fear AI will hurt their business. D.A. Davidson’s Luria says NRR is a good way to separate companies that have been unfairly punished from those that might in fact be struggling. He’s impressed with companies like Snowflake and Datadog where NRR has improved from recent lows because it shows their AI products are well received.

“The baby’s been thrown out with the bathwater on a lot of software stocks,” Luria said. “If you just sit down right now and focus on the companies where the NRR is higher than it was last quarter, that’s a really good clue as to who’s doing well right now.”
