Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery interview is with [Michael Morton](<https://www.linkedin.com/in/michael-morton-98a1272b/>). Morton is a senior research analyst at [MoffettNathanson](<https://www.moffettnathanson.com/>) with a focus on e-commerce and marketplaces. Morton has worked on both the buy-side and sale-side of equity analysis, and also worked on the YouTube strategy team prior to joining MoffettNathanson. I previously interviewed Morton in [April 2024](<https://stratechery.com/2024/an-interview-with-michael-morton-about-e-commerce-winners-and-losers/>).

In this interview we dive deep into LLM’s and their potential impact on e-commerce. First, we discuss the pre-LLM structure of the e-commerce market, and why LLMs are a threat to the pull category (Google and Amazon), but not the push (Meta). Then we dive into Amazon specifically: the company is positioned to dominate LLM-driven commerce, but they have business model reasons to resist. That, by extension, presents an opportunity to challengers, particularly Walmart. This was a fascinating discussion and covers topics I expect to return to regularly.

#### E-Commerce Push vs. Pull

**Well, you’ve written two remarkable reports, just to shift to LLMs because I think the question of LLMs and how it’s going to impact e-commerce is a fascinating one. There’s so many factors that go into this, so I just kind of want to walk through these reports. But I actually want to start with something you put in report number two, which is you noted that it felt like the e-commerce stack was sort of set, we knew how it all worked. What was that? What was the e-commerce as we knew it? It wasn’t going anywhere. All we’re doing is just seeing Amazon win more and more. Walk me through the structure of the market pre-ChatGPT, and why it felt so static.**

**MM:** Yeah, that’s a great way to level set this and it was the genesis of our prior conversation. These are rounding numbers and there’s consumer surveys on this, but half the time you know what you want, you open the app or you start on amazon.com, you get a bunch of different terminology for it, seek-and-destroy or spear phishing, and you’re going in and, “I need baby wipes”, or “I know that I can get my shoe on there”, or deodorant — you go in, you put it in, you click it. Half of what you see are advertisements—

**Half might be generous on Amazon these days, but yes.**

**MM:** Yeah. So half of them are ads sold on a cost-per-click basis and the other half, you would start on Google, and these are what I would call high consideration purchases, you don’t necessarily know.

**Yeah, you’re already pretty far down the funnel.**

**MM:** You’re like, “Hey, I think I need a rain jacket”, or the example that we’ve used a lot is — and I have flat feet, I try to test the channel and the models and search a little bit to get a little bit more specific than just like “AirPods”, or just something that’s very generic and there’s only a few of them — and if you searched for “Best running shoes for men with flat feet”, you would be presented with the carousel of PLAs, like the product listing ads, at the top. There would be six of them and then below that you’d get an advertisement for Runners World, it’d be a sponsored link and you’d go to Runner’s World and it’d be a review and you’d read the review and the review would say, “Here’s the best shoes for what you’re looking for”, and there’d be a little icon and it would say, “Go buy them at Amazon”.

**Just paying out an affiliate fee.**

**MM:** Yes, paying affiliate fees. So you either start on amazon.com, or you end up on amazon.com, and we’ve written notes that all roads lead to Amazon and that is the legacy kind of, I’ll call it just, incumbent funnel, that was very locked in, pretty set in stone, but now we think it’s changing because that is a bad experience for the high consideration purchases.

**Well, let’s get to that changing, but that is the, you had this push-versus-pull framework and so what was the other half of that?**

**MM:** Yes. So with the recent note, the way I’ve been trying to think about the coming disruption to consumer Internet and to be clear, the thesis isn’t dependent on if ChatGPT wins or if it’s Gemini in everybody’s pocket or Perplexity, I don’t care about the end state.

**Siri’s coming,[white label Gemini](<https://dithering.passport.online/>), get ready.**

**MM:** I don’t care about the end state product, I do think ChatGPT has a huge runway here. I care about looking at the digital economy, and this is oversimplified and you can put the exhibit in the transcript of your email up, probably help people visualize it.

![Push, pull, and AI-driven e-commerce](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/ai-commerce-1.png?resize=906%2C941&ssl=1)

On one side of this, you have what I like to refer to as the push-based economy. That’s where you sat down, you open the app, and whether it’s Meta or you’re in Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, you name it, they’re pushing content at you.

**You’re finding all products you didn’t know existed.**

**MM:** Yes. Lean back, kind of experience.

**Which to my mind, this is the part of digital advertising[I’ve always been a massive proponent and defender of](<https://stratechery.com/2022/an-interview-with-eric-seufert-about-the-post-att-landscape/>), because the problem with search advertising, it’s kind of exploitive. A lot of times all you are is paying for placement for something that would’ve been top of organic search anyway and the fact that all these search engines let other people buy your trademark as a keyword, sort of speaks to the game.**

**On the other hand, when I’m on Instagram, and by the way, I mentioned that one of my realizations of living in America, is I thought Instagram ads were good before,[now that it has me pegged as being in America](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-openai-ceo-sam-altman-about-devday-and-the-ai-buildout/>), it’s unbelievable, I want to buy every single thing that I see. I think it’s clear Facebook’s been really amping up and pushing — every other Reel feels like it’s an ad these days — and they’re all awesome, I want all of them, it’s incredible. I hyped it up how good it was, I didn’t appreciate how good it was till I was actually geographically in the US.**

**MM:** It’s really amazing how good the push world has got at showing you the right advertisements, and in my opinion today, I think the introduction of AI augments that world from a lot of different perspectives. Meta knows what to show you and they’re better at showing you things that are going to drive engagement but even behind that, if you want to think about this as like a flow chart and on the left side you have push, at the bottom lifting it all up is the generative AI kind of stack. So more content creation, more video creation.

But the right side of this digital economy is pull and this is when you open an app and you start with a query, and this is the world that annoys you. And trust me, I can really unload on some things about this that bother me but you had a group of marketplaces that are in case studies of aggregation. [You’ve coined it](<https://stratechery.com/2015/aggregation-theory/>), and they took this fragmented supply, whether it’s OTAs, whether it’s brands and manufacturers around the world selling goods via e-commerce, whether it’s restaurants or drivers. Now, I think DoorDash and Uber are more insulated and they aggregate it and they’ve built direct relationships with the consumer and what we talked about earlier, they kind of would find their way there half the time going through Google or half the time going directly to an app. I think the models, again, whether it’s Gemini or ChatGPT—

**We’ll just use, with that caveat, we’ll just use ChatGPT as a stand-in.**

**MM:** ChatGPT. I think ChatGPT is going to wedge itself in there.

So if you’re starting in your pull experience, I just call it an informational pull, and you’re starting your journey with a query. I think ChatGPT is a big risk to your business because ChatGPT takes what historically would be these searches that would show you where things are, and now they’re telling you the knowledge behind it, and in that ecosystem of pull, it is perfectly monetized for what we can call the paradox of choice or this extended funnel. Amazon has talked about consumers clicking 14 products before making a purchase and when you throw ChatGPT in there, and we can talk about why I think the answers are better and the evidence I have behind it, I think that funnel is going to smash down.

#### The AI E-Commerce Experience

**Let’s talk about this, because this goes back to the first note that I referenced, E-commerce and Search, the Coming AI Impact and you mentioned the thesis here is that AI e-commerce experience is superior and you mentioned you got a lot of pushback on it, about people questioning about shifting consumer behavior. So you’ve mentioned this user experience angle a couple of times, walk me through your thesis and why you think that the user experience is better and why that’s going to actually drive a fundamental shift in consumer behavior.**

**MM:** Yeah, I would love to. And again, in my world, I have to bring data to the table and the joke is like, “If I can’t put it in an exhibit, I can’t write a report about it because nobody will read it”.

What we started to do is we took a couple different products and we ran them through the traditional funnel and we’ll go back to the first example I used, shoes for flat-footed runners. What I did to start the exercise was I did hours and hours of research reading literally podiatry magazine posts, and every single post about the best running shoes for flat feet, I organized them, I ranked them, so what shoes got first and second, and we came out with some clear winners. “Here are the one, two, and three best running shoes for people with flat feet”, so we know what the best answer is.

Now let’s put it in Google search, and what you found was the PLAs at the top, the carousel you’ll see a set of icons that are horrible for getting the right answer.

**So are those pure payment to get there, or is Google actually making determination of what’s the best answer?**

**MM:** Yeah, for the work we did, one of the six was of the top ranked running shoes and when you looked at the models, their slugging percentage was, I would say 60 to 80% of the time, what they showed you out of the five icons were the best running shoe. So if they had five, they’d get one bad one.

Now, that’s a good question people have pushed back, “Well, how can these people be at the top of the feed if they’re paying for it” and this inevitably boils down to a conversion game. Shouldn’t it really only be the best products? And in an ideal state, yes, but this is also an output of which websites have better conversion rates? Who has bigger marketing budgets? Who’s looking to build a brand at this specific time? No one knows a perfect answer for the weightings and outputs of Google Search. Well, there are people, but their emails have @google.com, not our email addresses.

**So why did Google’s results get like this, to the extent that you feel one out of six was a good answer? And you contrast the ChatGPT where four out of six are good answers. Is this a matter of, to your point, they’re measuring things like conversion factors, what actually goes through? Is it some people just paid more? Was this something that they can fix or is it that the money flowing in is too much that they can’t actually recommend four out of six because two, three and four might not pay them very much? What happened?**

**MM:** This is probably an hour podcast in itself, but to try to simplify it as best as possible, I think there’s a lot of influencing factors. We are all very familiar with the gamification that has occurred with search, the entire giant industry of SEO, an army of marketing consultants to tell you how to win the keyword bidding game.

**Is it your point before, that it’s because it’s a keyword search, it’s not actually a product.**

**MM:** Exactly, and [Shopify CEO] Tobi [Lütke] talked about this two or three weeks ago [on the Cheeky Pint podcast](<https://youtu.be/eMSqlQMk480>) and he said, “Look, search is traditionally, everybody, if you go talk to people on search, they read academic papers on search, they’re very focused on keywords, it’s not built around product, and product search is different”, and why the models are so much better — and because look, Google looks at it and they’re like, “Okay, this person’s bidding X amount for that impression, and if we look at their website, they get Y level of traffic and Z amount of conversion, that must mean it’s good, we’ll weight that bid”, because it’s not just the highest bid always wins, there’s a lot of other influencing factors that makes a bid stronger.

**In Google’s defense, they’re not out there just saying, “You pay the most, you get to the top”, they’re trying to do a good job.**

**MM:** Yes, there’s not some person at Google like, “Give them the bad running shoes”, so that’s just was built out and where we ended up.

Now, why are the models so much better? The fun thing about researching what I’ll call conversational commerce, I was trying to fight the good fight of pushing back on the term agentic commerce because I’m not talking about autonomous agents running around buying stuff for me.

**I like conversational commerce, I’m going to steal that, that’s great. We’re talking about humans interacting with models, and then the human is making all the decisions.**

**MM:** I’ve lost the fight, by the way. Everybody calls it agentic now, but that’s fine.

**You know what? I’m fine to jump on a losing battle, I’m with you.**

**MM:** (laughing) Ben and Mike, holding down conversational.

So in conversational commerce, why it’s better and why it’s so fun to research this evolving subject is go ask the models. So I sat down, I asked ChatGPT, I asked Gemini, I asked all the different models, “Hey, when I ask you what’s the best running shoe, what do you do?”, and they’ll tell you, “We go read all the expert websites”. That’s what Mike did, they go look at the product build materials, they go read reviews like on Amazon and Zappos, that’s a whole other fight that’s going on. And so Google is looking at this more formulaic. Who’s bidding? What’s the conversion rate? Where’s the information? The models look at all the knowledge behind that.

**They’re doing what you did. It’s basically, it’s a one-to-one analogy.**

**MM:** Yeah.

**So on that reply, you came up with the same answer.**

**MM:** Yes. And look, before I came on here today, I re-ran the exercise, and search was again one for six for the shoe. But then I did AI mode in Google for the flat-footed running shoes — basically batted perfect, just incredible.

**So that’s the question. Can Google fix this?**

**MM:** Yeah. Michael Nathanson and I, I was like the devil on his shoulder while Google was going down every day, ChatGPT is just adding users and the bear case is just building and building, building and I’m over there, I’m like, “Oh, they got a problem, Michael, they got a problem”, and Michael’s been doing this for long enough where it’s really hard in these moments to see through this overwhelming wave of negative sentiment. And the day after Google I/O, I go into Michael’s office, I’m like, “Okay, I think they’re going to run towards this problem”, and now you’re sitting on the biggest distribution network in the world, the best AI infrastructure stack, and you’ve increased the friction from moving from being a Google user to a ChatGPT user. So people like you and I were ChatGPT probably day one, my mom and wife are now just going to end up being AI overviews and AI mode and maybe never ChatGPT people. So I think Google has the tool set to win this.

**What impact is that going to have on their margins?**

**MM:** That’s the really hard question, again, something that Michael Nathanson and I have discussed a lot. So you’re going from a world of six PLAs, where they’re not that great-

**And PLA is?**

**MM:** Product listing ad, I don’t even think they call it that anymore.

**No, I just wanted to emphasize the ad word. Those are paid placements.**

**MM:** Yeah, and you’re going to go from a world where — and then you could even click the carousel and it gives you more of, so you’re going to go from that world to where it’s three icons and I’ve noticed the models, they seem to be experimenting with three to five choices.

Now, my whole point is, and there’s some research that came out of Europe two weeks ago, the conversion rate is going to increase a lot. The consumer’s going to learn, “This is a good answer”. I don’t need to go read the running review website and the podiatry website, I can trust the model and what’s going to happen is the conversion rates on the three icons is going to explode, and then what people can afford to pay for those icons goes much higher. So it’s not obvious to me, and they told you they’re monetizing at equivalent rates, I get that, I’m always cynical from what companies say, and I don’t know how to-

**Actually, on one of the last earnings calls, someone was like, “Okay, I hear you’re saying your monetizing at the same rates, what is the cost to serve on this?”, and they completely did not answer.**

**MM:** Yeah, yeah. There could be air pockets at times, but from just a one-to-one basis, when you think about what will happen to conversion rates on these answers that you get from the models, should explode over time.

**So here’s the question though. If the models are going out there, they’re reading all the reviews, they’re coming to the same conclusion that you came to if you put in hours and hours of work to search the Internet, this is an example I guess of agentic commerce because they’re actually going and doing your job for you. Where does payment even enter into this? If they already found the right one, are they only going to recommend the right one if someone paid, is willing to pay for it?**

**MM:** There was a reason that this report we recently published, Consumer Internet’s ChatGPT Problem, Episode One. We called it an Episode One just because there’s just so many questions to be answered here. And today, the checkout experience in these models isn’t great. You get dead links when you try to click on stuff, but like we always all like to say in consumer Internet is, “Today is the worst it’s going to be”, right?

Now, [OpenAI CEO] Sam [Altman], [you had him on this podcast a couple of weeks ago](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-openai-ceo-sam-altman-about-devday-and-the-ai-buildout/>), and he talked about wanting to be inclusive of the system, and if you go read the protocols on OpenAI’s website, they’re letting the merchants own the relationship. You’re buying this clearly from Etsy, you’re buying this from the Shopify merchant, Shopify is going to maintain the revenue stream from the payments business today, so in the near term, it seems fine. It introduced a lot of risk that I think we’ll probably talk about, the on site ads and this big extended funnel compressing, but I think from a payments perspective, it shouldn’t dislocate anybody’s current profit stream that they generate from that, because that’s the moneymaker for Shopify.

**Right. Well, the question though is, and this is, I think —[I’ve repented](<https://stratechery.com/2025/the-youtube-tip-of-the-google-spear/>), I think, for my Google concern, hopefully somewhat just in time and they’ve done much better than I expected, but I think you’re putting your finger on a very specific concern that I have with the shopping carousel, the fact that everything is paid. ChatGPT can afford to always recommend the best answer, and for a product like your shoes that are sold by multiple retailers, there’s that extra layer where they can favor the retailer that’s going to give them a cut. And meanwhile, for any product that is only available in one place, they can just recommend that product even if they don’t get any cut. Doing it in big specialized fulfillment centers far away from the consumers does not necessarily work and there’s not a lot of evidence of that, and the margins are razor-thin.

Now, we’ve published this chart, where on the Y-axis, it’s e-commerce penetration, and then size, and you can see the smaller industries are all really high penetrated, and then way over to the right is grocery and what I’ll call consumerables, since it’s this $2 trillion category, with a low penetration rate. And then we’ve done a similar chart, where you show EBIT margins and penetration rates and it’s really similar, apparel and all these other industries, super penetrated, really high EBIT margins. And then, way over to the side, you have grocery, low penetration, really low margins.

So they have tried everything, and to the company’s credit, they’ve been really transparent. If you look back to the beginning, in 1999, I think it was, [Amazon made an investment in a company called homegrocer.com](<https://archive.nytimes.com/www.nytimes.com/library/tech/99/05/biztech/articles/19amazon.html>), some $50 million investment. They’ve been trying grocery for a long time, it is just incredibly difficult, and they’ve tried a lot of different iterations, Whole Foods isn’t big enough footprint to make it work at scale.

So what we’ve seen with this most recent iteration is, very quietly, they put refrigerators — I’m oversimplifying, making this sound funny just for enjoyment purposes — quietly, they put refrigerators in their whole same-day fulfillment network in a thousand cities, and they’re going to 2,000 cities. Now, this is a better offering than they’ve had historically.

What we have written though is there is some big limitations to this, because what you want to do, there’s a couple of different types of grocery purchasing decisions. Instacart has its own issues, we think it’s subscale that’s a problem, when you’re competing against Amazon, Walmart, DoorDash and Uber, that’s a really big issue. But Instacart got high-single-digit millions of people’s full grocery basket, then you end up with like plus-hundred-dollar baskets, because it’s a marketplace and it’s giving you the whole offering that a grocer presents, which is 30,000 to 50,000 SKUs and the reason to get the whole basket is because people all have their own different needs. My son is allergic to eggs, I’m lactose intolerant, you need the long tail to fill a full request of a family for their weekly grocery.

This Amazon offering that they’ve put out today has a couple of thousand SKUs, it’s pretty good, you can get organic stuff that you need, it’s a very good top-off order. And Amazon talked about, I was at Groceryshop, it’s a a conference in Las Vegas, a couple of weeks ago, and went to a breakfast, the grocery person was talking, the average grocery user right now buys 14 times a month from them, the traditional US consumer went to the grocery store six times, this is incredible for purchasing frequency. You’re going to take away the reason that people have to go to the grocery store and what I’ve written about in the past, [I actually think you put an exhibit in one of your notes about it](<https://stratechery.com/2024/google-decision-follow-up-amazon-earnings/>), was how they’re eating CVS and Walgreens’ front-of-store alive. **They changed the mental calculus, where even if it’s a day, it’s like, “Okay, I will just go to Walgreens”, and at some point you realize if they say they’re going to deliver in six hours, the likelihood of you not going in six hours and then regretting not just ordering it when you needed it, it flips in your head, it’s like, “Just order it”.**

**MM:** And what you’ve seen with CVS and Walgreens is they’re closing stores, front-of-store sales are declining. **Well, I think the issue is the fact that we talked so positively about Walmart earlier, the reason why Walmart could eventually come back and get there is ultimately because of grocery, and that is the advantage they’ve had, the one that they’ve built out the best, and it is just a fundamental advantage.
