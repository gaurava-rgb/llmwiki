### ChatGPT Image 1.5

From [Bloomberg](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-16/openai-s-chatgpt-updated-to-make-images-better-and-faster>):

> OpenAI is rolling out an update to ChatGPT that’s intended to generate images better and faster, the latest move by the artificial intelligence developer to bolster its flagship chatbot amid heated competition from Alphabet Inc.’s Google. The new version of ChatGPT Images, announced on Thursday, is designed to make and edit images more precisely, as well as to spit out pictures as much as four times faster than its previous AI image-generation model. The company is also creating a new section within ChatGPT’s mobile app and website meant for people to make images, rather than just doing so in an interaction with the chatbot.
>
> OpenAI has increasingly tried to position ChatGPT as a kind of everything app that offers a search engine, voice assistant and image and video generator. But the company faces growing pressure from companies like Google, which recently introduced a powerful new AI model called Gemini 3 and also offers a popular image generator called Nano Banana. Grok, a chatbot from Elon Musk’s xAI, also offers a similar feature.

This new image generation capability — which is being labeled as ChatGPT Image 1.5 — comes on the heels of [GPT-5.2](<https://openai.com/index/introducing-gpt-5-2/>), which appears to be quite strong, although still based on a GPT-4-class base model; OpenAI’s first next generation model is expected early next year. For now ChatGPT Image 1.5 seems roughly comparable to Google’s Nano Banana Pro; Ethan Mollick said [on X](<https://x.com/emollick/status/2000996663893352947>):

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/chatgptimage15-1.png?resize=1180%2C1198&ssl=1)

I personally am not super big into the infographics, so I was mostly playing around with images, and was reminded of a point I’ve made repeatedly: one of OpenAI’s big advantages relative to Google is being better at product. “Product” can mean a few different things. An obvious example is UI, and to that end, ChatGPT gained a new “Image” top level menu item:

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/chatgptimage15-6.png?resize=1330%2C1002&ssl=1)

This new screen aims to solve a rather obvious problem with all of the AI apps: what do you use them for? All of the options on this screen are achievable through a chat interface, but you need to know what to ask for, which is actually step 2 of the process: first you have to know what is possible, and most people don’t. This screen aims to solve that: there are obvious filters you can use, and ideas for images you might want to create, like a Christmas card. Again, all of these are doable from a text interface, but there is a reason why purely text interfaces are the domain of so-called graybeards: it’s not the typing that is the problem, or even knowing what to type: it’s knowing what you _could_ type.

What was more exciting to me, however, was a totally accidental discovery; I had a thread of images I had generated in Gemini over time in the context of a long-running conversation. Basically the way it worked is that some joke or point would be made, and I would think, “I could make a funny AI image to illustrate this”, and I would open up Gemini and make the image, drop it in a chat, and we would have a laugh.

On a lark I copied that thread prompt-for-prompt into ChatGPT. As far as the quality of the images went, I favored ChatGPT on average, but thought that Gemini had the single best image of the set. What was much cooler, however, is that all of the ChatGPT images had the same characters; Gemini’s images felt like they were generated in isolation. The way that realization made me feel was fascinating: suddenly I wanted to make more images in ChatGPT, because it felt like I was telling a story thanks to the consistency of the characters. In other words, instead of simply seeing the image generation as a tool I could use when needed, the image generation became its own driver of usage.

To explain what I mean — I don’t want to use the images from that chat, as they contain some personally identifiable information — I tried to recreate the general idea with a romantic story about Barry and Alice; the prompts were identical. ChatGPT is on the left, and Gemini on the right:

`Generate an anime image of a man named Barry delivering flowers to a Chinese woman named Alice. The woman has long brown hair and round glasses.`

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/chatgptimage15-2.png?resize=1330%2C746&ssl=1)

`Generate an anime image of Barry scarfing down items labeled "Alice's takes" as Alice works feverishly to generate more.`

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/chatgptimage15-3.png?resize=1330%2C430&ssl=1)

`Generate an anime image of Barry as a warrior defending Princess Alice.`

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/chatgptimage15-4.png?resize=1330%2C428&ssl=1)

`Generate an image of Barry and Alice riding a tandem bicycle along the beach`

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/12/chatgptimage15-5.png?resize=1330%2C746&ssl=1)

You can see the threads here — [ChatGPT](<https://chatgpt.com/share/69424071-2874-8011-bd7a-62cd18925239>) and [Gemini](<https://gemini.google.com/share/d97667702e68>) — but the images make clear what I’m talking about. ChatGPT makes you want to continue the adventures of Barry and Alice, who are consistent throughout the thread; Gemini is generating perfectly fine images, but by not incorporating the context of the previous images it doesn’t feel like something you would come back to unless you have a specific need. In short, it’s more than the model: it’s how that model is served, and how it anticipates the needs of the user that matters as well (notice that I never asked for the characters to be consistent in either case — but I liked when they were!).

To summarize, one role of product is to show you what you can do; another role is to inspire you to come up with more of your own ideas. ChatGPT is addressing both in a way that Gemini isn’t, and details like this are a big part of the reason why [ChatGPT still has a chance to withstand the Google behemoth](<https://stratechery.com/2025/google-nvidia-and-openai/>).

### Apple v. Epic, Continued

From [Bloomberg](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-11/apple-loses-appeals-court-ruling-in-app-store-fight-with-epic>):

> Apple Inc. lost its challenge to a judge’s rebuke for disobedience but won a new chance to argue for charging developers on transactions that are made outside of the App Store. In Apple’s long-running dispute with Epic Games Inc., a federal appeals court Thursday rejected the iPhone maker’s challenge to an April ruling that it willfully defied an order from a judge who concluded the company engaged in anticompetitive conduct in violation of California law. But in its 54-page ruling, the 9th US Circuit Court of Appeals directed US District Judge Yvonne Gonzalez Rogers to consider letting Apple collect some commission from developers for use of its intellectual property — just not at the 27% level that the company initially imposed.

This is exactly what I predicted after Judge Gonzalez Rogers’ contempt ruling; from a [May 2025 Update](<https://stratechery.com/2025/apple-held-in-contempt-the-crux-of-the-case-the-takings-clause/>):

> This right here is the crux of the entire case, both in terms of this decision and almost certainly Apple’s appeal. As I noted, Judge Gonzalez Rogers makes a compelling case that Apple only created an internal evaluation of the value of its intellectual property after it had already decided to charge a 27% commission for purchases made outside of the App Store; given that, it’s understandable that the judge dismissed Apple’s justification and held the company in contempt. The problem is that by throwing out Apple’s implementation and declaring a free-for-all, the Judge implicitly declared that the value of Apple’s intellectual property was zero, and/or simply “took” Apple’s intellectual property.

The Appeals Court wrote in [its decision](<https://aboutblaw.com/bkoj>):

> In our view, as the April 30 Order is written, it is more like a punitive criminal contempt sanction than a civil contempt sanction or modification of the Injunction. The biggest problem with the commission prohibition is that it permanently prohibits the compensation that Apple can receive for linked-out purchases of digital products, regardless of whether the commission is itself prohibitive…
>
> Rather than coercing Apple to comply with the spirit of the Injunction with a reasonable, non-prohibitive commission, the district court used blunt force to ban all commissions, abusing its discretion. Apple must be able to purge its civil contempt by complying with the Injunction. It is true, as discussed above, that the power to charge commissions on linked-out purchases is effectively the power to prohibit those purchases. But it is not true that the Injunction enjoins any commission and any fee. It bars only prohibitive commissions or fees. The district court’s commission prohibition goes too far by denying Apple any way to purge its contempt by, for example, imposing a non-prohibitive, reasonable commission or fee to ensure security and privacy for users. This was an abuse of the district court’s discretion.

I wasn’t completely correct, as I based my analysis on the Takings Clause, which the Appeals Court rejected as an argument; the overall point, however, remains: apps do use Apple IP, which Apple has the right to charge for. It can’t be 27%, but it also can’t be 0%. And so, the case continues, back in the court of Judge Gonzalez Rogers, who will have to figure out what that number should be.
