### Talent Wars

Speaking of the NBA, here’s the first of three seemingly unrelated stories. First, from [ESPN](<https://www.espn.com/nba/story/_/id/45539931/buss-family-agrees-sell-lakers-mark-walter-sources-say>):

> The Buss family is entering into an agreement to sell majority ownership of the Los Angeles Lakers to Mark Walter for a franchise valuation of approximately $10 billion, sources told ESPN on Wednesday, the highest ever for a U.S. professional sports franchise. The Buss family will keep a minority share of the team, just over 15%, for a period of time, a source said.

Second, there was a funny moment in [an Interview I did with Gregory Allen last fall](<https://stratechery.com/2024/an-interview-with-gregory-allen-about-transforming-u-s-defense/>):

> **Gregory Allen:** So what’s interesting to me is that if you look at the budget for this program, the Air Force is going to spend more on the Collaborative Combat Aircraft program over the next 2 years than all the precursor AI and autonomy efforts and all the DOD spent in the last 10, and when it gets to the full ramp in like 3 years from now, they’re expecting to spend $3 billion a year buying these aircraft.
>
> **So here’s the problem. $3 billion a year is like the bathroom facilities in the next data center for one of these large language models. I get your point, you have a great chart that shows this explosion in spending:**
>
>> [![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/talent-wars-2.png?resize=1330%2C970&ssl=1)](<https://www.csis.org/analysis/department-defenses-collaborative-combat-aircraft-program-good-news-bad-news-and>)
>
> **But when you sent the chart to me, I looked at the left side and I looked again, I’m like, “Is that only 3 billion?”.**
>
> **GA:** Yes.
>
> **Am I totally warped by tech here?**
>
> **GA:** The DOD is $800 billion a year, and then like half of that is salaries and facilities.

Third, from the [Financial Times](<https://www.ft.com/content/634b7ec5-10c3-44d3-ae49-2a5b9ad566fa>) in February:

> Spending by the four leading US tech companies surged 63 per cent to historic levels last year. Now executives are vowing to accelerate their AI investments, dismissing concerns about the vast sums being bet on the nascent technology. Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon and Meta have reported combined capital expenditure of $246bn in 2024, up from $151bn in 2023. They forecast spending could exceed $320bn this year as they compete to build data centres and fill them with clusters of specialised chips to remain at the forefront of AI large language model research.

These three stories are context for this detail from the [Wall Street Journal article](<https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/meta-ai-recruiting-mark-zuckerberg-5c231f75>) I linked to in [yesterday’s Article](<https://stratechery.com/2025/checking-in-on-ai-and-the-big-five/>):

> Mark Zuckerberg is spending his days firing off emails and WhatsApp messages to the sharpest minds in artificial intelligence in a frenzied effort to play catch-up. He has personally reached out to hundreds of researchers, scientists, infrastructure engineers, product stars and entrepreneurs to try to get them to join a new Superintelligence lab he’s putting together. Some of the people who have received the messages were so surprised they didn’t believe it was really Zuckerberg. One person assumed it was a hoax and didn’t respond for several days. And Meta’s chief executive isn’t just sending them cold emails. Zuckerberg is also offering hundreds of millions of dollars, sums of money that would make them some of the most expensive hires the tech industry has ever seen.

Tech, as I noted in that interview with Allen, is used to very big numbers. Those numbers, however, are usually seen in the context of company revenue and profits; the new addition over the last few years has been sky-rocketing capex numbers. This has given investors some indigestion, as those capital expenditures will take years to fully work through, diluting earnings via depreciation whether or not the investments pay off.

At the same time, it’s hard to begrudge tech companies capex in theory: the entire reason why the industry is so attractive from an investment perspective is the idea that money put in up-front pays off with zero marginal cost goods in the long-run. Of course AI challenges that theory as well, given the costs of inference, plus the fact that GPUs in particular are not particularly long-lived assets. Overall, however, tech is about leverage, and what has been so compelling financially is that the upside is larger than any other industry, even as the inputs are lower than you might think.

That $188 million is for one year; it’s a pure marginal cost for players to play games this year, which is to say that next year you have to pay them all over again. Luka Dončić, for example, is eligible for a four-year $229 million extension (although he will probably sign for three years with a player option, so he can sign an even larger contract in 2028); LeBron James has a $52.6 million player option.

What is fascinating about these salary figures is that in some cases they are actually too low; in the NBA there are a very small number of players that impact winning to an extraordinary degree, and Dončić is one of them (and obviously James was for many years). However, the NBA’s collective bargaining agreement specifies maximum salaries, which actually limits how much the very best players earn — particularly younger players — to the benefit of everyone else. Back in 2022, [Henry Abbot of True Hoop attempted to chart “Bonus Wins”](<https://www.truehoop.com/p/the-dollar-value-of-a-winning-nba>), i.e. wins that players generated above-and-beyond what their salary dictated:

> NBA regular-season wins last year cost about $3.35 million each…we’ve invented an intuitive new stat to assess the value of every NBA contract, combining highly respected, battle-tested on-court analytics with commonsense financial analysis: Bonus Wins.
>
> [![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/talent-wars-1.png?resize=1330%2C1142&ssl=1)](<https://www.truehoop.com/p/the-dollar-value-of-a-winning-nba>)

Back in 2022 Dončić was still on his rookie contract, which is why his surplus was extraordinarily large, but even James, in his 18th season, was still generating surplus wins; needless to say, a key to winning in the NBA is finding players who generate significant wins in excess of their salaries. That’s why the most important players are the very best players (who are underpaid), and unusually valuable role players who contribute in excess of their much lower salaries (which means they are often on their rookie contracts); the Oklahoma City Thunder, for example, had six players on rookie contracts, including Jalen Williams, their second best player.

Still, NBA players by-and-large get what they deserve, for two reasons: first, the NBA collective bargaining agreement mandates that players get 50% of team revenue, and second, performance is somewhat measurable — that’s why Abbott could come up with his chart in the first place.

### AI Money

That has traditionally not been the case in tech, which is to say that the best engineers in tech have long been severely underpaid. Anyone who works in tech knows that there are a few people that truly move the needle to the tune of billions of dollars of value; much of that value, however, is hard to measure precisely, and it’s often context specific to a single company. And, it should be noted, tech company executives have also actively sought to depress salaries. One of the more notorious examples was Steve Jobs:

> Steve Jobs emails Adobe's CEO
>
> May 26, 2005 [pic.twitter.com/PMaataxTrO](<https://t.co/PMaataxTrO>)
>
> — Internal Tech Emails (@TechEmails) [July 16, 2023](<https://twitter.com/TechEmails/status/1680654024578854912?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw>)

Those emails are from the [High-Tech Employee Antitrust Litigation](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-Tech_Employee_Antitrust_Litigation>), an antitrust action taken by the DOJ against Adobe, Apple, Google, Intel, Intuit, Pixar, LucasFilm, and eBay alleging coordinated employee compensation suppression; the companies settled, with Adobe, Apple, Google, and Intel paying a combined $415 million, and the other companies $20 million. It’s a number that was almost certainly too low: after all, Zuckerberg is reportedly offering $100 million plus for a single person!

Now granted, the AI opportunity is extraordinary — thus the capex numbers cited above — but what is also notable is that the opportunity space is, at least for now, independent of individual company context. It’s also somewhat measurable, flawed though LLM leaderboards may be. That combination — massive potential upside, company-independent context, and measurable output — explains why Zuckerberg is suddenly throwing around NBA-level money. And, to be honest, it’s probably still not as much as some of these people are worth.

At the same time, this is another reason, above-and-beyond sky-rocketing capex and marginal inference costs, why AI is one of the more risky bets the industry has collectively made: having to pay talent what they are actually worth is not how tech has traditionally operated, but AI really is changing everything.
