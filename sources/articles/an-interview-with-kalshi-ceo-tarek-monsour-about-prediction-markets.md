Good morning,

This Stratechery Interview is another installment of the Stratechery Founder series; as a reminder, one of the challenges in covering startups is the lack of available data. My solution is to go in the opposite direction and interview founders directly, letting them give their subjective overview of their companies, while pressing them on their business model, background, and long-term potential.

Today’s Interview is with Kalshi co-founder and CEO [Tarek Mansour](<https://x.com/mansourtarek_>). Kalshi is a prediction market, where you can bet on future events happening. Kalshi came to prominence in the 2024 election where it hosted a huge amount of activity on the Presidential election outcome, and proved to be more accurate than polls. Kalshi’s markets, however, go far beyond politics, covering everything from inflation data to sports outcomes. Kalshi itself is unique in that it is the only prediction market that has been approved for operations in the United States.

In this interview we discuss Mansour’s background in math and finance, and why the 2016 election helped convince him of the need for prediction markets. We talk about the Kalshi origin story, it’s path to being regulated, and how the company almost died under the previous administration. We then talk about the challenge of building new markets, and the balance between retail investors and market-making ones. We also discuss how sports has come to dominate Kalshi’s revenue, why Kalshi believes that new knowledge from insider trading isn’t worth destroying people’s sense of fairness about a new kind of financial company, and why prediction markets are essential for preserving market access to the general population.

#### The Kalshi Origin Story

**I was going to ask you, when did the finance angle come in?**

**TM:** It’s like, “Where are some of the smart people in math and what are the problems they’re excited about?” and, “Where can you do a lot of probability and this type of applied math?”, and it is in finance. That’s one of the really common applications, so I started spending time doing internships and all of that in finance.

One of the places that I — well, I first started at Goldman and it was really interesting. First, I did structured credit, so high yield bonds, that was cool, then I did equity derivatives and specifically equity exotics, that was really cool. I really liked that because there the vast majority of demand in the desk was essentially this notion of, “I’m trying to price something that may happen in the world that may impact me”, it was 2016, so there was two big, massive events. It [was Brexit](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_United_Kingdom_European_Union_membership_referendum>) and then the election, [the first Trump election](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2016_United_States_presidential_election>) and this was sort of when the early innings of Kalshi starts forming because the vast majority of the demand was basically, I want to get exposure to Brexit or hedge against Brexit and I want to get exposure to Trump and hedge against Trump.

**And, “Why could you just put a contract on it directly?”**

**TM:** It was just so obvious, and I didn’t even think about it immediately. It was first, “Okay, how do we do it?”, and then we would create bundles, so the specific trade that was a Trump trade that summer that we sold loads of was essentially you create a calendar spread, so it’s two options, one that expires right before the election and one that expires right after, it’s like the latest option that expires before the election and the earliest option that expires after and you go long one and short one so that you have an effective short on the S&P, that was a Trump trade, “The S&P will go down in that week if Trump wins” — consensus, there was no question about that consensus, we didn’t even get a question from the people buying it. It’s like, “Well, what if he wins and it goes up?”, it wasn’t even in question.

**I can’t remember, did it go up or did it go down?**

**TM:** It rallied really hard.

**That’s what I thought, I was trying to remember. Actually, it’s amazing how long ago that’s been now. That’s funny, you sold all these contracts and then they all went sideways.**

**TM:** It rallied incredibly hard. And so people are right about their prediction, but they lost money and so you had this, you kind of sit there and you’re like, “Well, this is a bad product, it’s a really bad product for what the intended use case is”.

**People went to your desk and said, “I think Trump will win”, and you sold them a financial product that actually punished them.**

**TM:** And if you think about it, why would it be a great product? I mean, there is obviously, there’s a huge basis for this, there’s a huge delta that you’re basically taking on here because really what you’re trying to price is the reaction of the markets, traditional markets, bonds or equities or whatever to the event.

**You’re introducing so many more variables to whether it comes through.**

**TM:** Exactly. It’s pretty much close to actually orthogonal, really, you don’t know what’s priced in and what’s not, and it’s really hard.

**I think about it like a car backing up with a trailer.**

**TM:** Exactly.

**Sometimes you turn the wheel one way and it goes one way and sometimes it goes another, there’s a few things going on there.**

**TM:** Exactly, that’s exactly it. You buy stock option before earnings because you think earnings are going to go well, you buy a call, then they go well, but actually it was already priced in that they were going to go well, so actually the stock goes down, and so it is actually orthogonal, it really is, it is a separate dimension that needs a separate instrument to get that exposure or hedge that exposure.

And there are separate other things which is like, and the idea that it was traded over the counter, something that common and mainstream, like an election or Brexit happening, and Brexit impacted everybody, was being traded as a bilateral instrument. It’s just you go to one party, which is the bank, and the bank prices it, and there’s no other competitive dynamic or transparent bank to discover the price, to discover the thing itself, it’s a little bit like the analogy, it’s like how a lot of this stuff, a lot of financial market started. But imagine today if we were to worry about — if you want to buy Tesla or Apple stock, there’s just one price here, it’s just like one bank that says this is the price.

**Well, it’s like an IPO, right?** **The complaints people have with IPOs is you’re trying to price versus a direct listing where the market discovery kicks in right away. If you’re trying to price an IPO, usually it’s always way too low as everyone will complain about in part because you’re dealing with these other dynamics. You want demand, you want long-term investors, and you end up selling a bundle when people just want the one thing and the bundle kind of screws up the one thing.**

**TM:** Exactly, and I think also the bank needs to make a lot of money.

**There’s conflict of interest.**

**TM:** Sort of, also the embedded spread, there’s a big spread that is embedded into the instrument and today, just for a rough sense, on prediction markets right now, you have one cent spread on most things. It’s like things trade 55, 56%, and you can buy 55, sell 56. At the banks, you would see things sometimes at 40% spreads. The bank, if you back imply whatever we’re pricing this thing at, it was huge, so we were charging a lot.

This was kind of the idea of you have a financial market to price companies, you have one for traditional commodities that started in the early 19th century with grain futures, and then it became commodity futures and metals. Then you got one for intangible commodities. I bucket it as interest rates, credit, currencies.

What if the next generation of financial markets is you build one for pricing events of economic and or social importance? And that was really exciting because one, I think the TAM could be much larger because a larger number of people would care, people care about economics, they care about politics, weather, sports, and so on and that set is bigger than the percentage of people that care about how IBM is going to perform next quarter.

Then the second thing is, if you believe in markets, I think what you really believe, and we can talk a little bit about this, but you’re believing that there is a pretty good mechanism, an efficient mechanism to bring out information from a system, bring information out in the open and then aggregating it pretty well, it weighs beliefs pretty well. And in this case, you’re bringing out information about questions about our future and so I got really excited about this idea and it started, I don’t know if you ever read [[Friedrich] Hayek](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Hayek>) back a hundred years ago.

**That’s what I was just going to ask about, you started at Kalshi in 2018, you’re thinking about this in the mid 2010s, so the idea of a prediction market was not new, but what was it about that time period that was suddenly starting to make it seem viable, this is something that could actually be done?**

**TM:** And I think it’s worth here maybe just recapping the history real quick if you’re up for it.

**Please do.**

**TM:** There’s a few things that are relevant. First, there was Hayek who talked a bit about this kind of idea of a [knowledge problem](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Use_of_Knowledge_in_Society>), which is basically a lot of the ways that we make decisions is in a centralized fashion, because that’s normal, like a CEO and a board running a company or like a government running an entire country. But there’s a problem, this kind of economic problem around resource allocation, which is that knowledge is often very dispersed, it’s not actually centralized the way that we think it’s centralized, it’s in local places, sometimes really hard to find how are you going—

**And it’s all in pieces too, right?**

**TM:** Exactly.

**There’s no central, all the knowledge is there. It’s all the knowledge is dispersed across thousands of actors.**

**TM:** Exactly. It’s actually kind of like four things. It’s very localized, so dispersed locally. It’s fragmented, so sometimes actually you have to have bend-

**It’s the blind men feeling the elephant.**

**TM:** Exactly, so you have to put the two pieces together from different places. Third, which makes it even more complicated, it’s dynamic, it’s not static in nature, so it’s actually changing over time, so you have to have a mechanism to pull it consistently, continuously. And then last, sometimes it’s kind of hidden, you may not even be able to know where it is, people may just not want to tell you that they have that information.

And so Hayek starts thinking about this idea that knowledge is decentralized and the best way to aggregate it is prices, like prices of things, that’s the closest aggregator of knowledge that we have today, and then it took basically, I think it was something like 40 years or 45 years after that for the first sort of prediction market to emerge, which is the [Iowa Electronic Markets](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iowa_Electronic_Markets>). I don’t know if you’ve ever heard of it.

**I have not. No, I haven’t.**

**TM:** Iowa University, very small, $100 per trader, few hundred traders, and they started with US elections and macro events. It was like a research university experiment and it was super non-liquid, very low capital, and it worked really well, it was overperforming the polls, so that was the first instance of a prediction market.

**What time period is this?**

**TM:** That was 1990s, 1985.

**Okay.**

**TM:** And it wasn’t the exact phrase because before that there were people that would take odds on elections, and the New York Times used to report on the odds because they were more accurate than polls. That was before the Great Depression, so that worked really well. And then you had in the 2000s, a bunch of companies like Google, Microsoft, HP did some internal versions of prediction markets that worked quite well, “How well is the launch going to go?”, and there there was a little bit of a weird thing there because there was a bit of a conflict of interest because the prediction markets may say things that leadership doesn’t like to hear.

**Right.**

**TM:** So that’s why they weren’t very free in that nature, so that was a problem. And I think to me, it’s like the biggest, I would say there was two big inflections.

The one inflection in 2015 was [Philip] Tetlock and [the book Superforecasting](<https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/227815/superforecasting-by-philip-e-tetlock-and-dan-gardner/>) and I think what it gave us is basically it was a little bit of a framework and scientific framework around, “Let’s measure if these markets work and how they work” and how judgment and belief get circulated in these systems. What he’s found is at the end of the day, actually a group of intellectually curious people will outperform experts any given day if you put them in a room, and so they were like, that have triggered things [like Augur](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augur_\(software\)>) and some crypto prediction markets to start.

The second big thing I think was like, honestly, I think we were willing to take the four or five years to get it regulated to do it right.

#### Prediction Market Regulation

**Well, so let’s talk about this. So you start Kalshi in 2018, there are other folks starting around the same time, they go offshore. And as a US citizen, you’re not supposed to bet on them. You guys decide, I think Coinbase is maybe a good analogy for your approach, which is we’re going to jump through all the hoops, convince all the relevant folks so that we can operate in the US. Is that sort of a good summary of what you decide your approach is going to be?**

**TM:** Yeah. I think we’re like, “Okay, we’re going to do this company, we’re very excited”, and by the way, that was 2016, we decided to start two-years-and-a-half years later, end of 2018.

**Yep.**

**TM:** So one of my co-founders spent time at Bridgewater and we both spent time at Citadel and it was the same sort of thing that I just described, we’d seen the same thing, they were like, “I think there’s clearly demand, it’s also very useful if we could build this”. Then yeah, what we defined in the early days were like, “What is the thing that hasn’t let these markets really thrive?” — it was the lack of a proper regulatory framework, it was the lack of clear legality.

**And why is that important?**

**TM:** Yeah, that’s a great question. So I think, look, I would look at it from two perspectives, I think one is maybe pragmatic and one is a little bit more philosophical. The pragmatic version of this is essentially, I think in financial services and healthcare, regulation is uniquely important because when things go wrong, people get hurt in bad ways. Obviously it’s your health, but also money. People really care about their money and their wealth and that they earned and they’ve worked very hard to earn and if you do something wrong with that, it’s pretty bad, and usually that kills a company. So this idea of, “Move fast and break things”, you could move fast, but try not to break things or really avoid breaking things at all costs because you’re handling people’s money or health and so there was a little bit of — I think we did not want to build a company that has a curse of scale, that the bigger it gets, the more risky it is. Actually having regulatory buy-in with decades of regulatory experience around us would help actually pressure test the company to make sure we can withstand the test of time, the test of large scale and so on and so forth.

The philosophical one is more like, “What do you want to build at the end of the day?”, there’s many ways to be successful and achieve scale and make money. We just weren’t interested in building some sort of offshore semi-regulated, not regulated site. What we’re really excited about is, could we make this a real financial asset? Could this be the next generation New York Stock Exchange? And that was really what we wanted to build. I don’t think you can build that without real buy-in from regulators, real buy-in from mainstream brokers, institutions, like real legitimacy and so we’re just much more excited about doing that. We were like, we’re going to take however long it takes to make it regulated because we really care about this existing in the world.

**Was this clear to you that this would be a big differentiator for you as well? Has it proven to be a differentiator or was that you sort of got that by accident?**

**TM:** I don’t know, it’s interesting. In some ways for us, we made the decision independent of business success. So it wasn’t like, “Oh, we think this is going to be the correct business strategy necessarily, and that’s why we’re going to do it”, it was more like we just felt like taking this type of risk is unhealthy, it’s not right, and I think not just for us personally, it’s more like if you’re building it and you’re handling customers — at the end of day, we’re 22 years old. We want to go talk to regulators and ask them what are the things that they’re worried about and let’s figure out how to resolve them and see how we can basically get it through.

And then yes, it ended up being a huge differentiator because I think today, Kalshi is, I would say the more trusted one, we’re more mainstream, you can access us without any weird VPN or anything like that. But it wasn’t by design, it was more like we made the decision that we’re going to be a regulatory-first company because we’re in financial services.

**Was there a pushback from investors about this? “This regulatory path is too difficult”, or was that actually an allure?**

**TM:** We were so clear and dogmatic about that belief that I think I wouldn’t know basically.

**Yeah, you self-selected your investors.**

**TM:** Yeah, we self-selected investors. And even if some of them may have felt like, “Hey, you should maybe stop”, because they waited for four or five years, it’s a long time.

**Yep.**

**TM:** So I’m sure some of them thought, “Okay, what are these guys doing? They’re just waiting for this Hail Mary”, but I think it’s a little bit one of those things that you’re not going to touch that subject with someone who’s so dogmatic about it, so I don’t think no one’s brought it up is what I could say.

**So you[get approval in November 2020](<https://news.kalshi.com/p/kalshi-designation>). Did you know that was coming for a while or was it suddenly like a bolt from the sky? What was the feeling when you finally are able to start operating?**

**TM:** It was a really great feeling. We were waiting for it for two years and honestly, those two years were really difficult because it was truly 12, 14 hours a day of just regulation, no product building, nothing, just working, all the things around market integrity, customer protection, all this stuff that we need to lay down.

It was amazing because we also got a bipartisan approval, it was a five-zero vote to legalize Kalshi. But what we didn’t know is that — so we got the approval at the end of 2020, what we didn’t know is that the last administration come 2021 was going to try to throttle prediction markets at all costs, a bit like what happened with crypto. Very similar.

**Yeah. So because it was bipartisan, you sort of didn’t expect that, you didn’t see that coming?**

**TM:** We didn’t see that coming.

**You needed a prediction market about how the administration was going to handle it!**

**TM:** It was tough because we were finally, “Okay, we can launch now”, but then the way that these things go is that you can start, it’s like a death by a thousand paper cuts. “Oh, you cannot launch because of this”, and then it goes on and on and on forever basically, and so that was a very, very difficult period, very, very difficult because you thought you were across the finish line, but now actually everything is going to be unwound and undone and it was really difficult.

Concretely what was happening is essentially if we were going to do this broad prediction markets about a variety of different things, now it’s like, “Oh, you can only list four markets and nothing else”, and it’s going to be about inflation next quarter, which is very useful and interesting and Fed interest rates, but that’s not enough to build a platform and it really wasn’t. There’s also other products for Fed interest rates, for example, so we were in this very difficult position and that went on for years.

**What did you perceive was the opposition? Was this just a fundamental mistrust of markets, like top down, “We get to decide what is bet on, what isn’t?”, where do you think this came from?**

**TM:** I think the opposition was actually broader than just prediction markets, it was also to crypto, also to dream new — and even some traditional financial markets that were trying to do new things or introduce new things. And you see it, the novelty is not just coming from the prediction markets to the crypto companies, they’re coming from NASDAQ, they’re coming from CME. Those big legacy companies are also introducing a lot more novelty in the last year or so, so they were also facing similar things, but they had a prior business before that, whereas we didn’t.

**Right.**

**TM:** We were too small still where if you get stomped on and blocked, you just cannot really breathe, you cannot do anything. Whereas at a big company, you’re still fine, you can water your plants and wait for next administration to raise new plans. But it was primarily, I think there was a blanket sort of, “We don’t want innovation in financial services, financial services are mostly risks and they endanger people”, and there was a little bit of a view that it’s like retail. Trading in the stock market is gambling and it’s a way to kind of rip off customers and so on and so forth and that view was predominant.

So it’s like, why do anything new with that? It’s already bad, it’s a bad thing, so why even let it go beyond? If that’s the view that you have about the stock market, imagine the view that you have about prediction markets. And look, to be clear, and what we always said, I think all financial markets have risks, it’s like the idea to come and say, “Hey, let the innovation loose and free for all usually leads to bad outcomes”, you need proper regulation, you need proper standards, you need proper frameworks around customer protections, and you need to figure out fair rules when people are participating.

**But you also need a shred of appreciation for markets actually being useful.**

**TM:** Yes, exactly, you also need to love markets, you need to believe that markets are actually a force for good, whether it’s the stock market, which is about resource allocation and derivatives market which is about risk transfer and risk pricing and I think that lacked and I think the balance between the two is critical.

I think as a company, I really feel like we tow that balance pretty well, we believe in both, we strongly believe in the power of markets, e also strongly believe in regulation and I think we put our money where our mouth is. We spent four years getting regulated upfront, but that was really the broad and then that gets to specific arguments, maybe there could be insider trading in these markets — well, there’s insider trading in all markets, that’s not a reason to carpet ban an entire market.

**TM:** So that’s the point of any market, any market does price discovery. Any financial market, at least to some extent, does price discoveries. It’s done on stocks, it’s done on interest rates, it’s done on credit, it’s done on currencies, and it’s done in our case on events, on the future, on the future forecasts.

But insider trading is banned in all of these markets, right? It’s banned in the stock market. And the reason and the principle line to take, I think there’s two reasons. One, and the way that we define it is you cannot trade on material non-public information, which means that you cannot trade on information that is not supposed to be public. If you work at the BLS, and there is rules in the BLS on the underlying, the national work, that you’re not supposed to release that information, trading it is a way to release it.

**Yeah, so there’s already a law in place, you’re already violating it by insider trading.**

**TM:** You’re violating the thing.

The second reason is fairness, there’s that reason for a stock market. The second reason trading is banned in the stock market is that if it’s not, then you won’t have a lot of liquidity. No one will feel safe being out there when somebody could come with illegally obtained, or information they have obtained that’s unobtainable for others to trade against them, they will be adversely selected and be hurt by that.

And so no, we are very much so the traditional market structure and traditional structure, because you don’t need to use that information to get enough price discovery. I think you can get a lot of price discovery without actually releasing the answer to something that you shouldn’t be releasing. And so, there’s a delicate balance like with all things, because you could argue that people should be trading on earnings ahead of earnings, but then how could we have a safe, fair stock market for anybody to find?

**Do you feel like as a startup, as a relatively new market maker, which do you feel more strongly? Do you feel the incentive to, “We want our prediction markets to be consistently ahead of the curve, so people realize this is super useful?”, or do you feel that counterbalance, which is, “We don’t want people to feel like it’s unfair and so we need to protect market participants”, even if it reduces the quality of the overall takeaways that people can get from our product.**

**TM:** I think the second one is very important, and I don’t think it takes away that much from prices currently. And the reason is because in a world where you’re letting insider trading and these things happen, which again, they’re not legal, these are not allowed, like current regs don’t allow for it. One, you could basically mess up with the underlying, like people now can release BLS data ahead of time, which is not a good thing. That’s just now messing up with a real — and you can extrapolate that to a variety of different examples. And then two, people will stop participating, because they believe that it’s not fair. And then I think if people stop participating over time, you’re not going to get a lot of the price discovery that you want to get.

#### Social Value

**This is my last question, but do you ever step back and think about you have product market fit, and there can be very narrow things. Obviously, you go back to the initial example of the Trump bet in 2016, and how you should be able to bet on that directly. You were able to bet on that directly in 2024 and to widely written about, widely discussed, you talked about it, major company. There’s also this broader philosophical debate about a lot of young people in particular feeling somewhat left out of the economy, or feeling that the upside isn’t what they were promised, that if they put in hard work and do the nine-to-five, that they’ll be a millionaire by the time they retire. And so, now there’s like a, “Whatever, might as well speculate”, is there an aspect, is there something to that? That’s part of your product market fit? A certain sort of disillusionment and malaise in the world generally, or is that above your pay grade?**

**TM:** I’ll give you some of my thoughts on that. I go back, similar to some of the questions that we had before. Look, I’ll say two things. Financial markets come with risk, and yes, there’s speculation at any type of financial markets. That argument that you just made could be made about options, could be made about crypto, could be made about really any mechanism.

**Right. This isn’t a prediction market point.**

**TM:** Yeah. But the important thing, and that’s going to go back to the regulatory piece, it can be managed appropriately. If you have the right safeguards and you actually put measures around customer protections, and you have federal government and regulators on top of you, the bad incentives in the system get drastically mitigated. So I think doing this on places where there is real customer protection and good incentives embedded into the system is much better than places, I’m assuming we all agree that offshore, which usually is where vast majority of this stuff goes, where sometimes you lose your money, you don’t even know where your money goes, or even in places where the incentives are messed up and they’re screwed. They actually want to promote more of that, I would say, dangerous type of behavior.

The second thing I think, and it’s honestly part of why we started Kalshi and it got me really excited is, if a lot of that energy is going to the stock market, not specifically stock market, options market and Wall Street, I call them the kind of Wall Street markets, I think there’s a little bit of a feeling generally that overall the game is rigged against people.

**Yeah, the house is back.**

**TM:** Yeah, right. A little bit, right? You feel that if you’re trading on stocks or options or some of the traditional stuff, how could you have more information than the large market makers, the large Wall Street banks and institutions, that are seeing everything way more than you do? They have 10x more, 100x more access to information than you do and there’s a little bit of elegance and beauty in Kalshi. We see it across the board. People that start this as a hobby and they do it full time and they read the news a lot, they care about the economy, they care about politics, they care about sometimes esoteric things like the weather and they study that every day, and it’s a bit of their home turf.

**Yeah. You’re reducing the variables in a bet to just one variable, because the more variables there are, the bigger advantages the house has.**

**TM:** I’m reducing the imbalance, there’s a level playing field aspect to prediction market, which is like — and I give this example, I think I told you about this last time, the best inflation forecaster on Kalshi is not one of the hedge funded institutions or sophisticated Wall Street guys, it’s a guy that lives in Kansas, never really traded the stock market, loves reading the news, very avid news reader and has just spent a lot of time thinking about how the economy progresses. And it’s pretty cool and pretty awesome this person can make a little bit of money on this passion and hobby of theirs and then also bring useful information to the markets for us to get informed. [Our inflation forecast](<https://kalshi.com/category/economics/inflation>) is the most accurate on the planet, so pretty much most of our forecasts are more accurate than any of the alternatives. But I think there’s just, in my opinion, it’s a much healthier place to be in than a place where the game is rigged against you.

**Do you ever worry though, that as you get larger and the importance of having institutional money and to provide liquidity, all these sorts of things, will you lose that? Will you lose the sense that the little guy’s on the same playing field as the big guy? Or is it the simplicity of the market, either something happens or it doesn’t, give you confidence you can avoid becoming the house?**

**TM:** I think simplicity and the fact that there’s a real fair value, like Brexit will happen or not. Whereas the stock market, it’s not just about, “What is the fair value of Apple?”, it’s, “Where are the mutual funds buying and who’s buying where?” and you have to have access to a much broader set of information to figure out the right price and that broader set of information is oftentimes inaccessible to the individual, to the person that may not. Whereas here, it doesn’t matter who’s buying what, either Brexit will happen or not, there’s a real intrinsic fair value. And if you’re smart about politics and you read a lot and you’re really interested in that, you can basically figure that out, independently of where people are buying.

**And this is good for society?**

**TM:** Definitely, I think this idea of getting smarter about a lot of these different topics and events is — and people could argue, are we getting insanely significantly stronger, smarter, or 20, 30% smarter? I would say even in 20, 30% case, it’s very, very important, very useful, because we can make decisions a little bit better, we can allocate resources a little bit better, and we can manage risks a little bit better. And that’s, I think, a very useful thing to have.

**Tarek Mansour, it’s great to talk to you. I look forward to seeing how you progress.**

**TM:** Thanks so much, Ben. Thanks for having me.
