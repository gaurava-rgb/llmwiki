Good morning,

Today’s Stratechery Interview is with [Dan Kim](<https://www.linkedin.com/in/drdankim/>). Kim is the [Chief Strategy Officer for TechInsights](<https://www.techinsights.com/blog/techinsights-appoints-dan-kim-chief-strategy-officer>), and former chief economist and director of strategic planning and economic security at the U.S. Department of Commerce for its CHIPS for America program. I previously interviewed Kim [last April](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-dan-kim-and-hassan-khan-about-chips/>) as he was wrapping up his tenure with the Commerce Department; in that interview we discussed Kim’s background, including roles as Chief Economist for SK Hynix, Director of Economic Strategy for Qualcomm, and the Senior International Economist for the U.S. International Trade Commission.

In this interview we very much pick up where we left off in April, particularly the point Kim made about TSMC and the personal impact the company has had on his life as a part of its broader impact on the world. The specific problem faced by the U.S., however, is where is headquarters? To that end we start with TSMC’s customers and why they don’t choose Intel, why the Trump administration took a stake in Intel, and how Kim would ultimately solve Intel’s demand problems. We also discuss why Nvidia wants to sell to China, and why many in Washington strongly oppose it; along the way we get into some fascinating philosophical questions about norms, the complexity of modern society, and what sort of leadership is necessary going forward.

We’re going to spend a lot of time talking about Intel and Nvidia generally. We didn’t spend as much time on China as I would’ve liked to the first time, so we should definitely visit that. But I do have to ask you, it is[Apple Week](<https://stratechery.com/2025/iphones-17-and-the-sugar-water-trap/>), they did just launch their new iPhones. Thinking back to the Jobs to Be Done theory, the job that I needed done was a way to communicate with my girlfriend overseas for free. I thought about that and we could think about iPhone as a collection of technical things, but at the end of the day, when a consumer buys an iPhone, there’s a job they want done, well, a lot of jobs now, but at the time, for me, personally, that was the job I needed done. I’m going to fast-forward quite a bit, and I think last time I was on this show, I shared the amazing story of my daughter being saved by a pacemaker at birth. What I didn’t share at the time was that the pregnancy we had before that we actually lost our son at birth. It was really, really hard for us.

**I’m sorry to hear that. I did not know that.**

**DK:** Yeah. And so, that made the saving our daughter with this medical device even more joyful. But as I was working at the time, dealing with grief, I was driving home, I think I was working at SK Hynix at the time, I was the Chief Economist, and I was processing some feelings around grief, and I turned to Siri and I said, “Siri, play a song for a grieving father”.

That’s a really hard question to ask actually for an AI to do because it needs to know what grief is and what songs might help and the song that it played was, [All I Do is Win by DJ Khaled](<https://youtu.be/WRc4lH6_bEU>), and the sheer ridiculousness of that choice actually made me laugh quite a bit.

**The nature of grief and therapy and all that sort of thing, maybe it didn’t really matter was the answer.**

**Right. That was a pretty tough question to be fair to Siri. But then, again, Siri was not good at anything, so it’s also fair.**

This morning I thought about that as I was thinking about the Apple launch, because a lot of the discussion was about what wasn’t announced, that AI really wasn’t mentioned very much at the Apple announcement. So, I asked the same question on my way into work this morning, “Play a song for a grieving father”, this time it played [Tears in Heaven by Eric Clapton](<https://youtu.be/JxPj3GAYYZ0>).

**That’s a good choice.**

**DK:** And I thought about that, very good choice. Then I said, “Tell me about the song”, because there must be a story behind the song and why he played it. And of course, the story of this song was Eric Clapton writing a song about his grief of losing his son when his son was four years old and Siri couldn’t tell me that, it just said, “Well, here’s a bunch of web results”. This is the classic frustration you have with Siri, doesn’t really give you much. So, then I transferred over to ChatGPT.

Maybe it senses, maybe it doesn’t, but maybe it senses that I want to engage a little bit about the story and so then it tells me, “He wrote this in a sense that it didn’t take the grief away, but it gave him a way to hold it, he described the song as a safe place where he could put feelings that were otherwise overwhelming”, and I found myself emotionally getting a little bit more involved here with AI.

And of course, playing it put him back in a raw place that he no longer wanted to be”. #### Fabless Chip Companies and CHIPS

**So, zooming out. When you were in the White House and on the CHIPS team, obviously, Apple is — I think this is a base level point you and I are obviously totally familiar with, our listeners I think are totally familiar with — but which is the difference between companies that design chips and companies that actually make chips. Now, I think it’s fair to say that the CHIPS Act, and therefore, your role was primarily focused on the latter. The companies that actually make the chips, the TSMC, the Samsung, the Intel, Texas Instruments, GlobalFoundries, etc — however, in that role, how did you interact with and think about the fabless companies, the design companies, the Apple’s, the Nvidia’s, the Qualcomm’s? What was the balance of interacting with them? What mattered? Who you listened to? And how did that influence the way you thought about the CHIPS program and the money disbursement?**

**DK:** Yeah, we recognized immediately that the lack of a foundry ecosystem, at least at the leading edge, was a big problem in the United States. That there was a concentration of customers in Taiwan, and that led to concentration of production in Taiwan and so it was imperative for us to engage with Apple and Nvidia and Qualcomm and AMD and all these fabless companies.

**But their concentration in Taiwan is downstream of the production being there, right?**

**DK:** That’s right, but at the same time, it’s also a function of a foundry ecosystem not being present in the US either.

**What do you mean by a foundry ecosystem? Explain what that is.**

**DK:** So, the fact that it existed in Taiwan, meaning the fact that TSMC had perfected this business model and taking on contract manufacturing and not competing with their customers and being so reliable and having that as basis for building out their capacity and that not being in the United States, was a challenge that we recognized right away. If you wanted to, without the TSMC Foundry ecosystem, the fabless ecosystem doesn’t really exist either, and it goes hand in hand.

We had to listen to the fabless companies first, generically meaning, “Hey, what kind of manufacturing ecosystem do we need to create here?”, and we had really smart leaders and the CEOs and the sourcing managers of these companies telling us the nitty-gritties of what they were looking for. But also when we were getting down to brass tacks about individual companies and doing due diligence on what they were seeing in terms of technical capabilities of the manufacturers themselves, we would engage, to the extent that we could, with the fabless companies to get a sense of what the demand would be if we supported the fab projects.

**One thing that strikes me as you’re describing that is I think this is another way to distill or understand the real fundamental challenges facing the US and facing Intel, which is, you alluded to this, but I’ll say it much more boldly, which is the most powerful and important companies in tech create their own customers.**

**The example I always use about this is Facebook. Where Facebook, you go back to 2020 and there was an advertiser boycott of Facebook[and right away I’m like](<https://stratechery.com/2020/apple-and-facebook/>), “This doesn’t matter, they’re going to be fine”. This is coming off [saying the same thing when COVID kicked in](<https://stratechery.com/2020/facebook-earnings-auction-dynamics-full-steam-ahead/>) and Facebook plunged, like everybody else and immediately snapped back. The reason was because there was a huge swathe of advertisers who existed because Facebook advertising existed. Facebook created its own customer base in terms of advertising and these companies were not predicated on brand advertising or whatever it might be, they were predicated on hardcore understanding their LTV, reaching customers, converting them and if they weren’t advertising on Facebook, they were going to die. That’s why they snapped back in COVID, that’s why when the advertising boycott happened, it had real no impact on Facebook because the advertisers that were boycotting were the big brand advertisers that were created for TV. TV created that ecosystem in many respects and Facebook was just an add-on.**

**The point of this analogy, and I think this applies to Google — Amazon’s a great example, Amazon is a massive advertising business just on Amazon.com, it’s like the definition of creating,[the whole cycle is completely self-contained](<https://stratechery.com/2023/amazon-earnings-the-logistics-virtuous-cycle-amazon-aggregator-ads/>). That’s what TSMC is that’s why they’re so important. That’s why they’re so entrenched, in that there’s an entire ecosystem of chip makers who are not chip makers, who are chip designers that exist because TSMC created the conditions for them to exist. [The problem for Intel](<https://stratechery.com/2024/intel-honesty/>), and the problem for you, or the government generally, you’re not part of the government, but when you were there, is you’re trying for this to be viable. You need to capture and get customers who, by their nature, are completely formed to and perfectly aligned with TSMC because TSMC made them possible in the first place.**

**Does that resonate with you and the challenges that Intel is facing above and beyond? Even if Intel has the perfect culture, which they don’t, there would still have this fundamental lack of perfect shape fitting that TSMC does.**

**DK:** Yeah, we learned a lot about TSMC along the way too. We knew a lot, but we learned a lot about TSMC. Universally, what customers would say is how much they enjoyed working with TSMC, which from an economist perspective was slightly-

**Where’s the spreadsheet entry for joy?**

**DK:** Right! It was also slightly surprising because you would not expect a monopolist, if you will, to behave that way. I’m not sure if you could go back and look at all the monopolists in history, and by the way, being a monopoly is not illegal, acting in monopolistic behavior as defined legally as illegal, but having market share and market power is not illegal, but they tend to behave in certain ways when they get the market share and it can be unpleasant for the customers.

But almost 100% of their customers were telling us how much they enjoyed working with the TSMC because of how collaborative they were, but also because they just delivered, they were reliable and you put that together with the scale they have and the cost advantages that scale gets you made a really challenging thing.

**Is it actually more important than the scale and the price advantages? Is that actually the deeper moat?**

**DK:** The trust, is that what you mean?

**The trust, the reliability, the fact that you know what you’re getting and what you’re getting is pretty great and you actually enjoy it?**

**DK:** Yeah. So one, I think, misconception that I think some people have about what a foundry is, when you choose a foundry, people might think we choose a foundry to get chips out. Actually, you’re not choosing that, you’re choosing a foundry to develop technology partnerships, and then you’re binding onto their technology roadmap. You are saying that for my products, it’s going to be built on that foundry company’s technology roadmap, and that’s not n+1 — that’s n+2, 3, 4.

Once you design yourself in, first of all, it takes two years at least to design yourself out of it if you need to design yourself out of it, but once you design yourself in, there’s stickiness there and because there is stickiness there, you tend to stick with it. Getting that trust is absolutely important, and getting that deliverable is absolutely important.

**Right, and it’s a trust like I trust that you’re not going to lie to me, it’s trust that you’re going to deliver on your roadmap that I’m committing to.**

**DK:** That’s right. The thing that I found to be a difficult proposition about [Intel’s five nodes in four years proposition](<https://download.intel.com/newsroom/2024/foundry/intel-foundry-node-roadmap-2024.pdf>) was, well, at some point you have to have volume to compete and the challenge is you need to get customers to put large volumes in that node and from a customer’s perspective, if you are an Nvidia or a Qualcomm or an AMD, if you’re thinking about switching your foundry, you’re not going to put your flagship product at high volume at a new foundry if you can help it. You might try it out with a smaller volume product that’s maybe not your flagship, and then if that experience works out and you really experience it and then you trust that roadmap going forward, then it’s the next node after that or perhaps the one after that, you might put more volume. So that’s another moat, if you will. You need volume to compete, but it’s hard to get the volume because the customers are inherently conservative in the volume they’re willing to give you at first.

We heard a lot about 18A, about being that foundry node, so we had to really dig into, “How much volume is actually possible at 18A versus these other ones?”, and the interesting thing about this, there was two transitions that was happening in the industry, if you recall. First, ChatGPT came out in November 2022 and so there was this beginning of scaling up of the hyperscaler AI industry, if you will so the leading edge was transitioning that way, or at least the beginnings of it. That was a shift and that was an important shift, because it wasn’t just about getting mobile customers to switch to you, there was actually now a market to get important high-volume customers at the data center level that could command big margins, and that was an important transition for the industry.

**And I think just on the Intel point, it’s the AI moment.[That was the real cost of not building a foundry a decade ago](<https://stratechery.com/2025/apple-and-the-ghosts-of-companies-past/>), that foundry would’ve struggled and probably would’ve captured not much of the mobile market for all the reasons that you’re sort of articulating, but at least it would’ve been ready for the new moment. It’s the lack of being ready for the new moment, that is the ultimate failure.**

**DK:** The interesting thing about that is it’s not necessarily being technically unready for it. It’s about the business culture, it’s about being trusted by your customers, all those things. These things take time, to your point, but there was another transition happening in the industry in that the industry was transitioning from the [FinFET transistor](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fin_field-effect_transistor>) to something else, whether [it’s gate-all-around](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multigate_device>) or others, there’s the backside delivery and all of that. But as good as TSMC was, there was a question of, “Well, TSMC is very good at FinFET, but Intel introduced it first” — Intel was very good at introducing new architectures first.

**Yeah, and has always been very good at it.**

**DK:** Yeah, exactly. When the industry then needs to transition to a new architecture to continue to scale down, then who would be best at that? So there was a moment in which there seemed to be a true competition for sub-3nm, sub-2nm, gate-all-around roadmap and so Intel found itself there. To your point, maybe they were a little bit late and getting ready for the foundry culture.

One other thing that we heard that I thought was really informative was a supply chain manager at one of these big companies saying technically they’re very good, Intel is a very good technical company, but one thing that does concern us is it’s very expensive to compete at the leading edge. They call it the bleeding edge for a reason, you have to bleed a lot of money to get there and to compete with TSMC. We’re not quite sure if they have the financial capacity to keep competing there and that’s something that we looked at.

So the task became a bit clearer to us. One way to frame it was, “If Intel is really going to compete here and we want a US-based source for not only the R&D capability and the production capability, how do we give them the financial runway to execute here?”. That was a different way of framing it than just, “Which fabs do we fund?”.

#### The U.S. Government and Intel

**Right. So this is obviously a natural segue to[the big Intel news of the summer](<https://stratechery.com/2025/u-s-intel/>) and I think that was a really good overview of the problem. At the end of the day, TSMC created these customers, which Intel now needs — they need the customers, the issue of volume has always been a thing. It’s not just about Intel exiting, it’s that the history you mentioned, the transition to gate-all-around. What marks each of these transition, and it’s usually a transition or transition where it happens, is a foundry falls off. There is a certain level, and we can go back to Motorola, we can go back to all these other entities that fell off, [I think that GlobalFoundries fell off](<https://spectrum.ieee.org/globalfoundries-halts-7nm-chip-development>) at, was it 14nm? Somewhere around there. IBM threw in the towel a couple years later. UMC’s throwing in the towel basically when it comes to EUV and every time there’s this transition, it requires that much more volume because the costs get so much higher and if you don’t have the volume, you just can’t make it. This is almost like a force of nature.**

**You mentioned a monopoly question. If the costs are so astronomical to build this, you need to basically collectivize everyone to invest in making it happen and TSMC is the one doing that. And given that, the Intel problem is you’re fighting against nature on almost every front to sort of make this possible and that’s why — that was a long preamble — I went against almost all my principles and[am supportive of the US taking a stake in Intel](<https://stratechery.com/2025/u-s-intel/>). The reason is the supply manager point, if you’re going to get any hope of getting you one, this doesn’t get people over the line of getting you one, they need some sort of assurance that Intel is going to continue to exist past 18A.**

**DK:** Yeah. So should [we steelman](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straw_man>) a bit more?

**Let’s do it.**

**DK:** I think that was the phrase you used in your post.

**I wrote a big Article on it, so this is your chance to give your take. I’m sure it would be painful to be sitting there knowing all about it and not be able to get it out, so this is your chance.**

**DK:** No, no. So if you want to hear the other side of it, [I think Mike Schmidt and Todd Fisher wrote a very interesting op-ed](<https://www.wsj.com/opinion/uncle-sam-shouldnt-own-intel-stock-ccd6986d>) as to why it was a bad idea, and so I would encourage people to read that at the Wall Street Journal so they make a good case for it. You also acknowledge there were other cases for not doing it, right? And I think we acknowledge that.

I would add to the equation of everything we’re talking about here to national security and national security is a bit nebulous term, but let me drill down into a specific experience that the US government had. You mentioned GlobalFoundries not pursuing below 14nm, I believe at the time that was even an EUV machine or two shipped and then maybe have to be sent back, and I believe at the time that the US government actually had quite a bit of involvement in purchasing that EUV machine and then was a bit baffled as to why it was being returned.

The US government has had direct experience of being reliant on a foundry that no longer is pursuing down that path, so whatever you may think the US government might need at the leading edge, some of it is open and others maybe behind closed doors but they’ve had this experience of what could happen if the supplier that you’re reliant on is no longer financially or commercially able to keep going down that path, I think that is instructive as to the Intel experience.

The other experience was, and I would say this, when we were executing CHIPS late 2022, early 2023, and that whole year, the world is quite frankly in a different place than it is today.

**How so?**

**DK:** Like I said, there was a genuine competition for these new nodes, different architectures. AI was just beginning-

**Would you say that, by and large, TSMC has exceeded expectations in its ability to move to a new transistor and frankly just the aggressiveness with which they are building out 3nm and 2nm?**

**DK:** Well, we haven’t quite seen the new gate-all-around format of TSMC yet, that’s coming down later. Right now it’s still 3nm, which that is FinFET. But what it spoke loudly was their ability and their reliability has been built over so many years that the customers have inherent faith that they can do it and I think that became stronger over time.

So two years ago or a year ago even, we had a CEO who had a certain mindset in Intel, Pat Gelsinger. [We have a CEO that’s of a different mindset now](<https://www.intc.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/1730/intel-appoints-lip-bu-tan-as-chief-executive-officer>) with a new strategy, and financially they’re just in a different place as well. So what I’m trying to say is whatever arguments we would’ve made about taking a financial stake or not in Intel, say a year ago or even two years ago, I think we’re in a fundamentally different place now.

**What’s the balance between the new CEO and the financial situation?**

**DK:** I think the key difference that I’m observing from the public statements is Pat Gelsinger seemed to want to just build capacity as soon as possible, right? With the idea that-

**[Build it and they will come](<https://youtu.be/o3c_pJ_CLJQ>), it was Field of Dreams.**

**DK:** “That day will come, the 18A is going to work”. And I think the theory was, “If 18A is going to work and we’re going to prove that it works, then we have to have capacity right away to serve them because if we only have the technology but not the capacity, then the customers won’t come to us, so we have to do both and that’s challenging, but we’ve got to do it”, which is a theory I could understand and sympathize with.

With this new CEO with Lip-Bu Tan, he made it very clear that they’re not going to pursue things until they do have customers. And so, it’s going to be get customers first-

**But then you have the chicken-and-egg problem, right?**

**DK:** Yeah, exactly. So it’s a hard problem for sure, either way and so I think from Pat’s perspective, he would say, maybe he would say, “I still feel like I had the right strategy, if the technology’s going to work, then we have to get customers right away and do this — and by the way, there’s this Taiwan risk lurking around, and so why not build it now so that we could migrate them?”. **DK:** —the average consumer is not going to really feel it right the way that a financial crisis would. But we’re in a situation in which the sun is still shining and we know perhaps that the rain is still going to come and we don’t have a roof, so what do we do in the meantime?

Nvidia and China

I think it’s a really interesting challenge and one other steelman that we can try to do is the [H20 and the Nvidia and the 15% export tax](<https://www.ft.com/content/cd1a0729-a8ab-41e1-a4d2-8907f4c01cac>) and all of that.

**We’ve gone long, I was going to end it by saying we didn’t get to Nvidia, we didn’t get to China, but I promised it at the top, so what is the link here and how does it connect?**

**DK:** So I think we make the mistake of having the US be the only actor that matters and I think China matters too, because they also have an agency.

**I totally agree, this is so core to my belief system. US politics is so wrapped up in the US itself, it treats everyone else in the world[as NPCs](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-player_character>) and a lot of the world is NPCs, but not China, China is a live player.**

**DK:** Yeah, and just as the US government doesn’t speak with one voice, there’s a lot of different voices that have to be unified somehow, China I think is the same way, which is there’s going to be portions of government in China that doesn’t want any Nvidia chips, there’s going to be portions that do want it until China catches up and then there’s going to be parts of industry that absolutely wants Nvidia and may not even like the idea of Huawei being the alternative.

There’s a lot of different voices, but why allow that? I actually understand [Nvidia CEO] Jensen [Huang]’s point, which is you don’t want to create room for them to compete against you, because it’s a formidable technology player that if you give them room, they’re going to create a platform to outcompete you 10 years down the line and if that’s the logic, then it would make total sense for him to go to the White House and say, “Let us sell chips there, sure, we’ll give you 15%, we’ll give you 50%”, because the point isn’t to make money out of China.

**It’s to prevent the rise of a competitor.**

**DK:** Yeah, that’s right. It’s almost like to give it away to them—

**It’s priceless. and this is why people are like, “Oh, we’ll buy Nvidia’s H20 at the price they’re selling them at”, it’s like, “No, they’re selling them at that price as a purely defensive measure”.**

**DK:** Right. At the same time, I will repeat to you what a former high ranking national security official told me. He would say, “China’s going to develop the AI systemm they’re going to develop weapons systems, they’re going to develop weapons system that’s infused with AI, they have every right to do that just as we do, but what I cannot allow them to have is American technologies that will enable them to do that, can not do it”.

So I totally get it from Jensen’s perspective, that from his perspective as the CEO of Nvidia and to his shareholders and to his board, he has to sell to China. No doubt about that. But from a national security perspective, you cannot allow advanced AI systems that are US-based into China for those reasons, and so what I’m observing here in DC is the current outcome is I don’t know if I’ve talked to anyone in the national security circles. I have not, to be very clear, I have not talked to President Trump who is championing this, I’m not sure if I’ve talked to anyone in the national security circles in the executive branch or in Congress that is a fan of this.

So in the long term, I think there’s going to be some tail risk for them, because I’m not sure if he’s convinced the national security circles that this is the way to go and what to do about it and so if the outcome that you want is for the entire world, including China, to be built on a US software stack, then I could see that argument. But if the outcome that you want is as big of a lead between the US and China in terms of AI hardware access, then also doesn’t make sense. So this is a hard one in which I think the language between the industry and the government is not quite cutting it right now in terms of squaring these two things.

**My frustration is the lack of consistency. If you ban Nvidia, you need to also[ban semiconductor manufacturing equipment](<https://stratechery.com/2023/an-interview-with-gregory-allen-about-the-updated-china-chip-ban/>) and maybe they should ban semiconductor manufacturing equipment anyway, because I think that’s a much more higher point of leverage in the long run. But to sell China as much semiconductor manufacturing equipment as they want, with the exception of EUV, and also simultaneously ban Nvidia chips, it’s the worst of both worlds, it’s very frustrating to me.**

**DK:** Well, let me push back on that. So if you did allow EUV and let’s say that you give—

**No, I don’t want EUV in China, I think that that’s the single most important thing that’s happened in chip controls.**

**DK:** So then let’s live in a world in which we allow Huawei to have complete access to TSMC. Then the market for H20 would decrease, because Huawei would put out a much better chip with better functionality and so the only way that you could really maintain a market for a degraded chip in China that’s US-based is if you degrade everything else, including foundry access.

This is an argument that I think pretty much every CEO has made to previous administrations, it’s just that this time, it’s carrying weight. You could think of it as an ASML or Applied Materials or SK Hynix or Samsung or any of these companies going to the White House and saying, “We need to be able to sell to China, because if we don’t, it creates a market opening for someone there to replace us and they’re going to oversupply the market and we’re out of the market altogether, this is how they operate”.

China, Taiwan, and Trade-offs

**Yeah, you’re right, you’re forcing me into prioritizing what it is, I think Huawei building at TSMC is the best outcome of them all. In my view, this is the flashpoint, Taiwan is what matters. Obviously, I’m biased,****[I may be less biased than I was previously](<https://stratechery.com/2025/a-personal-update-and-vacation-break/>), but if China is dependent on Taiwan being intact, that is a good situation in my estimation.**

**DK:** So you’re optimizing for maintaining and increasing the dependence on Taiwan from a Chinese perspective?

**Yes.**

**DK:** I think a lot of national security folks would say what we’re optimizing for is a inherent AI leadership of hardware perpetually, and again, this is a case in which it really matters what you’re optimizing.

**This is one where I think I could vociferously argue every single position, because they’re all legitimate. But to me, the great hope in the world is I think there’s a real cultural misunderstanding issue in my estimation. The US mentality is to drive to resolution and so it just bugs, I think, Americans that Taiwan, “Wait, what do you mean it’s not a country?”, “Yes, it’s a country”, “Can we clear this up and move on?”, and the best answer is “No, we can’t and ideally, we never will”, and I think there’s a world in which we do maintain the status quo in a way that is acceptable to China in the long run and I think that a way to get there and ensure it is just as the number one reason there won’t be a war is the economic interdependence of the US and China. The best way for China to not invade Taiwan is to make it too valuable to destroy.**

**DK:** Well, I’m certainly not a China expert or a Taiwan expert, I’m an economist at heart and a strategist in technology, but I think one thing that’s very clear and I think it’s obvious for people like you and me who observe the markets, but I think for most people, we frame the AI competition between US and China as who has the best capability, but it is absolutely 100% fact right now that neither the US or China makes any of the chips that drive their AI. So both countries are 100% dependent on foreign technologies in terms of hardware, the dependence is there in terms of Taiwan, Japan, even Europe and other places for the equipment and things like that.

So I think we’re living through a unique portion of history and I’m not sure how much parallels you could even draw to the Cold War, because at least during the Cold War and the nuclear battles, each country, the US and the Soviets, had more control over their own technologies, their weapons technologies, than before than what it is now with AI hardware. Neither country really controls that stack at the hardware side and both have fundamental weaknesses to try to win.

This ties us full circle, everything we discussed about Intel, we’re talking about fundamental weaknesses in the US system to outcompete Taiwan and TSMC and you could take it from the Chinese side too. You could look at that and say, “Well, the fundamental weaknesses that China has is about the lack of trust and the lack of innovative capacity”, and all of that to be able to do that, and so what I would love is you could come up with different solutions depending on the problem that you’re solving for and so I never approach a problem and say, “No, that’s stupid”, because you’re getting it wrong in that solution.

The positions that you’ve taken about Intel and H20 and others, there are smart people that I know that would vehemently disagree with you about all of those things, but you’re optimizing for specific problems and these people that would disagree with you are also optimizing for different problems.

**That’s right. Both of our arguments can be totally airtight, just we’re pointing it to different directions.**

**DK:** Exactly. So I think it is really important for policymakers to articulate very clearly what the strategy is and what problems they’re prioritizing for, so that the markets could react and bend around that.

**Yeah, this is a really important point. I think this was something that came up — we’re talking about broader societal questions — I think this is something that came up in COVID, where the problem with the tyranny of experts is that there is no right answer to so many of these questions. Everything is a trade-off and the fundamental role of a leader in society, which for us in a democratic system is the politicians, is to balance the trade-offs and to choose the order, the priority stack, what I call it. What is most important? What is second most and what is third most important?**

**The fundamental failure of our politics is not politicians not knowing all the answers, because in a technological age, that’s increasingly impossible. The fundamental failure is the lack of courage and frankly, cowardice to make trade-offs. If you make the trade-offs and you signal what’s most important and what’s not, the experts can come in and fill in the details of how to make that happen, and I think that’s what you’re saying. We need to choose what’s most important. Is it having a completely indigenous stack? Is it controlling the software stack? Is it doing whatever is necessary to prevent war? You have to make a choice, you need to communicate that and then we can let the market work. The market will figure it out after that.**

**DK:** Yeah. There’s a intermediate step and I agree with all that, there’s a intermediate step in making sure that those leaders have a clear understanding of the fundamentals. Fundamentals of how the market worked, how we got here, why we got here and what’s next and allow them to think long term, and what I found frustrating when I was in government was that there was generally a lack of that.

It’s hard to get, because there’s plenty of people that are incentivized to give you information that are biased and I don’t think it’s anything ill-willed, but a company will come to you or an association will come to you and they have their viewpoints for strategic things that they want accomplished, and it’s really hard for you to have a fundamental assessment of what is true and where things are going, so that the leaders can make decisions.

So people have asked me, “Why go to a company like TechInsights after being the architect of CHIPS? Why not go do something else?”. To me, it was really important for me to try to stay in a place where I could try to provide some of that on market assessment, fundamental assessment, so the strategy makers, whether it’s at a company level or government level, to look at this and say, “Okay, here are the pain points and here how things are operating, here’s how the demand works here and supply works and so let’s drive the ship this way”. Trying my best to do that, but it’s hard, it’s not easy.
