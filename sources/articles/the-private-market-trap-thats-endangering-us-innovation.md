Every investor knows what’s wrong with private markets right now. Venture capital and private equity funds have delivered weak returns, few exits and little cash for investors, resulting in a brutal fundraising climate for funds and therefore companies.

The bigger picture reveals a much worse problem: There is roughly $13 trillion trapped in private markets, and that could be killing the startup ecosystem.

The liquidity crisis is so dire that some of the most astronomically successful U.S. private companies including Stripe, OpenAI, SpaceX, Databricks, and a handful of others—together valued over $1 trillion—have developed their own systems (in effect running their own episodic, subscale, single-name stock exchanges) to get cash to those who want it and take in cash from those who have it.

For everyone else, it’s the Private Capital Motel: Money checks in but doesn’t check out.

![](https://dmxvlyap9srmn.cloudfront.net/article/pictures/cf287a6c-2ff8-457a-bf93-cfc5514f4eb4.png)

About 70,000 U.S. private companies have received some form of private investment—from seed capital through a buyout, representing $6.7 trillion of cash from pension funds, endowments and the like. The companies are currently valued at $13.3 trillion. But that value is increasingly trapped, with no way for investors and companies to tap it.

When financial investors buy illiquid shares in a private company, there have traditionally been three ways to turn those shares back into money. The company can:

  1. go public so its shares can be sold on a liquid stock exchange,
  2. sell itself to another company, either for cash or liquid stock, or
  3. sell itself to a private equity fund, which will eventually do A or B above.



The single point of failure is access to the public markets, which have seen growing barriers to entry and [increasing incentives to exit](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/come-software-boomerangs>) over 25 years of increasing regulation. The number of publicly listed companies has been cut in half to about 4,000 since 1996. The past three years saw the fewest new listings since the financial crisis.

Starting in 1999, a series of regulatory changes reduced the number of securities firms that handle initial public offerings, increased the regulatory burden on public companies, made trading less profitable for banks, and wrecked the Wall Street business model of doing analyst research to support IPOs.

Fund managers looking to put capital into cool new technology and growth companies increasingly found them in the private market, so as it got harder to go public, it got easier to stay private. The private market work-around ballooned, so that scaled giants like Stripe no longer need the public market’s primary capital or secondary liquidity and can avoid its regulatory brain damage.

The lines have crossed: companies big enough to go public don’t need to.

And everyone else is stuck:

  1. The IPO market favors big companies.
  2. There are fewer public companies left to acquire private ones.
  3. Private equity acquirers won’t buy a company if they don’t see a way to sell it.



The trend has become self-reinforcing, in a bad way. The lack of lucrative exits has pushed returns for venture capital, growth and private equity funds to near zero based on fund managers’ reported (paper) valuation benchmarks.

Another measure, distributions to paid-in capital—the actual dollars fund managers return to their investors—is similarly grim. As a percentage of a fund’s net asset value, funds returned to investors were in the single digits from the end of 2022 through the end of 2024. Going back to 2004, the only other time they fell this low was, again, during the financial crisis.

When poorly performing funds don’t give money back to investors, it’s not surprising they don’t want to invest more. Fundraising for the venture and growth categories in 2024 was down 40% from the 2019 pre-pandemic benchmark.

And this is very bad news for U.S. private companies. Stock-based compensation, much of the rocket fuel of the entrepreneurial ecosystem, is becoming more of a pay cut than an upside incentive. It represents trillions that can’t be spent on a house or tuition—or invested in other cool companies.

This lowers the incentive to take a job at a startup. Why not take a salary and some options from a big tech giant?

How will the U.S. stay competitive and birth new companies like SpaceX and OpenAI with decreased capital to fund them and crippled incentives for employees to staff them?

The market has become highly distorted, with funding going mostly to big private companies and big VC firms. Of the $77 billion of new U.S. VC funding raised in 2024, almost $30 billion went to the top 10 funds—with an average fund size of $2.9 billion. First-time funds accounted for only 7% of funds raised.

Cash for companies after their initial funding round, known as follow-on investments, is concentrated in unicorns. Last year, these billion-dollar companies accounted for 15% of the deals but 52% of the $90 billion worth of follow-on capital deployed.

Pity the small company that’s not doing artificial intelligence. In the first quarter of 2025, 70% of first financings that VCs made into startups were in AI companies, claiming 50% of the capital deployed.

So VC is increasingly concentrated in a small number of established megafunds that can only invest larger checks in larger companies. And most of those dollars are going to old unicorns or new AI startups.

That might be rational at the level of each investor, but it’s not great for the country or for innovation generally.

The traditional American system for simultaneously generating progress and wealth relies on a straightforward mechanism: Founders and employees can—at scale—trade good ideas and smart hard work for stock in companies that appreciates as a function of the value of the ideas, the effectiveness of the labor and the resulting ability to attract financial capital. Eventually, contributors of intellectual capital, sweat capital and financial capital can exchange their stock for money.

It’s that simple. But without a liquid stock exchange, it doesn’t work.

Bill Gurley, longtime general partner at VC firm Benchmark, summed it up nicely: “We have a problem. The system as it exists today promotes less liquidity, less traditional high-quality company building, and way higher burn rates,” he said. “That’s not a great combination, and it’s all self-reinforcing.”

There are two possible solutions: Remove the barriers to entry blocking the U.S. liquid exchanges, or build a new exchange serving companies not massive enough to do it themselves.

Returning to the principles of world-leading capital markets—access, efficiency, and transparency—only requires the removal of obstacles built up over the years.

At stake is $13 trillion of capital waiting to be redeployed into innovation, creation, improvement, advancement and growth.

America’s leadership positions in both capital markets and entrepreneurial innovation—two sides of the same coin—depend on it.
