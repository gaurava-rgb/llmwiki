Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery Interview is with [Horace Dediu](<https://x.com/asymco>) of [Asymco](<https://asymco.com/>). Dediu started Asymo 16 years ago, and was a major inspiration for Stratechery; while Asymco remains free, [Dediu recently launched Asymco One](<https://asymco.com/2026/02/17/introducing-asymco-one-01/>), a [paid offering](<https://asymco.com/one/>) primarily focused on Apple that includes additional analysis, research, newsletters, a conference, and more.

In this interview we discuss Dediu’s fascinating background, including coming to America as a refugee, working as a researcher at an offshoot of Bell Labs, and what he learned — and didn’t learn — at business school. It was there Dediu met Clayton Christensen, who he worked with closely: we discuss disruption in the context of both the iPhone and AI, and why Christensen got the iPhone wrong. We also cover Dediu’s career at Nokia, and why the company was so oblivious to the iPhone threat. Then we jump to AI: how long will it take to become profitable and fully diffused, and is Apple the only big tech company that might be disrupted? Finally we discuss Apple at 50, and why Asymco One is the ultimate evidence that Dediu continues to be an Apple believer.

**Topics:**

Background | Business Theory | Nokia | iPhone | AI | Apple and AI | Apple at 50 | Asymco One

#### Background

**Horace Dediu, welcome to Stratechery.**

**Horace Dediu:** Hey, it’s great to be here, it’s nice to see you Ben, long time, I’ve always admired from afar.

**The honor is mine, it’s a real honor to have you here, not just because you’re one of the[preeminent analysts covering Apple](<https://asymco.com/>), whose [50th birthday was this week](<https://stratechery.com/2026/apples-50-years-of-integration/>), so it’s a great time to finally have you on, we’ll get to that in a little bit. You are also a major influence on Stratechery. Your site, Asymco, was one of my favorites before I started Stratechery. A great site name that I kind of tried to mimic, and mine was terrible, I’m still very jealous of Asymco. Great name, great logo, you got it all working. And you gave me real confidence that there was a market for the sort of analysis that I felt I could provide. And when I started, I was a big reply guy for you, I was super excited. You would compliment my posts on Twitter occasionally, and you gave me a bunch of links on Asymco. So thank you for that, I’m here to say thank you.**

**HD:** Well, you’re very welcome. I was always happy to see your work and actually, I was very grateful that people picked up the torch and ran with it. It was the beginning of an era at the time where analysts could actually have a voice — either you worked on Wall Street, you worked for big corporate, but suddenly going out on your own and doing it online was a big new thing with blogs. I was happy to see more and more people take up that challenge.

My inspiration was [John Gruber](<https://daringfireball.net/>) also, because he was doing it, but he was doing it as like more of a tech product and not so much business — it was more like technology analysis, but and obviously product reviews and all that, but those were amazing times.

And by the way, just to plug myself a little bit, although Asymco has been around ever since before Stratechery, we just relaunched into [Asymco One](<https://asymco.com/one/>), which is for the first time a subscription version. 80% of what we do is still public and is all readable, there’s a little bit behind a paywall, but it’s like mostly that’s intended for people who are investors, who may want to get some of the data, some of the numbers, and so we keep some of that a little bit more private.

I’m still very willing to share a lot of information though, so we’re still publishing a lot, and we just launched it a couple of months ago and kind of excited to get back in the game and writing two to three times a day. So it’s super exciting.

**That is the other reason to have you on. You are, like you said, back in the game, let’s get to that in a little bit, before we get to what you’re doing now though, this is the first time — I actually[appeared on your podcast called High Density](<https://asymco.com/2013/08/30/high-density-3-staying-hungry-with-ben-thompson/>), I think I was the third guest that was on there, so we did talk once that was in 2013, but you have never been on Stratechery and whenever someone comes on the first time, I love to get the backstory and I think you have a particularly interesting one. Lots of pieces I want to dive into, but let’s go back to the beginning, where you grew up, not the United States, how you came to America — I think your education path is particularly interesting, let’s go back to the beginning.**

**HD:** Sure, I’m gonna try to make it real fast. First of all, I’m older than Apple.

**(laughing) I’m not, that’s how I started my Article this week — most tech companies I can remember the origin story, Apple is not in that camp for me.**

**HD:** We’re separated a few years actually I was born in 1968 and Apple in 1976, but the thing is that I was born in Romania at a time when it wasn’t part of you the European Union, it was behind the Iron Curtain, and my father was wanting to leave and became a dissident. So at that time, wanting to leave a country that was communist was considered a crime, so you were persecuted, and so it was pretty tough times, but we made it out. We were refugees for a while, and we first moved to Italy as stateless persons, I didn’t have a passport, I was not technically a citizen of any country.

**Is there a dramatic story about how you actually made it out of Romania?**

**HD:** Slightly. They took away all our possessions, so all we had was one suitcase per person, and I was like nine years old, so I had to carry my own possession with me. Four people, two parents, two children, having to leave a country with nothing, no money, no cash, no jewelry was allowed. It was very tough but we left on an airplane so we didn’t have to fight our way through, swim across something.

**Not quite[Andy Grove style](<https://www.amazon.com/Swimming-Across-Andrew-S-Grove/dp/0446529923>), but yes.**

**HD:** Yeah, exactly. But we made it out in Italy, and then we applied for immigration to the United States under some allowance for people who are refugees, and we made it. And we got there about 1978, so I was a year in Italy and I learned Italian, by the way, I was like thrown into a school because we left in August and in September the school starts and I had to go to attend a school in Italy. I didn’t speak a word in Italian, but I learned it pretty quick because Romanian, if people don’t know, is actually a Latin language.

**Right.**

**HD:** So it’s pretty easy to pick up another one, and at that age, you pick it up real easy. So I stayed in Italy, we stayed for a full year and I took a whole year of schooling there, which was great, it was elementary school, but still. Then we went to the United States and we had to find sponsorship, it was tough to find a place to live and so on, and we ended up in Cleveland. We thought we’d be in Boston at first because there was a church willing to sponsor us there, we ended up in Cleveland. And that’s where I grew up, more or less, up until high school or late high school, actually.

**Oh, interesting. I didn’t realize you’re a fellow Midwesterner. I see all the good analysts come from the Midwest, it’s great to know.**

**HD:** Oh, absolutely. Yeah, the Midwest made a big impression, that was my first real view of America, so we’re talking 1978 to 1984, I lived in Cleveland or its suburbs. At the time, it was Lakewood, Ohio, it was right next to West Cleveland, and then we moved to Boston at the time, 80s, we’re talking 1984 now, classic birth of the Macintosh-type time. But it was also booming in tech, especially around the [128 belt in Boston](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massachusetts_Route_128>), my dad got a job there.

**Was your dad technical as well?**

**HD:** He was a mathematician, actually, so he was a professor in university. So a very, very math-oriented family, my mother also was a teacher of mathematics, so I was brought up in that tradition, and plus you’re not really educated in business or finance — people don’t realize this, but when you grew up in those places at that time, you didn’t know what a credit card was, you didn’t know what the Wall Street Journal was, you didn’t know what a stock price was. You weren’t taught this in school, you didn’t go have a summer job or learn any of these things, a lot of Americans learn by osmosis. They learn about business, they learn about every aspect of finance and accounting and all these things.

For us, it was a very abstract thing and so I actually applied myself to try to figure it out, but that’s a story slightly later because I took up engineering as my undergraduate degree and I did that at Tufts University, which is in Medford, near where we lived in Boston area, and then I actually did a master’s as well. I got a job in the meantime in between those two degrees working at a place called GT Laboratories.

**By the masters, are you referring to your MBA or you also got a master’s in engineering?**

**HD:** No, I got a master’s in computer engineering, completely nothing to do with MBA.

**Got it.**

**HD:** This was 1992. I had an undergraduate degree in computer science and another one in electrical engineering, it was a complicated situation, I got two undergraduate degrees.

**You could make the whole computer, not just half of it.**

**HD:** Yeah, long story. But anyway, I also had a master’s in computer engineering from the same university but in the meantime, I was working through my master’s. I was working in the GT Laboratories, which was actually pretty prestigious because this was like a mini Bell Labs, this is like where you were a researcher doing really basic stuff. But at the time, the research I was working on, I was a junior guy, but it was a senior, what they call member technical staff, and the senior project leader was working on information retrieval. Now, information retrieval, just to give you a background, was the name of what search became.

**Right.**

**HD:** Search was born with essentially the Internet or even a little bit after that with AltaVista, which was one of the first search engines, and you had other alternatives, but Google is the one that ran with it. But basically, I was doing this 10 years earlier, so we were not yet in the Internet world, but there was still a question of how to retrieve documents from large data sources, mostly textual.

**Was this structured or unstructured data that you were working with?**

**HD:** Unstructured. We were training ourselves on things like 20 years of the Wall Street Journal, but it wasn’t hyperlinked data, it was like plain text, huge amounts of text.

But the big thing that happened between when I was there, which was 1992, roughly, and 2000, was that the Internet took off. Now, we were using the Internet in a much, much simpler way without the World Wide Web, it was the Internet back when it was just FTP and Telnet and other things that was done in laboratory-type environments or academic-type environments. I was very much into all of this stuff because I grew up with, I shouldn’t say I had a personal computer at home, but I grew up with mid-size, mid-so-called mini computers, which were from [Digital Equipment](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Equipment_Corporation>) or from IBM or from various like, you know, workstations, Sun, Apollo, these things. So I was like a command line guy using Unix and for me, PC was actually passé by about 1989, I was already jumping into these computers that were much more powerful. And so we had the [X windowing system](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X_Window_System>), which was very cool, it was like today you would say it would be like a Chromebook, it was like a terminal, but it was graphical.

A lot of stories but basically, I was in that world for doing research for six years until I kind of had, a curiosity about business, I just didn’t understand how it worked and I thought, “Hey, the way you learn business is by going to a school that teaches you business”, that was my naive idea. I said, “Well, what schools teach business?” — it would be business schools! And so I applied and I didn’t get in the first time I applied, and the feedback was that I didn’t really have business experience because you needed a little bit, you needed something, and all my work was being a very technical researcher.

**Look, I’m the guy that went to business school from being an English teacher so I know exactly the challenges that you’re talking about.**

**HD:** They just gave me nice feedback and then I said, “Okay, I need a job in ‘business'”, so it turns out I was headhunted by someone at Chase Manhattan, now it’s called Chase, I guess. But it was back in 1993 and I went to New York and I worked in effectively an IT role. So it was not very challenging technically, but it was at least in a business environment and then I reapplied the year after and lo and behold, I got in.

So I had to cut short my New York career, my IT career, my banking career as it was and I went back to Boston and I did two years at Harvard and that was when I was partly blown away, but also partly disappointed because actually, there was no theory of business.

#### Business Theory

**Right. Tell me about this, because you said before you were naive. In what way were you naive?**

**HD:** Because when you learn engineering, you learn first science and you learn basic physics and chemistry, you learn mathematics, and you learn that things are axiomatic and things are built on top of each other so that there’s consistency all the way up the stack.

So the way a computer works can be traced all the way down to the physics or subatomic physics, if you wanted. VLSI, circuits, all of these things are based in physics and so there was this notion that you start with the axioms and you build on those and you make proofs or you establish causality, at least. A implies B implies C, therefore A implies C, things of that nature, so you do a lot of math and you think that that’s what business also depends on these things.

But then when you go to a business school, you realize the way I put it retrospectively is that it’s like equivalent of sitting around a campfire telling stories to one another.

**Especially at Harvard where everything is a case study.**

**HD:** Everything’s a case study. And they got their idea, by the way, of case studies from the law school, which was just across the river, because when the business school was started, they didn’t know how to teach business, and so they just said, “Hey, they have this thing called cases in the law school”, and basically, “If lawyers can learn law this way, then maybe business managers can learn business this way”, that’s where it came from. And I think Harvard was like the second school so at the time, it was like 1903, the birth of the business school was not that long ago.

Anyway, so you go through the case method and yeah, there was an inkling, however, of a theory and it was maybe some heuristics and there were some rules of thumb and things like that. And in finance, you do a bit of math, that’s fair about that, but the thing that really influenced me was my professor at the time who was teaching us operations and his name was [Clayton Christensen](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clayton_Christensen>) and he stood up and then said things to me which resonated deeply, which were that, “Hey, we don’t know what the causality is, but we are going to find out”, and there are times in history where businesses or parts of business management were completely unknown, but we figured it out.

The example you would give was like in operations is that the factories used to be either good or bad, and the only determination we knew whether they were good or bad is whether there was a good manager or a bad manager. So a good manager was worth his weight in gold and was always getting poached to run another factory because somehow he had the magic touch, but that was like in the 50s and by the 70s, we knew, or even the 80s, we knew that you could manage factories to be reliable in their output without having to rely on some genius. And the Japanese, partly Japanese, are responsible for teaching us this, although they themselves learned it from Americans much earlier, long story. But basically, at the business school, I was like, “Wow, hey, this is actually exciting because this is an emerging theory”, so maybe I can help even figure this out.

So at the time, Christensen was writing his book that became a classic, which is [The Innovator’s Dilemma](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Innovator%27s_Dilemma>), he was actually writing it while he was teaching us, so he was even using some of the materials on us, and we didn’t know at the time, but they came out as chapters in the book. And two, three years later, I think it was 1998, well, I graduated in 1996, but in 1998, the book came out, and the Internet blew up and suddenly everybody was doing startups.

It’s funny because in my graduating class, normally you would go do management consulting or you would do finance, these were the two major categories of employment for graduates of the business school. Historically, it was more like general management, but that had gone out of favor by the 90s, nobody really went to become a CEO of IBM from there. But finally, as the late 90s rolled around, suddenly five years after everybody graduated, they were all working in startups. But I actually went straight to a startup in 96, which was very unusual.

**I want to ask you real quick about the Professor Christensen: was there an aspect where it was actually less the theory that compelled you and more the fact he was trying to systematize and produce a theory in the first place?**

**HD:** Yeah, it was just that there was a hint of a theory that he he proposed that there are heuristics that work, for example the [Toyota Production System](<https://global.toyota/en/company/vision-and-philosophy/production-system/>), TPS, famous — [The Machine That Changed the World](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Machine_That_Changed_the_World_\(book\)>). We went through that and he explained, for example, just asking five questions why is better than asking one question or no questions why and the Japanese kind of had this rule of thumb that you should ask [five whys](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Five_whys>) because you’re going to eventually come to the real cause of a problem. And although that’s not really a very strong theory, it’s actually an extremely powerful idea that you should seek out the causality.

**Right.**

**HD:** And then you ask the same questions about why people buy things, and as hard as it is to believe that the determination of why people buy things was a complete unknown and probably still is, to solve that, they propose a theory called the [Jobs to Be Done Theory](<https://www.christenseninstitute.org/theory/jobs-to-be-done/>), as opposed to the kind of, “Women buy this and young people buy that”, it was not demographic based.

**Which were just descriptors, they weren’t causal.**

**HD:** Yeah, the attributes of the person, not the attributes of the thing they’re trying to do, which was completely different. It’s like outcome-based, circumstances-based and all you had to do was be patient enough and ask the people who were making the purchase decision, follow them, not to ask them why they do it, because they themselves don’t know. That was another piece of the puzzle, you can’t just ask.

So what he had was he called it like drawers you would pull on the cabinet, and inside each drawer there was a theory, you try to pick and choose from a bunch of ideas, it’s the basic beginning of empiricism, it’s like “Oh, pattern recognition”, “Oh, maybe we should categorize things”, “These seem to go together and these are separate”. This is very basic stuff and with my own background, I wouldn’t say it was very deeply scientific, but at least you get a bit of introduction to the scientific method. And I was like, “Wow, actually, this is exciting because there’s probably a theory that’s out there, which may or may not be true”, by the way, in retrospect, but I was excited at the time to think that there was a theory and secondly, that it seems like this is the way to discover it, but I wasn’t yet ready to dive into being a researcher on business theory. What I was hoping for is to get a job and pay off my loans.

**Let’s circle back to Christensen and some of the hints you just gave about the theory and how applicable it might be, certainly something that[I’ve written about](<https://stratechery.com/2013/clayton-christensen-got-wrong/>) and thought about a lot over the years.**

#### Nokia

**But did you go straight to — you mentioned you went to a startup, where does Nokia come into this journey here after business school?**

**HD:** Nokia was like my my third job. I did one startup with a partner, then I went on my own and did another startup, this was like about 1999, I hopped out and did my own, this was going to be in the eBook space.

My first startup was document analysis. We were using — today, we wouldn’t call it AI, but back then it was called AI — we were trying to look at a document and break it up into its components and then try to reconstruct the document and that got applied to PDF. PDF extraction is something that people are kind of doing now, but that takes essentially, and it took an expert system to do that, so that was the first startup.

The second one was actually, with the knowledge of PDF, I was able to take all printed materials and make eBooks out of them. Essentially, a production system for eBook publishers. So they could take their pipeline, which is print-oriented, and then reorient it to this new thing we call eBooks. And at the time, these were going to be read on computers mainly. But on the horizon, I was watching and talking to a lot of people on the horizon were devices.

Initially, they were PDAs, but we were looking further down and I said, “Phones are going to be huge”. Already, dumb phones or cheap phones were quite popular in about 1998/1999, and millions and millions in the world were sold every year. And I said, “That little screen could get bigger”, that’s all I observed, and then I got on an eBook standards committee and met somebody from Nokia there. And I told them — and this was in the U.S., but the Nokia guy was Finnish — but I told them, “Guys, you’re the ones that are going to dominate this because the phone is going to be the reading tool of the future”, and they’re like, “You’re the only person who agrees with us”.

**Well, that’s interesting. So you’re the one that agrees with us, that implies that they saw the future.**

**HD:** Oh absolutely they did, this is 1998 — in 1997 there was a movie called [The Saint](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Saint_\(1997_film\)>) in which it featured a smartphone, this is 1997, Val Kilmer was in it, and he had this opening phone which they called the brick but it was a phone that opened that had a screen and a keyboard inside a phone in 1997 and by 1998 they introduced a new version and by by 2000, they had a color version and that was called the [Nokia Communicator](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nokia_Communicator>), already on the third version and that was like such a cool product for me, it’s what I carried for many, many years, and I read books on it in 2000, I read the Harry Potter series on the phone in the year 2000, 2001.

**We’re two peas in the pod here, I remember I got my first cell phone I want to say 1999, 2000 somewhere, but I quickly figured out how to get an RSS reader or I think it was like some like WAP site where you could get your feeds and I would just read on there. It was like four lines of text all the time, which was great until I got my phone bill, which was astronomical. I’m like, “Oh, shoot”, the data was quite expensive back then.**

**HD:** So the Nokia people were like, “This is the only guy in America who seems to agree with us”, the Japanese had ideas, the Europeans had ideas, but I was in the US at the time and then they said, “Would you be willing to just be a consultant for us? We want you to write a report on how phones could be used to deliver content in the form of written content”. It could be newspapers, could be magazines, could be books, but these were all businesses, these were publishing businesses.

And I said, as far as media is concerned, this media problem can be solved with phones, media distribution, how do you make a profit? What’s the margin? How do publishers work? How do you get deals with them? How do you protect it? Just the whole business plan for distributing and I wrote it up about books and then they said, “Great, now we want to actually implement some of that, would you be willing to come on?” — and by now, we’re looking at year 2000. In 2001, as you know, as we crossed into 2001, the great crash occurred.

**Suddenly a job offer from a big phone company sounds pretty great!**

**HD:** The crash occurred and it impacted my startup because I couldn’t raise money anymore and that’s why I said, “Okay, I’m throwing in the towel, I’ll take this idea to Nokia and build it inside of this company”. So for the first year, I actually worked out of the US and then they invited me to come to Finland and I fell in love with it, not to mention finding someone to fall in love with, I’m still here.

So we created an internal venture called Media Distribution, and it struggled for a couple of years, we partnered with a publishing company here in Finland that is the biggest newspaper and magazine company and we worked with them, 9/11 happened, and we worked with them throughout that period to test this idea of distributing content on phones and that’s how I met my wife, actually, because she was working for the publishing firm and we were in the meetings together.

And my point is this, that remember when Apple News launched I forget how many years ago it was already, it was five, six years ago and she texted me during the keynote, she goes, “You realize that’s what we were working on together in 2001”, it was called Project Melissa, I can say that now, Project Melissa was this idea of Apple News today and that was 25 years earlier, or maybe 20 years.

That just goes to show you, sort of the story of my life, I was doing search 10 years before Google existed, I was doing eBooks 10 years before Amazon launched the Kindle, I was doing smartphones and media distribution on smartphones 20 years before Apple, more or less showed how it’s done, and so my problem has been that I kind of see things happening or see where things are going, but way too early. That’s why one of the theses I have over time is like, I don’t know what to call it, a quote I have, it’s the idea that the difference between a futurist and a billionaire is knowing when one thing happens. So it’s not about knowing what’s going to happen, a lot of people are very good at that.

**Including Nokia, to your point.**

**HD:** Nokia, science fiction writers, Elon Musk, a lot of people predict this, but getting the timing right is where the money is made and so that was one of those lessons I learned.

**When you talk about the publishing and Nokia and the reading and all those sorts of things, what it makes me think of is when the iPhone comes along in 2007, and I’m very curious to get your perspective on what that was like inside Nokia when the iPhone launches. But to this publishing point specifically, the key bit was the Internet was permissionless in terms of producing content. It’s great that you met your wife, but the fact that you needed to be in a situation where you could meet your wife was actually the reason why it wasn’t going to happen, because if you have to make a deal to do everything, it’s just never going to get there. Whereas if you can just surf the Internet, the real Internet, and no one needs to do anything to adapt to it, suddenly you get it all for free and then you get the virtuous cycle going where they’re making stuff for you and that’s the timing bit is it’s like the the external structure around what you’re doing.**

**HD:** Yeah, it’s complex and it’s multi-causal. And you’re right, it’s multiple reasons why things happen the way they are and it’s just not foreseeable. And by the way, the thing that really caused the project to fail and be abandoned, this Media Distribution inside Nokia, was actually a fixation on DRM, and DRM actually killed a lot of great businesses with content ideas back in the day, whether it was music or video or textual. It was people just obsessed about protecting it before they obsessed about distributing it and making it great and this is why when iTunes came along, even though there were some circumstances there with being on a Mac and not on the PC, that the publishers finally said, “Why don’t we go ahead with this?”.

So long story there, but as far as what Nokia was doing, that was like for four years, I did this startup inside of Nokia that was kind of abandoned and then they switched me to, because I was kind of, “He’s the guy with an MBA, he’s the guy with an engineering degree, he can speak both languages, let’s put him in charge of doing some analytics”. So finally I got into the job of essentially developing strategy analysis and doing competitive analysis initially.

**What you do now.**

**HD:** It was like they asked me to do Microsoft, Microsoft was the biggest worry for everyone because they had intentions of being in the phone business, they made no secret about it, they had prototypes, they were working with competitors of Nokia to give them the operating system so that they would swap Nokia with like Samsung running Microsoft Mobile, it went through many names, but essentially that was the effort. There’s a couple of tests, litmus tests, and one of the things I immediately came to as a conclusion with AI was that it was very much sustaining. Now, the question is to whom? It’s certainly disruptive to some job categories, but is it disruptive to technology companies that are extremely wealthy today and have a lot of money or a lot of capital.

So the test for that is, is this something that repels incumbents or causes them to embrace it and control it? Because the key thesis of disruption is that incumbents are allergic to the new thing. They’ll either say, “No, this is uninteresting”, or, “Not for us” or, “Not for our customers”. Or if they look into it, they say, “We can’t make money with this”, “The components are too expensive”, “The bill of materials is too high, we can’t build millions of these profitably”, “None of it makes sense”, “We know the price is going to go down because of competition so our margins are going to disappear” — you keep rationalizing why not to do it.

And so my take on AI is like when people said that it costs hundreds of billions of dollars to create a leading model, well, what startups can do this? I will put it to you, I did this with mobility, I would ask myself every time, “Can a company in Africa today become a global giant manufacturer of mobility vehicles?”, well, if it’s bicycles, yes, if it’s scooters probably but when you start to say talk about cars, probably not now. When you then ask the same question, “Who can make this thing now?”, there’s some hints that open source guys are doing something in China and like I get interested, I’m much more likely to get excited, but if you’re telling me it costs half a trillion dollars to be in the game, I say well, this is a big game, let them play at it, but that doesn’t make it disruptive.

#### Apple and AI

**HD:** Here’s how I’m going to answer this, I was speaking in New York to investors once in January recently, and I said this, what Apple is not allergic to, what they’re most attracted to, is having the interface between humans and computers — having or controlling, whatever you want to call it, but they’ve always lived on being right there. That begins with the Apple II, where they had the interface, the first personal computer, the interface happened to be a keyboard and a monitor. Then they moved into being a mouse and bitmap display and then they moved to a scroll wheel and then they moved to multi-touch and gestures. And all of these things, for each of these major leaps forward, platforms were built, empires were built. Now, they happened to be competing ones, but it usually settled into one or two or three.

But in the case of — we had the DOS era, we had the Windows era, we had the iPod and we have the iPhone and I said, if Apple wants to be on the next thing, it’s going to try to figure out, “How are we going to communicate with computers better in the future?”, and a lot of people said, “It’s voice”, and that was all the Alexa thing, some people said it’s Watch. Well, that kind of helps, but it’s very short duration, glances only, so we kept debating and debating and then the big thing, which also captured the imagination of Zuckerberg, was like, “What if it’s a visual interface, but it’s really head-mounted?”, and so I still am a firm believer that that’s the next frontier is human interfaces and then in the middle of that, somebody comes along and says, “Hey, how about we go back to the command line?”, it’s like 1976 again.

**Right.**

**HD:** I remember those years fondly, at least the years when I was using a command line for everything on a computer. I still know by heart all the Unix commands I can right now open a terminal and do anything I want, the point is though that that’s what the state of the art of AI interfaces was at least a few years ago and then it’s slowly moving into the voice but when I ask, “Why would Apple want to go there?”, the answer is they wouldn’t, there’s no challenge in figuring out anything.

**Well, here’s sort of the counterpoint. If this is a completely new type of computing, and it’s almost like AI is like business versus traditional computing, which is like engineering, it’s very deterministic versus probabilistic, and if you have to go back to the beginning, Apple did start with the command line. Like the Apple II, you had to type in what you wanted it to do. I guess in the short and medium term, I think Apple is clearly fine because you need a device to access AI and they make the best devices. The question, though, is if the way to interact with AI has to go through that progression from command line to[WIMP interface](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WIMP_\(computing\)>) to multi-touch to whatever it might be, by the time we get to multi-touch, could you have gotten there without having gone through the whole path? And at that point, what would be a problem?**

**HD:** Yeah, it’s unlikely and just to complete my theory, I said Apple would lose AI if AI becomes enabled through a new interface that is low end, that is voice-only, which I create an equivalence here, the bandwidth of voice, even of type, it’s a little bit more than typing, but it’s like a 1200-baud modem. The brain has to actually work very hard in cognition to understand what’s being spoken.

**And there’s an analogy to AI here where we’re stuck on these tokens that are like words, it’s very inefficient compared to how this could be done.**

**HD:** Exactly. Words are terrible for the interface because they depend on cognition, not on perception, perception always wins against cognition, this is why people are not thinking, they’re acting and reacting so there’s a huge gulf between these worlds. Apple is always very conscious of the perception, very, very much from the beginning it’s about really triggering people and perceptions.

So my point is this, I’m not dismissing the low-end disruption, but it has to evolve. So it has to convince the world that you need to put aside your phone and we’re going to work with you as an agent and the metaphor there is like, what if you had — there’s always this great thought experiment, ask what rich people do today and ask how can that be done by billions of people? And almost everything in history has gone that way. We live, an average person lives far better than any king would have in the Middle Ages or even 200 years ago. And today, if you think about rich people and how they lead their lives — seriously, somebody should do you know a jobs to be done analysis, just follow a person who has at least net worth of $100, maybe $200 million and see what they do all day and to see to what extent they depend on other people. Now, I’m sure, for example, for travel, they don’t go on search for their flights, I’m sure when it comes to getting to the airport, they don’t figure out, “Should I drive or take an Uber and deal with parking?”, they have an assistant to do all that and they used to have an assistant to do a lot more, but like that became part of the phone.

But the thing that is disruptive is that the interface for most people will not be a screen, but will be effectively like almost a earpiece with maybe other displays around you. Maybe every display is your display and then it becomes more like, “Hey, I’m your best friend, I’m your companion, I’m your assistant, I’m maybe even your lover”, and people will totally trigger on that. Because companionship and all those aspects of interacting with other humans, those are far more powerful hormonally than any cognition or perception. Then AI has a shot, which is why I always think that the AI boyfriend or girlfriend is a disruption potentially but we got to be thoughtful about how we get to transition through that into something that you trust. You will be divulging information to this assistant that is so personal that they’ll know more about you than you know yourself and more than any doctor so we’ve got to be very careful to earn that trust but my point is that, yes, if that interface becomes so pervasive or so subliminal or so non-visual that Apple would find it like, “We can’t add value here, there’s no surface for us to paint anything on”, then it’s a worry.

On the other hand, I also say that the “smart” in the smartphone can get smarter. There’s no reason why the phone couldn’t be the best AI device the world could imagine. Maybe it’ll take 20, 30 years to move beyond it and even so, I still think putting a 4K display on top of every retina is more interesting than, than putting an AirPod in your ear, but that’s just me. #### Apple at 50

**This is the 50 year question, right? MacBook Neo,[arguably the most perfect Mac yet](<https://asymco.com/2026/03/18/the-biggest-surprise-of-the-macbook-neo/>). Where is Apple in 50 years? Are they still around? What are they doing?**

**HD:** [I’m writing all this up on my blog](<https://asymco.com/2026/03/23/the-next-50/>) and I’d love to have conversations there but I even asked today, I asked the question, “What are the odds that Apple survives?”, and the data — again, I go back to the data — and I point out that actually being in a Fortune 500 is very fragile. In other words, I call it the unfortunate 500, those who are there, because you tend to live a very short life in a Fortune 500.

But if a company reaches 50 years in the Fortune 500, it’s most likely it’s going to stay forever because there are a few what I call “immortals” that have been around since like a century ago, they’re still there. Oil companies, IBM, a bunch of railroads, stuff like that. And Apple actually was born in 1976, but it didn’t join the index until 1983, so I’m kind of using the clock from then. There’s this sort of, as opposed to human beings who live long lives, but we know what the limit is — with companies, they live very short lives, except for a few that are becoming more and most people are fixated on those and don’t realize that the tens of thousands of bodies that are buried to make those other ones survive. It’s not at all guaranteed that if you have even a big business, you’re going to survive. So there’s a myth about corporations being somehow superhuman and all that. There’s a duality to it, my first observation was that already by the fact that it’s made it this far, it’s kind of buying itself a long life.

But the more important thing, and I think there isn’t really a good theory of Apple that’s been published, but I think the company is an exception. Not the original Jobs, but Jobs 2.0, which came in in 1997. You know, they say [that the unexamined life is not worth living](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_unexamined_life_is_not_worth_living>). When I was at Nokia, I even pointed out to people that because of turnover, there is no institutional memory, there are some old timers maybe who remember things, but most people have not been there long enough to know what was the causality of success and failure for the firm from the beginning. They might read about it, there’s a lot of lore, most of it is written by journalists. No offense, but it’s written by people who want to tell a good story and that’s how you get published.

**To that point, when I was at Apple, I was at Apple University, which at that point, this was the goal, “What makes Apple Apple?”, was the point.**

**HD:** Exactly, so that’s the evidence of introspection. They’re trying to think, “What do we do to pass this on?”, and I think they were conscious of it in Steve’s time and I think Tim has had this on his mind for the whole tenure. And the oldest are Japanese, and they actually have a trick up their sleeve, the Japanese last for hundreds of years because they adopt people as adults into the family because they don’t trust their own children because they feel that they’re born with a silver spoon in their mouth and they’re not going to fight very hard but if you brought into the company as an adopted son or daughter, you might fight harder. This is so it’s fascinating to understand longevity, mortality and all these things. Apple, I think, is cognizant, I’m not how good they understand the problem, but at least they’re aware of the problem and they’re trying to figure it out.

There’s other things about the story of Apple, which is that the focus and the idea that the customer is the most important thing. The customer comes back based on their satisfaction, there’s nowhere in the financial statement where that number can be read, however. And by the way, there’s a numerator, you divide everything in the financial statement by the customer dissatisfaction and so those are the two most important numbers that companies should have and everyone should look at them as a KPI every single day yet hardly anybody knows what they are or what they should be.

It’s just because we’ve become kind of, it’s glazed over, it’s like, “yeah, yeah, everybody says they want to have happy customers and stuff”, but I do believe that Apple cares about it a bit more than others.

That unlocks a lot of puzzles because when they went through all these weird decisions and other companies went different ways, Microsoft was an example, Google was an example, and I highlighted some of these facts.
