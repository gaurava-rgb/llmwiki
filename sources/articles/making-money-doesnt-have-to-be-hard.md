**the insight most people walk straight past**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBxGb6pWUAA-Jy1.jpg)

there's a logic that keeps showing up in every successful content operation right now, and once you see it you can't unsee it.

individual AI tools used in isolation are incrementally useful. the same tools connected into a deliberate pipeline are categorically different. not 2x better. not 5x. operating on a completely different plane.

the pipeline he built comes down to three tools doing three specific jobs in sequence.

**claude handles the brain work**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBxGLLLXYAAsO1X.png)

you open claude and you prompt it like this: "give me 10 surprising facts about nikola tesla that most people have never heard."

thirty seconds later you have a tight, research-backed script. hook, facts, structure, done. run the same prompt for napoleon. for marie curie. for alan turing. for abdülhamid. for anyone with a photograph and a story.

at normal speed this takes hours per video. with claude it takes minutes per script, and you can generate dozens in a single sitting.

**seedance 1.0 handles the visual gap nobody noticed**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBxG-TpXcAApEGc.jpg)

here's the source advantage that makes this whole thing work, and it's hiding in plain sight.

photographs of historical figures exist everywhere. they're free, they're public domain, and there are thousands of them. but video of these people? actual moving footage? almost none of it exists, and what does is grainy and 10 seconds long.

seedance 1.0 closes that gap. you feed it a still photograph and it outputs a 5-second realistic animated micro-video. the person moves. the image breathes. it feels like footage that shouldn't exist.

suddenly you have a content format historical figures in real video that people want to watch, that the algorithm hasn't seen a million times, and that costs you almost nothing to produce because the source material is free.

that's a source advantage. and it compounds.

**capcut turns it into a finished product**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBxHfoYXUAAurNw.png)

take 12 of those 5-second seedance clips. drop them into capcut. combine them in vertical shorts format. add the claude-generated script as captions. turn on capcut's built-in TTS to read the facts aloud. drop royalty-free music underneath.

you now have a finished, polished, platform-ready short-form video. moving historical figure in the background. flowing captions in front. voiceover reading the facts. the whole thing took maybe 20 minutes once you know the workflow.

and here's the thing: the marginal cost of the next one is nearly zero. same pipeline, new script, new figure, new clips.

**the move most people miss**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HBxH8Q4WIAAxdrb.jpg)

when people figure out this pipeline their instinct is to post immediately. generate a video, upload it, repeat. stay reactive. chase trends.

that instinct will keep you on the treadmill forever.

the actual play is collect-then-distribute. you spend 2-3 months running the pipeline at full capacity and you produce 365 videos. then you schedule one per day for an entire year and you walk away from the posting calendar entirely.

why does this matter? because the algorithm rewards consistency before it rewards content quality. a channel posting every single day for 12 months builds compounding momentum that sporadic posting can never match regardless of how good the individual videos are. the consistency is the strategy. and you can only guarantee consistency if you engineer it during production ,not by trying to maintain discipline across 365 separate posting days.

one sprint. one year of output. the machine runs, you move on to whatever's next.
