For high-stakes situations, the outcome is paramount.

Your team member’s learning is quite secondary.

So make trade-offs accordingly, and don’t be afraid to make the unpopular but correct decision.

Examples:

**1**) If an important customer meeting is very likely to go better if _you_ present, then you may want to just do that. Your team member might insist on doing the presentation (saying it is a learning opportunity or a challenge), but as a leader you must try to maximize the odds of success here.

**2**) Often, your team members will want to be a “fly on the wall” for important reviews with the CEO. Usually, the CEO wants such meetings to be small (for candid discussion and higher-quality decisions). So depending on the specifics, you may have to say **No** to your team members.

**As a leader within a company, your primary responsibility is to your company.**

And to fulfill that responsibility, you’ll need to make unpopular calls like this. [1]

While all of this sounds fairly pragmatic (and perhaps even obvious), I often see that leaders avoid making the tough call because they don’t know how to communicate the “bad news” to their team member. And when they do make the tough call, they often fumble the communication, leaving their team member either frustrated or combative.

So here’s the most important thing to understand:

_As a leader, all your problems are messaging problems._

By getting really good at communication, you can soften the blow when you do make such correct, but unpopular calls.

So focus on how you can communicate better, more candidly, and with greater empathy. Don’t sacrifice outcomes because you don’t know how to have an awkward conversation.

Also, make sure you provide your team members with equivalent learning opportunities elsewhere (for example: presenting at town hall in lieu of presenting to the CEO).

**1) You’re underestimating compound returns on development**

Your framing treats each high-stakes situation as discrete. But leadership is about building capability that multiplies over time. **2) You’re missing second-order effects on team dynamics**

When you consistently pull rank in high-stakes moments, you train your team to be cautious and deferential in _all_ situations, not just the high-stakes ones. They learn: “When it really matters, leadership doesn’t trust us.”

This creates a perverse dynamic where your team members never develop the judgment to distinguish truly high-stakes situations from merely important ones. They also never develop the resilience that comes from working through high-pressure situations, even if some go poorly.

Your strongest future team members may self-select out. The ones with real ambition and capability will leave for environments where they’re trusted with meaningful risk. **Key learnings:**

**1) Company trajectory is the dominant career factor—and it’s verifiable**

Not learning opportunities, not manager quality, not even individual results. The company/product success that shows up on your LinkedIn is what recruiters filter by and what opens doors. This is observable (just look at how recruiters search) but conflicts with feel-good narratives about personal development.

**2) Company success and individual learning are the same thing, not trade-offs**

Hypergrowth periods—driven by company success—create more learning than any individual “learning opportunity” could. Optimizing for company outcomes IS optimizing for team development through the resulting growth and chaos. The framing of “outcomes vs. development” is false.

**3) Situational judgment beats theoretical frameworks**

Leadership decisions aren’t about applying consistent policies or managing “portfolios of development bets.” They’re about reading specific dynamics: this CEO meeting needs you, that one doesn’t; this customer situation is fragile, that one is resilient. Theoretical arguments about compound returns or risk-taking frameworks miss that you’re making context-specific calls, not implementing a philosophy.

**4) Messaging is the actual blocker, not the decision**

Leaders typically know the right call but avoid making it because they can’t communicate it well. The problem isn’t “should I present or should they?”—it’s “how do I explain why I’m presenting without demotivating them?” Reframing leadership challenges as communication challenges is more actionable than debating decision frameworks.

**5) The adaptive approach only works in growth contexts**

In slow-growth/maintenance companies where the trajectory is flat, the calculus changes. Company success won’t be a career catalyst, so individual development opportunities become relatively more valuable. Optimizing ruthlessly for outcomes makes sense when outcomes compound into hypergrowth; it makes less sense when they just make things marginally better with no multiplier effect.

**6) Theoretical objections often signal sophistication rather than truth**

Online discussion rewards contrarian, framework-heavy pushback over simple observable truth. “You’re underestimating compound returns” sounds smarter than “company brand matters most,” even when the second is obviously correct and the first falls apart under examination. People (including me) optimize for impressive-sounding arguments rather than grounding in evidence.

**7) Most career advice confuses stated preferences with actual behavior**

People say they value learning and mentorship, but their actual behavior (obsessing over company timing, brand names, trajectory) reveals that company success dominates. This gap between narrative and reality makes most career advice actively misleading.
