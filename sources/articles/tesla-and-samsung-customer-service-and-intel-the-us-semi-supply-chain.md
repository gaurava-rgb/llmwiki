### Tesla and Samsung

From the [Wall Street Journal](<https://www.wsj.com/tech/samsung-signs-16-5-billion-chip-supply-contract-with-tesla-a0d61216>):

> Samsung Electronics will manufacture artificial-intelligence chips for Tesla in Texas under a $16.5 billion multiyear deal, a major win for its U.S. foundry business that sent shares of South Korea’s largest company sharply higher. Tesla Chief Executive Elon Musk confirmed the deal on X, saying Samsung’s new facilities in Texas will be dedicated to making the U.S. electric-vehicle company’s next-generation AI6 chip. “The strategic importance of this is hard to overstate,” he wrote.
>
> The contract win comes as Samsung, the world’s largest memory-chip maker, struggles to catch up with Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing in the foundry business — making chips on a contract basis for customers such as Nvidia, Qualcomm and Apple, which design chips but don’t have their own factories to produce them. Samsung currently makes the AI4 chip, and TSMC will make the AI5 chip, Musk said. The AI6 chip is intended to be used in humanoid robots, self-driving cars and AI data centers.
>
> “Samsung agreed to allow Tesla to assist in maximizing manufacturing efficiency. This is a critical point, as I will walk the line personally to accelerate the pace of progress,” Musk said in a subsequent post. Tesla designs specialized AI chips that go inside of its vehicles to run its driver assistance software, which is known as Full Self-Driving (Supervised), as well as the more advanced software used by its autonomous vehicle taxi service, known as Robotaxi. Tesla also has an AI datacenter chip known as Dojo, which is specially designed to handle the vast amounts of data that Tesla expects to process once autonomous vehicles become more common. Both projects are central to Tesla’s objective to restructure its business away from selling electric vehicles and into selling AI and robotics-based services like self-driving cars.

Musk isn’t wrong: the strategic importance of this is hard to overstate, and not just for Tesla — or, for that matter, not just for Samsung.

Samsung is, in some respects, more comparable to Intel than they are to TSMC. Like Intel, Samsung started out making memory, before adding logic to its portfolio; unlike Intel, which abandoned the memory market completely in the face of Asian competition, Samsung still does both. And, like Intel, Samsung first and foremost makes chips for itself. This has caused problems: Apple, famously, sought to cut Samsung out of its supply chain after the South Korean phone maker started competing with the iPhone [rather shamelessly](<https://stratechery.com/2013/shameless-samsung/>), and other one-time customers like Qualcomm have expressed similar concerns with Samsung learning a bit too much from their foundry business.

Samsung’s memory heritage, meanwhile, has also been a mixed bag: on one hand memory, when the market is booming, generates massive amounts of profits that can fund expensive fabs; bust cycles, on the other hand, can breed conservatism in future investments (although Samsung built its original dominant position in memory by investing through bust cycles). It’s the cultural angle that is more interesting: memory rewards aggressive investments in new lithography in particular, but is very tolerant of iffy yields given that you are making the exact same memory cells over and over again, and can simply turn off the ones that don’t work; logic chips, however, have lots of individually unique components, which means that aggressively managing defect density is critical to yields (and thus profitability). To that end, Samsung’s logic struggles over the last decade are not just a function of competing with their customers but also acquiring the discipline to have effective yields with EUV lithography in particular.

Samsung has improved, however; it’s EUV utilization is very solid at this point, and undergirds their 7nm, 5nm, and 4nm processes; 3nm hit a number of speed bumps, but that was due to its struggles in implementing gate-all-around transistors. However, reports currently are that the 3nm process is improving steadily and that the company’s 2nm process is looking good. That, by extension, explains Tesla’s on-again/off-again relationship with Samsung: while AI4 is made on the very solid 7nm process, the 3nm delays sent the company to TSMC; for 2nm Tesla is coming back home, as it were.

### Customer Service and Intel

It was just last fall that Samsung’s planned fab in Taylor, Texas was in doubt; from [Reuters](<https://www.reuters.com/technology/samsung-delays-taking-deliveries-asmls-chip-gear-its-new-us-factory-sources-say-2024-10-18/>) in October:

> Samsung Electronics has postponed taking deliveries of ASML chipmaking equipment for its upcoming factory in Texas as it has yet to win any major customers for the project, three people familiar with the matter said. Samsung has been also holding off on placing orders to some other suppliers for the $17 billion factory in Taylor city, prompting them to look for other customers and send staff deployed on site back home, three other people familiar with the matter said. The delay in equipment deliveries is a fresh setback to the Taylor project, which is at the heart of Samsung chairman Jay Y. Lee’s ambition to expand beyond its bread-and-butter memory chips into contract chip manufacturing, which Taiwan’s TSMC dominates. It underscores the widening gulf between Samsung and rivals such as TSMC and SK Hynix which are ramping up production of high-end chips to cater to booming demand from artificial intelligence applications. ASML, the world’s biggest chipmaking equipment supplier, cut its 2025 sales forecast on Tuesday, citing weakness in markets other than AI, and delayed fabs. The Dutch company did not name clients who have delayed their fabs. Reuters is the first to report that Samsung has pushed back deliveries of some ASML equipment. Two of the sources said the delayed shipments to Samsung’s Taylor factory involve ASML’s advanced chipmaking equipment called extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography.

This new fab is now clearly going forward, thanks to Tesla, and I can definitely see the appeal to Musk:

  * As I noted above, Samsung is a familiar partners for Tesla
  * Tesla will be priority number one for Samsung, instead of needing to fight — and pay — to get capacity at TSMC
  * Musk gets to be very involved!



I actually think this tweet from Musk is a big deal:

> Samsung agreed to allow Tesla to assist in maximizing manufacturing efficiency.
>
> This is a critical point, as I will walk the line personally to accelerate the pace of progress. And the fab is conveniently located not far from my house 😃
>
> — Elon Musk (@elonmusk) [July 28, 2025](<https://twitter.com/elonmusk/status/1949675406384366051?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw>)

It’s fair to wonder how useful Tesla and Musk will be in terms of walking the line in a fab; on one hand, what expertise does Musk have in silicon manufacturing? Then again, you could have said the same about electric cars and rocketry not that long ago! I’m not going to rule out the possibility that Musk actually has some very useful insights on chip production. The more critical point, however, is that having the chance to walk the line was important to Musk, and if that was part of the price of acquiring Tesla’s business, then Samsung was willing to pay it — and justifiably so, given that Tesla is poised to make this entire fab viable, which is critical not only for the 2nm process, but the processes to follow.

This gets to the critical point that extends far beyond this deal: can you imagine, in a million years, Intel agreeing to such a stipulation? There are plenty of reasons that Intel didn’t win this contract, including the fact they don’t have a dedicated automative fab offering; the fact that while backside power is theoretically useful for automotive, it needs to be proven out in product first; and the very real issue that the company just admitted that it might not even make the 14A process. Ultimately, however, I believe that the most fundamental reason why [Intel’s attempts to become a foundry have failed](<https://stratechery.com/2025/tsmc-earnings-a16-and-tsmcs-approach-to-backside-power-intel-earnings-architecture-and-ai/>) is because Intel is incapable of being a customer service organization.

Samsung isn’t necessarily great, to be clear! I noted the IP concerns above, which also apply to Intel; ultimately, however, Samsung has it in themselves to service customers. Go back to Apple and the iPod and original iPhone; Tony Fadell told me in [a Stratechery Interview](<https://stratechery.com/2022/an-interview-with-father-of-the-ipod-tony-fadell/>):

> Truth be told, the iPod and wouldn’t have been as successful as it was if it weren’t for Samsung. The iPhone wouldn’t have been as successful as it was in the first generations if it weren’t for Samsung. A lot of people remember the hundreds of millions of dollars Apple used up to sue Samsung for patent infringement on the iPhone UI and stuff like that. But you have to look at Samsung providing displays, batteries, CPUs and custom parts, flash memory, DRAM. The number of strategic components in the iPod, and then in the iPhone, that were necessary from Samsung, was an incredible set.
>
> I had a partner in Oh-Hyun Kwon, he was the head of all Samsung semiconductors, he started as a guy just doing custom processors inside of Samsung based on their embedded stuff, and that’s where he and I both met. And we worked really hard on putting together that whole set of Samsung processors for custom processors that we designed with them, and we spec’d what we wanted exactly and went through it all. But then they muscled it through and built those processors for the entire line, from Shuffle all the way up to the Classic, they made all those processors…
>
> So what happened with Samsung was I think it was called the Blue line, I think it was Blue was the line of processors that they had. And it was these small embedded processors at ARM 32-bit. They were run-of-the-mill processors like anyone else had for ARM. You take the ARM package and you make your processor and what have you, there were not a lot of custom modules and stuff.
>
> And that’s when we started going, “Oh, well you have this.” and they’re like, “Well, we have our fab capability. We have these design teams sitting around, we have this ARM set.” “Oh, thanks. But this is not good at all. These processors aren’t good. They don’t have this, this, this, and this and this.” And so, O.H. took it upon himself, and he went back to Korea and four weeks later comes back to Cupertino who goes, “How about this part?” I’m like, “It’s better, but I don’t know if you can really make it.” And then I said, “But you need to change these things.” And Tupman was going, “We need to change these things.” Four weeks later, again, they come back. “How about this?” I’m like, “Oh my God, that’s what we want!” And I’m like, “Could they really pull it off?” I’m like, “Nah, I don’t know”. So we said, and this was against PortalPlayer at the time, who was the only chip supplier for the iPod, the main CPU supplier at the time and Tupman and I looked at each other, we’re like, “Yeah, it’s close. And you’re there, go fab it and let us know.” So they literally went out and they started fabbing it and saying, “We’re going to have parts in ten weeks or twelve weeks. Do you want to try it?” And we’re like, “Well, we need all this software. We need these base level modules, whatever.” And they’re like, “Okay, well go do that.” And we’re like, “What?” And literally through this testing process, Oh-Hyun Kwon, and that team muscled it together.
>
> To me, they were as great as any Silicon team that I saw in Silicon Valley. Maybe not the most innovative, but the hardest working and they made it happen. And I was like, “We got to give these guys a shot”. So these guys are just, they’re like us, and we were bonded together at the hip and we were like, “Let’s do this, let’s do this, let’s do this. And then let’s do this one part.” Then we’re like, “Well, why don’t you do the Shuffle part? And so, “Okay, you’ll do the iPod Shuffle part.” And then we were like, “Shit, they pulled off the shuffle part. Okay. Now you’re going to do the Classic part.” So it was this testing, testing, testing to see if they did it, and they stepped up each time and we were amazed and that’s where they really, they earned the partnership. They truly earned a partnership with us, and that’s why we were successful, because when we said we wanted to change display standards, so the inner phase, the internal interface between the CPU and the chip, which was the display drivers, which was at the time, people were talking about MIPI. MIPI was this new serial interface, high speed serial interface for LCDs.
>
> And the world was all navel gazing, going, “Yeah, MIPI sounds great, but who’s going to jump in? And Oh-Hyun Kwon and I just looked at each other over dinner and we said, “We’re going to do MIPI. Okay. We’re MIPI.” And we changed the whole industry to MIPI overnight because we said, “We’re going to do it.” iPod was the number one product, Samsung had displays. They had all the display drivers, so they made all the MIPI drivers, we made the iPods and the processors to be made with it and then all of a sudden MIPI rolled throughout the entire LCD value chain, which was amazing. That’s the stuff we got to do together.

In that interview Fadell explains why Intel was never under serious consideration for the iPhone, for reasons that go back to [what I wrote about yesterday](<https://stratechery.com/2025/tsmc-earnings-a16-and-tsmcs-approach-to-backside-power-intel-earnings-architecture-and-ai/>): the company was totally focused on performance instead of efficiency. This anecdote, however, is another reason, and arguably a more important one: Intel has always been a take-it-or-leave-it culture, and what was striking about CEO Lip-Bu Tan’s [earnings call](<https://seekingalpha.com/article/4804322-intel-corporation-intc-q2-2025-earnings-call-transcript>) was the extent to which he basically admitted that was still the case. Intel did what they wanted to do, with the focus on what they cared about, and barely bothered to confer with customers about what they wanted built, or what mattered to them. That, needless to say, is not the sort of attitude that Apple wants in its suppliers, and I’m sure you can say the same thing for Musk.

### The U.S. Semi Supply Chain

At this point, and with this news, combined with Intel’s threatened pullback, I’m ready to call it: the only two foundries on the leading edge are going to be TSMC and Samsung; Intel is done. The company says it needs a 3rd-party customer to build 14A, but who is left? I think that the Tesla commitment makes it much more likely that Qualcomm commits to Samsung, and potentially even Nvidia down-the-line (for automotive, in all likelihood; Samsung doesn’t have a clear backside power plan for AI chips). Apple and AMD aren’t going anywhere. Broadcom is a possibility, but I think that Broadcom would actually prefer to buy the Intel product business. Who, in the end, wants to commit to a company waffling on the future who is solipsistic in its design and has no history of actually being a compliant supplier?

This, to the extent it is true, means that TSMC’s U.S. plans loom large, and there is reason to be encouraged on that point. From CEO C.C. Wei’s prepared remarks on [TSMC’s earnings call](<https://investor.tsmc.com/chinese/encrypt/files/encrypt_file/reports/2025-07/1f4f86c935f1de837672a6973154e64b26bdae57/TSMC%202Q25%20Transcript.pdf>):

> With the strong collaboration and support from our leading US customers and the US federal, state, and city government, we announced our intention to invest a total of USD165 billion in advanced semiconductor manufacturing in the United States. The expansion includes plans for six advanced wafer manufacturing fabs in Arizona, two advanced packaging fabs, and a major R&D center to support the strong multi-year demand from our customers. Our first fab in Arizona has already successfully entered high volume production in 4Q 2024, utilizing N4 process technology with a yield comparable to our fabs in Taiwan. The construction of our second fab, which will utilize 3-nanometer process technology, is already complete. We are seeing strong interest from our leading US customers and are working on speeding up the volume production schedule by several quarters to support their need. Construction of our third fab, which will utilize 2-nanometer and the A16 process technologies, has already begun, and we are looking to speeding up the production schedule as well, based on the strong AI-related demand from our customers. Our fourth fab will utilize N2 and the A16 process technologies. And our fifth and sixth fabs will use even more advanced technologies. The construction and ramp schedules for those fabs will be based on our customers’ need.
>
> Our expansion plans will enable TSMC to scale up to a GIGAFAB cluster in Arizona to support the needs of our leading-edge customers in smartphone, AI, and HPC applications. We also plan to build two new advanced packaging facilities and establish an R&D center to complete the AI supply chain. After completion, around 30% of our 2-nanometer and more advanced capacity will be located in Arizona, creating an independent leading-edge semiconductor manufacturing cluster in the US.

The “GIGAFAB” reference is notable; Wei explained what a GIGAFAB was later in the call in a question about how TSMC would utilize older process nodes like N5:

> I’d like your comment on that we have to sell our value because we’re tight in N3 capacity. It will be continued for a couple of years, very tight. And in fact, N5 also very tight. The demand is high because a lot of our AI products still in the 4-nanometer technology node, and they will transition to 3-nanometer probably in the next two years. So in meanwhile, N5 are still very tight in capacity. N3, even tighter. And so we are working hard.
>
> One of the TSMC’s advantages is that we have GigaFab cluster. And so we have between N7, N5, N3, even the future N2, we have almost for each node, we have about 85% to 90% common tools. So it’s not free, but it’s much easier for TSMC to adjust or convert the capacity between those nodes. And today, let me share with you, we are using the N7 capacity to support N5 because the N5 is too tight. And then we are converting N5 to N3 as you just pointed out. We’ll continue to do that. And so today, our leading edge technology’s capacity, we define N7 and below are all very tight. And seeing that, we are working very hard to — again, using my sentence, to narrow the gap between the demand and the capacity.

This flexibility between process nodes is fairly new to TSMC; in fact, [it looks a lot like Intel](<https://stratechery.com/2024/tsmc-earnings-the-trailing-node-netflix-wwe-follow-up/>) in terms of repurposing equipment from one process to another. What is uniquely TSMC about this approach, however, is the implied flexibility: Wei is suggesting that TSMC can move fairly easily from one process node to another, which is probably overstated on one hand, but also worlds more flexible than Intel’s strict copy-exactly approach.

The more important point for this Update, however, is that Arizona is going to be a GIGAFAB in its own right: it’s not just one fab, copied exactly from Taiwan, but it’s own independent and flexible entity. In other words, it’s real resilience for the U.S. in a Taiwan war scenario.

Samsung’s Texas investment is another. Yes, South Korea has its own geographic challenges with regards to China, but at least the country isn’t under active threat; still, it would behoove the U.S. to push the company to make sure that Taylor can stand alone should the worst case scenario occur. That’s especially important because it’s hard to see Intel standing alone that much longer.
