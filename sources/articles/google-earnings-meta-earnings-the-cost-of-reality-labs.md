### Google Earnings

From the [Wall Street Journal](<https://www.wsj.com/tech/google-earnings-alphabet-q3-2025-goog-stock-c202d18a>):

> Google’s parent company reported a 16% surge in third-quarter revenue, with growth in its digital-advertising and cloud-computing units helping to finance robust artificial-intelligence spending. Sales reached a record $102.3 billion for Google-parent Alphabet, ahead of analyst expectations, and net income was about $35 billion, a 33% increase over the same period a year ago. Shares rose more than 6% in after-hours trading.
>
> Like other large tech companies, Google is pouring tens of billions of dollars into AI development. It lifted its estimates for capital expenditures this year to a range of $91 billion to $93 billion, up from $52.5 billion in 2024. The company said it expects a substantial increase in capital expenditures next year. Much of the money will be used to build data centers to develop and run AI models.
>
> Google’s cloud division, which sells computing power to data centers, has grown as a result of the race to develop AI. The cloud unit had $15.2 billion in quarterly revenue, up 34% from the same quarter last year.

Google was strong across the board, with double-digit revenue growth across Search, YouTube, and Google Cloud. Management continued to claim that AI Overviews in particular were increasing search volume, which naturally spilled over to there being more commercial queries, although dodged a question about whether the revenue gains outpaced the increased cost to serve.

It’s Google Cloud Platform, however, that was the real standout. Here’s the table I have been posting for years now:

I’ve been arguing for the last several quarters that Google’s big increases in capital expenditures were eminently justified by Google Cloud in particular, which was clearly capacity constrained. [From February](<https://stratechery.com/2025/google-earnings-search-status-china-antitrust-actions/>):

> I think that last bit, about Google’s forecast for a massive increase in capital expenditures (the Street was expecting $58 billion), probably has more to do with the stock’s reaction than the Cloud miss, but to the extent it is both, well, the reaction seems a bit overwrought. Last quarter Google had arguably its best Cloud results yet, with 35% growth on 17% margins; this quarter saw only 30% growth on 18% margins…
>
> Notice how those numbers differ from Q3 2023, the last time Google Cloud disappointed: in that quarter revenue growth disappointed and margin decreased, which suggests that Google had too much capacity for the revenue it earned, which is to say that the problem was a lack of demand. In Q4 2024, however, the opposite happened: revenue growth was a disappointment, but margins still rose; this suggests that the problem was a lack of supply in terms of infrastructure, and that is indeed exactly what management said on the earnings call…
>
> And now we’re back to the capex expansion: yes, that is a big increase, but there is at least one area of Google’s business — and it happens to be the area of Google’s business that gives outsiders the most visibility into AI demand — that doesn’t have enough.

This quarter we clearly saw some of that investment come online, as GCP revenue increased by the fastest rate since Q3 2024 (and you have to go back to Q3 2022, off a much smaller base, to find another higher growth quarter). Moreover, margin jumped significantly as well, which indicates all of that new capacity was immediately being utilized. CFO Anat Ashkenazi said on [the company’s earnings call](<https://abc.xyz/investor/events/event-details/2025/2025-Q3-Earnings-Call-2025-4OI4Bac_Q9/default.aspx>):

> As I’ve mentioned on previous earnings calls, while we have been working hard to increase capacity and have improved the pace of server deployments and data center construction, we still expect to remain in a tight demand/supply environment in Q4 and 2026.

The rational response to this is not to be upset at Google’s increase in capital expenditures; it’s to wish they had invested more previously, even in the face of a 41% increase in depreciation expenses. The ultimate goal is profit, not margin; one of the benefits of Google’s investment in GCP has been slowly conditioning investors to the reality that 90% margins aren’t going to last forever, but also that that is ok, as long as there is an expansion in money-making opportunities.

### Meta Earnings

From the [Wall Street Journal](<https://www.wsj.com/tech/metaplatforms-meta-q3-earnings-report-2025-e0666e9c>):

> Meta Platforms said it would “aggressively” ramp up spending to stay competitive in the artificial-intelligence arms race. That plan has investors sweating. The company reported record revenue in the third quarter on Wednesday, but warned that capital expenditures around AI are likely to accelerate significantly next year, sending its stock down more than 7% in after-hours trading. Meta also reported a surprise one-time tax charge of $15.9 billion that dragged down its net income. The company said it brought in $51.2 billion in sales, a 26% year-over-year rise, and net income of $2.7 billion, far below analysts’ expectations. It attributed the net income shortfall to an accounting adjustment to reflect future tax liability, resulting from the One Big Beautiful Bill Act. Meta said sales for the current quarter are expected to be between $56 billion and $59 billion, roughly in line with analysts’ expectations. The company also said it expects total expenses to “grow at a significantly faster percentage rate in 2026 than 2025,” primarily driven by infrastructure costs.
>
> On a call with analysts and investors, Meta Chief Executive Mark Zuckerberg said the acceleration was necessary to support its pursuit of what the company calls superintelligence, or AI that vastly outperforms human capabilities. “That way, if superintelligence arrives sooner, we will be ideally positioned for a generational paradigm shift in many large opportunities,” Zuckerberg said. “If it takes longer, then we’ll use the extra compute to accelerate our core business.” Zuckerberg added that in the worst case, “we would just slow building new infrastructure for some period while we grow into what we build.”

This was [a pretty interesting earnings call](<https://s21.q4cdn.com/399680738/files/doc_financials/2025/q3/META-Q3-2025-Earnings-Call-Transcript.pdf>), particularly in contrast to Google’s rather boring-yet-impressive one. There was, I think, a specific message that Zuckerberg and CFO Susan Li were trying to land, which is that (1) the company wants to be ready for massive AI breakthroughs when they come (i.e. they don’t want to wish they had invested more, a la the point above), and (2) Meta is already benefiting hugely from AI so the investments are justified by that alone. To that end, Zuckerberg said in his prepared remarks:

> Now there’s a range of timelines for when people think that we’re going to get superintelligence. Some people think that we’ll get there in a few years, others think it’ll be 5, 7 years, or longer. I think it’s the right strategy to aggressively front-load building capacity so that way we’re prepared for the most optimistic cases. That way, if superintelligence arrives sooner, we will be ideally positioned for a generational paradigm shift and many large opportunities. If it takes longer, then we’ll use the extra compute to accelerate our core business — which continues to be able to profitably use much more compute than we’ve been able to throw at it. And we’re seeing very high demand for additional compute both internally and externally. And in the worst case, we would just slow building new infrastructure for some period while we grow into what we build.
>
> The upside is extremely high for both our existing apps and new products and businesses that are becoming possible to build. Across Facebook, Instagram, and Threads, our AI recommendation systems are delivering higher quality and more relevant content, which led to 5% more time spent on Facebook in Q3 and 10% on Threads. Video is a particular bright spot, with video time spent on Instagram up more than 30% since last year. As video continues to grow across our apps, Reels now has an annual run rate of over $50 billion.
>
> Improvements in our recommendation systems will also become even more leveraged as the volume of AI-created content grows. Social media has gone through two eras so far. First was when all content was from friends, family, and accounts that you followed directly. The second was when we added all the creator content. Now, as AI makes it easier to create and remix content, we’re going to add yet another huge corpus of content on top of those. Recommendation systems that understand all of this content more deeply and can show you the right content to help you achieve your goals are going to be increasingly valuable. Our ads business continues to perform very well, largely due to improvements in our AI ranking systems as well. This quarter, we saw meaningful advances from unifying different models into simpler, more general models — which drive both better performance and efficiency. And now the annual run-rate going through our completely end-to-end AI-powered ad tools has passed $60 billion.

Forgive the long excerpt, but there is a lot to unpack here. First and foremost, I think that Zuckerberg’s core argument is fair: Meta is increasing engagement, and thus inventory, _and_ increasing price-per-ad; those are two metrics that almost always move in opposite directions:

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/11/meta-earnings-1.png?resize=1330%2C648&ssl=1)

The last time Meta increased both of these metrics at the same time was in early 2023 when the company cracked ATT before anyone else, figuring out how to probabilistically attribute conversions after the loss of deterministic signal. That the company is once again benefiting from increase computation — which is at the core of probabilistic calculations — is extremely plausible to me; moreover, I agree with Zuckerberg and Li on the call that there is a ton more the company can do in this regard, including using LLMs specifically to improve recommendation algorithms (and, as management noted, there are still a lot of companies who — foolishly, in my opinion — are still not using Meta’s fully automated ad suite which both delivers better results and has higher margins). Moreover, as [Eric Seufert notes](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/meta-q3-2025-earnings-26-advertising-revenue-growth-expenses-ramp/>), these improvements are not a one-off: they pay off every quarter forever.

### The Cost of Reality Labs

The problem for Meta in communicating this message — that the company’s core business benefits from increased computation to a massive degree — is threefold. First, Zuckerberg has never been that interested in the advertising business, and thus makes a poor salesman for it. Second, what Zuckerberg is interested in — SuperIntelligence — is vague both in terms of the subject and in terms of timing. Third, unlike Google, Zuckerberg can’t point to a concrete business like GCP that is benefiting from AI; he’s betting on a business that might in theory exist in a future that will arrive at an indeterminate amount of time.

One Zuckerberg comment that I found particularly notable was this response to a question about his product vision:

> But fundamentally, I would sort of expect this exponential curve in new technology capabilities that are going to become available. And the other thing that I expect is that I think being the best in a given area will drive great returns, rather than this is not like a check the box exercise of like, okay, we can generate some kind of content and someone else can. I think that like the company that is the best at each of these capabilities, I think, will get a large amount of the potential value for doing that.

This was arguably the most bearish thing that Zuckerberg said on the call: while at other points he highlighted Meta’s ability to distribute content and scale, and even in the excerpt above expressed optimism about AI content benefiting the company’s existing channels, this statement is counter to all of that. His argument is that the best product will win — and thus, implicitly, not the one with the best distribution — and, while that certainly explains why Zuckerberg spent so much money on acquiring talent, it suggests that this investment is going towards a plane of competition where Meta is not at an advantage (and, given its size, probably a disadvantage).

What I also think is happening is that Meta is finally paying the price for the billions of dollars it has spent on Reality Labs with precious little financial return to show for it. Yes, investors have long grumbled at how much money Meta has spent on AR and VR, but ultimately I think Zuckerberg has gotten a pass; even the stock market freakout in 2022 was more about TikTok and ATT than about the Metaverse (the Metaverse spending was taken as an indication that Meta’s core business was faltering).

This time, however, Zuckerberg once again seems to be asking for a blank check, and given that the last blank check is growing, not cashing, I think that investors are hesitant about writing another one. Again, I’m not sure this is necessarily fair — Meta is clearly leveraging more compute effectively in its ad business, and AI is not the Metaverse — but unless Zuckerberg wants to become the world’s biggest ad business salesman he’s going to need to show some tangible evidence sooner or later that Meta can in fact win on product.

Note the contrast to Google: both companies benefit more than anyone else from the application of AI to advertising. The difference is that Google spent the last decade building a cloud business that is both profitable and also clearly benefits from AI; Meta spent tens of billions on a business that has only increased losses and which benefits from AI in theory. No wonder increased capex from the former is now cheered, and the latter panned.
