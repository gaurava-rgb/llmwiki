### Nike on Amazon

From [The Information](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/nike-sell-amazon-first-time-since-2019>):

> Six years after Nike stopped selling through Amazon in favor of going direct to customers through its own stores and website, the two companies are getting back together. Amazon plans to start selling Nike products with supplies coming directly from the company, an Amazon spokesperson confirmed on Wednesday. Winning back one of the world’s best-known brands is a victory for Amazon, which has long tried to expand its fashion business by wooing skeptical brands. Nike’s deal with Amazon meanwhile comes as the sneaker maker is trying to revive slumping sales.
>
> Nike stopped selling on Amazon in 2019, two years after it began, after complaints that the company wasn’t doing enough to weed out counterfeit merchandise and unauthorized sellers on its site. Nike had complained about those issues for a long time before it began selling on Amazon in 2017 on the condition that Amazon would strictly police both issues. Nike ended the relationship due to lingering counterfeit issues and as the company decided to focus on selling directly to consumers.
>
> While Nike products have continued to appear on Amazon, they’re sold by independent merchants. This week, however, Amazon notified some of those merchants that it will ban them from selling certain Nike products beginning on July 19 because it was working with Nike directly, according to a message to merchants reviewed by The Information…
>
> Nike is in the midst of a broad turnaround, after its strategy to go all-in on direct to consumer sales went bust. After an initial boom during the pandemic, when online shopping thrived, Nike fell out of step with consumers who returned to shopping in stores and at retailers with a range of brands. Sales slumped sharply over the last year and Nike replaced its chief executive in October.

I’ll get to this specific news in a moment, but there is some interesting Nike-specific context that is worth exploring.

### Nike’s Disastrous Pivot

There has been a lot written about Nike’s struggles over the last few years, most notably [this LinkedIn post by former Nike executive Massimo Giunco](<https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/nike-epic-saga-value-destruction-massimo-giunco-llplf/>). Giunco points to three mistakes made by former CEO John Donahoe:

  * The elimination of categories like running, basketball, etc.
  * A massive deprioritization of wholesale in favor of direct-to-consumer sales.
  * A shift away from brand marketing to direct response advertising.



The first mistake certainly seems like a bad idea, but what are most interesting to me — and pertinent to Stratechery — are the second two. In fact, they are the very same mistake. Let me use Giunco’s own words to illustrate. From “End of Wholesale Leadership”:

> Before the beginning of FY21 (4 years ago), the CEO and the President of the Brand gave clear directions to their direct reports about the development and the composition of the new distribution construct by 2025: decline wholesale to become the second source of revenues; grow Nike Direct business heavily to transform it in the first and major source of company revenues; and lead with Nike.com.

Then, from “Lead with Digital Marketing”:

> Nike has been built for 50 years on a very simple foundation: brand, product, and marketplace. The DC Investment model, since Nike became a public company, has been always the same: invest at least one tenth of the revenues in demand creation and sports marketing. The brand model has been very simple as well: focus on innovation and inspiration, creativity and storytelling based on athletes-products synergy, leveraging the power of the emotions that sport can create, trying to inspire a growing number of athletes* (*if you have a body, you are an athlete) to play sport. That’s what made Nike the Nike we used to know, love, admire, professionally and emotionally.
>
> What happened in 2020? Well, the brand team shifted from brand marketing to digital marketing and from brand enhancing to sales activation. All in.

The best defense of Donahoe I can muster is that at least these two changes were coherent. Nike’s old model was exactly how commerce worked in the pre-Internet era: the goal of advertising was to increase brand awareness and affinity, so that when the customer was in a position to make a purchase — which is to say, not in front of a TV watching a commercial, but in the aisle of a store — they were more likely to purchase your product. This approach, meanwhile, was augmented by a smart channel strategy that both associated your product with the right set of retailers, and sought to dominate scarce shelf-space so that there were fewer choices to make at the moment of truth.

A direct-to-consumer model, on the other hand, goes hand-in-hand with direct response advertising. Direct response advertising is about targeting individual customers and driving them to a purchase, if not immediately, then shortly thereafter via a trackable checkout flow (thus collecting useful data to improve your targeting models). In this world offline sales are considerably less useful, not only because you have to give up margin to the retailer, but also because you can’t track who bought what based on what ads. And, down the road, you want to target the same customer for repeat purchases, effectively increasing the return from your initial investment in advertising.

In other words, Donahoe’s mistake wasn’t tactical (although I’m sure he made plenty of tactical mistakes along the way), but rather strategic. And, even in terms of strategy, you could argue that simultaneously reforming the product organization, channel strategy, and marketing approach was strategically coherent; the real strategic problem was misunderstanding the market and Nike’s unique position in that market.

In short, people still like to buy shoes in person (one of the reasons Donahoe was able to drive Nike so far into the ground is because COVID obscured Nike’s channel strategy missteps, given that consumers were temporarily buying everything online), and when they went to shoe stores, not only were there fewer Nikes — particularly the most current and popular ones — but there were suddenly a lot more alternatives that they might not have considered otherwise. In other words, Nike stopped building their brand, stopped putting their best products in front of consumers, and opened up space for the competition. It was a strategically coherent failure on every front!

Compounding the loss of the retail channel is the fact that online commerce works very differently. The first important channel is search, which I wrote about in 2014’s [How Technology is Changing the World (P&G Edition)](<https://stratechery.com/2014/technology-changing-world-pg-edition/>):

> There are two big challenges when it comes to winning search:
>
>   * Because search is initiated by the customer, you want that customer to not just recognize your brand (which is all that is necessary in a physical store), but to recall your brand (and enter it in the search box). This is a much stiffer challenge and makes the amount of time and money you need to spend on a brand that much greater
>   * If prospective customers do not search for your brand name but instead search for a generic term like “laundry detergent” then you need to be at the top of the search results. And, the best way to be at the top is to be the best-seller. In other words, having lots of products in the same space can work against you because you are diluting your own sales and thus hurting your search results
>

>
> The way to deal with both challenges is the same way you break through the noise: you put more focus on fewer brands.

The second important channel is discovery, which I wrote about in [2020’s Email Addresses and Razor Blades](<https://stratechery.com/2020/email-addresses-and-razor-blades/>):

> The old value chain in consumer packaged goods (CPG) looked like this:
>

>
> CPG companies like P&G harvested most of the value by integrating research and development, manufacturing, marketing, and shelf space; raw materials, retail, and logistics were modularized and commoditized.
>
> DTC companies, meanwhile, saw research and development as increasingly unnecessary in overserved markets (as I noted in [the context of Dollar Shave Club](<https://stratechery.com/2016/dollar-shave-club-and-the-disruption-of-everything/>), razors are a particularly salient example of overserving), and shelf space on the Internet was effectively infinite. Their goal was to integrate marketing, retail, and manufacturing:
>
> ![A drawing of Theoretical DTC Value Chain](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/Stratechery-20200225-2.png?resize=1024%2C464&ssl=1)
>
> […] The problem is that in the process of depending on Google and Facebook for marketing, the DTC companies gave up their planned integration in the value chain, and the associated profits, to Facebook and Google:
>

>
> The actual integrated players — Google and Facebook — integrate customers and research and development to dominate marketing; DTC may have online retail operations, but that is a modularized — and thus commoditized — part of the value chain (and meanwhile, Amazon was in the process of integrating retail and logistics).

One of the company’s anchoring the second article was Harry’s Razors, which had recently shifted to selling through bricks-and-mortar retail; in other words, right about the time that Donahoe was deprioritizing Nike’s bricks-and-mortar business in favor of direct-to-consumer, I was explaining why tiny brands were trying desperately to go the other way! Nike had, over the course of decades, established the sort of position independent from the Aggregators that everyone else aspired to, and then somehow decided to pretend they were a startup.

### Inevitability, Intentionality, and Amazon

So back to Nike on Amazon, after one more quick detour: there is an interesting analogy here to the mistakes that entertainment companies made in terms of abandoning MVPDs for streaming; I wrote in [an Update last week](<https://stratechery.com/2025/the-espn-streaming-service-the-status-of-u-s-sports-rights-the-danger-of-intentionality/>):

> As we look back over this disastrous (for Hollywood) unbundling, the overarching mistake that I think entertainment executives made was trading inevitability for intentionality, and I think the repercussions of that trade are going to echo for a long time, eventually reaching the sports leagues themselves.
>
> The old sports framework was one of inevitability: new homeowners inevitably got cable, and then they inevitably sat down on their couch and looked for something to watch on the TV grid, or by channel-flipping; often that something was sports, but there was also general entertainment, news, etc.
>
> Everything about watching sports going forward is about intentionality: first, customers have to choose to subscribe to a streaming service. Then they have to, of their own initiative, want to watch a game, and go to the trouble to find out where it is airing, and then go and find it in whatever app it happens to be streaming in. This is a lot of work, and it is work that has to be repeated for every single game, every single day; at what point is it easier to simply scroll TikTok and catch the highlights on YouTube? And, once you’re doing that, why not just unsubscribe from the service you’re not watching anyways? Maybe you’ll resubscribe for the playoffs.

Nike’s old strategy made them the inevitable choice for people buying shoes; now they need people to intentionally choose Nikes. That, by extension, means they need to be in the channels where people express that intentionality, and the most important place to do that is the Amazon search box.

This shift — which is also being accompanied by an attempt to rebuild Nike’s physical distribution channels — should be accompanied by a shift in marketing. Remember, search means recall, not just recognition; that means that Nike needs to double-down on the Nike brand in particular. And, by extension, it may result in some product consolidation: Nike needs best-sellers (and, thanks to its abandonment of physical retail, it has access to less shelf space now than it did previously).

What is fascinating is the contention by MoffettNathanson’s Michael Morton that being a first-party seller on Amazon (i.e. selling products at wholesale to Amazon) is actually the optimal e-commerce strategy; from his note about Nike’s decision:

> Despite higher gross margins for Amazon 3P and DTC, advertising expenses and logistics costs weigh on the channel EBIT margins, which we believe is the driving force behind a large brand’s decision to accept Amazon’s 1P. Eventually, brands will come to terms with the fact that Amazon has no peers when it comes to driving ecommerce traffic efficiently.
>
> ![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/nikie-1.png?resize=1330%2C750&ssl=1)
>
> Despite the math pointing to a better EBIT picture, apparel brands are not convinced on the advantages of Amazon, with many of the large apparel brands we sampled last year not endorsing Amazon selling (Exhibit 2). Conversations with brand managers consistently suggest that it’s the lack of brand control making Amazon 1P a challenging concession to accept.

Amazon is, notably, helping Nike out on this point, kicking 3rd-party Nike sellers off; beyond that, however, Morton’s argument that lower advertising costs and no logistics costs more than make up for lower wholesale prices is a compelling one. If he’s right then maybe Nike switched from the dumbest e-commerce strategy to the smartest one — as long as they get back to making good products and building a strong brand. The great thing about letting Amazon — and physical retailers — do what they do best is that you free up focus and resources to do just that.
