### SpaceX Buys More Spectrum

From [Bloomberg](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-06/spacex-to-pay-echostar-2-6-billion-for-more-spectrum-licenses>):

> EchoStar Corp. reported a $16.5 billion impairment charge and agreed to sell more spectrum licenses to Elon Musk’s SpaceX for $2.6 billion as it works to unwind parts of its 5G wireless network. The deal adds to a September agreement that the satellite TV and wireless company inked to sell SpaceX about $17 billion in wireless spectrum. Between that sale and another to AT&T Inc. for $23 billion, which EchoStar brokered under pressure from federal regulators, the company has been forced to give up on becoming a fourth major US wireless carrier as originally envisioned. As a result of the multiple transactions, EchoStar began decommissioning parts of its 5G network that it won’t use, leading to the one-time noncash charge, the company said in [a statement](<https://ir.echostar.com/news-releases/news-release-details/echostar-agrees-sell-full-unpaired-aws-3-spectrum-license>) Thursday.
>
> The smaller tranche of unpaired AWS-3 spectrum, sold to SpaceX for stock, had been left behind in both of the previous sales. The entire AWS-3 block was listed by EchoStar in a previous filing as having a carrying value of $9.8 billion. The sale price was “roughly 28% better than we expected,” at roughly $1 per share of additional after-tax value, Michael Rollins, an analyst at Citigroup Global Markets, wrote in a note…
>
> The deal gives SpaceX, a rocket and satellite company, an expanded toehold in the mobile-phone industry. It already offers home broadband internet through its Starlink service and has partnered with T-Mobile US Inc. to provide satellite service to cell phone users in remote areas, where gaps exist in ground-based 5G networks. EchoStar’s Boost Mobile will offer SpaceX coverage to its mobile customers through the companies’ partnership.

In September [I wrote about SpaceX’s original deal with EchoStar](<https://stratechery.com/2025/spacex-buys-spectrum-spectrum-specifics-spacexs-big-bet/>), which seemed geared towards partnering with terrestrial carriers:

> This acquisition makes Starlink more independent in terms of the service it can provide. The most immediately useful bit of spectrum is the H-block downlink capacity, which operates at 1995–2000 MHz; that spectrum is supported by phones today, but it is still small. What is surprising about this deal, however, is that Starlink can’t actually use the rest of the spectrum it acquired:
>
>   * H-block uplink capacity operates at 1915–1920 MHz, but there are not cellular bands that cover that range, which means that no phones support it.
>   * AWS-4 has much more capacity: 2000–2020 MHz (which EchoStar designated as downlink; this could be reversed, but there is no ecosystem support), and 2180–2200 MHz (always designated as downlink), both supported by existing phones. However, AWS-4 has not been approved for use by satellite-to-handset communication.
>   * Matching uplink capacity for AWS-4 is in the AWS-3 block (1695–1710 MHz); however, that spectrum is not in this deal.
>

>
> In short, even with this massive investment, Starlink cannot offer service on its own (unless it actually sold its own phone with support for H-block uplink capacity), and the second reason for partnerships — service in areas where most people actually live — remains.
>
> What this deal does give Starlink is tremendously more leverage in forming those partnerships: all of that spectrum is very useful to carriers, and can be traded for the spectrum Starlink needs for uplink in particular. The real unlock, however, will come from FCC approval for use of the AWS-4 spectrum for satellite-to-device communication, which will enable far greater downlink capacities. This approval isn’t trivial — in particular, AWS-4 spectrum is adjacent to spectrum reserved for federal space operations and telemetry — but one would imagine that SpaceX likes its chances under the current administration.
>
> Even then, however, Starlink will be partnering for uplink capacity at a minimum. The big difference now is that instead of paying for that capacity, Starlink will be trading for it. That’s worth a lot; at the same time, if I’m Charlie Ergen, I’m pretty happy with $8.5 billion of cash and, even better, $8.5 billion of Starlink stock for spectrum that the FCC was threatening to force them to share.

Forgive the long excerpt, but this is complicated stuff. To summarize, SpaceX acquired valuable downlink spectrum, but not any useful uplink spectrum; this meant that the company could not go it alone with mobile service even if they wanted to. However, I surmised that wasn’t the point: given the technical constraints that affect uplink in particular (phones aren’t that powerful, and generally need line-of-sight to satellites to work), SpaceX appeared to be making a play to be the default provider for all of the carriers; this would change satellite support from a differentiating feature to table stakes, meaning SpaceX would be the only one collecting profits.

### SpaceX’s Pivot

However, as [I noted in an Update last month](<https://stratechery.com/2025/the-openai-hype-cycle-microsofts-game-pass-failure-verizons-satellites/>), Verizon and AT&T refused to play ball: both doubled down on their commitment to AST SpaceMobile, a company pursuing a strategy of fewer but higher capacity cellular satellites, but one that still has to actually deliver; I wrote at the time in the context of SpaceX’s attempted powerplay:

> Verizon decided that it didn’t want to play that game. The carrier giant is going to run the risk that its satellite service is delayed or potentially inferior to SpaceX’s offering, but at least it won’t be under the thumb of SpaceX.
>
> This is, I would note, very much in character for Verizon. Just look at the iPhone, which Apple offered to Verizon first; the company declined, because they didn’t want to give Apple the total control of the experience the company wanted, and had to bleed subscribers for four years until finally getting the iPhone 4S in 2011.
>
> The task for SpaceX, then, will be to make Verizon rue their decision, just like Apple did, but frankly, I think they have pretty long odds. Cellular from space is an incredible capability, but it’s also one that just isn’t that relevant to that many people, particularly since any meaningful coverage plan will require terrestrial-based coverage. If AST SpaceMobile continues to face delays in their next generation satellites, Verizon may suffer a bit; the company has decided that leading the industry into dependence on SpaceX would hurt much more.

Well, the most obvious way to interpret this additional spectrum purchase is that SpaceX has accepted the challenge. From [EchoStar’s press release](<https://ir.echostar.com/news-releases/news-release-details/echostar-agrees-sell-full-unpaired-aws-3-spectrum-license>):

> EchoStar has entered into an amended definitive agreement with SpaceX to sell the company’s unpaired AWS-3 licenses for approximately $2.6 billion in SpaceX stock valued as of September 2025. This transaction builds on the agreement the companies entered into in September. EchoStar’s unpaired AWS-3 licenses are nationwide and are part of 3GPP Band 70n (1695-1710 MHz uplink).
>
> “This transaction with SpaceX, in addition to our previously announced spectrum transactions and commercial agreements, will strengthen EchoStar’s ability to develop new business opportunities and growth in value for our shareholders,” said Hamid Akhavan, CEO, EchoStar Capital. “The combination of AWS-3 uplink, AWS-4 and H-block spectrum from EchoStar with the rocket launch and satellite manufacturing capabilities from SpaceX accelerates the realization of powerful and economical direct-to-cell service offerings for consumers and enterprises worldwide, including our Boost Mobile customers.”

To summarize, SpaceX now has uplink spectrum that matches its downlink spectrum; this now means it is possible for SpaceX to offer its own cellular service — technically, anyways. The missing piece is regulatory: AWS-3 (for upload) and AWS-4 (for download) work on current handsets, but they’re not approved for satellite-to-handset communication, at least not yet. AWS-3 in particular covers federal weather-satellite communications, which can be worked around, but it’s not trivial technically or from a regulatory perspective. Elon Musk, however, is certainly well-placed to solve the latter issue in particular.

### Why Apple and SpaceX Should Partner

T-Mobile remains a strong partner for SpaceX; it’s possible — and probably desirable — that T-Mobile remain SpaceX’s primary go-to-market partner in the U.S. However, if SpaceX ever did want to go it alone, who would they want as a handset partner? The answer is surely obvious: Apple. That seems the angle to [this Bloomberg story from late last month](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-30/globalstar-draws-spacex-interest-in-sale-process>):

> Globalstar Inc. is exploring a potential sale and has held early talks with Elon Musk’s SpaceX among other potential suitors, according to people familiar with the matter. The satellite company, whose shares rose as much as 31% Thursday, is working with an investment bank to assist it, the people said, asking not to be identified because the information is private. SpaceX is one of the parties the company is speaking with but it’s possible another buyer could emerge, the people said. Globalstar hasn’t made a final decision on pursuing a sale and could opt to remain independent, they added.
>
> Apple Inc. likely has a say in Globalstar’s future, the people said. It provides a Globalstar offering that lets consumers send texts and contact emergency responders when out of cellular range. Last year, the iPhone maker invested about $1.5 billion in Globalstar to beef up the infrastructure. Apple took a 20% stake in the company as part of the deal.

SpaceX doesn’t need Globalstar’s satellites; what they would be buying is the partnership with Apple. Meanwhile, Mark Gurman in [his weekly newsletter](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-11-09/apple-iphone-satellite-plans-image-texting-third-party-apps-low-cost-macbook-mhrq10p2>) hinted that Apple was considering the idea:

> Bringing most of these new features to market will require major upgrades to Globalstar’s satellite infrastructure — improvements that Apple has helped finance. If SpaceX ultimately acquires Globalstar, those enhancements could roll out more quickly than otherwise possible. But such a deal would also force Apple to rethink its business model and long-term strategy for satellite services.
>
> Apple’s approach has been to own a core set of features and offer them at no cost — from Emergency SOS to the upcoming enhancements — as a way to drive iPhone sales, encourage upgrades and keep users within its ecosystem. For more advanced capabilities, Apple plans to let customers pay carriers, SpaceX or other satellite providers directly.
>
> A SpaceX-Globalstar merger would complicate that equation, but it may also bring opportunities. Apple could collaborate more deeply with SpaceX on premium satellite features — perhaps even creating a new paid tier for extended connectivity. Apple has no current plans to enable phone calls, video chats or web browsing via satellite, but SpaceX is aggressively moving in that direction.

I actually really love the idea of Apple and SpaceX teaming up.

Stepping back, for years and years people wondered why Apple didn’t get into the carrier business. After all, weren’t they the company that was all about integration, and what could be better than integrating the handset and the carrier? In fact, that is only one part of an integrated strategy: the other part is to [commoditize the rest of the value chain](<https://stratechery.com/2015/netflix-and-the-conservation-of-attractive-profits/>) such that everyone else competes to make your product better, and you harvest all of the profits. Apple did this brilliantly with the carriers; I explained back in 2013 in one of the first Articles on Stratechery, [Why Do Carriers Subsidize the iPhone?](<https://stratechery.com/2013/why-do-carriers-subsidize-the-iphone/>):

> My hypothesis is as follows:
>
>   * The carriers see the iPhone as a strategic threat because Apple owns the customer relationship; the carrier is reduced to a utility. Therefore the leading carriers do not carry the iPhone
>   * Customers strongly prefer the iPhone; in fact, they prefer it so much that they switch carriers to get the iPhone (something that is very difficult and rare). Second-and-third place carriers add the iPhone in order to steal customers from the leader
>   * The leading carrier is forced to choose between losing the customer relationship to Apple or losing the customer completely
>

>
> The fact that iPhone users are fabulously profitable makes this situation (and the associated subsidies) tolerable.
>
> To put it in theoretical terms, the Bargaining Power of Buyers (i.e. wireless consumers) drives iPhone carrier adoption:
>
> ![A drawing of the US Carrier Industry in 2010](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/04/Photo-2013-04-16-11.07.51-PM.png?resize=1024%2C768&ssl=1)
>
> In this view, instead of carriers hiring the iPhone to attract high use/high revenue wireless buyers, the lack of iPhone repels wireless buyers and drives them to rival carriers, forcing the holdouts to give in to Apple’s demands.

The end result is that wireless carriers around the world not only were forced to accept the iPhone on Apple’s terms, they actually spent billions and billions of dollars building out infrastructure to make the iPhone experience better!

Satellite connectivity, however, is a different market. Carriers were well-established by the time the iPhone came around; that meant the conditions were in place for Apple to commoditize the market, but that also meant that there was no room for Apple to differentiate itself in the market. It’s the opposite in this case: satellite functionality is new, and it’s not good enough; that means there are real benefits to integration, wherein the satellite connectivity piece is developed hand-in-hand with handsets to deliver a differentiated experience.

The problem for Apple is that GlobalStar, their long-time satellite partner, simply doesn’t have the resources to keep up with Apple. Apple already had to give the company an infusion of cash, and that was simply to maintain an experience that is worse than what you get from SpaceX; SpaceX, meanwhile, has an unassailable launch advantage that it is leveraging to cover the sky with cellular-capable satellites.

The problem SpaceX is having, meanwhile, is that they lost out on their gambit to be the provider for every cellular carrier, which means the best way to bring their advantages to bear is to also pursue an integrated strategy, but they don’t have the handset expertise or go-to-market capabilities necessary to build a fully integrated experience of their own.

In short, Apple and SpaceX can solve each other’s problem/opportunity: SpaceX could allow Apple to push the envelope in terms of satellite capability on iPhones, capability that could exist on top of existing carrier services; Apple, meanwhile, could bring SpaceX’s potential mobile service to market in a far more effective and compelling way than SpaceX could on its own. It’s not pure integration — I’m talking about a partnership here — but there is a real path to delivering a truly differentiated experience to the benefit of both companies.

There are two big problems with this idea. The first one is obvious: what are the business terms, and who calls the shots? The obvious way to make money from this partnership is to increase iPhone differentiation, which implies Apple paying SpaceX but owning the user experience; that may be intolerable to both companies. The second one is physics: it’s reasonable to wonder, as I have in past updates, if satellite connectivity ever actually matters that much to most people who would be better served by choosing the best terrestrial carrier. What is certain is that the best way to disprove this objection is for Apple and SpaceX to work together.
