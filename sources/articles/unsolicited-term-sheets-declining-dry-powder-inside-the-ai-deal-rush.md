Last year, when money was surging into artificial intelligence startups, even some venture capitalists involved in those deals worried that the [high costs of AI](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/some-openai-investors-sit-out-share-sale-reflecting-ai-divide?rc=zjctrx>) and [unreliable demand](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/at-googles-cloud-conference-lofty-ai-visions-meet-customer-reality?rc=zjctrx>) from businesses were setting up many startups for disappointment.

Such skepticism, if not dead, is on its last legs. Monthly revenue that is doubling or quadrupling this year at startups such as [OpenAI](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-hits-12-billion-annualized-revenue-breaks-700-million-chatgpt-weekly-active-users?rc=cmz1ru>), [Anthropic](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/anthropic-revenue-hits-4-billion-annual-pace-competition-cursor-intensifies?rc=cmz1ru>) and Cursor-maker [Anysphere](<https://www.theinformation.com/briefings/anysphere-startup-behind-cursor-hits-500-million-annualized-revenue?rc=cmz1ru>) have shown that businesses and consumers are consistently paying for AI apps, not just dabbling. That’s sent dealmaking, including into companies that are less proven, into overdrive.

Wary of missing out on fast-growing AI startups, venture capitalists are offering to invest shortly after a startup raises money. These preemptive rounds are just one sign of how heated investing in AI has become.

In one sign of venture capitalists’ heightened excitement, they are increasingly offering to invest in startups well before they start formally fundraising, a process that usually involves a presentation by founders, financial forecasts and multiple meetings.

In some cases, founders have asked their own investors to stop offering to invest unless the startup’s leaders say they plan to fundraise.

“We’ve actually had to ask investors not to send term sheets so it is not distracting us,” said Brendan Foody, CEO of Mercor, a startup that helps OpenAI and other AI developers find contractors and employees to help train new models.

He added: “It all comes back to making sure every decision is optimizing our probability of building a great company—and not getting distracted when there is so much happening.”

But venture firms aren’t letting up. Some are approaching founders with offers, sometimes just days or weeks after startups close their last funding rounds. Rather than fielding pitches, the venture capital firms are pitching themselves, prepared with 30-page research presentations on a startup’s competitors and its growth potential. And if the startup bites, the VC partners may quickly hash out the decision internally, sometimes pulling the trigger on an investment within a day.

These kinds of unsolicited, or preemptive, investments were popular during the 2020 to 2022 pandemic funding boom but faded in the aftermath—until AI dealmaking took off.

The AI dealmaking exuberance is starting to deplete some of the venture firms’ dry powder—the capital investors have raised from their limited partners to back startups.

“Everything is happening faster,” said Aydin Senkut, managing partner and founder at Felicis, an early-stage venture firm that has invested in AI startups such as AI video model company Runway and Mercor.

[](<https://www.theinformation.com/deep-research>)

Senkut said Felicis has made preemptive offers for one of every five investments it’s made lately, and he expects the pace to grow. And the firm has made investment decisions within 24 hours of meeting with a company, speed that Senkut said has been more important this year than in years past.

“You had more luxury of time five years ago. But now, if you haven’t made up your mind…it is going to be very, very difficult to succeed,” he said.

While many founders are trying to avoid impromptu fundraising talks, sometimes they take the bait.

Last October, Felicis successfuly made an unsolicited offer to invest in Mercor at a $2 billion valuation, at about 100 times the startup’s run rate at the time, Foody confirmed. Four months later, the company had increased its revenue nearly four times to a $75 million run rate, making the price look more reasonable.

Senkut declined to comment on the deal and revenue.

Recently, the two-year-old startup received several unsolicited term sheets at an even higher valuation, according to a person close to the company. Foody declined to comment on fundraising offers, including some speculation about Mercor raising at a $10 billion valuation.

The competition among VC firms is driving up prices and round sizes.

“If your firm used to write a $50 million check, you’re now writing a $100 million check because the valuations are getting so high,” said Mike Volpi, founder of early-stage venture firm Hanabi Capital.

**Dry Powder Decline**

One of the most striking consequences of the excitement for AI deals is what it’s doing to venture capitalists’ stockpiles of cash, or dry powder.

After peaking at $297 billion in 2023, dry powder fell to $221 billion in the first half of this year, estimates venture capitalist Jon Sakoda of Decibel Partners, who based his analysis on data from PitchBook and the National Venture Capital Association. The investor, who has been compiling statistics on dry powder for more than a decade, now expects the level could sink to $164 billion by year end—its lowest level since 2019.

“The AI supercycle is incredibly capital intensive, and because it is capital intensive, in a period of prolonged illiquidity, we are seeing unusual fast periods of drawdown,” he said.

Stoking excitement among investors is the fact that Meta Platforms, Google, Microsoft and Amazon Web Services are [spending heavily to support their AI efforts](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/big-tech-cloud-revenue-growth-catching-capex?rc=zjctrx>)—putting out more than $300 billion for data center build-outs and other capital expenditures over the 12 months ending in June. Even though some of that spending will help the public companies become more formidable competitors to AI startups, the expenses validate the view that AI is turning into a big business.

“The size of the prize, many people believe, is larger than ever before,” said Nina Achadjian, an Index Ventures partner who led Index’s investment in Vizcom, an AI design tool for physical products.

The fast pace of AI startups’ [revenue growth](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/ai-native-startups-pass-15-billion-annualized-revenue?rc=cmz1ru>) has also given investors more confidence in backing companies at higher and higher valuations. Investors who want to proceed more cautiously can find themselves priced out.

“Follow-on rounds are happening faster than before with larger amounts of capital,” said Achadjian. The dynamic leaves “little margin of error for very early-stage investors to get into the company before the valuation becomes astronomical.”

### **Revenue Rocket Ship**

Rapid revenue growth at very young startups, such as [two-year-old coding startup Lovable](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/replits-margins-illustrate-high-costs-coding-agents?rc=zjctrx>), as well as leaders like decade-old OpenAI, have been enticing investors to pour $114.6 billion into AI startups in the first half of this year, putting total startup investing on track to hit a five-year high.

OpenAI, for instance, was recently generating [$12 billion in annualized revenue](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-hits-12-billion-annualized-revenue-breaks-700-million-chatgpt-weekly-active-users?rc=zjctrx>), implying it’s generating $1 billion in revenue per month. That’s double the monthly revenue from the start of this year and up three times from around the same time period last year.

The company’s valuation and the size of funding rounds have similarly ballooned. OpenAI recently set out to sell shares held by employees at a price that values the ChatGPT maker at $500 billion—nearly six times higher than in an employee tender 15 months ago. It’s in the process of raising $40 billion from SoftBank and others.

If an AI startup does set out to raise money, many founders are finding they can set their own terms on valuation and governance, such as voting rights or how much board control to give to new investors.

In July, for example, AI video startup Decart got to call the shots when it decided to raise a round of new funding. The founders themselves set the valuation of $3 billion, which was more than six times that of its last round raised at the end of last year, according to a person with direct knowledge of the company’s fundraising.

That’s a change from the typical fundraising process, which usually requires a lead investor to set the terms of a round. Last week, Decart announced it had raised $100 million at a $3.1 billion valuation from Sequoia Capital, Zeev Ventures, Benchmark and others.

Some investors say savvy founders have realized they can capitalize on the investment frenzy—say, by implying to potential investors that they’re receiving unsolicited offers, which may invite other firms to submit high-valuation term sheets. While such tactics aren’t new, investors are saying they are seeing them more frequently as the excitement over AI intensifies.

In several cases, investors suspect founders are trying to get word of their rounds leaked to Arfur Rock, an [anonymous](<https://x.com/ArfurRock>) X account, so it will post about a high valuation that’s in the works.

Despite intense investor interest, AI companies still have to prove that they can retain the explosive revenue they’ve generated. That’s why venture capitalists have been looking at [metrics like net retention rates](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/why-net-retention-rates-are-all-the-rage-for-ai-investors?rc=cmz1ru>) and gross margins in some of the fastest growing companies.

And offers of interest, whether verbal or written, may not lead to term sheets, some investors warn.

“Just because 25 firms are calling you, it’s not 25 term sheets,” Hanabi Capital’s Volpi said. “And it definitely might not be 25 term sheets that you want to accept.”
