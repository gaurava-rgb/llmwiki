### OpenAI Instant Checkout

From the [Wall Street Journal](<https://www.wsj.com/articles/openai-lets-users-buy-stuff-directly-through-chatgpt-db8a93f9>):

> OpenAI is letting ChatGPT users buy things through its popular artificial intelligence chatbot, all without leaving the confines of its platform. The San Francisco-based AI company said Monday that U.S.-based ChatGPT users will be able to buy goods from online marketplace Etsy’s domestic sellers, as well as some merchants on Shopify’s e-commerce platform. The service, called Instant Checkout, currently only supports single-item purchases.
>
> OpenAI also unveiled an open-source technical standard for merchants to build integrations with ChatGPT, called Agentic Commerce Protocol, which the company hopes will draw more merchants onto its chatbot platform. The protocol allows merchants to make their products shoppable inside ChatGPT. Amazon and Walmart, the nation’s two largest digital retailers, aren’t currently using the protocol, OpenAI said.

This is an announcement that is not necessarily surprising, but exciting all the same. It has been clear for a long time now that ChatGPT is a powerful tool for commerce, and the company has been taking baby steps to close the loop, with things like product carousels, an in-app browser (which lets OpenAI capture web usage and attribution), and the overall concept of having a router deciding how to answer every query. This, however, is an explicit step into the transaction itself — and it’s almost certainly not the last.

### AI and Long Tail E-Commerce

Shopify and Etsy have long been poised to be winners from AI-driven commerce, which figured to be a boon for the long tail. To explain what I mean, consider what the implications of search-driven commerce were; I explained in 2014’s [How Technology is Changing the World (P&G Edition)](<https://stratechery.com/2014/technology-changing-world-pg-edition/>):

> The other big technological change that is affecting P&G’s strategy is e-commerce. As [I’ve previously noted](<http://stratechery.com/2013/amazons-dominant-strategy/>) in the context of Amazon:
>
>> Jeff Bezos’ critical insight when he founded Amazon was that the Internet allowed a retailer to have both (effectively) infinite selection AND lower prices (because you didn’t need to maintain a limited-in-size-yet-expensive-due-to-location retail space).
>
> That’s great for Amazon, but not so great for P&G: remember, dominating shelf space was a core part of their strategy, and while I’m no mathematician, I’m pretty sure dominating an infinite resource is a losing proposition. What matters now is dominating search…
>
> There are two big challenges when it comes to winning search:
>
>   * Because search is initiated by the customer, you want that customer to not just recognize your brand (which is all that is necessary in a physical store), but to recall your brand (and enter it in the search box). This is a much stiffer challenge and makes the amount of time and money you need to spend on a brand that much greater.
>   * If prospective customers do not search for your brand name but instead search for a generic term like “laundry detergent” then you need to be at the top of the search results. And, the best way to be at the top is to be the best-seller. In other words, having lots of products in the same space can work against you because you are diluting your own sales and thus hurting your search results.
>

>
> The way to deal with both challenges is the same way you break through the noise: you put more focus on fewer brands.

There are certainly some AI queries that are basically search queries, but many commerce related queries come higher up in the funnel: you have a vague idea of what you want, or know the problem you are trying to solve, and what you are looking for is help in refining that vagueness into a concrete product that you can purchase. Sometimes the answer is a popular product, but what is compelling about AI as a commerce driver is that it has the capability of finding products in the long tail that you might never have found normally* but which the AI, with its vast corpus of knowledge, can surface for you. These kinds of products can certainly be found on Amazon or Walmart, thanks in particular to their large third-party merchant platforms, but the chief home for the long tail is Shopify (for companies) and Etsy (for individual sellers).

To give a personal example of the latter, I was running out of ports on my Unifi switch; if you haven’t bought a rack-scale switch lately, they’re expensive, and I only needed a few more ports. It turns out that I had a couple of smaller switches from my setup in Taiwan, which would meet my needs; the problem is that they aren’t rack mountable. I asked ChatGPT how to solve this problem, and it pointed me to [the exact product I needed on Etsy](<https://www.etsy.com/listing/1586379043/rack-mount-for-ubiquiti-unifi-switch-8>): there just so happens to be a gentleman in Australia who 3D prints basically every possible configuration of rack mounts for non-rack-mountable Unifi switches, and I got exactly what I needed.

Now it’s theoretically possible that I might have found this seller through Etsy search, but the problem is that I wasn’t even sure what I was looking for, and Etsy wasn’t really on my radar in terms of solving my problem; the other way that I might have found this seller — and this is what the asterisk above is referring to — is that he might have found me, most likely through an Instagram ad downstream of Meta sussing out that I was interested in Unifi equipment. That may seem far-fetched, but a big part of the reason why Meta is so valuable — and [anti-fragile](<https://stratechery.com/2020/apple-and-facebook/>) — is because they solved the merchant-customer matching problem for the long tail better than anyone else.

What AI-driven search is poised to do is to solve that problem but from the other direction: instead of long-tail merchants finding customers who might be interested in their product, it helps customer find merchants in the long tail who solve their unique needs. The end result is more long tail merchant volume, which is particularly good news for the platforms whose success is predicated on the long tail — Shopify and Etsy.

### Is AI Different?

I suspect this heading is one that I could get a great deal of mileage out of; in this case, it’s prompted by [Eric Seufert’s skeptical reaction to this news](<https://x.com/eric_seufert/status/1972747838863155514>):

> OpenAI's integration with Stripe isn't "agentic commerce": it's in-product checkout, similar to the native checkout experience that Meta recently eliminated because it was unpopular with users and didn't improve Meta's ability to collect conversion data. [pic.twitter.com/KLIaeaqNTJ](<https://t.co/KLIaeaqNTJ>)
>
> — Eric Seufert (@eric_seufert) [September 29, 2025](<https://twitter.com/eric_seufert/status/1972747838863155514?ref_src=twsrc%5Etfw>)

Seufert linked to an excellent article he wrote earlier this year about [The Retreat From Social Commerce](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/whats-wrong-with-social-commerce/>), including Meta’s elimination of its native Checkout experience. As Seufert recounted, an emphasis on native Checkout was an early response to the way in which App Tracking Transparency eliminated the explicit link between an ad and a conversion; he argues that the initiative failed for four reasons:

  1. Native commerce tools were inferior to the tools offered by merchant platforms like Shopify.
  2. Western audiences never acclimated to social commerce.
  3. Retailers had a strong preference to drive traffic to their own storefronts given the benefits in terms of building a long-term relationship with a customer.
  4. Meta’s probabilistic measurement tools became so good that the delta in signal between capturing the actual conversion and estimating it became too small to justify the decrease in transaction volume that came from an inferior purchase experience.



Considering this in terms of ChatGPT Instant Checkout:

  * Reasons number one and three clearly apply in this case: the commerce tools on Shopify or Etsy are and probably always will be better than those on ChatGPT, and there remains real value in establishing a direct relationship with the buyer.
  * Reason number two doesn’t really apply; this is an entirely new kind of commerce experience, which gives this direct integration into ChatGPT a better chance.
  * Reason number four also doesn’t apply, for the simple reason that ChatGPT is starting from scratch: there isn’t any alternative approach to compete with.



I would also add two reasons why this integration might work. The first ties into what [I wrote about yesterday](<https://stratechery.com/2025/meta-vibes-good-vibes-vibes-vision/>) in terms the difference between leaning back and leaning forward. Instagram et al are lean back experiences, which are good in terms of being open to advertising, but bad in terms of jumping through hoops to make a conversion work; Shop Pay is particularly brilliant when it comes to buying something from an ad you saw before you barely even realize what you are doing. ChatGPT, however, particularly when you are explicitly on the hunt for a product, is a lean forward experience; when you find what you want, you’re liable to take the direct route to buying it, even if that means a somewhat unrefined and clunky purchase flow within ChatGPT itself.

The second goes to the incentives that OpenAI is seeking to create around this product. From [the company’s post about Instant Checkout](<https://openai.com/index/buy-it-in-chatgpt/>):

> Merchants pay a small fee on completed purchases, but the service is free for users, doesn’t affect their prices, and doesn’t influence ChatGPT’s product results. Instant Checkout items are not preferred in product results. When ranking multiple merchants that sell the same product, ChatGPT considers factors like availability, price, quality, whether a merchant is the primary seller, and whether Instant Checkout is enabled, to optimize the user experience.

What I take away from this is that my Australian 3D printer doesn’t necessarily need to utilize Instant Checkout, because what he sells is so unique that, once I find him, I’m willing to purchase on whatever platform he wants me to. For any product that isn’t completely bespoke, however, ChatGPT is basically saying that if you want to be the link that is purchased, you need to use Instant Checkout, and need to give them a cut. That this is potentially possible is a function of ChatGPT helping customers find products, as opposed to Meta’s goal of helping merchants find customers.

This has always been how I’ve thought a ChatGPT ad or affiliate model would work: if there are multiple merchants for a recommendation, then the merchant who pays ChatGPT the most would be the one who is featured — that’s exactly what is happening here. What is interesting is that this approach does mean that ChatGPT actually isn’t directly monetizing its most valuable function, which is uncovering the unique product you can’t find anywhere else; that, though, is par for the course for consumer tech companies: the idea is to generate so much consumer surplus that taking a skim off of only a fraction of the attention you generate pays the bills (and a whole lot more!).
