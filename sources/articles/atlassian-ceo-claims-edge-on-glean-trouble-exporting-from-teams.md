Enterprise search startup Glean has attracted customers and venture capital thanks to an AI search tool that lets employees quickly find corporate files and data in all of the apps their companies use. But Mike Cannon-Brookes, the CEO and co-founder of productivity app Atlassian, thinks his company is ahead of Glean in enterprise search—despite launching its rival product, Rovo, three years after Glean did.

Cannon-Brookes [said in an interview on The Information’s TITV streaming show](<https://youtu.be/TwBp4nRMZqw>) that he “would be shocked” if Rovo is not seeing more usage than Glean “by quite a long distance.” Atlassian launched Rovo last October as a feature for its applications, while Glean sells its search product on a standalone basis.

Atlassian, founded in 2002, has spent many years building a type of database called a knowledge graph, which contains more information about the relationships between the documents and chat conversations it contains, he said.

While Glean also has a knowledge graph, Cannon-Brookes contends that Atlassian’s wider range of applications and data gives it an advantage.

“We have built what we believe is the best enterprise search engine out there for the knowledge that your company has,” Cannon-Brookes said in the interview, adding that the Rovo product has seen “extremely good customer reaction” so far.

Cannon-Brookes’ comments highlight growing competition in enterprise search, an increasingly crowded market where Atlassian is duking it out with Glean and other AI-native companies such as OpenAI and Cohere, as well as older public companies such as Google and Snowflake. There may also be a whiff of sour grapes in his comments, since Atlassian made an unsuccessful run at acquiring Glean back in 2023, [as we reported in May](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/corporate-data-wars-intensify?rc=zjctrx>).

A Glean spokesperson didn’t address the Atlassian CEO’s comments, saying in a statement via email that Glean’s search product offers “the most complete view of how companies work.”

Atlassian has also quietly participated in the AI data wars, which came into sharper focus in May after Salesforce blocked Glean and other software firms from searching or storing Slack messages even if their customers permit them to do so. Earlier this year, [Atlassian began imposing limits](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/corporate-data-wars-intensify?rc=eqgjah>) on how often other software providers like Glean can pull data from its apps via its application programming interface.

In the interview, Cannon-Brookes downplayed the significance of the move, asserting that Atlassian’s limits are “very generous and perfectly usable” while also noting that many of its rivals are far more restrictive, without giving examples.

“We have all sorts of ways to get information into and out of the Atlassian platform, and we have hundreds of commercial partners that we move data to and from on a regular basis,” he said.

Atlassian, like other subscription software providers, also faces the risk that AI apps could replace them. Cannon-Brookes has a similar take as Marc Benioff, the co-founder and CEO of Salesforce, who characterized such speculation as “nonsense” on an earnings call last week.

Atlassian gives customers a place to store data about business processes and other types of internal knowledge, which they can augment by bringing in data from outside sources, and AI can be “supercharging” to that, Cannon-Brookes said.

“There is nothing inherently to do with AI that should mean that software-as-a-service ceases to exist,” Cannon-Brookes said in the interview. “In fact, I think it is most likely to be the opposite, but we'll have to all wait and see how it plays out.”

### Is Microsoft Slow-Walking Teams Data Exports?

In February, Microsoft announced a tool that gives small and medium-size customers of its Teams collaboration software a 90-day window to take their data with them when switching to another provider, free of charge.

But one startup that tried to use the tool said it didn’t work—prompting the startup’s founder to question whether Microsoft really wants to enable customers to export their data to a rival.

Alya Abbott, chief operating officer at workplace collaboration startup Zulip, published a [blog post](<https://blog.zulip.com/2025/09/10/teams-export-vaporware/>) this week describing how her staff had tried unsuccessfully for more than a month to get Microsoft’s data exporting tool to work. During this period, Zulip has had more than 20 calls with nearly 30 email conversations with five different Microsoft customer support teams, she said.

As a result, a project the startup launched this summer to build a Teams-to-Zulip data exporting feature is on hold, Abbott said.

The situation highlights the challenges companies face when they want to move from one workplace collaboration product to another, but are unable to because data access is a barrier, Abbott said via email. “They don't want to lose access to years of chat history, which likely contains institutional knowledge that exists nowhere else,” she wrote.

If Microsoft’s data exporting tool worked, it would allow customers that move from Teams to Zulip to use AI bots built with Zulip's API on their Teams data, she said.

A Microsoft spokesperson didn’t have a comment on the matter.
