We got big news this afternoon from the folks at OpenAI and Microsoft: They are close to a peace deal. Yes, after nearly a year of talks over changes to their partnership, the companies [announced they had signed](<https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2025/09/11/a-joint-statement-from-microsoft-and-openai/?ocid=msftnews_x>) a “non-binding memorandum of understanding for the next phase of our partnership.” Contractual terms now have to be finalized. Hmmm. That sounds like a deal you have when you don’t really have a deal. (More [coverage is here](<https://www.theinformation.com/briefings/microsoft-openai-announce-tentative-deal-startup-aims-restructure?rc=9byxri>)).

The two companies have been at odds for a while, over all manner of things, as we reported [here](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/microsoft-and-openai-wrangle-over-terms-of-their-blockbuster-partnership?rc=9byxri>), [here](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-plans-slash-revenue-share-microsoft-restructuring?rc=9byxri>) and [here.](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-microsoft-duel-agi-high-stakes-negotiation?rc=9byxri>) A key issue is that OpenAI wants to convert its for-profit business into a company whose investors own shares, which would make it easier for the ChatGPT creator to raise money and eventually go public. (Raising money is something OpenAI needs to do a lot of, [as this story explains](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/115-billion-reason-openai-will-raise-money?rc=9byxri>)). Today’s statement was bereft of details indicating whether the two companies have resolved any of the contentious issues that have divided them (like what happens with Microsoft’s right to share in OpenAI’s revenue).

One thing we did learn was that the nonprofit that controls OpenAI’s for-profit business will have an equity stake of more than $100 billion after the changes. But OpenAI didn’t disclose the nonprofit’s percentage stake. As OpenAI was [recently valued at $500 billion](<https://www.theinformation.com/briefings/5feb2c?rc=9byxri>) in an employee share sale, the nonprofit would seem to have at least a 20% stake. [How will Elon Musk,](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/musks-legal-battle-openais-structure-total-long-shot?rc=9byxri>) who has sued OpenAI over the corporate restructuring, react to that? Today’s news in some ways raised more questions than it answered.

### Ellison’s Risky Business

Who isn’t tempted to go crazy on a shopping spree when they have a little more money in their pocket? Oracle founder Larry Ellison seems to be one of those people. On Thursday, The Wall Street Journal [scooped the news](<https://www.wsj.com/business/media/paramount-skydance-prepares-ellison-backed-bid-for-warner-bros-discovery-0b921c20?mod=hp_lead_pos1>) that Ellison was backing his son David in plans for a mostly-cash bid for Warner Bros. Discovery. That’s a deal that will likely cost at least $70 billion, including assumption of debt.

In theory, the Ellisons can easily afford it. On Wednesday, the elder Ellison saw his already humongous wealth rise by $100 billion, as shares of the software-giant firm rocketed on unexpectedly high revenue projections issued by the company related to artificial intelligence-cloud contracts it has struck.

In a sense, then, the mountain of venture capital money pouring into AI firms is making its way, via the Ellison family and Oracle, to shareholders in WBD, whose Warner Bros. film studio is one of Hollywood’s most iconic names, with a history running from Bugs Bunny to Batman. Ain’t capitalism great! Still, the deal also heightens the risk that the Ellison family is taking in entertainment.

Just a month ago, David Ellison completed his $8 billion purchase of Paramount—a deal that gave him control of a company more dependent than most TV firms on the slowly shrinking cable channel business. According to the WSJ report, Ellison wants to bid for all of WBD, including its cable channels, even though the company was preparing to carve those off into a separate company. By buying all of WBD, Ellison is doubling down on his exposure to a slowly declining sector.

And given that WBD has around $30 billion in debt and Paramount likely close to $10 billion, the combined company would have a strained balance sheet. Having a lot of debt with businesses whose revenues are falling isn’t a recipe for success. It is possible Ellison will invest enough cash to bring down the debt a lot. But is it likely? How much is Dad going to put into this deal?

And let’s not forget that the Oracle rally that enriched Larry Ellison is itself based on rickety foundations. As [we’ve pointed out](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/oracle-frenzy-spreads?rc=9byxri>), Oracle’s revenue forecasts [are predicated on](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/oracles-blockbuster-projections-apples-new-iphones?rc=9byxri>) AI cloud deals signed with OpenAI and others going out several years. OpenAI has to raise money to fund the contracts. If the AI boom falters, then OpenAI might have to pull back. As it is, some investor enthusiasm for Oracle dissipated on Thursday, as the stock fell 6%.

There’s no question a combination of WBD and Paramount holds promise (and given Ellison’s ties to President Donald Trump, it has a good chance of getting regulatory approval). It would catapult David Ellison into one of the leading positions in Hollywood and make him a force in streaming. Disney CEO Bob Iger would suddenly face competition as captain of Hollywood. But the potential for this turning into a disaster movie is real.
