Good morning,

Today’s Stratechery Interview is with [Apple in China](<https://appleinchina.com/>) author [Patrick McGee](<https://x.com/PatrickMcGee_>). [Apple in China](<https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Apple-in-China/Patrick-McGee/9781668053379>) is a masterpiece: it’s one of the best books about Apple ever written, one of the best books about China ever written, and absolutely belongs in the canon of essential books about the tech industry. I strongly suggest that everyone reading or listening to this interview [buy a copy](<https://www.amazon.com/Apple-China-Capture-Greatest-Company/dp/1668053373>).

In this interview we merely scratch the surface of “Apple in China”. We discuss McGee’s background, how he was perfectly positioned to write the story of Apple in China, and how he structured the story. That story, to my mind, comes in three parts: the first is early Apple and why they had no choice but to outsource; the second is Apple achieving stunning success while still being ignorant of the forces that were driving their success in China; the third is about Apple’s awakening to their position, and when and where they crossed the line to being a company that did more to build China than any other.

#### Background

**I do always start these interviews asking more about who I’m talking to, what’s your background? Where’d you get started? I think it’s probably particularly pertinent to the topic at hand. So starting from the beginning, where’d you grow up? How’d you get started in journalism? Let’s go from there.**

**PM:** I grew up in Calgary, Alberta, so I’m Canadian. I have two older brothers and my older brothers and my two parents, we all have a degree or two in religion, so that’s actually the sort of family conversation. My dad was an Anglican priest when I was a young kid, and my parents had met at divinity school, and then I think I would fairly say we sort of grew up in a theological library. So lots of questions about various religions and cultures and that sort of thing. So we all have, I mean, this is the argument over the dinner table all the time, right?

**You have anticipated a question we’re going to get to at the end, but this is actually a fantastic framing — sorry, continue.**

**PM:** I’m a reasonably staunch atheist, but I find religion to be a fascinating lens through which to view history or culture or anything else. I’m inclined maybe to mysticism, if anything. Then I have various family members who of course, if my dad was an Anglican priest, would feel differently about the crux of these issues. But I suppose what I’m really pointing at is that we’re a humanities family, reading, writing, thinking in some way, shape or form, that’s what everybody in my family does.

I studied anthropology at university and then sort of morphed into a degree in religion. I maybe wasn’t anticipating doing that, but I spent a lot of time focused on Papua New Guinean religions from a hundred years ago because the birth of anthropology through the likes of [James Frazeer](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_George_Frazer>) and [Bronislaw Malinowski](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronis%C5%82aw_Malinowski>) was absolutely fascinating to me. Some of my favorite subjects were about the [Trobriand Islanders](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trobriand_people>), who according to Malinowski, didn’t understand the connection between the act of sex and procreation. I got all interested in the psychoanalytic take on this, which is that Malinowski misunderstood his own ethnography and that this was the Oedipus complex transmitted in the Papua New Guinean Island. So anyway, I’m going off on a tangent, but I’m just, my history is not computers—

**I already told everyone that you’re a good storyteller, so I am eagerly anticipating to see how we’re circling back. So please continue.**

**PM:** (laughing) Yeah, so I got really involved with Freud, with Wittgenstein. I really studied intellectual history, but through the prism of religion. The problem with that is, it’s fascinating for four years, it’s good for cocktail conversations, I’ve read a lot of interesting books, but it doesn’t have any job prospects so I graduated with honors and stuff and then met immediate crisis as I had no idea what I wanted to do with my life and I wasn’t going straight into graduate school.

So the short version is that I fell into journalism just in my home city, living in the garage of my best friend, and did that for a few months. The magazine unfortunately collapsed, I happened to be in DC at a time visiting my girlfriend, and from there I just sent out 25 letters and I ended up getting an economics reporting job, so I stayed in DC, and this was all one year before Lehman Brothers collapsed, so we’re just beginning to hear terms credit crisis at a time when I didn’t know what a credit crisis was, but it ended up being the perfect time to become a macroeconomics reporter, because by the time the world was dealing with the Lehman troubles, I was already reporting on it for a year.

I sort of had 12 months of getting up to speed reading the Greenspan biography and everything like that, that basically led to becoming a bond reporter and getting a job at the Wall Street Journal, and then I had this reputation at the Wall Street Journal walking in every day and grabbing the pink paper, the Financial Times, and reading that because I preferred it. And somehow that reputation found itself over-

**Made its way to the Financial Times?**

**PM:** Made its way to the FT and they were hiring for a service they were calling Fast FT, they wanted to hire me for New York. I eventually didn’t get the job, but I said, “That’s okay, I’ve been in New York for five years, I think I want to move on anyway”, and they sort of said, “Well, wait, would you move to Hong Kong?”, because the same position was available in Hong Kong. So that was my serendipitous connection to Asia. I’d been to Southeast Asia before and whatnot, but I hadn’t been to Hong Kong so I ended up basically running Fast FT for three years out of Hong Kong, that was just at the time when the [Umbrella Revolution](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umbrella_Movement>) took place, that’s like 2013 to 2016.

Then if you do that, Steve Jobs connect the dots backwards thing for your life, my book idea basically comes out of covering Hong Kong and the greater China region for three years, moving to Germany, covering the automotive industry and supply chains for three years, and then going to California to cover Apple for four years. When you couple those things together — China, supply chain, and Apple — you sort of get the origins of the book.

**That’s amazing. I mean, you’re right, it is a classic Steve Jobs looking backwards idea.**

#### The Story of Apple in China

**I do have to ask, the book is called Apple in China. Were you tempted to title the book, “Designed in California, Made in China?”**

**PM:** Yes! That was actually one of the iterations. I guess the trouble was, you wanted a book where you didn’t need the subtitle to understand what the context was, and the only way to get both entities in the book was to have a kind of boring title.

**Well, you also cover retail and you cover sales and things along those lines, so it’s not just the supply chain.**

**PM:** And it’s not just China. When the New York Times reviewed the book, they were like, “China doesn’t really enter the picture until page 90”, and that’s true, but how would you encapsulate that in the title? You’d have to give some boring textbook eight word title.

**To me, that is one of the things I liked about the book, so we’ll come back to that famous phrase in a moment, but I read the book as being in three parts. I think you actually had five parts or something with those 42 chapters.**

**PM:** Six.

**Six, got it. So my three that I took away was first, why Apple went to China in the first place, and I thought it was so important and valuable that you went back through some of this history. I was just writing about[Apple and China stuff just a few months ago](<https://stratechery.com/2025/apple-and-the-ghosts-of-companies-past/>), and I went back to that Fremont factory for the Mac and things along those lines, and I think it’s really important to appreciate this fact that they were late in many respects.**

**But then number two, part two, Apple’s combination of staggering success in China, both in terms of sales and manufacturing, all of which happened to a — you very adroitly explained — a very clueless company in many respects.**

**And then third, Apple’s loss of innocence and double down. To me, that’s the arc of this story, and there’s a bit about the end about their current predicament and what they might do next, but is that the structure you were going for? That three-piece story, or have I done my own meta-editing on top of you?**

**PM:** You’ve kind of done your own thing, but I wouldn’t disagree with any word that you said. I mean, by the time I went on book leave in September 2023, I had the contours of the story, and for me it was four parts so now I’m just trying to figure out if I remember those four parts. I think I needed to tell the early history when Apple was building its products itself, and then one early realization I had was that it’s usually a sentence or two in a magazine profile that basically says, “They hired Tim Cook, who closed down Apple factories and moved to China” — that’s a gross oversimplification. So there’s the seven-year experimentation phase where the iMac alone, the translucent iMac —

**This the part I learned the most, by the way.**

**PM:** Yeah, it was being built in six different counties.

**Right, with LG and then the Mexico thing. To me, that was the huge unpack. Obviously I’ve dived a lot into China in the context of diving into early Apple, but that whole intervening period, I’m glad you highlighted that because to me, that was like every page, I’m like, “Wow, that’s interesting, I didn’t know that”. It was really valuable.**

**PM:** I remember having this thought in my head when writing that I wanted an Apple nerd, let’s say, to open the table of contents and be like, “Well, I don’t even know, LG goes global? Why is LG in this book?”. So they built the iMac in Korea and then they built it in Wales and Mexicali, and who knows that history well? So I wanted the reader to be impressed that I’m talking to Welsh technicians who ran the supply chain out of Wales.

**I was impressed. I’m impressed, I think I’ve made clear, I’m impressed!**

#### Apple’s Reluctant Shift to Outsourcing

**Well, let’s go through this. Tell me about that first part, which ties into this and their initial forays with outsourcing. I thought you did a very good job of describing the financial imperative for outsourcing. It’s marginal work as opposed to tech, where it’s all sort of upfront and it’s like cloud computing — or not even that. It’s more tangible of that physical goods and doing it as a service.**

**PM:** It’s variable cost instead of fixed cost.

**That’s right, that’s what I was going for.**

**PM:** The problem with running your own factory is you are paying for idle workers when you don’t have products being built, whereas manufacturing-as-a-service changes that completely.

**Absolutely. So this bit of just them being the last holdout, and them wanting to build it themselves, talk more about that, because I do think that’s important and sort of a mollifying — What’s the word I’m going for? — the book ends up I think, pretty rough on Apple, and appropriately so, but I don’t think you’re mean. Actually, I thought there were opportunities for you to take shots that you didn’t because some things weren’t clear, like iCloud keys in China. You’re like, “Well, we don’t know for sure”. You talked to some people, they think we do know for sure, but you weren’t taking cheap shots. But if you read this book, it’s very sobering. That’s why I think it’s useful to say, “Look, Apple started out in a very, very different place”.**

**PM:** It’s like the founding ethos of the company in the late 1970s that you build your own products. I mean, that’s what Steve Wozniak was skilled at. He could just take a computer apart, retool it to some degree, and then end up with a faster, more beautifully designed computer, and then Steve Jobs realizes the marketing potential of his friend and then just wants to redo the aesthetics of it or what have you.

That’s sort of the DNA of the company, but it’s not the DNA of the companies that begin to clone the PC, let’s say 10 years later in the mid-1980s. They don’t have the same ethos. Because the IBM PC was, to quote someone involved in it, “Bandaged together with existing parts”, those existing parts began to be created in places like Taiwan and Korea where you had this supply chain where you could just import everything, and you didn’t have to be building it yourself.

So this isn’t a philosophical thing so much as a timing thing. For Apple, you had to be building it yourself because there was no electronic supply chain in the mid-’70s. By the mid-’80s, that’s changed, but it’s changed on behalf of the PC clones and so companies like [Space Craft Incorporated](<https://www.sci.com/about/>), the Foxconn of its day, begin doing the circuit board for the IBM PC because they have this automated method of doing it, and then they begin doing the final assembly as well, and SCI ends up playing this instrumental role, not just for IBM, but the PC clone industry. This sort of gives rise to Selectron, Celestica, Jabil, companies that people probably don’t know but actually they become a M&A dream in the 1990s on Wall Street, because companies you’ve never heard of are merging and merging because there’s all sorts of cutthroat demand or cutthroat pricing techniques that are taking place.

So if you remember the Compaq shock, I think this might be 1992, I don’t believe it’s in the book, but this is when Compaq dramatically cuts prices by 20 or 30% on PCs, and it’s because they’re the first to really go to Asia and exploit the opportunities there of having the final assembly done.

So sometimes people say that Tim Cook is the architect of moving to China, and I don’t think he is. That’s not a knock against him, there is no architect. The architect, if you will, is the suppliers themselves, who by competing for orders, are realizing that the place to go is Asia and then within Asia, the place to go after 2001 essentially, when China enters the WTO, is China.

So you had to tell that story because you needed to understand that Apple really tried to build everything themselves. That is the DNA of the company. Steve Jobs is really controlling, even when he leaves the DNA stays, and it’s only because everybody else has done so well with logistics, manufacturing and efficiency, in all those respects that Apple is near days away from missing payroll.

**And Apple can’t sell enough to fill their factories so that whole variable cost issue versus fixed costs, that’s what was killing the company, quite literally.**

**PM:** Yes, but that’s why it’s so important not to write a polemic, because I’m deeply sympathetic to Apple in this regard. They are the last holdout. Nobody should blame Apple one iota for deindustrializing or hollowing out American manufacturing in the ’80s and ’90s, they’re the one company trying to do it all there, and even when Steve Jobs goes to NeXT and found another company, he says, “My favorite thing about the computer is that it’s not built in Osaka”, he really cares about that. So it’s out of total necessity. The company would not exist if they didn’t adopt a global manufacturing strategy, but the significant thing about that is that is adopted before Steve Jobs comes back and ipso facto before Tim Cook comes back and people don’t understand and I actually give a little bit of credit for [Gil Amelio](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gil_Amelio>) for making that choice.

**Well, part of what makes this book special is that it is, intentionally or not, I think a really fascinating look at some aspects of Apple’s culture. To that end, one of the themes I picked up early on in the book is a certain desire, even a need, for Apple to be the one in charge and them telling others what to do. Even if they don’t know what to do, they need to feel like they are telling the others what to do. Everything has to be a teacher-student relationship, and they are always the teacher, and it’s almost as if suppliers helping them hurt them more than losing massive amounts of money. That just seems to be a continuous theme, particularly in this LG, initial Foxconn era. That’s a theme that comes through very strongly.**

**PM:** Maybe I could tell the story of how Foxconn comes on board, because the line that frankly my wife wrote about this is that Jony Ive and Steve Jobs came up with products that made Apple unique, but the meeting of the minds between Tim Cook and [former Foxconn Chairman and CEO] Terry Gou has made them ubiquitous.

Essentially what happens is that Apple is under such pressure, I mean literally the brink of bankruptcy, to come up with the iMac design and then to produce it at reasonable scale that they don’t know how to manufacture it. It’s LG that basically understands the specs and the production features. Even though Apple has trained them, they haven’t done any of the writing [down of the specifications] or whatever. When the iMac becomes a massive success and it becomes America’s best-selling computer, it’s LG that knows how to build it and LG is refusing Apple’s demands to cut prices because of scale effects.

So Apple, as a secretive project, really unknown even to ID and PD, this is the ops team run by Tim Cook, basically takes a call from Terry Gou who says, “Let me fix this”, and they begin surreptitiously reverse engineering the product so that Foxconn is basically getting up to speed on how to build this thing. What’s interesting is they do such a good job that even though when ID gets involved, they look at it and they say, “Well, it’s been done all wrong, this isn’t to our specifications”, they’re nevertheless impressed with what Foxconn has been able to do, and so they come on as a second supplier to LG and they replicate the triple continent strategy that Apple had.

If you remember in the ’80s and ’90s, Apple made their computers in California, also Colorado, but America is what I’m trying to say, Ireland and Singapore. So when Apple has LG come on to become the first real contract manufacturer who’s going to build their main desktop computer on three continents, LG does it on three continents, and then Foxconn to win the order basically says we will mimic what LG has done, so they end up building in the Czech Republic. Which again, a narrative I don’t think anyone’s really familiar with. And Fullerton, California, which is never a big volume player, but of course you’ve got the proximity to Cupertino.

So it’s actually where a lot of the great lessons are taught between students and master, if you will, Apple and Foxconn. So the proximity to Cupertino is significant, even if the volume is coming out of Fullerton, never are. As you’re building products in three continents like that, the more you’re seeing that China is just winning out every time, the affordable labor, the abundance of labor, the efficiency of the labor and my narrative here on the Czech Republic, it’s a little bit heartbreaking because essentially what happens is that Foxconn realizes that in China you don’t have labor unions, you don’t have a free press that’s writing negative stories about you, etc and so the reason why the Czech Republic doesn’t really work out, although the factory is there for 10 years building Apple products, is that the lower labor standards, the lower environmental rights, etc. of China just allows Foxconn to work more efficiently in China.

**One of the things that was really interesting is that initial wave going into China, they were seeking efficiency and low labor costs, Apple was pursuing something else or found something else. Tell me about that.**

**PM:** Yeah, so I say that when the likes of Dell and HP were moving to China, they were doing so for the reasons you just mentioned, which I might just call margin. When Apple goes there, they’re seeing unconstrained design potential. So you could build an automation unfriendly product knowing that if you had a conveyor belt set up where each person has 10 seconds to fulfill their task, you could basically get anything done. They don’t just see low cost and abundance of labor, they understand the possibilities of creating something that would be extremely difficult to make, but with people being paid 50 cents an hour and having tens of thousands and later hundreds of thousands of people in a factory, they could get it done.

**This is a really important piece of what China gave Apple, because it’s so easy to get anchored on the low cost bit. But if you want to make something that’s cutting edge, by definition, that whole initial wave you’re talking about, someone figures out how to automate PCB boards, so they can just make a bunch of them. Everyone has the same one or they go through these assembly lines, it’s all standardized, it’s all the same, and Apple’s like, not that “We can go there and tap into standardization”, it’s, “We can go there and be more customized than anyone else but do it at scale”. That’s a reality very, very early, you could not get that in America or anyone else, and it’s under-appreciated. If you like Apple for what Apple does where they actually create new things and new ideas and new phones that look different than anything that came before, China was part and parcel of that.**

**PM:** And this is why I think the first 25 years of Apple actually really instructs how the company operates today, because when they were building things on their own, they had that “Control your own destiny through manufacturing” mentality, and so when they moved to outsourcing, they do something that is unlike what anybody else does.

I don’t even think outsourcing is the proper term, it is in the sense that it’s the third party building your product, but they’re taking such control over those factories that they’re doing something nobody else is really doing and this is where you get into their effect being something like a nation builder when we’ll come back to that later because we’re too early in the narrative.

**We will definitely come back to that for sure.**

**PM:** But essentially in order to get it done in Korea or to build the iPods in Taiwan manufacturing design engineers from Apple — and by the way, back then they’re called supply base engineers, this is just a footnote in the book. But basically the people that understand how to build the production processes, they’re flying over to these countries in Asia to train and equip the teams, basically teach engineering one-on-one.

**I knew this very early on. Back when I started making enough money to fly in business class, and I’d be on that United flight to Taipei most frequently, I sat next to someone from Apple every single flight, because this is the old United, the 2-4-2, where you were seatmates with someone so you’re going to have a conversation whether you wanted to or not. And man, I met a lot of Apple people, I can promise you that.**

**PM:** That is hilarious. So yeah, I would talk to people that worked on the Pixel, let’s say, or worked at Motorola in the late 2000s, and that’s what they would say. It would be like you’d be the one Pixel guy and everybody else in first class worked for Apple.

**Yep, totally.**

#### Apple’s Clueless Success in China

**One of my favorite parts of the book was the story of Apple retail in China.**

**PM:** Yes, that’s my favorite part.

**It kind of seemed like a distraction coming off the LG and the Foxconn stuff, and I’m like, “What’s the point here?”, but I thought it was — to me, this was one of the keys of the book, because what made it so interesting and notable was the extent to which it showed just how clueless Apple was about China. I’m sitting there watching as someone who’s not lived in China, but lived in Taiwan, and there’s some aspects that are similar, there’s some aspects where I know, I personally probably wouldn’t really want to live in China for a lot of the things that are expressed and articulated in this section that is just much more intense and a reality there and it sort of speaks to another cultural observation, which is the Apple bubble and the complete inability to have a theory of mind for anyone but themselves. People listening to this, and you have a bit in here about Steve Jobs getting mad at third party developers earlier in the other parts, but all these cultural things that we see or the people listening see, and things like the App Store, they see things like Apple’s presentations or Apple getting snippy with journalists or whatever it might be — they all come through in the book in these totally unexpected other places.**

**PM:** Yeah, this is my favorite part of the narrative, and partly it’s because of the behind-the-scenes writer thing, which is that I wrote a 70-page book pitch, and we had five publishers bid on it, and then Simon & Schuster won out and it’s been an incredible process since then.

But I, as the author of the 70-page book pitch, knew that I had nothing interesting to say from the period 2007 to 2013. In other words, I knew that 2013 was Apple’s political awakening in China. Obviously I knew that the iPhone came out in 2007. But I thought I was going to have this really dull section in the middle that sort of explains in a boring way how the iPhone was built vis-à-vis a Nokia phone or something like that.

Instead, I discovered this narrative of, it’s called, “Insatiable Demand of the Birth of the iPhone Market in China”, without giving too much away. What happens is in 2010, the iPhone 4 just goes wild. It just becomes the gangbusters, conspicuous symbol that every young person in China wants to have, and supply/demand imbalance is so great that these scalpers, who call themselves “Yellow Cows”, recognize this imbalance and they want to amass tens of thousands of iPhones and go to a second tier city. This is a time when there’s 40 different cities in China have a population greater than 2 million. None of those, aside from Beijing and Shanghai, have an Apple Store and so they’re amassing these products and they’re selling them in these other markets.

Well, how are they amassing them? You can’t buy more than two iPhones per person, so they’re busing in migrants by the literal thousands to snake outside the store for everyone to buy two iPhones each, and then they’re putting them in these suitcases and going to another city to sell them.

That’s already fascinating, and that’s probably a whole chapter of just describing yellow cows and the gray market, but it gets even more fascinating because they’re often mixed up with the triads, these organized crime groups similar to the mafia, and they get greedy. And so with factory connections, they begin hollowing out the phones, selling the memory to markets in Shenzhen, and then replacing it with lookalike memory that doesn’t actually function, but to the human eye at the Genius Bar they can’t tell what’s going on. And then the yellow cows then go to places like Portland where there’s no sales tax, and they’re avoiding the value added tax in China by smuggling in phones from Portland. But then they’re also zapping the processor so that it renders the phone inoperable. But at the same time, it masks the retail origin, it masks the fact that it’s a US-sold iPhone. So they’re buying these phones with fake IDs, they’re paying one month of a 24-month contract, smuggling the phone into China, and then still selling it for $8 or $900. So they’re finding ways, legally and illegally —

**You’re skipping the part where they’re going in and asking for a return, saying, “My phone is defective”.**

**PM:** Yeah, they’re buying the phone, breaking the phone, smuggling it into the country, breaking the phone, taking it to the Genius Bar, and then Apple, unaware of what’s going on, is giving them a brand new phone, and then they’re selling it on in the gray market. So they’re finding ways to make more money per iPhone than Apple, which is just this extraordinary narrative and essentially what happens—

**And it goes on for years! That’s what’s so fascinating about it.
