### Walmart on ChatGPT

From [Bloomberg](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-14/walmart-partners-with-openai-to-offer-shopping-on-chatgpt>):

> Walmart Inc. is teaming up with OpenAI to enable shoppers to browse and purchase its products on ChatGPT, the retailer’s latest push to incorporate artificial intelligence. Users will be able to shop Walmart’s assortment directly on ChatGPT by clicking a “buy” button, Daniel Danker, Walmart’s executive vice president of AI, product and design, said in an interview. The catalog includes apparel, entertainment, packaged food and other products from Walmart and its Sam’s Club chain. “We view this as an opportunity to deliver convenience in a way that meets customers where they are,” said Danker, who joined the company this year from Instacart Inc. to accelerate AI adoption.
>
> Fresh food isn’t included in the offering, partly because consumers buy similar products weekly. The new function will launch in the fall. Shares rose 5% on Tuesday, closing at a record high. Customers’ current Walmart or Sam’s Club accounts will get automatically linked to ChatGPT. Goods will also be available from third-party sellers.

File this one under shocking but not surprising — and definitely interesting. Walmart is signing up for Instant Checkout, [which I wrote about when it launched with Etsy and Shopify](<https://stratechery.com/2025/openai-instant-checkout-ai-and-long-tail-e-commerce-is-ai-different/>). In that Update I focused on the possibility of ChatGPT providing the inverse functionality of Meta advertising: the latter let niche merchants find customers, while the former helps customers find niche products.

Walmart fits into this story more than you might think: yes, there are the products sold by Walmart directly, which are by-and-large anything-but-niche, but Walmart.com has a huge third-party marketplace a la Amazon; a lot of those merchants you discover via Instagram ads or whatnot also have their products on Amazon, and a good number have them on Walmart too. That means that Instant Checkout-enabled product recommendations that have gone to Shopify by default might now go to Walmart too — which means it’s possible that OpenAI could set up some sort of bidding system to be the merchant listed, instead of just taking a flat fee.

At the same time, the consumer staples that Walmart is known for are not necessarily found on Shopify sites: that’s a big opportunity for Walmart to consistently be the recommended link for products that yes, are available elsewhere (like Amazon), but as long as elsewhere isn’t a part of Instant Checkout than Walmart is going to get the links for those recommendations.

The fresh food exclusion is interesting: yes, you buy fresh food regularly, and I guess that is where Walmart drew the line in terms of giving that cut to OpenAI, but it’s worth noting that recipes are a killer ChatGPT use case; it seems like that would be another area where basically owning recommendation links would be pretty valuable.

### Walmart (and Amazon) Motivations

I mentioned Walmart drawing the line somewhere; what is notable is that they are signing up for a platform where they have to pay any sort of fee period. This is the company that is still a holdout when it comes to Apple Pay: Walmart would prefer to own the billing relationship with the customer and the associated data, they want their app to be the only option for mobile payment (driving app usage), and while no one knows this for sure, I wouldn’t be surprised if Walmart gets some slight discount on its processing fees for saving card issuers that 0.15% Apple Pay fee.

Instant Checkout works against all of this: the transaction happens in ChatGPT, as does the entire customer journey, and ChatGPT collects a small fee. There are, however, important differences that I suspect drive this different view of payments.

First, there is the point I made above, about customer journeys starting on ChatGPT. If a ChatGPT user gets a product recommendation, and needs to go search for that product in the browser, the most likely place they are going to go is Amazon; now they can just click into Walmart. This is the exact opposite situation with Apple Pay, where you’ve already made the choice to enter a Walmart store; at that point, you’re a captive customer, and Walmart doesn’t see the need to cater to your payment preferences.

Second, the customers that Walmart is likely to acquire through this integration are, I suspect, less likely to be regular Walmart customers. That means at a minimum whatever commerce comes of this deal is likely to be incremental; at best Walmart enters into the consideration set for an entirely new customer base who might have previously defaulted to Amazon.

Third, while Walmart is a solid number two in e-commerce, they are a distant second to Amazon. That behooves jumping on new market opportunities, even if that new opportunity means giving up a bit of control. We’ve seen this before, with the iPhone, and how Apple would regularly launch with a second or third place carrier in a country — starting with AT&T née Cingular — because the leading carrier didn’t want to give up the amount of control that Apple demanded; eventually, however, so many consumers ended up switching that Apple eventually got the iPhone everywhere.

That naturally leads to the most interesting question this news raises: what about Amazon? Amazon is where a huge amount of e-commerce searches start, and I’m sure they don’t want to cannibalize that business: not only do they own the transaction and the data and not have to pay a fee, but they also get to cram a lot of ads into the experience, which is a massive and growing part of their business; simply being a link in ChatGPT sounds a lot less attractive. And besides, Amazon can make their own chatbot!

I do think this would be a mistake: Amazon should take this news as an impetus to get on ChatGPT as well. I think that the commerce funnel ChatGPT has created is totally distinct from the search funnel that Amazon carved out from Google: the latter is for stuff you already know about; the former is for stuff you didn’t know existed. In other words, I think that even for Amazon ChatGPT commerce will be incremental in the long run.

Of course Amazon and Walmart both being on ChatGPT will create the same dynamics I talked about above, where multiple Instant Checkout partners have the same product, which ultimately will give OpenAI the opportunity to sell the link to the highest bidder, eating into margin. That, though, is the privilege of being an Aggregator, of owning demand: suppliers have no choice but to go where they must to meet it, and given Amazon’s massive investments in logistics they need to sell everywhere they can.

That part about linking your Walmart and ChatGPT accounts is interesting as well. This probably solved some of Walmart’s data concerns; it also is probably logistically necessary in terms of customer service, managing returns, etc. Those are all big concerns for Amazon — the returns question was a big hold-up in terms of getting Amazon fulfillment working on Shopify — but the framework for solving them is already there. The real challenge will be in accepting a subservient position in this new opportunity in e-commerce, but I think that’s inevitable; the only question is if Amazon will accept that sooner rather than later.

### Spotify Podcasts on Netflix

From the [New York Times](<https://www.nytimes.com/2025/10/14/business/media/netflix-spotify-podcast-deal.html>):

> After months of speculation, Netflix is taking its first step into the world of video podcasting. The streaming giant announced a partnership with the audio company Spotify on Tuesday that would bring numerous video podcasts on sports, culture, entertainment and true crime to Netflix early next year. The deal prevents the shows from airing in their entirety on YouTube, Netflix’s biggest competitor.
>
> The 16 shows in the deal are produced by Spotify Studios and The Ringer, the website and podcasting network that Bill Simmons started and that Spotify acquired in 2020. The shows include two popular true crime podcasts, “Conspiracy Theories” and “Serial Killers”; “The Bill Simmons Podcast”; and “Rewatchables,” which features Mr. Simmons and a roundtable of movie lovers discussing their favorite films…
>
> Netflix will not run ad breaks with the initial set of shows, even for subscribers who pay for the less expensive plan that shows advertising during its programming. But the advertising Spotify sells that are integrated into the shows will remain.

Now here is a story near-and-dear to my heart, both as a listener (and occasional guest) of the Bill Simmons Podcast, and also as a podcaster. One of the best things about being a podcaster is that your listeners are usually very loyal; the flipside of that is that listeners are very difficult to acquire. This is why I made the case in [Grantland and the (Surprising) Future of Publishing](<https://stratechery.com/2015/grantland-and-the-surprising-future-of-publishing/>) that it made sense to combine text and audio: text is good for sharing and acquiring customers, whereas podcasts are a good way to keep customers and monetize them. That’s the exact approach Simmons took with The Ringer, and which I have taken with Stratechery Plus.

YouTube is a bit different. On one hand, it’s probably the best platform for acquiring customers, thanks to the algorithm; on the other hand, it’s harder to have the sort of direct connection with customers that comes from a direct feed putting podcasts into their player, or even better, a direct billing relationship with them. It seems to me — and this is certainly the case with Simmons — that podcasters who got big through traditional podcast distribution have a hard time crossing over to YouTube; I think this might go the other direction as well, or it just may be the case that YouTube’s combination of audience generation and monetization means that YouTube-centric podcasts don’t really want to go anywhere else.

To that end, it makes sense that Simmons and the Ringer are the guinea pigs in this new experiment. From Simmons’ perspective, this Netflix deal is pure upside: he and Spotify get to earn more money on their content via whatever fees Netflix is paying for the podcasts, and potentially gain new customers (but if he doesn’t that’s ok); meanwhile, he’s not giving much up by not being on YouTube. Netflix, meanwhile, gets an established podcast to get this new product category off of the ground, and they don’t have to drastically overpay to make up for whatever revenue and audience might be lost from moving off of YouTube.

The question for Netflix is how many Simmons are there? It’s hard for anyone big on YouTube to leave YouTube, and it seems likely that anyone who becomes big in the future will do so on YouTube; that means the candidate field are people who became big podcasters a decade ago, of which there are, by definition, a limited supply.

The other way to think about this deal is about it being less about Simmons and more about the companies: YouTube is the biggest threat for both Spotify and Netflix. I’ve talked in the past about [The Anti-Amazon Alliance](<https://stratechery.com/2020/the-anti-amazon-alliance/>); it seems we are witnessing the formation of the anti-YouTube alliance. The difference is that the anti-Amazon alliance was made up of the long-tail of e-commerce merchants, primarily powered by Shopify; YouTube, however, is the long tail of video content, which means that Spotify and Netflix will, at least for now, have to make do with podcasters that made it work independent of YouTube.
