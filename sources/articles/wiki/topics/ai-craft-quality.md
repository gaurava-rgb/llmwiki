# AI Craft and Quality

## Summary [coverage: low -- 1 source]
Published March 26, 2026, Michal Malewicz's essay argues that "vibe coding" — building software through AI prompts without foundational skill or deliberate intent — has reached the point of diminishing returns. The explosion of AI-generated apps, landing pages, and SaaS products has not raised quality; it has converged output toward a soulless median. Malewicz, a designer active since the late 1990s, contends that slop predates AI — it was already endemic through Figma's auto-layout era and flat-design templates — but AI widened the no-skill group and inflated the middle of the quality distribution. The fix is not better prompts or newer models but foundational knowledge, developed taste, and the willingness to slow down and make deliberate decisions.

## Key Thesis [coverage: low -- 1 source]
AI is a multiplier, not a substitute for skill: multiplying zero skill by any number still yields zero. When craft, curiosity, and heuristics are absent, AI produces "algorithmic sameness" — an average of averages — that is functional but forgettable. The builders who survive the current era are those who never needed prompts in the first place, because they could already articulate what they wanted and why. Speed without direction is running in circles; the competitive window for "cheap and fast" is closing as consumers get better at detecting slop.

## Key Insights [coverage: low -- 1 source]
- **Slop is structural, not new.** Malewicz traces design slop across 2006, 2016, and 2026. Figma's auto-layout allowed people with no design foundation to produce passable-looking interfaces without understanding what they were doing. AI extended the same dynamic — removing the friction of learning while also removing the friction that forces thinking.
- **The quality distribution has inverted dangerously.** The author estimates roughly 1% bad websites, 1% genuinely good ones, and 98% in a widened average. AI didn't raise the floor significantly — it massively inflated the mediocre middle. Memorable, inspiring work has become rarer even as total output explodes.
- **Tool obsession and prompt hoarding are proxies for avoiding craft.** Malewicz ran an experiment: two joke posts offering useless prompts attracted 300,000+ saves. He even wrote "Comment PROMPT if you're stupid" under one post and hundreds of people did so automatically. Builders spend more time benchmarking models than building. A great designer with Figma from 2019 will outperform a mediocre designer with the latest AI tool from 2026.
- **"Minimum Viable Precision" is the real MVP standard.** Malewicz coined this framing in 2017: an MVP isn't just Minimum Viable Product but also Minimum Visible Precision. With AI tools, failure to reach that precision is no longer a time constraint — it is laziness. Shipping fast at 2x with extreme polish beats shipping at 10x with none.
- **Economics favor quality on a long enough timeline.** Once market saturation means everyone has a similar average product with similar perceivable value, consumers shift to whoever demonstrates effort. Fast and cheap execution creates short-term revenue but risks compounding revenue drops as differentiation disappears. The AI companies' incentive is to push vibe-coding; the individual creator's incentive is the opposite.

## Industry Context [coverage: low -- 1 source]
This article sits at the intersection of the "move fast" startup orthodoxy and the backlash against AI-generated mediocrity. The author positions himself against the constant CEOs who argue "get your products out ASAP, coding will be extinct in 2 years," characterising this framing as designed to maximize AI subscription revenue rather than creator outcomes. The essay captures a specific cultural moment — March 2026 — when AI-generated landing pages had fully converged on a single aesthetic: gradient text hero sections reading "Revolutionize Your Workflow," three Lucide-icon feature cards, stock-photo testimonials, and three-tier pricing tables. The Apple-inspired Braun design lineage is invoked as a counter-model: taking inspiration and making it distinctly your own through taste and accumulated craft.

## Implications [coverage: low -- 1 source]
- Learn design fundamentals regardless of whether you plan to use AI: typography, spacing, hierarchy, and color theory are prerequisites for articulating what you want — and for knowing when AI gets it wrong.
- Own decisions actively: when you relinquish control to AI defaults, the output has nothing unique in it and you become the redundant part of the equation.
- Use saved time to improve quality, not to ship more things: the compounding return is on depth, not breadth.
- Develop taste by studying great products analytically — understand why every button is there, why the label reads as it does, what happens next — rather than passively consuming AI-generated work.
- Treat the "it works and was fast and cheap" window as closing: plan product economics around quality differentiation rather than assuming cheap execution is defensible long-term.
