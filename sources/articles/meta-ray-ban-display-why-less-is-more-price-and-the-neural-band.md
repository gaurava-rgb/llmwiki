### Meta Ray-Ban Display

From [Bloomberg](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-18/meta-launches-799-glasses-with-screen-in-bid-for-mainstream-hit>):

> Meta Platforms Inc., seeking to turn its smart glasses lineup into a must-have product, on Wednesday unveiled its first version with a built-in screen. The latest model, the $799 Meta Ray-Ban Display, features a screen in the right lens. It can show text messages, video calls, turn-by-turn directions in maps and visual results from queries to Meta’s AI service. The subtly integrated display can also serve as a viewfinder for the camera on a user’s phone or surface music playback…
>
> The glasses introduce a new control system. While users can still swipe along the frame as with previous models, the primary interface is now hand gestures, detected by a neural wristband strapped around the wearer’s dominant hand. The user can select items by pinching their thumb and index finger, swipe through items by sliding a thumb across their gripped hand, double tap their thumb to invoke Meta’s AI voice assistant, or twist their hand mid-air to adjust music volume and other controls.
>
> In addition to app interactions and the ability to handle AI queries, the glasses include a live caption feature that displays spoken words in real time — including translations — similar to closed captions on a TV. The video calling function lets wearers see the person they’re speaking with while sharing their own point of view. Users can reply to texts by sending an audio recording or dictating a response. Later this year, the wristband will add another option: handwriting words in the air. A future update will also let the glasses focus on the person a wearer is speaking with while filtering out background noise.
>
> The new glasses will go on sale Sept. 30 and will include the wristband. Meta is offering two sizes and two color options: black and a brown shade called sand. They will be sold by EssilorLuxottica SA’s Ray-Ban, Lenscrafters International Inc., Best Buy Co. and a limited number of Verizon Communications Inc. locations. At launch, they’ll support apps like Facebook Messenger, WhatsApp and a music app powered by Spotify Technology SA. The Instagram app will initially only support direct messages, but Meta plans to add Reels viewing later this year.
>
> The glasses’ display offers a limited 20-degree field of view with a resolution of 600 x 600 pixels. Its brightness ranges from 30 to 5,000 nits, providing decent visibility in most outdoor conditions, though it can struggle in the brightest sunlight. Some prescriptions are supported, but only as a built-to-order option. The external camera matches past Ray-Ban glasses with a 12-megapixel sensor but falls short of new non-display models, also introduced Wednesday, in video resolution and battery life. The glasses record 1080p video and last six hours per charge, with the external case providing an additional 30 hours — roughly four full recharges.

That was a big excerpt, but (1) there are a lot of details to note and (2) you should [read the article](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-18/meta-launches-799-glasses-with-screen-in-bid-for-mainstream-hit>) as it includes extensive comments from Chief Technology Officer Andrew Bosworth and Vice President of Wearables Alex Himel.

Unfortunately I do not have hands-on — face-on? — experience with the Meta Ray-Ban Display; I was already in New York this week, and didn’t realize that the 5pm start-time for the keynote (weird!) would have made it logistically possible to attend in person. And, if I’m being honest, I figured that since [I had already tried Orion](<https://stratechery.com/2024/an-interview-with-meta-cto-andrew-bosworth-about-orion-and-reality-labs/>), I was covered.

The Ray-Ban Displays are not Orion, to be clear: Orion is a standalone computing device with displays in both lenses and a puck for running a full operating system; Ray-Ban Displays pair with your existing smartphone and are more of a heads-up display than a full-blown AR experience.

### Why Less is More

Still, must we be so parsimonious with our definitions? When you are wearing the Ray-Ban Displays you can see reality, and that vision of reality can be augmented with little bits of information and UI; sounds like augmented reality to me!

I’m not just being pedantic; the things that Ray-Ban Displays can do today were my favorite parts of the Orion experience. From [an Update about that experience](<https://stratechery.com/2024/more-on-orion-where-vision-pro-went-wrong-apples-response-and-metas-motivation/>):

> The notification flow was as cool as you might imagine. You can be doing something else — either in augmented reality, or just in the real world — and you can see and click into a notification simply by moving your eyes and touching your fingers together. The lack of friction is amazing.
>
> One thing that really surprised me came in the context of taking video calls. My first call was with another person using their phone camera who was standing just outside the demo room. My second call was with someone using the webcam on their computer in a weird box-like office, or so I thought. In fact, it turned out that person was using a Quest headset and a Codec Avatar, Meta’s name for photorealistic avatars. I was legitimately fooled, at least for the first 15 seconds or so. Now I suppose you could chalk this up to Orion’s relatively low resolution, but at least for this call the avatar was good enough that it escaped the uncanny reality effect that you might expect from photorealistic avatars. This is, I should note, another feature that hasn’t shipped, but the quality was definitely much higher than I expected.

What grabbed me about notifications on Orion — including the notifications for the video calls — is that it was “just-in-time UI”; there wasn’t anything in my field of vision until it was appropriate to have something in my field of vision. I found this much more compelling than trying to use an AR app. By extension, the fact that this is the primary interface of the Ray-Ban Displays (which have the screen off most of the time) actually strikes me as a good thing. This isn’t an app platform, and, more generally, I’m not sure AR will ever be an app platform; phones are very good at doing that, with screen space and processing power to spare.

That’s not to say that there won’t be more capability; rather, it makes much more sense that that capability will be surfaced via an AI interface. In this Meta’s lack of a phone platform is arguably an advantage: while Apple and Google will be sorely tempted to rely on their app advantage to add functionality to glasses, they run the risk of anchoring in the wrong paradigm instead of being forced to build an entirely new — and far more appropriate — interface paradigm from scratch.

All of this, however, does touch on a couple of flies in the ointment of Meta’s presentation: demo fails. There were two of them: in the first instance, CEO Mark Zuckerberg asked a chef to come up with a new Korean-inspired steak sauce; despite multiple efforts, the chef couldn’t get Meta AI to understand his request. The second demo fail was around video calls: Zuckerberg received several calls from Bosworth, but could never figure out how to answer them: sometimes the notification disappeared, and other times clicking “Answer” did nothing; the end result is that I don’t know how the experience compared to the one I documented above.

I personally am not bothered by the demo fails. They are an excellent explanation as to why Apple now fully pre-records their product announcements, but it’s the risk of demo fails happening that makes them so compelling. I’m firmly on the side of giving Meta credit for running that risk, even if they rolled snake-eyes. At the same time, the fact that the primary AI demo failed did highlight Meta’s weaknesses in that particular area (even if the failure probably didn’t have anything to do with the quality of Meta’s AI).

Even so, what struck me about this announcement is that while Meta is very enthusiastic about AI enhancing this product, along with the new non-display models they announced, it still seems secondary: this was a proper hardware product announcement that was compelling in its own right, even without any AI features. I think that’s actually really important, because having a compelling product is the price of admission to anything greater. I wrote in 2015’s [From Products to Platforms](<https://stratechery.com/2015/from-products-to-platforms/>):

> What’s fascinating to consider is that it’s arguable the iPad would actually be in a much better position were it owned by Microsoft: the company is at its core a platform company that has long bent over backwards to accommodate its developers even at the expense of the user experience. That, though, is the rub: in consumer markets the only way to gain the prerequisite scale to be a platform is to first have a superior product, like Apple. More than ever each has what the other needs.

The challenge for AR products (and VR products, for that matter) is that, unlike the smartphone — which was a vastly more capable product than the cellphone you already carried, making it a no-brainer upgrade — you have to actually convince the end user why they should care about the product category at all. You don’t do that by doing everything poorly; you do it by doing some small number of things exceptionally well, and growing from there, and I’m optimistic about Ray-Ban Displays in that regard.

### Price and the Neural Band

We’ve all heard the phrase “jaw-dropping”; I’m actually not sure if I’ve ever been so intensely aware of that physical reaction as I was when Zuckerberg announced the price: $799. My jaw literally dropped, because I was bracing for something much higher.

The big reason for my expectations of a price in the four figures — well into the four figures, to be honest — is that the Ray-Ban Displays include the Neural Band, a bracelet that detects electrical impulses, which allows you to navigate the UI surreptitiously, without having to hold your hands out or manipulate a controller. I used the neural band as part of my Orion demo, and it’s extraordinary: it’s one of those experiences that seems obvious, like multitouch for smartphones, or the mouse for the PC. It also seems so futuristic that I was genuinely surprised the price point came in where it did.

A low price is, needless to say, critical for building that product beachhead that gives you the opportunity to build an ecosystem. $799 is certainly not cheap, but it’s accessible, and the fact that it gives you the input controller of the future is very impressive.

More generally, what impressed me so much about this launch — and this covers my dismay at not having attended _and_ the demo fails — is the extent to which Meta is going for it. The best way to bend the cost curve on devices like this is to actually ship devices; the best way to improve software quality is to actually have it used for critical functionality; the best way to figure out killer use cases is to actually let people try things in the real world. Meta, despite the fact they are nearly 20 years old and worth nearly two trillion dollars, is very much throwing caution to the wind here, and it’s fun! And, needless to say, [the contrast to Apple](<https://stratechery.com/2025/iphones-17-and-the-sugar-water-trap/>), their most obvious (potential) competitor in this space, is very stark.
