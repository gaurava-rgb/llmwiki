Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery Interview is with [Eric Seufert](<https://x.com/eric_seufert>). Seufert is, in my opinion, the world’s pre-eminent digital advertising analyst. Seufert is [Stratechery’s most frequent interview subject](<https://stratechery.com/?s=eric+seufert&category=interviews>), and my main takeaway in preparing for the interview is that I don’t have him on enough! Seufert writes full-time at [MobileDevMemo](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/>), and is well worth the subscription.

In this interview we dive deep into advertising and AI, including why OpenAI is terribly late in launching an advertising product, and why affiliate advertising is not the right model for ChatGPT in particular. The better option is personalized advertising, and we have a long discussion about why that is, as well as how the preeminent personalized advertising company — Meta — is doing currently, and why they are risking their future by struggling in AI. We also discuss the changing privacy environment, why agentic commerce probably won’t be a thing, and why Netflix’s advertising business has turned the corner.

#### OpenAI Needs Ads Now

**Eric Seufert, it has been too long. Welcome back to Stratechery.**

**Eric Seufert:** Ben Thompson, it’s great to be back. Thank you for having me. It’s always a pleasure.

**I keep trying to schedule these interviews for ideal calendar times, which is often post-earnings and then other things get in the way and back and forth and then I’m preparing for this interview and I’m like, “Crap, it’s been seven months”, and there’s so many things to talk about, I barely know where to start. But before we get into the last six months, do you have any early takes on what you’re looking for with Gemini 3,[which is reportedly dropping imminently](<https://x.com/sundarpichai/status/1989481514393121239>)?**

**ES:** Yeah, I mean any minute now. I think it’s going to be an impressive model update, I think 2.5 was an impressive update, I think 3 will be an even bigger milestone.

I think it’s interesting how the narrative has changed about Google and AI within the last 12 months, like dramatically changed, fundamentally changed where they were a laggard, they didn’t really know how to apply it and now, you look at AI Overviews, AI Mode becoming one of the dominant ways that people interface with its core products. It hasn’t actually cannibalized engagement, it’s increased engagement, I’m sure we’re going to talk about that. Monetization’s at parity, I’m sure we’re going to talk about that, but they’ve also really led the way on the foundational model development front and it’s hard to not look at them as the AI leader or at least among the front of the pack.

**Yeah, I think that product question is still the big one. I think I agree with you on AI Overviews and AI Mode and there’s something to be said for the fact[that the most important part of the company is the one that moves the fastest](<https://stratechery.com/2025/google-earnings-meta-earnings-the-cost-of-reality-labs/>). I think overall, that’s probably a sign of health and exactly what you want to see, but is that going to be sufficient or are they going to actually start peeling people away from ChatGPT as far as direct interactions or is it just not going to matter? It is almost like, is a Facebook versus Snap metaphor going to be sufficient, where I don’t know that Facebook actually won back people that went to Snap, but they completely stopped the bleeding and maybe that’s what Google’s going for, or maybe they’re going to win it all?**

**ES:** Well, the question is, is this Facebook-Snap or is this Facebook-TikTok? Because it didn’t matter at the end of the day. Why? Because Facebook monetizes better. Why is it not going to matter, Google versus OpenAI? Because Google monetizes better.

OpenAI needs to launch its ads product today, that needs to be introduced now, they cannot wait. It’s not only important for OpenAI at this point, if people are looking at OpenAI and saying, “Look, this is a structural economic risk given the integration”, they need to launch it today just to make sure that there’s a path cleared for investors to remain excited about this, but the thing is Google is monetizing it now. Google has applied its monetization engine now to AI Overviews and AI Mode and it is monetizing it at parity, and it’s driving more engagement, so they are winning.

**This is a structural advantage because it’s not just that of course today they’re an established company funding from cashflow, it’s laying the foundation to having a structural advantage in not just monetization, but research and development, infrastructure, all those sorts of things for the next X number of years.**

**ES:** Exactly. It’s a commitment to being disciplined around a fundamentally viable business. Google was founded in 1998, they’re breakeven in 2001, they were profitable in 2002. This is a company that has operated with that kind of discipline from the very beginning.

OpenAI is not a new company and I think people are looking at it, it’s like, I wrote this tweet a while back and I said, “Look, OpenAI is just throwing a bunch of stuff to the wall and hoping something works. Why don’t they just take the playbook that has been written for them and integrate it and it will work?”, and people said, “You can compare them to Google and Google’s got all these other bets” — yeah, but they started with the ads. They could fund all those other bets from the very beginning, they were profitable within a few years of launching the company, and so the comparison is flawed because OpenAI refuses to do that.

**Yeah, I completely agree. I thought they announced[this group chat thing for ChatGPT](<https://stratechery.com/2025/chatgpt-group-chats-meta-and-the-encryption-trade-off-network-effects-and-ad-models/>), which by the way I think is fantastic, I think the group chat experience — [I’ve talked about, I have personal experience hacking this together with someone that I’m working with](<https://stratechery.com/2025/claude-4-anthropic-agents-human-ai-agents/>), and it is such a productive way to get particular tasks done. But [in their announcement](<https://openai.com/index/group-chats-in-chatgpt/>), there’s this really discordant paragraph talking about — good they’re launching it to everyone free or paid, but then the quality of the response or the rate limits will depend on who asked ChatGPT the question last. This product number one, good idea. Number two, establishing network effects, even better idea. Number three, you have to have an ad-supported model to make this work at scale and to gain those effects.**

**ES:** Right, exactly. Now, you’ve got what, two tiers, three tier user base—

**Yeah, so you have a Plus, a Pro, and a free user in the same chat getting different experiences in the chat, based on the subscription model. Just the pure complexity of implementing that is ridiculous.**

**ES:** The complexity and the awkwardness because it’s like, “Oh, no, Jim asked a question”, you know what I mean? You need to unify the experience and then you need to monetize with ads, which by the way, will probably drive more ARPU.

[I did a deconstruction of their unit economics](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/understanding-chatgpts-unit-economics/>) from the numbers that [were released in The Financial Times](<https://www.ft.com/content/a169703c-c4df-46d6-a2d3-4184c74bbaf7>), because a lot of the analysis was mixing up a bunch of things. They weren’t backing out the enterprise piece and they were confusing yearly ARPU with, they were projecting out the ARPU in a weird way. But if you look at the unit economics and you make those adjustments, they monetize like Snap.

And so, the thing is if they implemented a top-tier ads engine, which they could by the way, I mean you read [The Information article](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-readies-facebook-era>), they have 700 people from Meta that they’ve hired. They have so many people from Meta, there’s a dedicated Slack group in their company for ex-Meta employees. They have the institutional domain knowledge to build a leading ads platform, they should do that and they will monetize better.

**Well, not just that, but it feels like there’s a real, “We have to get this right”, and you don’t get things right on the Internet. What you do is you launch and you learn and you iterate and that comes down to speed and it comes down to just doing it. The longer the wait, the more the pressure is to get it right. You’re actually in a — what’s the opposite of a virtuous cycle? You’re in an unvirtuous cycle because—**

**ES:** Vicious cycle.

**Yeah, vicious cycle. Thank you. You’re putting so much pressure on this initial launch to have it right, whereas if they had just thrown out an ad product within say six months of ChatGPT blowing up, it would’ve been bad and ridiculous, it wouldn’t have worked, and we’ll talk about Netflix in a little bit, which I think makes this point, but they didn’t. And now we’re coming up on what, two years on, three years on and yeah, I couldn’t agree with you more. Now, you got me fired up, you were so fired up in your answer.**

**ES:** Well, what bothers me though is because you’ve got the typical voices talking about, “Well, the chatbot experience is about to get enshittified, here come the ads”. Well, you know what’s a shitty experience is being in a group chat and you’re getting different quality answers based on who asked the question, based on the tier of subscription they have.

Why don’t you make it free, first of all? Everyone gets the best possible access because it’s all monetized via ads, and then unify it. The truly enshittified experience is when you’re actually revealing which people have subscribed at which level and which that, to me, is just this weird mixture of approaches. That’s just not going to work and that’s not going to produce the network effects they want to achieve. You want everybody to get access to the same high quality of output and that will get people excited about the product and that will get advertisers excited about reaching those people.

#### Ads and Conflicts of Interest

**Well, let’s talk about the sort of advertising they should build. You just wrote a tour de force article last week entitled[Affiliate Links, Personalized Ads and Chatbot Revenue Optimization](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/affiliate-links-personalized-ads-and-chatbot-revenue-optimization/>). I’m going to tell you, Eric, that is a title that undersells the article, even though it is very descriptive, and I’m an expert on doing exactly that, so I could relate. We could do this whole podcast on this article, but tell me the genesis of it — is this downstream from [your initial negative reaction to Instant Checkout](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/chatgpts-instant-checkout-is-an-advertising-data-feed/>)? It feels like a summary of all your thoughts on AI advertising to date, is that a fair way to put it?**

**ES:** Yeah. So, [you interviewed Michael [Morton] from MoffettNathanson](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-michael-morton-about-ai-e-commerce/>) and it was a fantastic interview, very thought-provoking, very insightful, I learned a lot in the interview. But when I read it, I had this piece in the drafts folder, it had been there, so I started writing it [when the partnership between OpenAI and Walmart was announced](<https://corporate.walmart.com/news/2025/10/14/walmart-partners-with-openai-to-create-ai-first-shopping-experiences>), and I thought there were just fundamental differences between Walmart’s positioning here and Amazon’s positioning here that makes it less of a no-brainer for Amazon to do it, or actually, maybe even highlights the reasons why Amazon should not do it.

I think maybe we have some daylight between us there, but we can talk about that. So I started writing it then and it’s like a long piece, like 4,000 words, I was adding to it and then that interview that you did inspired me to finish it and publish it. So I kind of make the case that the ads model is better than the affiliate model, and I provide three reasons.

**Let’s walk through these. Actually, you start out with three flawed assumptions that people make about AI commerce and I love these assumptions because, in many respects, they’re not just a articulation of the AI advertising case, it’s like an all-up defense of advertising in general. So, let’s start with number one. “In-chat context will be the only input available”, that is a flawed assumption, tell me why.**

**ES:** Right, and I think that’s honestly the principle assumption that people are working from when they think about ads in chatbots in general, but specifically within ChatGPT. The idea there is that the ads need to hue to the content of the conversation, that’s the only input that you have to target the ads, and that obviously raises some conflict of interest concerns, right? Because if the chatbot is seen as this objective arbiter of truth, basically, this thing that just absorbs all the information and ranks it for your use based on the contextual signals that you’ve given it, then once you insert ads, it calls into question how objective actually its advice is.

**Why does search get away with this? Because search is like the canonical example of all the context you need is in the search box.**

**ES:** So, I wrote a piece a while ago about why I think it’s different, and I made the point a little bit in this piece, [but I just referenced back the old article that I wrote on that topic](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/perplexity-introduces-advertising-exploring-the-ai-search-incentive-problem/>).

I do believe search is different because search is not applying any editorial creation, search is saying, “We’re going to go and find a bunch of stuff and we’re going to rank it, and then we’re going to put some ads at the top”. So Google, they’re best served when the ad that they show you is the thing that they would’ve showed you first anyway. When they create this impetus for people to bid on that, but also, because it’s necessary to be there knowing that you got to be above the fold to even get the attention and get the clicks. When the ad that they show you is for the thing that they would’ve served right there anyway, then everyone’s better off and that’s what they optimize for.

But the thing is that’s maybe not the case in a chat situation, because they are applying editorial curation there. They’re making decisions about what to show you, what not, not just ranking it, but actually summarizing it. So there’s a difference in terms of the size of the output window, call it, the size of the amount of information, their whole point is to minimize that. You’ve got a couple paragraphs and that’s the whole point, I want to summarize, I don’t want to go do the work myself, I want you to summarize everything that’s available for me, but I want you to be objective about it.

Google’s like, “We’re just going to rank stuff based on what people historically have clicked on, and that’s what we’re optimizing against”, and so I do think they get away with ads based on the search term, whereas ChatGPT doesn’t, because they are applying editorial curation to serve you the most objective, yet best summary of the information that you’re seeking.

**Yeah, it’s another point that I’ve been coming around on a lot more. The extent to which ChatGPT really is competing with Meta and not Google and this is a point there, where search engines, we’ve talked about it before, you and I, search engines succeed because they assume human input, they assume the human choosing the link, which lends itself beautifully to the human chooses to click on an advertiser and that’s the actual winning. You don’t have to pay anything to be shown, you pay to be clicked, and this bit about what you’re seeing in a paragraph and people are raising this question, “Well, how do you show ads relative to that?” — well, maybe the ads shouldn’t actually be about what’s in the paragraph at all.**

**ES:** Right, and they don’t have to be. You build a CAPI [Conversions API], you build a pixel, you collect data from advertisers — the advertisers want you to have this data, they want you to have the conversion data because then you can do targeting on that basis, they want you to have it. And so, the way you sidestep that editorial problem, the conflict-of-interest problem, you sidestep the issue of the user questioning whether the output is serving the purpose of framing the ad or vice versa, is you don’t show ads related to the context at all.

Contextual ads don’t work as well as personalized ads, what you do is you take data on the back-end from the advertisers that have sold stuff to you, you use that to determine what the right ad to show to that person is, and you put it alongside the content. You put it between content blocks, but if it’s unrelated to the actual content, then no one ever questions whether you’re making the decision on what goes in the content on the basis of who’s advertising against it. So, you side-step that conflict issue all together.

**Well, I think one area of pushback on this is, and I think this ties into the myth, people have a myth around Facebook advertising, maybe less so now, but back in the day, they just assumed your Facebook ads were based on what you posted on Facebook, and it’s like, no, that really doesn’t matter at all. Facebook has pixels all over the web, they have SDKs in all these apps, they build up these incredible profiles that are completely incomprehensible to a human. They’re just these random numbers in a vector database that just magically — that’s how you get an ad for something you’ve never posted about or talked about, that’s how you get the conspiracy theories that, “Facebook is listening to me talk”, it’s like, “How did Facebook know that I was interested in that?”, it’s because they’re getting data on you from everywhere, and that is sort of what you’re talking about in this case.**

**I think the question is, and maybe we just don’t know the answer yet, this is why you need to experiment, is the beauty of Facebook as an advertising platform, and by Facebook I mean Meta generally, including Instagram, is where you’re at in the experience. You’re sitting on the couch, flipping through Instagram. If you actually examine the time you’re spending, you probably would stop doing it because you’re probably wasting time, but if you get distracted and go buy this thing, it’s not really a big deal. Whereas ChatGPT, you’re there, you’re focused, you’re doing stuff. Is that going to be a canvas where this sort of advertising is going to work?**

**ES:** Yeah. I get your point where it’s just a much more passive scrolling experience, I guess what I would compare this to though is ads in a mobile game. Which actually, if you look at AppLovin’s case for their ad product, it’s that the engagement is so much higher, you get so much more watch time in the ads.

They talked a lot about in the last earnings call that they need to bring the creative generation component into the ads platform because when people just apply their Facebook ads to the mobile gaming environment, it doesn’t work as well because people are used to trying to get the hook in on a Facebook ad in three seconds. But actually the engagement time in a mobile gaming ad, because it’s full screen interstitial and a lot of times you have to wait a certain amount of seconds before you can even close it out, is so much greater, so the ads that work best there are not be not necessarily going to be the ones that work best in a social media environment and so my sense is the same is true with games, this is an active experience. It’s an active engagement experience, but mobile gaming ads work tremendously well.

So I think I do take your point that the ad format probably can’t be exactly the same as the social media format where it’s just a content block among content blocks that you’re scrolling past because there is a lot more active engagement, but maybe that lends itself to a much better performing ad.

**Right, that’s a good point.**

#### The Archaic Affiliate Model

**So let’s get flawed assumption number two, “Chatbots will always be text”.**

**ES:** My point there was that, okay, chatbots now, they produce principally text responses but you can imagine that — I mean the models exist now to do diffusion based images, which I use ChatGPT for all the time.

**By the way, the delay in getting an image would be a great time to show me an ad.**

**ES:** It would, it would absolutely. I mean I think those are going to get a lot faster and more responsive, but I think that’s a great ad-

**Hey, they could have had three years of revenue while waiting for these stupid images to generate that they’ve foregone, so get a move on!**

**ES:** Yeah, exactly. But you could think about a video response being the optimal response and the example I gave is, “How to fix a flat tire?”, well is the best format of the response like a number of steps written out in text or is it just a video of someone changing a flat tire? And you can imagine you could personalize that to whatever the tire was.

**I personally like the text, but I am the weirdo here, everything is a YouTube video these days. Drives me up the wall, but anyhow. Continue.**

**ES:** Pick whatever situation but where you actually need to show the physics of it, I think it would make sense to have a video. And well, if you’ve got video content, well then you’re invoking pre-roll ads, mid-roll ads that probably are very well suited.

**Which by the way, all these sorts of ads, everyone’s listening to this and feels horrified — then you pay! This is the whole thing, and then you can balance the ARPU. The price of the subscription is the whatever you’re not earning in terms of advertising, that’s like where YouTube is with YouTube Premium. If you want to know how much Google’s making on YouTube advertising, see how much you’re paying on YouTube Premium, it’s probably about the same.**

**ES:** I just think this is a canonical case for why ads are so positive sum, right? If you’re already paying, you don’t have to worry about this and then if you’re not paying, you get access to a higher quality experience.

**Imagine if you had watch limits on YouTube because you weren’t on the right level or whatever it might be.**

**ES:** Or you got a less relevant video or something, people would reject that out of hand. But this idea that it’s okay to do it because there’s subscription tiers and that’s sort of more egalitarian versus just ads, it’s ridiculous.

So the point is the ad can match the format of the content that serves you and if you’ve got video, you’re talking high engagement, you’re talking video ads, that’s a high CPM ad placement and so it’s not just text and therefore an affiliate card, but you could have a video which is more linear, and so you could start ads pre-roll, you could start it mid-roll and then you’d be competing for advertisers with YouTube.

**Yeah, I think you made this point that the affiliate model, we’ve done the affiliate model thing before, that was really a vestige of the time when the Internet was text back then and we’ve moved past that.**

**ES:** It’s a relic. And then we could talk about the actual arguments that I make later when we move past the assumptions, but that tends to favor low cost, high conversion products and so then you’ve just got your ads are going to be dominated by—

**Casper Mattresses and whatever it might be.**

**ES:** Well, no, it’d be Temu-type stuff, it’d be Shein-type stuff because the Casper Mattresses are high price point, very low conversion probability. And so my point later on, maybe we’ll get to that, but as an advertiser, I can defeat the gravity of the conversion value by having a bid, by introducing a bid, by telling you how valuable this is to me and that’s why the ad system with the bid maximizes the total value dispersion.

**Yep. Okay, we’re going to get to that, but let’s get to the last flawed assumption, “Chatbot commerce review data will stay static”, what do you mean there?**

**ES:** So there the point was, okay, well the chatbot is good at going out and synthesizing reviews for any number of niche products and so that’s why it’s really valuable, and that’s why the affiliate model makes sense there because it can then sell that to me, it’ll find the best possible product and it’ll also have an affiliate card there for that product and it can convert me there, so it’s informing me while also monetizing me.

But the thing is, at some point you’re eating a seed corn here, you’re synthesizing all of the review content downstream that is also trying to monetize with affiliate ads, if you’re starving them from of that revenue, then they can’t monetize, you’re not going to write reviews anymore.

**Yep. And to the extent they write them, they’re writing them using you and then you have[a real ouroboros](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ouroboros>).**

**ES:** Or what would happen I think is then you get them to aggregate and then they put up paywall and then instead of Bob the Camera Aficionado who in his spare time writes reviews of DSLR cameras, he’s joining the camera review magazine and they’ve got a paywall and the chatbot can’t access it anyway.

**Cut off anyway. Well, that leads to the three arguments for personalized advertising, which we’ve touched on them a little bit, but number one, “Personalized advertising maximizes the value of user attention”.**

**ES:** Yeah. We just kind of talked about it, but the ability for the advertiser to produce a bid. So the modern ad platforms, for the most part, and I need to caveat this because the economists got mad at me in this article, but there’s a number of assumptions baked into this and it’s not always true.

**Caveats registered.**

**ES:** Yeah, caveats registered. What these modern ad platforms do is they incentivize an advertiser to bid with the true value of the thing.

**They’re basically tell you what this customer is worth to me because that’s what they’re willing to pay.**

**ES:** Exactly, and then that goes into the calculus of how the ads are ranked because that gets applied to the conversion probability to produce an expected value. But because it’s the bid and not just the price doing that, then you’re maximizing the total value that is transacted.

**Is that right there — did we just articulate what is the key thing that AppLovin is doing?**

**ES:** Well, I would say any ad platform, any scale of modern ad platform, not just AppLovin. I’d say Meta does that.

**Oh, for sure. I just meant relative to why they just took off the way they did is really matching and understanding the sort of bid and what that said about the particular advertiser and getting signal based on that.**

**ES:** Yeah. That’s been my hypothesis since the [MoPub acquisition](<https://investors.applovin.com/news/news-details/2022/AppLovin-Closes-Acquisition-of-Twitters-MoPub-Business/default.aspx>) was that they were using the sell-side product as a proxy for doing price discovery, and then that way they could monetize the longer tail of products where there was no visibility into what the actual value being derived from those ads was because so few people bought them, because they were low DAU games. And so in understanding what those low DAU games themselves were bidding for traffic, you understand how much users worth in that low DAU game, so you know what to bid into that low DAU game. And by the way, since there’s very little competition for those impressions because other people don’t have access to that data, you get to capture a lot of that margin, you bid just enough to win it, but you keep everything else.

**You keep the liquidity and then you own it all on top of that. Yeah, all right. Well, number two, “Affiliate links only monetize queries with a commercial focus”.**

**ES:** Yeah, that’d be an obvious one and Google has talked about this. They talk about, “We only monetize a minority of the queries because we can only monetize a query if it has some sort of commercial intent behind it”, and so if you’re using ads, you get to monetize everything because it’s every single engagement. If you’re just using affiliate links, you can only monetize the ones that are like, “What’s the DSLR camera?”.

**You are giving up the advantage of computation because you’re asking retailers to be smart enough to bid on all the right things. And it’s like, “Wait, I thought you were the AI company, shouldn’t you be able to figure this out on your own?”.**

**ES:** Right, exactly. But you can make the argument that, well, if they monetize it at a higher rate, it might net out to be better. But my whole point from the first bid is you can’t monetize it at the higher rate and without having the bid as the proxy for value, what you’re very likely to do is to prioritize the low price point high conversion probability products. So not only are you reducing the actual take there because it’s probably a fixed fee, the affiliate commission, it’s probably just a fixed fee, but you’re also kind of flooding your product with ads for low quality products, which could then ultimately have retention repercussions.

**Right. That sort of leads to number three to go sort of full circle, “Affiliate model engineers incentive misalignment between chatbots and retail platforms”.**

**ES:** Yeah, and there it’s really just Amazon, when you’re talking about retail platforms, I’m talking about Amazon. And so why would Amazon want to participate? My point was they probably will generate some incremental conversions, does Amazon really need that? Because what Amazon does is it monetizes — it uses the whole buffalo, it monetizes everything, it monetizes attention with ads $60 billion a year, $57 billion in 2024, it’s got its third party DSP [Demand-Side Platform] now that’s monetizing other people’s websites, so it’s not even its own service area. It’s monetizing third party apps and website service areas, and it needs that data to do that and it only gets that data as a function of that first party relationship and so if it gives that away in exchange for just some incremental conversions, is that really worth it or are they losing value in that partnership?

**So what do you think about the idea about LLMs being the inverse of Meta ads? Where Meta ads help niche merchants find customers, in this case,[helping niche customers find merchants](<https://stratechery.com/2025/openai-instant-checkout-ai-and-long-tail-e-commerce-is-ai-different/>)? **Which is so dependent on them owning the relationship and what’s happening on their site.**

**ES:** I think that’s fair. I think I’d make another point in favor them not embracing it, which is that they will embrace it, they’re just going to embrace it with their own proprietary tools. [Rufus](<https://www.amazon.com/gp/help/customer/display.html?nodeId=Tvh55TTsQ5XQSFc7Pr>), Amazon said it’s $10 billion of expected incremental sales in 2025, that’s a lot of money. And so I think if they could embrace it, they could just embrace it on platform and have it generate the same benefits for them. It’s just not some third-party agent that’s doing it, it’s their own agent.

**So you don’t think there will be a Amazon-ChatGPT deal, even with Walmart jumping in, it’s not going to force their hand?**

**ES:** It’s very different profiles of companies. I mean, Amazon is all e-commerce, Walmart’s percentage of e-commerce is a small minority. Walmart did $4.4 billion in advertising in 2024, the only reason the advertising revenue numbers on a yearly basis, $4.4 billion in advertising revenue in 2024 against $15.5 billion net income, Amazon did $56 billion in advertising on $59 billion of net income.

**But I mean, the Walmart e-commerce deal now is really good, they’ve finally figured out the local thing. You order something, if you want something right away, you’re actually more likely, even with Amazon really nailing next day or whatever it might be, Walmart is next hour and just go on there. Actually, this is a perfect example. There’s a possibility that Walmart, on ChatGPT, is going to introduce this actually pretty fantastic e-commerce platform with real meaningful advantages in some areas to a whole cohort of people that never would’ve thought to try Walmart otherwise.**

**ES:** It’s true, but I think that also highlights just how much more important incremental transactions are to Walmart relative to Amazon. Amazon doesn’t need them, Walmart does.

**But they don’t want to lose strength and they don’t want to lose customers though.**

**ES:** Yeah, fair. But I mean, I think they’ve got a stranglehold, Amazon is a diversified business, you’ve got the subscription with Prime and you’ve got all these other touch points for on video. So I think my sense is the value of that direct customer relationships supersedes anything else and actually, if you would cannibalize that by embracing a partnership with OpenAI, you’d be destroying a lot more value than you’d be generating and maybe that’s a challenging place to be because maybe they just can’t stop. They can’t stop the consumer embrace of chatbots and they’re probably not going to be able to offer that much of a compelling chatbot experience in the Amazon because it doesn’t make sense.

**Once you’re on Amazon, you know what you’re doing.**

**ES:** Right, you know what you’re doing. Or you could use Rufus for discovery stuff, but you’re not going there to do product research maybe. So there could be an argument here that they’re in a very dominant position, but that position would require some sort of wholesale reinvention of the customer relationship to keep the chatbot threat at bay. My sense is if I was just going to bet, that they probably can overcome that, but I think you could probably easily make the argument against that. Like, no, they can’t because this is going to be a transformative social shift.

**Right, I agree with that, I think that makes sense.**

#### Netflix Advertising

**You did write last summer, and I was really annoyed I didn’t write this at the time, I didn’t really pick up on until this quarter, “[Netflix’s advertising pivot is complete](<https://mobiledevmemo.com/netflixs-advertising-pivot-is-complete/>).” What was that pivot? Tell me more.**

**ES:** Well, they pivoted to their own proprietary ad tech stack, they’ve got their own ad tech. It’s live now across all their ad geos.

**And why was that so important?**

**ES:** Because they were never going to be able to build a modern, functional, high performance ads platform if they were relying on a third party ad server. They were basically doing this as a means of spinning up a brand advertising business very quickly, which they did. So to their credit, they did that. But I think the reality is, if you want to compete for the actual prize of the actual crown of the digital advertising marketplace is SMB direct response ad budgets. That’s the scalable piece, that’s the substantial piece, and if you want to compete for that, you have to have your own ad platform, you have to be integrating with your own measurement, you have to be building proprietary ad formats, and you have to be providing targeting that’s actually actionable, and so I think they were always going to have to do this, and they’ve done it, they’ve instituted it and so I think that was the pivot. It was more like an infrastructure pivot, but they’ve done that, but I think it was also like a wholesale mentality pivot, which they did manage to complete.

**This is where I mentioned we would tie this in before, so let me sort of complete the bow here. What Netflix did is they suddenly completely — arguably, they were late to decide to do advertising, but once they decided, now today it’s been a couple years and they’re where they should be — but they didn’t just sit on their hands and say, “Yeah, we’re going to figure it out in the long run”, they got something out there and that’s something out there, which you pointed out all along, was not going to monetize as well as they said it was, it was probably going to diminish ARPU, not be neutral, and I think there was still benefit all along in that it let them increase prices I think on the paid tiers more than they might’ve otherwise. It obviously drew new users.**

**But the big thing is they launched, they launched within months of saying they were going to do it, and now they’re in a great spot right now and I think that they have other issues in terms of engagement and things along those lines just like anyone else but this is going to be a huge tailwind, I think for a long time going forward. And it’s downstream of just launching it and launching the wrong product and then forcing yourself to iterate and get it better. Real object lesson there for a company we were discussing earlier.**

**ES:** Yeah, but I think they’ve scaled it too. So November 6th, they published this blog post where they reported this new metric that they invented called [Monthly Active Viewers](<https://x.com/eric_seufert/status/1986541667617296690?s=20>), where they try to estimate given an account like an average household size, how many people are actually watching the ads. They came up with 190 million, my own personal model for the ad-supported MAU-growth gets into 116 million, that’s a lot. And they talked about the growth in this earnings, they’re seeing strong growth in ads revenue.

**That was the big thing this summer, I think that was the first earnings call, or it might’ve been in the spring where before they were talking about ads, “Yeah, it’s there, it’s immaterial”, now, it was a material contributor to their forecasts, and that’s a big important shift.**

**ES:** They more than doubled their ads revenue. That’s from a small base, and those numbers got leaked in the Wall Street Journal from their strategy meeting, they said it’d be like $9 billion in revenue by 2030. Okay, that’s not going to change the world, but that’s a pretty meaningful amount of money that’s basically all profit. So I think the pivot is complete, and I think they’re executing against the strategy, and I think the strategy is the right one now.

**ES:** We can talk about it, yeah, we could finally talk about ChatGPT ads.
