Good morning,

Today’s Stratechery Interview is with [Robert Fishman](<https://www.linkedin.com/in/robert-fishman-92b986191/>). Fishman is a senior research analyst at [MoffettNathanson](<https://www.moffettnathanson.com/>) focused on the media, entertainment, and sports industries.

Fishman has been covering Hollywood for two decades, and I wanted to do a survey of all of the major players in the wake of Paramount’s purchase of Warner Bros. In this interview we discuss how Netflix impacted everyone, why the company tried to buy Warner Bros., and what Paramount’s plan actually is. We then discuss YouTube, and why they are so hard to compete with, Disney’s prospects under a new CEO, including the question of whether or not ESPN should be spun out, and why sports is both essential and also increasingly difficult for customers to navigate.

**Yep.**

**RF:** But my point is that there has been opportunities that investors that have stayed closer to media have been really able to identify and get some really nice returns on but clearly relative to tech stocks and the tech space, it is a different game that they’re playing. **Just looking backwards, you mentioned the red envelope and how Netflix transformed the industry, as you look back over the last decade or 10-to-12 years or whatever it might be, to what extent did Netflix force these companies to change versus they thought they were forced to change and actually they maybe didn’t need to or didn’t need to do it so aggressively and actually shot themselves in the foot?**

**RF:** I think that goes hand-in-hand with your last question, because I think that all of the media companies saw the success that Netflix was having, both from an operational standpoint and the business that they have created today. **If you could get a time machine and go back 12 years and you were writing the report, how should you respond to Netflix? What would you say with the benefit of hindsight?**

**RF:** We think that the biggest mistake that the media companies made was throwing all of their valuable content over the top and and frankly we’ve written this in the past — basically we called them the cheaters of the ecosystem, and so when you think about how you could give away NFL and all of the other premium sports content at a significant discount to what they were charging to their pay TV distributor partners, that just led to the natural disruption that happened because customers could get it for a couple of dollars a month on a discounted price, whereas you were still paying $100+ dollars to your pay TV distributors. So I’d say that was the biggest mistake looking backwards is giving away all the content almost for free and that premium content and how important that is for the entire pay TV ecosystem still sitting here today, looking at where NFL is, is looking to negotiate for their next deal, all of that comes back to the pay TV dollars and where media has made their money over the many years.

**Do you think if you went back and told them what happened and this is why you should keep all your content, or you should keep premium content on pay TV, not let people sort of desert, would they listen? Or is this a situation where because it’s so easy to desert, actually, in sort of game theory terms, it’s not a stable equilibrium. People are going to desert and we’re going to end up exactly where we were. Yes, it’s easy to look back and say you should have done differently, but the dynamics were such that once you could desert, you would and that’s exactly what happened.**

**RF:** I think they all knew where we needed to get to, right, to your point. How we would have gotten there and whether we could have had a slower decline on the pay TV ecosystem, probably would have been the case.

**But would it have happened? That’s kind of my question, right? Because you look backwards and it’s like, “Yeah, this is really stupid, we were pursuing Netflix and actually who killed the bundle? It was us, we put everything over the top, there was no reason to run out of the bundle outside of sports”. If anything,[Disney did more to hold it together](<https://stratechery.com/2023/the-rise-and-fall-of-espns-leverage/>) for as long as possible than anyone else, particularly with ESPN, but I guess it’s almost like a human nature question. I have this question, can you even make bundles anymore on the Internet when it – like bundles are so good for everyone, but maybe you actually need a forcing function like [people in rural towns can’t get TV](<https://stratechery.com/2022/cables-last-laugh/>), so they’re going to work together and have a cable system. Maybe you actually need a forcing function because if anyone can desert from a bundle, they will, even if in theory it’s good for them in the long run.**

**RF:** Yeah, I do think it’s inevitable that streaming, as soon as Netflix took over and showed consumers and subscribers how much better that experience was, naturally there would have been that disruption, right? So you can’t just put your blinders on it.

**Right but you can imagine a world where Netflix stays there and there and actually it’s just a new window so you have all these studios that they keep their premium stuff on pay tv and Netflix is the home of reruns and so maybe you lose your edge cable channels that lived on reruns but Netflix is paying top dollar for it and meanwhile original content is still all you have to pay that $100 a month.**

**RF:** That’s right. So if you want to go back further and call out the other mistake, you start with licensing all of your content to Netflix and creating Netflix unto itself, whereas you could have created that for your own service. And listen, they have this company called Hulu, which was a joint venture, and they had the opportunity to create a true Netflix competitor.

**They just hated each other too much to actually make it work.**

**RF:** There’s personalities involved in media that you know very well that lead to maybe not the best business decision and how Hulu could have evolved into a true Netflix competitor from a global standpoint. Clearly, they already compete very much so on the US front, especially thinking about the advertising opportunity there. But yes, I think there’s lots of mistakes that we could have looked back on. But with full 20/20 hindsight, it’s easy for us sitting here today.

**Yeah for sure, it probably happened as it was meant to happen.**

#### Netflix and Warner Bros.

**So you’ve used the word “we” multiple times in this interview, you have taken over coverage of a lot of these companies from Michael Nathanson, who you mentioned has been on this podcast many times. **Yep, Michael’s going to be reading this, so this is your chance.**

But taking full ownership of the Netflix coverage gave me the opportunity to really take a step back and understand investors think about this company not just over the next two to three years, but really for the long term and what the opportunity is to grow subscribers when they were giving us that metric on a regular basis. But how the company is able to monetize the meaningful global platform that they have today really just allowed me to take this step back. So we did this report at the time, really talking about how streaming scale matters and how Netflix is clearly in a world of its own. No groundbreaking conclusion there.

**No, it’s okay. Michael has[already admitted the opposite side of this](<https://stratechery.com/2023/an-interview-with-michael-nathanson-about-netflix-and-the-media-industry/>), so you’re in safe territory. Don’t worry.**

**RF:** What it allowed us to do is really understand at the current levels for Netflix, how much opportunity they have in terms of monetizing the existing engagement and content that they have today, what we showed is sitting here today using the Nielsen numbers and the engagement levels, apples-to-apples compared to all the other streaming services today, Netflix is basically half of the monetization on a revenue-per-hour basis compared to most of the other US streaming services today. So when we thought about the advertising opportunity, we can dig into that, when we thought about what the ad tier did for this unlock of additional subscribers coming on to their service and how they can increase their price on the premium side but capture them on the lower tiered prices, that just allowed us to think a little bit differently for Netflix.

**You’ve talked about it, I thought it was very compelling this point that you mentioned about how Netflix’s[revenue generated per hour is the lowest in the industry](<https://stratechery.com/2025/encryption-and-the-uneasy-compromise-netflix-earnings-the-aggregators-compounding-advantage/>) and I guess it’s easy to lose sight of that, because you just think about the streaming services and you sort of think about them, “Oh here’s streaming service 1, 2, 3, 4, 5”, and while their prices should probably be approximately the same and everyone indexed their price to Netflix’s price — do consumers do that? Could Netflix, do you think in the long run, actually monetize at a price commensurate with how much they are being watched, which to your point could mean like double the price of what they charge today?**

**RF:** I think Netflix definitely has the opportunity to push pricing higher, I’m not sitting here today saying that they’re going to double their price anytime soon, but the way that the company communicates around the value that they provide to their subscribers as the key metric in terms of figuring out where that pricing algorithm should go makes a lot of sense to us. And when you think about how they can add to that value, to us at the top of the list is going to continue to be sports and that takes us back to the NFL potential negotiations coming over the next few months.

But really the overall sports strategy, I know Netflix is very vocal about talking about eventizing their sports content that they have today, we just think that leaning into sports is a way to really accelerate the advertising side of this monetization story. Let’s take a page out of the traditional media playbook — one of the key reasons why all of the media partners want and need to stay in business with the NFL is because it allows them to really share that amazing IP with their broader portfolio of advertising and sell their advertising portfolio to Madison Avenue and really help sell other inventory that might not be as valuable. So it really will allow Netflix to disrupt the traditional media ecosystem on a whole different level going forward.

**Right. The bundling that we’re talking about is bundling NFL advertising with advertising of random show XYZ, you’re going to buy it all, you’re not going to buy just one or the other.**

**RF:** That’s right. And so what the NFL allows you to do is it would allow Netflix to take their advertising story, which is growing quickly, but still pretty nascent, and really kind of throw the accelerator on that.

**I’m going to come back to the advertising and the sports bit, I think particularly in the context of Disney. But with Netflix,[why do you think they tried to buy Warner Bros.](<https://stratechery.com/2025/netflix-and-the-hollywood-end-game/>)?**

**RF:** So there’s a lot of different opinions on this one.

**There’s a lot of different opinions there’s a lot of what Netflix has said or not said, I want the Robert Fishman thesis.**

**RF:** We’ve been pretty consistent on this and actually felt that Paramount was the one that was most likely to end up with Warner Bros. asset. The reason why we felt that way is because really taking what Netflix said and applying that to Paramount, this was more of a must-have for Paramount, and more of a nice-to-have for the Netflix side.

So let’s go to Netflix first. On the Netflix side, if they were viewing this as they were communicating as a massive content licensing deal, that’s how they were valuing this company. And for Netflix to own Warner Bros. and own DC Comics and own Harry Potter, that would have been an amazing accelerator for their company when you think about over the next 5, 10 years. That does not mean to us that this was something that they needed to have in order to grow their company as fast as we’re projecting and the lead that they already have on all of these other competitors is still tremendous.

So when you think about where Netflix stood and how they made that decision, I think they were surprised in the opportunity once they started to jump in and do the analysis, how they could make an acquisition for Warner Bros. Discovery work for them. But they were also very disciplined in understanding what the value of that content was because they would know as best as anyone out there what the value to their own platform is because they license a lot of the great content already from HBO and Warner Bros., so I think they were very clear in how much they wanted to pay and did not want to go into a bidding war with Paramount over it.

**Were you still surprised that they had no counter-offer[once Paramount surpassed their bid](<https://stratechery.com/2026/technological-scale-and-government-control-paramount-outbids-netflix-for-warner-bros/>)?**

**RF:** We were surprised, the work we did at the time suggested that they could go higher. There’s a lot of different variables and inputs that from the outside we have to make assumptions, but we thought that they could go as much to $30. And again, their bid was not apples-to-apples with Paramount, because Paramount was buying the whole company.

**Right, because they were going to spin off the cable networks.**

**RF:** So that was another part of this debate is how much global networks would be worth on a standalone basis.

**Do you think Netflix was relieved to be outbid?**

**RF:** I think that coming back to your question, there was a part of Netflix that clearly started to do the work here in order to make sure that Paramount would not walk away with this on the cheap and part of what they did was actually drive up the price for Paramount.

**Do you think that was on purpose?**

**RF:** I think clearly that played into some of the decision-making factor, yes.

**Interesting.**

#### Paramount’s Plan

**Well, let’s talk about the deal that did happen. Paramount and Warner Bros. Discovery. Are we doing ourselves a disservice by, as analysts, trying to evaluate this rationally? Is anything about David Ellison and Paramount rational?**

**RF:** I would say what when we think about this deal sitting here today there are pieces to this that have to be factored in and the external piece is, and through this whole process has been, has this been the plan all along for Skydance? Even before they bought Paramount — why were they just buying Paramount? I think it’s pretty clear at this point that Warner Bros. was part of the plan all along and they clearly tried to buy it at a much lower price and credit to Warner Bros. and the management team there for bringing a Netflix in so that they could drive the price up higher for their shareholders.

So when you think about from Paramount’s side to your question, there is the business logic of it, and then there’s the other pieces that come into play in terms of owning a studio and the benefits that come there. There’s the long-term entrepreneurial mindset that the Ellison family and investors bring that clearly is very different than anything we’ve seen in terms of who has been the owners of Warner Bros. over the many different iterations.

**How so? How might this be different this time?**

**RF:** I think if you are a long-term investor, which the Ellison family is sitting here today, and have the opportunity to see how this plays out, not just over the next couple quarters after the deal closes, which is what most of the management teams had to deal with owning Warner Bros. Discovery or owning Warner Bros. in the past, even going back to AT&T and all the other different iterations.

**Let’s go back to AOL, let’s go back a long ways.**

**RF:** We can go all the way back. But as part of being a public company, which again, Paramount is today, we can’t ignore that part, but still controlled by the Ellison family and the investors alongside it, they have the opportunity to think about this, not just for the next quarter or two or year or two, but really invest in this company from the next five plus years. And that allows, from their angle, whether it’s rational to Wall Street sitting here today and how we would value the company over the next couple of years, versus if they’re in this for the endgame and their incentives are aligned for the long-term investors, that allows their decision-making process to be very different than how even the current management team has invested behind it.

**What would look different? What would look different with that sort of different mindset than they might have had otherwise?**

**RF:** So what the limitations of what happened with Warner Bros. Discovery was the debt, right?

**(laughing) Which, by the way, there’s going to be a lot of debt.**

**RF:** There’s a lot of debt coming as part of this deal too. The company has said it will be over six, six-and-a-half times from the get-go. So when you think about how debt limits your flexibility to invest in content, what the backing that the Ellison family brings potentially, and this is the part that investors will have a hard time with, but how do you value the company based on what you know today versus having a backstop from the Ellison family where if debt is not coming down as quickly as possible, is there an opportunity to recapitalize the company? Or really allow debt not to be the limiting factor when thinking about how to invest in content going forward?

So far that the messaging initially from Ellison and Paramount Skydance is that they have the ability to lean into the content spending despite the leverage situation that they will be in. I understand there’s a lot of skepticism around that, our work suggests that the profits are not going to be as high as they’re projecting on their own. But at the same time, if they can pull off that balance between investing behind the content while getting cost efficiencies out of the combined portfolio from the linear assets and even putting two streaming services together, there is a path there where you can see a better long-term outcome than I think most investors understandably are skeptical about.

**Isn’t the best example of what you’re talking about would be what they do with licensing? And what I mean is in the short-to-medium term, the best way to make money on their existing content would be to license it to Netflix. But by the same token, if the best way to build up their streaming services and to get larger scale to theoretically make more money in the long run would be to keep content exclusive. Is that an example of what you’re talking about where your hope is that with a more long-range viewpoint, they will do less licensing? Or is this where the financials are just going to rule the day?**

**RF:** So when we think about the licensing opportunity for Paramount Skydance, I think looking at Netflix as the example clearly is the best place where you start. Netflix has had a tremendous amount of success because of all of the original content on their platform that’s exclusive. That’s very clear. But also, they’ve licensed a lot of content from Paramount, from Warner Bros., and all of the other studios, not just in the US, but globally.

When you think about what Paramount is going to be likely forced to do because of that leverage, we would expect them to very much license their content to Netflix and the other streamers. But what I think they have to make a more difficult decision is where that line is, where when you look at Warner Bros. Discovery, they probably crossed that line by licensing a “Young” Sheldon” to Netflix or some of the HBO content. So how you draw that line will determine how much value you can get out of your own library while also trying to monetize it in different windows. So clearly this is a super complicated question in terms of how Paramount will approach it based on that balance of leverage and how they want to drive the growth on the streaming side.

**I guess it all is going to come back to the rationality question because at what point do you — like how far, how large do they need to get to where it now is, to your point, as long as they’re carrying this huge debt load, the “rational” thing to do is going to be the licensed content. But as you noted, if they keep licensing content, they’re just not going to be growing, their streaming service is going to be a, “I’ll subscribe for a month or two, catch some shows that I missed”, and then it’s not a necessity to have have all the time. I guess it just feels like to me this is like, “Ok, Larry, how much money? When are you going to say no? When are you going to hit the stop button?””**

**RF:** One thing that you just brought up on the streaming side is I think the hope when Warner Bros. and Discovery combined—

**And by the way I will say I liked that combination, and[when I wrote that I liked it](<https://stratechery.com/2022/warner-bros-discovery/>) at the time, it might have been Michael, he was like, “You’ve got to look at this debt load, this is way more of an issue than you’re appreciating”, and I took that to heart, got that one wrong, I didn’t properly appreciate the limitations that it would put on the business. This just feels like a replay.**

**RF:** Why I think this is a little different than just, “You’re running it back again”, is because the lesson learned from the Discovery Warner Bros. content and all the different iterations of Max and HBO Max and back and forth was that the Discovery standalone content — that was HGTV and Food Network and all of the cable networks that come with that — that was not must-have content that would drive additional subscribers to the HBO content. Where I do think this is different, and to give Paramount some credit, having that sports portfolio and the other content that they’ve built out around it with the Taylor Sheridan universe, that has created Paramount Plus to what it is today.

I do think the combination of having an NFL with HBO gives the opportunity to broaden out the subscriber base more so than what just Discovery standalone did. And then back to the scale point, what this really does is help allow the company to think more internationally as thinking about the benefits of putting these two streaming platforms together. Because back to Netflix, their success of getting over 230 million international subscribers outside of the US, that is the game changer that they created that all these other media companies are trying to replicate, even going to Disney, and the upside that we see there.

**How much of a loss then was it that,[Taylor Sheridan is going to NBCUniversal](<https://sharptext.net/2025/why-taylor-sheridan-is-worth-a-billion-dollars/>), I guess there’s a separate question of like what is NBCUniversal doing here? Do they need to be a part of this as well or is that how they get Taylor Sheridan back?**

**RF:** I think for Peacock, they’re clearly left on the sidelines after this consolidation and they have articulated a strategy focusing on domestic-only at this point. I think there’s real questions around what the long-term horizon looks like as a standalone US platform and whether that’s enough to get scale. I think they very much leaned into the sports strategy, which we commend, but at the same time, the way to make money in streaming over the long-term is that international piece to get that global scale and that seems to be something that is still lacking for them. So are they going to be forced to do something different over the next couple of years? We’ll have to wait and see. But I do think a standalone U.S.-only subscription service will have a more difficult time really scaling on the profits.

**How does Paramount lose Taylor Sheridan then? It seems like, yeah, he signed a billion dollar deal. That seems a lot cheaper than Warner Bros. Discovery.**

**RF:** I think they’re probably pretty happy that they ended up with Taylor, but if that’s the consolation prize.

**But Paramount. It seems like Taylor Sheridan was Paramount to a certain extent, what are they without him?**

**RF:** Well, I do think that sports is really the key going forward and that’s why this NFL negotiation is so critical to all of these streaming platforms.

**Right, but the NFL knows that, right? So it’s really just the NFL is going to — the NFL famously, there are deals throughout history, the networks don’t make money on the NFL, it’s sort of around it. Is that sort of the thing that they’re still going to be going for here?**

**RF:** For the traditional media partners, they know that they need the NFL to continue. I think we expect them to pay more for less quantity and less quality games as part of their new packages, because the NFL is probably the smartest media owner out there and they have the ability to slice and dice their packages so that we do expect Netflix, and we haven’t even talked about YouTube yet, as part of this media landscape that Michael has just declared them the king of all media. So when we think about who Netflix is even competing against going forward and all of the traditional media players, we would put YouTube within that conversation as well.

**Do you think they’re talking about spending, Paramount, like $33 billion dollars on content or something like that which is like nearly double Netflix — is that just a function of sports? Do you think that’s realistic? It seems awfully high to me.**

**RF:** I think that the benefit of owning Warner Bros. Discovery and why we think it was the plan all along was because if you’re standalone Paramount and you’re looking at this NFL negotiation, you need to reallocate a lot of your budget. But your budget probably needs to be much, much higher to help in that reallocation, especially when you want to leave a significant piece of that to towards streaming. What this deal does is bring in all of the linear spending that Warner Bros. was doing so that they can reallocate that towards NFL and other kind of premium content going forward.

#### YouTube
