Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery Interview is with [Matthew Ball](<https://x.com/ballmatthew>). Ball is the Managing Partner of Epyllion, an investment, investment advisory, and strategic advisory firm, as well as a Senior Advisor to KKR, Venture Partner at Makers Fund, Co-Founder of the Roundhill Ball Metaverse ETF, and an Occasional Contributor to The Economist. Ball’s 2022 book, The Metaverse, was an international bestseller. Ball was previously the global Head of Strategy for Amazon Studios, a Senior Advisor to McKinsey, Director at The Chernin Group’s Otter Media, and an Executive at Accenture. His writing is on [his eponymous website](<https://www.matthewball.co/>).

Ball is a regular Stratechery Interview subject: we previously spoke in [March 2020](<https://stratechery.com/2020/an-interview-with-matthew-ball-about-bob-iger-and-disney/>) and [February 2023](<https://stratechery.com/2023/an-interview-with-matthew-ball-about-disney-streaming-and-the-metaverse/>) about the entertainment industry, [August 2021](<https://stratechery.com/2021/facebooks-metaverse-opportunity-an-interview-with-matthew-ball-about-the-multiverse/>) and [June 2022](<https://stratechery.com/2022/an-interview-with-the-metaverse-author-matthew-ball/>) about the Metaverse, and then, in [February 2024](<https://stratechery.com/2024/an-interview-with-matthew-ball-about-the-vision-pro-and-the-state-of-gaming/>) and [January 2025](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-matthew-ball-about-the-gaming-slump/>) about the state of the video game industry.

Today’s interview continues on that latest theme. Ball just released a 164-slide presentation about [The State of Video Gaming in 2026](<https://www.matthewball.co/all/presentation-the-state-of-video-gaming-in-2026>), and what I appreciate about these presentations is that they are not simple year-over-year updates: each edition has a different focus, so that it reads more like a new chapter in a book than like a revised edition.

This year the big focus is on the fight for attention — a fight that video games are losing to a whole host of alternatives, particularly amongst young men. We discuss where video game growth is actually coming from, how consoles are harvesting their position as Aggregators but failing to grow the market, and why Roblox is crushing everyone. We then get into price discovery for app store fees, the impact of rising memory prices, why EA went private, the impact of AI, and whether the Metaverse is still — or ever was — a thing.

#### Video Games and the Fight for Attention

**Matthew Ball, welcome back to Stratechery.**

**MB:** Glad to be here.

**So, we’re here on the occasion, as we have been every year for the last few years, the annual release of your[State of Gaming](<https://www.matthewball.co/all/presentation-the-state-of-video-gaming-in-2026>) presentation. We’ll have a link in the show notes, but stepping back, before we get to the presentation itself, what’s your motivation for doing this every year? Just so I guess in general for you, what’s your focus on these days and how does this presentation fit into that?**

**MB:** It is usually about trying to answer some question that is surfaced [in last year’s report](<https://www.matthewball.co/all/stateofvideogaming2025>) and/or the dialogue that surrounds it. I actually only started three years ago, I didn’t anticipate doing it on an annual basis, but three years ago is when the industry first started to feel like there was something strange. Yes, there was a COVID pullback, but even books grew post pandemic and gaming contracted. Digital video grew, gaming contracted. Music, in its totality, grew.

So [the original report released in January of 2024](<https://www.matthewball.co/all/gaming2024>) basically said, “Are the common explanations…” — which at that point had really started to border on excuses, “sufficient to explain what’s going on”. We had, well, IDFA wreaked havoc on mobile gaming, the console and GPU shortage of early in the pandemic led to delayed adoption of next generation consoles and gaming, the hybrid workforce disruption meant that the 2021 and 2022 content release slates were weaker than anticipated, but I was making the argument in 2023, “Yes, those matter, but they didn’t explain it all”.

In 2025, in January, [the purpose of that report](<https://www.matthewball.co/all/stateofvideogaming2025>) was to say, “What else is going on? If we talk about what isn’t to blame, let’s take a look at the other structural forces, the rise of China, cost changes, the concentration of power, Metcalfe’s Law and UGC dominating gaming and how that was changing growth opportunities”.

This year’s report is different. It basically answers two different questions, “Why isn’t there more growth?”, as opposed to explaining common explanations for the lack of it, and then, “Where do we see growth and what does that mean for market participants?”. There’s commonalities, but I’m not very interested in just adding n+1 years to old trends, it’s a question of, “What new can we explore?”.

**I’m going to put[a link to our interview last year](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-matthew-ball-about-the-gaming-slump/>) because I thought it was actually pretty striking. You put out this huge report and I thought there was actually very little commonality with what you did last year or maybe the year before. They’re sort of running together in my mind a little bit, but I remember one of my favorite insights was understanding the nature of games that users keep playing for years and there’s just less room for other games, and you didn’t really talk about that much at all. Am I right that you have to read these presentations, this is the third chapter of a book, it’s not the update of what came before?**

**MB:** It is certainly designed to stand alone and I think the themes are largely distinct. There’s some commonalities, but I’ll give you one way of thinking about it. I think one of the most controversial to hear but uncontroversial, when you observe the points about the gaming industry, that I reveal in January, is that gaming has kind of been a definitive loser in the attention economy over the last half decade.

**Yep.**

**MB:** Crazy.

**We sort of talked on that a bit last year, but that felt like that was the thing this year. It’s like, “Look, the ultimate market is not video games, it’s time and attention and video games are losing”.**

**MB:** Yes, and I think it was easy to take a look at gaming at the end of the last decade and say, “It’s riding Moore’s Law more than any other category, there’s tens of millions being onboarded annually, the medium is changing”. But, in this last five years, those interactive tailwinds have gone to other products.

Now, where it intersects with the point that you just made is, if indeed video gaming has been losing, at least in the mature markets, I think it’s different in the less-mature markets, then the aforementioned problems, the focal point of last year’s report, which you just highlighted, which is the concentration of time on extant games, those two merge. It is hard for new entrants to break in, in a market where engagement is weakening, player growth is non-existent to shrinking, and overall time for customers has withered.

**This is one of my last questions, I was going to use it to tie this all together, but you’re sort of leading right into it. It occurs to me that the entire era of the, even going back to the ’80s and Nintendo or whatever, but ’80s, ’90s, 2000s, 2010s, attention was greenfield, it turned out just technology in general, whether that be consoles or whether that be the then computers and phones and all that sort of thing, phones obviously the most important one, there were all these minutes of the day that were waiting to be filled by something and it turned out that maybe a lot of the analysis of why video games are so popular, XYZ, just actually came down to the fact that they were competing with nothing. They were competing with null, they were competing with reading the paper or watching TV or going to the mall or whatever it might be, and in that comparison, it was a very easy space to win and the reality is, I guess post-COVID, I would say probably most definitively post short-form video, the TikTokization, everything’s brownfield now. You’re actually having to fight tooth and nail for every minute of every day and this is an industry that maybe was completely unsuited to do that.**

**MB:** I think that is a little bit too uncharitable. I would put it in this frame, I don’t think it competed with nothing, what I would say is that it had unique space for certain aspects. Interactivity? There wasn’t really an interactive product in the home. There weren’t really remote, real-time social experiences. When we talk about typical game design theory, skill mastery and progression, instantaneous feedback loops, i.e., “You’re getting better”, your journey is continuing and you’re feeling that payback, “I couldn’t do this and now I can, I couldn’t open that door and now I can”. It’s not that those succeeded because there were no alternatives, it’s actually now that there are a myriad other things in the home that are interactive, that are social, that are real-time, that have progression and crucially also have social signaling now.

It’s contrarian to think, but I believe strongly, that especially in capitalist nations and especially where sports fandom is particularly high for many, especially young men, sports betting actually resembles a lot of that.

**That’s super interesting. Is there a bit to where, over the last decade, the attempt to harvest and grow revenue and things like purchases and all this, in-app purchases and all the transaction bits diminished the payoffs that were used to easier to come by? How much of a role does that play?**

**MB:** It’s a good question. I think it would all be speculative, and this is where we get into differences of culture and game type and platform.

When you look globally, we see similar effects happening in the United States, in Korea and in Western Europe. Even though the regulatory environment, the devices, the cultural norms around monetization and the legality of alternatives, like sports betting, all differ. And so there’s not a cohesive picture there, but I do think that there’s a claim to be made that many of the growth catalysts of the 2010s, the rise of free-to-play, of microtransactions, of heavily social games, there was a newness that really advantaged incremental spending and the attraction of social gamers. By social gamers, I mean like social smokers, you’re not a gamer, but you’ll game with other people.

**Your friends are gaming. Exactly, yeah.**

**MB:** Yeah. You’ll have a cigarette because your friends are having a cigarette, that type. That probably did go a little bit when massively exposed to that category, there are compensatory effects obviously when we take a look at younger demos, especially around Roblox, we see higher uptake and more spending than any precedential generation.

But at the same time, when we take a look at markets like the United States or UK, we see a weakening of player engagement all above 18, but it is disproportionate at the older age groups that we probably over-assumed had become gamers and didn’t.

**Right, that they would keep gaming forever.**

#### Where Growth is Coming From

**Well, let’s go through the presentation. Those are sort of some of the big picture things I was thinking about, but there are some specific points I wanted to get to. One thing that’s interesting, that’s right at the top, is unlike previous years, well, previous years, I think when you started, gaming revenue had actually had dropped. Then it grew a bit the year after that, it grew last year, there’s record revenue, so do we need to be pessimistic here? Isn’t everything fixed?**

**MB:** So, this is actually what really motivated me to write this year’s report. Every year I don’t know if I’m going to do another.

**Right, you need a hook that’s going to pull you in.**

**MB:** What was really interesting to me were two fundamental and interconnected points. Number one is, as you mentioned, 2025 finally hit a new high for the industry, revenues were up about five-and-a-half percent, that’s an incremental 10% year-over-year and similar five plus percentage points above the prior high of ’21, $10 billion. So we finally have a lot more revenue and over a three-year basis, pretty substantial revenue growth and yet, when you talk to most in the industry or even you look at their P&Ls, it’s hard to figure out where that money is going because it doesn’t seem to be showing up in coffers.

**It’s crazy. Everyone seems to be suffering, people are still getting laid off, there’s no investment.**

**MB:** Totally. And then there’s a more challenging thing, which is, I went through this very laborious process of trying to figure out what is median profitability, and this is a little bit wonkish, but we’ve basically got three different cuts of the gaming ecosystem. One is the Chinese ecosystem, complex because most of it is controlled by Tencent and NetEase, which both distribute and create and publish games. Then you have the platforms, Sony, Nintendo, Microsoft, Steam, and because they’re vertically integrated, their own content is often used to subsidize the platform, different economics.

That third tranche, what is the life for a independent, non-Chinese game maker? That can be EA, that can be a small studio. It turns out that, of that last cohort, about half, just over half of all of their P&Ls are public, and so I said, “Let’s take a look at six years of data, let’s factor out extraordinary events, let’s factor out M&A amortization, but let’s keep out write-downs of canceled games and severance and you can just tally up operating income”. And they, as a collective, that half of the industry, which over-indexes in some areas and under-indexes in others, make significantly less money than it did in 2019, when the market was roughly 40% smaller.

**But their revenues are still up though, right?**

**MB:** Yes.

**So this is a cost issue.**

**MB:** Well, it’s a cost issue in part, but then it has continued to trickle down and then not respond to what you just mentioned, that three years of increase. And so I started to ask this question, “Where is the money going?”, and when you start to do that, you see an industry that yes, on its high, is higher than ever, but the money that is actually reaching those developers has shrunk. And that reflects a fundamental transformation not just in user behavior, not just in competition that you and I discussed, but in where that money goes and how it is spent, and that is the big challenge for developers today.

**Is this just a, “The world has changed”? If you were a big AAA developer in the 2010s, it’s just over? Money is still being — basically is this a, “Video Gaming Is Slumping”, story or a, “The AAA Era Has Past” story?**

**MB:** I don’t really think it’s either. What I think it gets to is just this fundamental restructuring that is happening in several different ways.

One is, where the growth is. One of the things that I’ve done in the report is tally up what I call the “Major Market 8”. This is the US, UK, Germany, Italy, France, Japan, Korea, Canada. They are 60% of worldwide PC console revenues or about 50% of the global market overall, and they have been flat now for five years. All of the growth that I just mentioned has happened in what we typically call ROW plus China. And so the slump, if you say that those Major Market 8 have retreated and the market has grown overall, it means you’ve had actually much greater compensatory growth elsewhere.

The problem is not that AAA is in decline per se, there’s cost challenges, it’s in, “It’s not your backyard, it’s not the customers that you think of and it may, in many instances, just not be the developers, regions, cultures and device specs that you think of”.

**Right. Well, doesn’t this make the case though for my greenfield versus brownfield point? Which is, the way you could characterize the rest of the world is, it’s still greenfield opportunity. There’s still people coming online or still people getting a sufficiently good enough device or connectivity to be able to game. And turns out, when that’s a new thing to do, they will game a lot. But if you’re in a saturated market, whether they’re saturated in terms of video games or saturated in terms of attention grabbing devices, then there’s not much growth to be had.**

**MB:** Totally. Now, there’s a few different challenges with that. I mentioned earlier that some of that is you don’t have the cultural prowess, you may not operate on the devices, you may not have the monetization models for it, but part of it is cost structure. These customers, the average Korean gamer, a subset of the total population, spends about 600 bucks a year. The average American is about $250, high penetration levels. And so we have huge variance, but the average gamer, even in markets where 90% of the population over 18 games, might spend dozens of dollars per annum, that is a big challenge. Even if the revenue is there in aggregate, you may not have the unit economics to launch or even sustain at that level.

**Does this compound upon itself where, as you have to change your model and your structure and your game types and the level of devices you’re targeting to get this growth, you’re actually hurting your prospects in your established markets because what they want and desire and are capable of ends up being totally different?**

**MB:** Yes. In the sense that you have finite resources, you have to make a decision to prioritize, in particular, cultural investments.

**Right, you’ve made that point, the IP that a lot of these platforms are targeting, you’re like, “None of this is relevant to the US market, but that’s what they’re focusing on”.**

**MB:** Yeah, so it’s not really a disruptive trade-off, but it’s just one of the fundamental-

**It’s just a trade-off.**

**MB:** Do you bank after the customers that you know will monetize, the customers that you need to keep, versus betting on uncertain monetization and content that isn’t going to engage your customer base in the pursuit of those who don’t.

When you take a look at PUBG, Free Fire, some of the biggest games of the world, very popular in the United States, but among first, second, and third generation immigrants from Southeast Asia or Latin and South America, they know these titles. But when you take a look at the intellectual properties, I had no idea. I know PUBG [did an event with a musical artist called G-Dragon](<https://pubg.com/en/news/9089>), and I love when this happens, because it happens over and over and over and you’re like, who is-

**I’m very familiar with G-Dragon!** **My wife is a huge fan. But yes, that is only, to your point, that is just because I have a connection to another country that I would not know that if I was in the US.**

**MB:** And look, I think I’m reasonably savvy, though I’m about to self undermine on the K-pop and the cultural primitives and differences of popularity. But I had the classic event — you go, you Google, “Who is G-Dragon?”, you get to Wikipedia and it’s like, “Oh, one of the best-selling artists globally of all time”, that surfaces the very bias that I think is happening all over.

**Honestly, pretty solid as far as K-pop goes, I will say, G-Dragon is.**

#### Consoles and Stunted Aggregation

**One thing that I thought was interesting, you mentioned that in the established markets for console growth in particular, more and more of the growth is not going to games, but it’s actually going to the platforms themselves. So tell me about that.**

**MB:** So this is one of these interesting transformations where, let’s look at console. Actually, 2025 is a great example. I mentioned earlier that the market was at an overall high, every major platform was at a new high, console, PC, and mobile. Console peaked at about $41.1 billion in 2021, it retreated several billion, but last year it hit $41.6 billion, so a half billion dollar hot. But over that four year period, more than 150% of growth happened to platform services, that’s Game Pass, PlayStation Network, Nintendo Switch Online. So over that four year period is one such example, half billion in total consumer spend increased, but PlayStation network went up $1.7 billion,

**Wow. So more than triple.**

**MB:** And so you can see that the growth is actually being eaten at the platform level. And so even though we look at the market and we say console’s up half a billion, not huge, but up, the actual amount that gamers spent on standalone games and microtransactions, whether packaged or free to play, shrunk by several billion dollars, about 10%.

So this is one of those good examples of when you look and you say, “Where is the revenue going?”, and I outlined earlier you have the Chinese ecosystem, then you have the platform ecosystem, then you have the independent publishers. I don’t mean indie as in small, I mean anyone outside of those two aforementioned groups, their economic opportunity has actually shrunk by most measures.

**So I love this point for I think reasons that will probably be very obvious to you. You point out that the annual releases of games on these platforms have absolutely skyrocketed. PC, 150% more games than — I should have wrote down the date of when you were measuring since.**

**MB:** 2019, yeah.

**2019, thank you, that’s what I thought it was because that was pre-COVID, and console releases are up 300%. As far as the number of games released, this sounds to me like there is an abundance of supply. There’s an abundance of games, which, if I may invoke it,[Aggregation Theory](<https://stratechery.com/2015/aggregation-theory/>) would suggest is actually going to drive value and profits to the discovery layer, which is basically the platforms. Isn’t that basically what’s happening?**

**MB:** Yeah, and look, you’re spot on and I will tell you in some of the conversations that I have, people say, “Look, the challenging part about this is we are seeing a pro competitive outcome, we have more supply, margins have been whittled down” — most gamers, most everyday gamers, not the cynical online type like myself, will say that quality has never been higher. It’s not necessarily higher in the longstanding franchises, but we have a absolute abundance of outstanding new titles, whether that’s Expedition 33 or PEAK or ARC Raiders on through the mainstays.

And so yes, where does the profit tend to go? They go to platforms like Steam or the App Store or increasingly, those super meta level platforms, be it Discord, Fortnite, Roblox, etc.

**When it comes to the platform revenue, people will think of fees and I want to get into the discussion of App Store fees and things along those lines, in a moment, but is most of this, you mentioned PlayStation Network and the Nintendo, is this subscription or Game Pass? Because it’s so much stuff, it’s just easier, you’re overwhelmed with choice, you’re not going to make individual decisions on titles because the cost feels very high, I can just get the subscription and try a bunch of stuff and see if something sticks.**

**MB:** So it’s interesting because there’s really a few different sub-SKUs here and this is where the subscription bundle for games versus audio or video is a little bit different.

The primary utility of the PlayStation Network service or Nintendo Switch Online is it’s a pay gate for multiplayer play, and so you can think of this essentially as in some characterize it as a tax or a toll to do certain things on the system. It’s a form of customer discrimination. If all you want to do is play Last Of Us, an offline title on PlayStation, you have no need for online multiplayer, you don’t need PlayStation Network. The vast majority of expenditures for PlayStation network are for that.

They have added a lot more value by also affording catalog releases, sometimes you can get Last of Us for free. But what we are mostly seeing, at least in my view is PlayStation saying, “We’re going to shift more of the monetization to the services layer”. And that, by the way, is one of the reasons why PlayStation has been able to thus far keep the PlayStation hardware price flat even absorbing some of the DRAM costs. But they continue to suggest that the consumer willingness to pay for services remains durable, that their price hikes have not led to large turn in excess of the revenue that’s generated incrementally and that they will continue to do so, and we’re seeing that across the ecosystem.

**Is this a scenario where they’re stuck on a local maxima though, given the stickiness of social and the way that the customer acquisition effects of social, where the more of your friends that are on, the increase in marginal value to you should increase, that they’re kind of shooting themselves in the foot? They’re making a lot of money from making social gaming attractive, but the barrier to social gaming is so much higher that they’re actually not getting as much growth as they might’ve otherwise?**

**MB:** Yeah, without a question. Look, a fundamental problem here is when we have a market where we have player contraction in the major markets, we simultaneously have a weakening of consumer spending, and we have an ecosystem like console that, as an exaggerated example, that over 25 years has not grown its install base by much, your response is to monetize the customers who have growing propensity to spend through higher end subscriptions or more expensive hardware or accessories; but that does eventually constrain the network effect.

And when you take a look at a title like Roblox, I think as much as we think of Grow a Garden or Steal a Brainrot and Roblox and the iPad as American products, when you take a look at the unison of those platform services, games, and titles, 70 or 80% of the audience is in, again, ROW or APAC. And they are consumers who monetize barely at all, who use devices that sit entirely outside of the console ecosystem and have no reason to onboard to that ecosystem, and the more that costs through upfront hardware plus services fees keep them out, you’re just going to have a diminishing of that network effect and a strengthening elsewhere.

#### Roblox and Advertising

**This is a good lead-in to the Roblox point. Is that the key thing there? They are the sort of “free” social gaming experience and you start there, you get the network effect customer acquisition, and then once people are in there,[they’re just going to get games there](<https://stratechery.com/2021/the-roblox-microverse/>) instead of somewhere else?**

**MB:** That’s the big bet. And look, there’s a lot of conversation happening around, “Will Roblox be able to massively scale into older audiences?”, “What will the retention curves look like after 17, 18?”.

**The curve’s really bent for them in the last year, right?**

**MB:** Yeah. Again, this gets to the primary theme of, “Where’s the revenue going?”. If you can believe 60% of net growth outside of China since 2021 went to Roblox, 67% of global non-China spending growth last year, 67% went to Roblox. In effect, if you say the gaming industry had a great year last year and you net out Roblox as the sole participant, no, gaming did not have a great consumer spend year.

**Roblox did.**

**MB:** Roblox did, yeah. Without question. And therefore these questions of how does that change and then how disruptive is it? Roblox as an ecosystem, primary growth driver, most engagement of any platform, most users monetizes at probably a sixth to an eighth of the revenue per hour. This gets to that macro YouTube, Facebook alternative forces of man, how could we ever flex down? Putting aside content, suitability and audience, can anyone make those margins work except for the independent developers who now are doing quite well?

**Yeah. I just think it’s really interesting because to the aggregation point, yes, the platforms are pulling off, they’re capturing value in the value chain, but they’re not capturing the full ecosystem benefits of being an aggregator because they’re gating their network effects. And the Roblox example I think is just really striking. You can get a Roblox version, my son was playing some F1 on Roblox thing as opposed to the actual F1 game, but that’s because his friends were on there and they tried it and that’s where they went. And yeah, it just seems like, this is kind of the Sony PlayStation thing in a nutshell. If you could sum up Sony for the last whatever, they won the market and they’re just doubling down endlessly on it and because they won so convincingly, they’re still by-and-large okay, but they are resolutely opposed to any sort of new model or expansionary aspect. And it’s like, they’re just locked into where they are and now Roblox is probably in the long run set to eat their lunch.**

**MB:** I mean the way that you would see this is in some respect, Roblox and Fortnite are this UGC model resembles WeChat on top of iPhones, you are just shifting where the behavior of audiences in discovery and socializing is. It used to be that you would load up a console and choose the game that you were going to play, it used to be that you would load up the console, take a look at your social feed, I’d see Ben’s online, Ben’s playing game A, I send him an invite, we chat, we choose what to do. All of that behavior still exists, it just sits within the application layer. And then application is a aggregation application that is completely indifferent to the specific hardware stack it sits on, and therefore, as it strengthens, it commodifies everything it’s sitting upon and then in doing so, gives you and I no real reason to stick on that specific stack.

**This is tied into this because the natural business model for a social-first aggregator is ads. We brought this up last year, sort of the Mary Meeker paradigm where the time spent is not matched by the monetization, at least in terms of ads, is that still the case in gaming or are we getting closer?**

**MB:** This is where we have a massive difference between mobile and PC, console or what some people call HD. I have kind of sworn off if I do another report next year, I will not just do the traditional consumer spend lens because advertising on mobile is now roughly matched.

**Because last year you hinted at the TikTok bit, that this year’s like, “Okay, this is a big problem”, you hinted at this this year, like, “Actually, we need to think more broadly up monetization”, and now you’re just basically predicting, “Yep, next year that will be the big focus”.**

**MB:** It looks like in 2026 that total ad spend against mobile games will exceed total consumer expenditure on PC and console games so we have to think about this as essentially now the third or fourth leg of the entire games industry. There are real challenges in the PC console implementation, but when you take a look at revenue opportunities, especially in stagnant expenditure markets and how rewarded advertising is now starting to come to traditionally higher fidelity games, that’s going to be key.
