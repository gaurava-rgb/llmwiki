A new Harvard Business Review piece just exposed why most AI adoption fails at work — and it has everything to do with product management.

Drawing from 18 months of research at Google and insights from professionals across industries, [Amanda Pratt](<https://www.linkedin.com/in/amandaprattt?trk=public_post-text>) and [Melissa Valentine](<https://www.linkedin.com/in/melissavalentine?trk=public_post-text>) examined how teams use generative AI in their daily work. They identified a key trend: successful teams aren't necessarily experts at crafting prompts. Instead, they excel at applying product management principles.

What does that look like in practice? Instead of asking “where can I use AI?”, they start by isolating a real bottleneck in a workflow — something repetitive, error-prone, or time-intensive. They define what success would actually mean for that step (time saved, quality bar, reliability), then shape the AI use around that constraint.

Moreover, they approach AI as an integrated component of the overall system, rather than a standalone tool. The real effort lies in determining how the AI output fits into the next steps, who will use it, and how the workflow evolves with automation. Without this, AI implementations often feel unreliable; with it, they deliver compounding benefits.

None of this feels entirely novel, but it's especially relevant now as we're still in the early stages of AI integration, and many teams are grappling with building products that drive strong adoption. At its core, it draws on the same fundamentals PMs use to deliver sustainable solutions: sharp problem definition, iterative experiments, tight feedback loops, and seamless process integration.

To me, this aligns closely with what's being called the AI PM's newest essential skill—AI Product Sense—or at least a key aspect of it. Good piece!
