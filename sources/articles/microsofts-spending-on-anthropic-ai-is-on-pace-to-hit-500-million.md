OpenAI is Microsoft’s most important AI provider and its biggest cloud server customer.

But as OpenAI does more business with Microsoft’s cloud rivals, [Microsoft](<https://www.theinformation.com/org-charts/microsoft>) is doing more business with [Anthropic](<https://www.theinformation.com/org-charts/anthropic>), OpenAI’s archrival.

Microsoft has quietly become one of Anthropic’s top customers and was recently on pace to spend nearly $500 million a year for Anthropic AI to power Microsoft products, according to a person with direct knowledge. At the same time, Microsoft is putting more emphasis on selling Anthropic AI models to its cloud customers, which could generate more revenue for both companies.

  *   *   *


To that end, Microsoft recently told some salespeople in its Azure cloud unit that selling Anthropic AI models to Microsoft cloud customers will count toward their sales quotas just like Microsoft-made software, according to two Microsoft employees.

Microsoft doesn’t typically offer such a sales incentive for non-Microsoft products, as such products are less lucrative to Azure. The current Anthropic sales incentive runs through the end of June, one of the people said.

Microsoft has offered salespeople the same incentive for selling OpenAI models, which Microsoft also sells through Azure. The new quota system functionally means salespeople have just as much incentive to sell Anthropic models as OpenAI ones, the people said.

The moves show how Microsoft, similar to other established tech firms, is spreading its bets by working closely with multiple AI developers that represent both a major revenue opportunity for and a potential threat to some of its core businesses. Microsoft is racing to use OpenAI’s and Anthropic’s technologies to make its own AI automation products useful for corporate customers to avoid the [risk of ceding such business to the AI upstarts](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/microsofts-nadella-pressures-deputies-accelerate-copilot-improvements?rc=c48ukx>), which have their own enterprise software ambitions.

Microsoft’s growing ties to Anthropic follow its decision in September to invest up to $5 billion in Anthropic. As part of the new deal, Anthropic became a customer of Microsoft servers, and the startup agreed to let Microsoft sell its AI models to businesses the same way cloud rivals Google and Amazon have long done.

Microsoft has invested more than $13 billion in OpenAI, giving it a 27% stake in the ChatGPT creator. It benefits more financially from selling OpenAI models than Anthropic ones. Microsoft keeps 80% of sales of OpenAI models to Azure customers, based on the terms of the companies’ agreements, whereas Microsoft retains a smaller percentage of Anthropic model sales, according to someone briefed on the situation.

Anthropic primarily generates revenue from selling its AI directly to businesses, but some salespeople at Microsoft and Anthropic are working together to pitch businesses on buying Anthropic models through Azure, said one of the people. Anthropic has similarly collaborated with Amazon and Google to jointly sell Anthropic models [to large customers](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/anthropic-aws-give-customers-ai-agents-helping-hand?rc=geicgp>), which sometimes have additional security requirements that large cloud providers say they can handle better than AI startups.

Microsoft sales leaders recently told Azure salespeople Anthropic’s models could “unlock new use cases for customers” and convince them to develop AI applications using Azure servers rather than ones from Google and Amazon, one of the people said. Anthropic’s models are particularly adept at generating code or completing coding-related tasks.

**Shifting Interests**

Microsoft can use OpenAI’s technology for free in its own products, thanks to the business partnership between the firms. Yet Microsoft increasingly is choosing to spend money on similar technology from Anthropic.

It pays the startup hundreds of millions of dollars a year to power AI coding features in its GitHub Copilot product and for other AI-powered Copilot features in its products that automate financial tasks in Excel or monitor companies’ cybersecurity systems, for instance.

As of July last year, Microsoft was spending more than $40 million per month, a pace of nearly $500 million a year, on Anthropic models that powered coding agent features in GitHub Copilot, such as the ability to monitor a website for outages and automatically fix them, according to someone with direct knowledge of the spending. Since then, Microsoft has expanded its purchases of Anthropic models to power features in other products such as 365 Copilot and Security Copilot, including complex actions like generating PowerPoint presentations based on short written prompts. (Anthropic previously projected it would generate between $7.5 billion and $9 billion in annualized revenue as of the end of 2025.)

Anthropic is giving Microsoft a significant discount on using Anthropic models for internal purposes such as creating prototypes or analytics and other research, compared to the AI company’s listed retail prices, according to two people with knowledge of the situation.

The multitude of financial arrangements between the two—discounts Anthropic gives to Microsoft on buying models, Microsoft’s funding of Anthropic, and Anthropic’s rental of servers from Microsoft—make it hard to calculate which company is providing more financial value to the other.

But the deepening relationship is arguably more important to Anthropic, which is considering an initial public offering as soon as this year. It also shows how the biggest cloud, software and AI firms increasingly depend on each other financially.

For its part, OpenAI is hedging its risks by working with numerous large tech firms to develop server clusters for developing AI after earlier working exclusively with Microsoft.

For Microsoft, working closely with Anthropic could help the software giant fix deficiencies in its Copilot products, which its executives have worried the customers that pay for it aren’t using enough. Microsoft first moved to add Anthropic models to 365 Copilot after executives grew frustrated with how some OpenAI-powered features were struggling to perform well, and CEO Satya Nadella has [more recently pushed product groups](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/microsofts-nadella-pressures-deputies-accelerate-copilot-improvements>) to use the Anthropic models to speed up development.

Anthropic engineers have recently given support to Microsoft engineers developing Copilot features powered by Anthropic models, two people with knowledge of the situation said.
