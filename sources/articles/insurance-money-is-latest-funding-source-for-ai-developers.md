Wall Street is turning to life insurance capital to meet artificial intelligence’s insatiable funding needs, melding the AI boom with a fast growing source of assets for the biggest private equity firms.

Apollo Global Management, one of the largest asset managers, recently bought Texas-based Stream Data Centers, which is targeting the big cloud and AI developers known as hyperscalers. Once the deal is finalized, Apollo executives see an opportunity to invest funds from the firm’s insurance arm Athene into new developments being constructed by Stream, said people familiar with the strategy.

That strategy is becoming a familiar one among large private equity firms. KKR has used funds from its insurance business Global Atlantic in a debt offering for CyrusOne, a large data center developer it partly owns.

The growing use of insurance capital in data center deals comes as the owners of other big pools of capital—including banks, governments, private debt firms, institutional investors and tech giants—have already committed hundreds of billions of dollars to data center projects. Bankers are optimistic insurance capital can help meet the growing funding needs of data center developers, which are developing bigger facilities to keep up with AI.

Large insurance fund managers including Blackstone’s credit and insurance arm are expected to invest in a $22 billion debt financing to help Vantage Data Centers build a new campus in Texas, said people familiar with the talks. Insurance funds until recently have largely invested in debt backed by data center facilities with several years of operations behind them.

The move by insurance providers into early-stage developments such as Vantage’s could raise risks for policyholders if the data center boom begins to cool. Insurers have historically invested in safe corporate and government bonds, but private equity firms use a broader range of often riskier assets to boost returns. The firms argue that insurance capital is a good match for data centers because both are based on agreements spanning a decade or more.

“These are long-term contracts with committed revenue streams, so they should operate very similarly to the bonds that some of these hyperscalers are issuing directly,” said Julie Brewer, head of finance at EdgeCore Digital Infrastructure, a Denver-based data center developer.

Apollo and KKR have led the push by private equity firms into insurance assets. That has given them hundreds of billions of dollars in funds that are earmarked to pay life insurance claims. They invest that cash with the goal of having a surplus after claims are paid.

Apollo was the first private equity firm to build a life insurance company but was slower than peers to purchase a data center developer. KKR and Global Infrastructure Partners paid about $15 billion in 2022 for CyrusOne.

KKR last year turned to Global Atlantic to lend $1 billion to CyrusOne as part of an $7.9 billion credit facility, one of the largest deals of its kind. CyrusOne said it would use the money for new developments as it tries to meet growing demand from AI.

Global Atlantic’s investment helped KKR attract assets from other insurance companies and pensions, KKR co-head of credit and markets Adam Smith said at a Morgan Stanley conference in June. Apollo and KKR declined to comment on how much insurance capital they have invested in data centers.

In other deals, companies with close links to insurers are making big investments. Bond investing giant PIMCO is leading a $26 billion debt offering for a data center campus Meta is building in Louisiana, which could cost about $50 billion.

PIMCO, which oversees $2.1 trillion in assets, is a big manager of insurance funds and is owned by German insurer Allianz. PIMCO typically spreads a deal like Meta’s across several funds with a range of investment strategies, said a person familiar with the firm’s strategy.

Blackstone—which doesn’t own a major insurance business like Apollo and KKR do, but oversees several investment strategies tailored to insurers—has also begun making more loans to data center developers. Last month, Blackstone’s credit and insurance arm expanded a debt facility for Texas-based Aligned Data Centers to more than $1 billion.

The same Blackstone arm has also lent to QTS, a major U.S. data center developer Blackstone purchased in 2021 for about $10 billion, said people familiar with the firm’s strategy.

The insurance industry is one of the largest sources of investment capital, with about $5.8 trillion at U.S. life insurers, according to the National Association of Insurance Commissioners. Apollo manages $425 billion for insurance providers Athene and Athora, a bit over half of the firm’s total investment capital.

Tech giants like Amazon shouldered much of the data center buildout and its associated costs before the AI boom forced them to [lean more heavily](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/tech-giants-spreading-risk-ai-buildout>) on outside developers. While those companies have traditionally relied on bank loans to fund construction costs, they’re increasingly looking to other sources of funding as projects get bigger.

There are few signs the buildout will slow any time soon. Oracle last week said it signed an additional $300 billion of cloud agreements over the next five years from OpenAI and other customers, suggesting it will need to quickly secure more data center capacity to fulfill the contracts.

Insurance assets have long been [used to finance](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/cash-hungry-data-centers-embrace-new-source-debt>) mature data center developments. But until recently, they’ve been unable to participate in the financing of more speculative early stage data center projects. Banks dominated that market in part because the deals didn’t have credit ratings, which insurers typically require before they invest.

Data center developers and their bankers are trying to get the rating agencies onboard. Vantage in June raised $2.3 billion in debt for an Ohio campus through what it called one of the first data center construction loans to receive a private investment-grade rating.
