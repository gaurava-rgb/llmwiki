For years, Roku founder and CEO Anthony Wood ran the streaming device maker with some strict rules. He wouldn’t allow advertisements on the Roku home screen—the first thing its viewers see when they switch on their TV—for anything besides other entertainment companies. He also wouldn’t allow the home screen to promote individual shows, preferring icons of streaming apps.

But as Roku’s once-explosive growth has slowed and the streaming TV market has matured, Wood has relaxed those strictures. Roku now permits all kinds of ads on its home screen. It even recently allowed something that would have once been unthinkable: cruise line Carnival Cruises ran an ad that took over the entire Roku home screen, Wood told investors at a conference in March.

His explanation was that Roku had focused in the past on “keeping [the home screen] simple” to ensure “a great viewer experience,” but that “we didn’t really focus on ‘how can we use it to drive our business?’ And so that’s what we’re doing now.” What Wood didn’t say was that Roku staffers in the past had repeatedly pressed for the ability to sell to a broader range of advertisers, and that the advertisers were also keen—but Wood had opposed it, according to two former employees and an ad industry executive.

“I was always pushing them on the home screen unit,” said David Nyurenberg, a senior vice president of digital at ad agency InterMedia Advertising. He said that Roku’s home screen is among the most attractive ad space available in the entire industry: “Everyone sees the home screen unit when they turn on the TV.”

**Change of Heart**

Wood’s change of heart has likely helped boost Roku’s ad business—revenue from that part of the company grew 17% in the first quarter, roughly in line with last year—but it remains a much slower-growing operation than in Roku’s heyday six years ago, when its revenue was expanding between 70% and 80% a year. (Ad revenue is included in what the company calls ‘platform revenue’ that also includes a cut of streaming services’ subscription revenue).

As viewers across America embraced streaming TV, the number of households watching TV on Roku-powered devices mushroomed from 9.2 million to 90 million between 2015 and 2024. Its platform revenue exploded from about $50 million to $3.5 billion annually over the same period.

But that growth phase is over: 84% of U.S. households now have internet-connected TVs, streaming dongles or internet-connected gaming consoles that let them watch streaming TV, according to research firm Parks Associates.

Roku, a pioneer of streaming devices, still has a leading market share, at least in the U.S., where its software powers nearly 25% of streaming devices, including TVs, up from 20% in 2020, according to Parks Associates.

But the market remains highly fragmented, despite Wood’s expectation that it would consolidate around two or three entrants, just as the smartphone operating system market coalesced around Apple and Android. Apple and Android have nearly 100% of the mobile operating system market; the top three streaming OS systems only had 65% market share in 2025, according to Parks Associates Data.

Roku is still fiercely competing with Samsung, which has 23% of the market, according to Parks data, along with Amazon’s Fire TV, Xumo from Comcast and Charter and the operating systems of hardware makers like LG and Walmart’s Vizio, as well as Google’s Android TV, although Google has lately taken steps to scale back its smart TV efforts, [The Information recently reported](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/google-cuts-smart-tv-budget-doubles-youtube>).

Meanwhile, the streaming ad market has become more crowded, as bigger companies like Netflix and [even social media apps such as Meta Platforms’ Instagram and TikTok try to get a cut](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/tiktok-instagram-plot-tv-apps-following-youtubes-success>).

They’re all fighting over a relatively small market: Advertisers spent around $18.8 billion in the U.S. on streaming television last year, according to ad-buying firm WPP Media. That’s around 11% of Meta’s revenue last year, and less than half of the $46.8 billion advertisers spent on traditional TV ads last year, according to the WPP Media Data.

Roku’s average revenue per user has stalled since 2021, although the company stopped reporting the metric in the first quarter of last year. It also stopped reporting the number of households that use its service. (Wood said on an earnings call last year that reporting things like streaming household growth was no longer representative of how its overall business was doing, as it expands overseas.)

Roku’s stock price has been flat for three years, trading at less than a quarter of its peak 2021 value.

This performance raises the question of how long Roku will remain independent. Some in the TV industry, such as Dave Morgan, CEO of TV ad technology firm Simulmedia, say Wood should have sold the company some time ago or acquired a company like Vizio to increase its scale.

Morgan argues that Roku tried to do too much. In addition to selling its devices and building an ad business, it has also invested in programming and other streaming services, which put it into competition with the services it carries.

“Roku is spread across three or four different market offerings, and you probably have to pick one,” said Morgan. “You want to win in content. You want to win in tech. You want to win in data. You want to win in ads. You want to win in OS. That’s hard.”

As for whether Wood will sell, the jury is out. In its early days, Wood [rebuffed acquisition offers](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/why-roku-acquisition-could-make-sense-for-disney-or-walmart?rc=9byxri>), The Information reported previously. He argued that Roku would be worth $10 billion one day—many times what it was worth at the time of those offers. He turned out to be right: The company now is worth $11 billion on an enterprise value basis, which is its market capitalization excluding cash. A Roku spokesperson didn’t have a comment about the prospects of a sale.

**OS Wars**

Roku was founded in 2002 as part of Netflix, which wanted to make sure TV viewers could watch shows over the internet. Spun out of Netflix in 2008, Roku initially offered dongles that attached to TVs, allowing them to connect to the internet and offering a menu of apps. Later Roku began licensing its operating system to TV set manufacturers.

Roku’s software was lightweight and purpose-built for TVs, making it appealing to hardware makers, according to two former Roku employees and three TV executives. Other operating systems, including those sold by Google, weren’t as lightweight. To compete, Google—as well as Amazon, which offered the Fire TV software and hardware—paid TV manufacturers to use their software. They also offered payments for marketing, one executive said.

Roku didn’t need to pay as much because it was the market leader. Its market lead also allowed Wood to be strict about what Roku’s home screen would look like.

He repeatedly rebuffed employee suggestions for Roku to sell home screen ads to a wide array of marketers, three former employees said. That was despite the fact that the home screen is the highest-margin ad inventory Roku sells, one former employee said. When Roku hired salespeople, its staffers told recruits the company prided itself on the fact that its viewers didn’t see random ads from car dealerships or insurance companies when they turned on the TV, said a person who interviewed with the company.

Roku allowed a broader array of ads to be sold on its eponymously named free channel, which carries mostly reruns and other TV shows. But there, Roku must split ad revenue with content owners, the former employee added.

And the Roku channel didn’t seem to attract as many eyeballs, said Nyurenberg of InterMedia, adding that for years he had wished the home screen was available to his ad clients outside the media and entertainment sector.

Wood was also resistant to changing how the home screen looked. Roku employees for years had pushed for a user interface that emphasized individual programs rather than apps, as competitors like Amazon Fire debuted these types of home screens, three former employees said. Wood typically rebuffed these ideas, two former employees said.

The program-focused interface led viewers to spend more time watching the shows it promoted, a former Amazon employee said.

Wood’s attitude changed as the streaming ad market became more competitive. First, Google and Amazon improved their technology, undermining Roku’s advantage in operating systems, a TV executive said. Meanwhile, Netflix, Amazon, Disney and Warner Bros. Discovery’s HBO Max all began selling ads on their apps.

Roku’s hopes of building an ad business from its own channels suffered. More than half of the Roku channel’s available ad slots went unsold during 2023, for instance, two former employees said.

Wood now takes a very different attitude to the ads he’s willing to put on the Roku home screen. In March, at a Morgan Stanley conference, he said Roku now offers marketers across many different sectors the opportunity to buy commercial space on its home screen, both as video ads and as wallpaper where the ads are a backdrop to the home screen.

And last year, Roku started featuring TV shows and movies more prominently on the home screen, following the lead of Amazon in showing teasers for programs rather than just icons of channels. Above its signature grid of streaming apps, the company added a “Top Picks for You” bar, where it recommended shows and movies users might be interested in. Last month, Roku debuted a new home screen design featuring even more content that it’s testing in beta.

“The update we currently are testing is designed to drive deeper engagement, surface more meaningful content and deliver greater value to our customers,” a Roku spokesperson said.

Roku’s share of the operating system market remains under pressure. In recent years, Amazon began paying higher bounties—more than $30 or more per set, according to industry executives—to persuade TV set manufacturers to use its operating systems, according to two people familiar with the deals. (Amazon didn’t have a comment). Roku responded by launching its own Roku-branded smart TVs in early 2023.

At the end of last year, Walmart acquired the TV set maker, Vizio, which has its own operating system. Walmart is a major seller of TVs, including many powered by Roku. At the Morgan Stanley conference, Wood said he expected Walmart would “shift some of their assortment to the Vizio TVs away from Roku TVs” and “we’ll probably sell less” than in the past as a result of the deal. He noted, though, that other retailers that used to sell Vizio TVs are replacing them with Roku TVs.

Walmart said last month that its house brand, Onn, will use Vizio’s software. Previously, Onn TVs used Roku’s OS.
