Good morning,

Today’s _emergency_ Stratechery Interview is with Michael Nathanson, long-time Wall Street analyst and co-founder of [MoffettNathanson](<https://www.moffettnathanson.com/>). MoffettNathanson, which was founded in 2013, is a subscription-based research group primarily serving Wall Street, and Nathanson specializes in media and tech, with a specific focus on digital advertising. I previously interviewed Nathanson in [March 2025](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-michael-nathanson-about-the-streaming-endgame/>), [December 2023](<https://stratechery.com/2023/an-interview-with-michael-nathanson-about-a-tumultuous-year-in-media/>), [January 2023](<https://stratechery.com/2023/an-interview-with-michael-nathanson-about-netflix-and-the-media-industry/>), and [June 2022](<https://stratechery.com/2022/an-interview-with-michael-nathanson-about-streaming-and-digital-advertising/>).

This interview was recorded on Sunday evening, before I wrote [Netflix and the Hollywood End Game](<https://stratechery.com/2025/netflix-and-the-hollywood-end-game/>); Nathanson and I have debated Netflix for years (although Nathanson no longer covers the stock), and I wanted to discuss the acquisition of Warner Bros. with him while I formulated what I wanted to write. That conversation is below.

In this interview we discuss what Netflix has done right to date, and why they are motivated to pay for a library. We review why Hollywood failed in the streaming wars, and the overall logic of the deal, and whether the deal will be derailed by regulatory concerns. At the end we do sneak in a little discussion of AI; Nathanson has been very bullish about Google since before it was consensus.

As I noted, this was an emergency interview that was recorded [before Paramount announced their hostile bid for Warner Bros.](<https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/08/paramount-skydance-hostile-bid-wbd-netflix.html>) (which Nathanson expected). In addition, there is a previously scheduled interview on the calendar for this Thursday as well.

**MN:** Thanks, Ben. Nice to be here.

You said we had to do it because it was the culmination of so many of our conversations.**

**MN:** Yes.

**It might be a direct quote, it sounds like it’s a direct quote. Well, I went back to our old conversations and this is what you had to say, this is our conversation number one in[the summer of 2022](<https://stratechery.com/2022/an-interview-with-michael-nathanson-about-streaming-and-digital-advertising/>). First you said that you were skeptical about the relationship between money spent on content and time spent on customers and I thought I had you nailed — look, Netflix is spending money on content right now. But point number two was you said there was real value in libraries. Point number three, you said you were skeptical about how well content travels which I’m not sure is pertinent here, maybe it is, maybe it’s not.**

**MN:** It is.

**MN:** Yeah, they started to get more library content. Basically, there was a [writer’s strike](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Writers_Guild_of_America_strike>), [an actor strike](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_SAG-AFTRA_strike>) and, all of a sudden, people started licensing them library content again and everything just took off for them. **I was going to ask you, you’re not the Netflix stock analyst anymore.**

**MN:** No, no.

**[Robert Fishman](<https://www.linkedin.com/in/robert-fishman-92b986191/>) is so this is more of a celebratory tour, not stock analysis.**

**Well, I was going to say back then, you were on this stock. That conversation was fairly bearish about Netflix and their stock is up 5x since then.**

**MN:** I know, it’s freaking terrible for me. Luckily, I have other ideas.

**What did you get wrong about the company or how they surprised you? You just listed a bunch of things they did well but is there an overarching theme there?**

They hit every button perfectly, it was a flawless execution of these critical elements. And very few companies pull that off, Ben, as you know, from observing all these companies. The ad tier, to me, was the biggest hit of all.

**Is there a bit here where one of your conclusions, you just dropped a piece a couple of minutes before this interview, I think in preparation for this interview.**

**MN:** Yup, yes.

**One of your lessons is, “Don’t look at what Netflix says, look at what they do and what they do is act like a media company”. Is it possible though that maybe the thing is they actually were more of a tech company than you thought? And what I mean by that is you look at business after business that people are skeptical about because they have huge losses and the reality is, those costs, at least in theory, do flatten out at some point because you have leverage on those costs with your massive customer base, you can steady state at $13 billion a year or whatever it might be?** **And maybe the story is as simple as that, they were able to steady state and keep the business going and, inevitably, margins exploded and that was that.**

**MN:** Yeah. For those that don’t watch us, I was vigorously nodding my head. I picked up coverage of the large cap Internet names in 2015, 2016 and I wish I had started on Internet and then done media, because the lesson in Internet is simply that. I learned enough from my mistakes at Netflix to not downgrade Meta at $80 a share in the third quarter of 2022. We defended Alphabet when people were railing about the ChatGPT fear. I think you’re totally right, I think they won because their platform was better and they understood how to use technology to build a better mousetrap and their competitors only knew how to make better content, they did not know how to leverage technology. You’re right, Ben, and that’s why, in the beginning, when you and I would have those debates, and I think it happened way before 2022, what I missed in the debate-

**Right. Our debates, it took us a long time to start recording them but we’ve been arguing about Netflix for years and years.**

**MN:** Exactly. And I’m like, “No, you have to spend more on content, you have to burn more on content”, and they didn’t partly it’s because they’ve leveraged licensed content really well. But you’re right, I think not coming at it from a tech vantage point was my biggest mistake in covering this stock and I wish I could have gone back and looked at the way we look at technology now. And that’s the lesson I learned more than anything else.

**(laughing) I wasn’t trying to make you feel bad right up front.**

**MN:** No, it’s cool. Look, you know what? I’m fortunate that our clients still want to talk to me even when I get things wrong so that’s life.

#### Netflix’s Motivation

**Well, you just wrote, to go back to the piece I mentioned that you just wrote, it’s entitled A Tale As Old As Time. What did you mean by that title?**

**MN:** Ah, tale as old as time. That, over my career, I’ve seen many cases, many mergers just like this. It was the birth of Time Warner was essentially this deal. It was Time Inc., owner of HBO, [buying Warner Bros.](<https://www.nytimes.com/1989/03/05/us/time-inc-and-warner-to-merge-creating-largest-media-company.html>) for their library and their production capabilities, that was the beginning of Time Warner. Then you have Paramount, Showtime, you’ve had Turner Time Warner. We’ve seen this over and over again, people have to consolidate to get more access to libraries and production capabilities.

**Which of those is more important, the libraries or the production capabilities?**

**MN:** Libraries, because you and I would agree that Netflix was able to build this massive machine never having a library. They found production capabilities but they didn’t have a library and yet they built this amazing company.

But I think, [and you had written this a couple weeks ago](<https://stratechery.com/2025/netflix-earnings-kpop-demon-hunters-and-netflix-hit-production/>), that chart that they put in their investor relations earnings call that showed the flat lining of growth around a couple countries at Netflix, to me, not covering the name, I was always focused on slowing engagement growth as a problem and I think they’ll never acknowledge it because they never tell you these things. But I think, at the end of the day, slowing engagement growth is why they needed to get Warner’s and HBO’s libraries.

**Expand on that because I think this is probably the critical point. Because, at the end of the day, I think the ultimate bull case for Netflix is they don’t have to buy anyone, everyone just sells to them.**

**MN:** Right.

**Why did they need to buy in the end?**

**MN:** Because I think their fear was, if Paramount had gotten hold of Warner, Paramount would pull their content and Warner’s content from Netflix. If you look at, not one show from Warner drives Netflix engagement but, in total, they have Big Bang Theory when they had it, they have some CW shows, they have Friends around the world, different places, there’s enough stuff from Warner that helps drive the flywheel and losing that would’ve driven slowed down engagement and I think they’ve realized that.

And also, the idea of Pay-One movies and films, they’ve not had a great track record at their own original films, they just haven’t. And maybe they can go back to the old HBO model of Pay-One meaning the movies appear in theater for a limited amount of weeks then they go onto HBO and they drive value in terms of consumer value. I think they’ve realized that they needed to add more elements from Warner to drive their flywheel and they needed to keep it out of Paramount’s hands because, [Paramount Skydance CEO David] Ellison, at his heart is a technology, he’s a technologist, he really is. I think people underestimate the knowledge he has from a technology side. Obviously, his dad [Oracle CEO Larry Ellison] is legendary but I think David Ellison sees himself as a technology person and I think if I’m Netflix, I didn’t want that guy with his bank roll to get these assets.

**Okay. I want to come back to Paramount and David Ellison in a little bit, but this is where I think I was with you a bit, I was raking you over the coals, hanging you out to dry. But one of the things I always posited about[why music was different than the video industry](<https://stratechery.com/2022/spotify-netflix-and-aggregation/>) is because the power of the library and for most people, the vast majority of listening is to library content. The moment a album is released, it’s immediately part of the library and that was why the labels, ultimately, despite the fact they were the poster children for the disruption from the Internet via Napster and whatnot, ended up coming out, all things considered, I think, pretty well and still exert a fair amount of power over the Spotify’s and Apple’s of the world as it were. I think I underestimated the extent, to your point, that video library content matters and I think I wrote a mea culpa at one point about, okay, I underestimated Friends or just things like that.**

**MN:** Suits.

**[Suits, yeah](<https://stratechery.com/2023/netflixs-data-drop-power-laws-netflixs-motivations/>).**

**MN:** That they were having Suits. This was the biggest show in 2023, it was an 11-year-old show that cracked Netflix top 10 of all time.

**I’m curious about this because I think the Friends thing is it’s this cultural staple that people will just watch again and again and again and again and simply having that provides a massive amount of churn prevention and whatever it might be for a service. Suits though is the counter case and the optimistic case for this deal because Netflix made it back into a thing.**

**MN:** Yes.

**Suits was never Friends.**

**MN:** No, it wasn’t, you’re right. But Ben, isn’t that, financial math aside, you have to be optimistic that what they get on their hands from Warner will have real shelf life on Netflix’s platform, they’re going to drive engagement by getting all this content.

**Yup. I’m just thinking now they could drive entire — one of the things is what are they doing with[Bill Simmons and Spotify and the podcast thing](<https://stratechery.com/2025/walmart-on-chatgpt-walmart-and-amazon-motivations-spotify-podcasts-on-netflix/>). Well, I suddenly now have a wishlist for a Sopranos re-release focused podcast, the whole thing around it. Let’s relive that one, that was a good one.**

**MN:** That was a good one. You know what? There are shows in HBO that Netflix’s core audience have never seen. Even Game of Thrones, it was the biggest show in the world but it wasn’t as big as Netflix’s shows. You can imagine Game of Thrones coming back on the home screen, Game of Thrones month or something.

**Yeah, because that’s the thing. All these shows that have huge cultural cachet, actually, the audiences are very small.**

**MN:** They’re not that big, they’re great. The thing is HBO, pound for pound, makes the best content and it blows me away. Even Succession, which is one of my favorite shows, it never was that big of a show. For people like me, it was the show but it was a coastal elite show, it wasn’t a worldwide smash for all it was.

#### Why Hollywood Failed

**You mentioned this combination of bringing HBO to Time Warner’s content being one of the original OG media merger deals that you were referring to. Why couldn’t that work for streaming? Why was HBO such a successful play before but not after?**

**MN:** Yes, that’s a great question and that’s an indictment on the people who ran Time Warner and HBO beforehand. The problem they had was they had existing distribution agreements with cable operators, cell operators and they had pricing terms that were pretty high, call it $10, $12 a month wholesale, they could not easily pull their content from those cable operators and offer a lower tier Netflix light package to go over the top. They needed to basically work distributor by distributor, year over year, pull back and change the licenses to their contracts.

They were making so much money and they optimized for profitability at HBO that the idea of going backwards and they also — you know what, Ben? They sold their shows internationally. So, they would make shows in the US and they would license into Sky in the UK, they would sell it for a dollar, two bucks a month in Asia Pacific, they never had a global footprint and they didn’t want to go global because they were, again, maximizing profitability.

And again, to your point about technologists and media companies, technologists don’t give a shit about short-term profitability, they’re like, “Let’s build the best consumer product possible”. But HBO was 25 years in at that point, they were maybe even 30 years in, they just didn’t have it inside themselves to rip up the model and the playbook. And in 2013, I think Netflix warned them they were coming at them but, “So what, okay, that’s fine, we’re not doing anything about our business”, and it was too late.

By 2016 when they sold themselves, it was just too late, they had let the opportunity slip and they sold themselves to AT&T which was not a good partner for that asset. I like [Jason Kilar](<https://x.com/jasonkilar>) who ran it, I don’t know if he’d ever been in your podcast, he had the right vision but they didn’t have the balance sheet to take this on at that point.

**I think you’re a little hard on these people. That’s disruption, you can’t give up the profits that you have. The ironic thing is they felt like they needed to have a lower price for going over the top, they probably just got over the top for more money, maybe they could have went the way to higher prices all along and that might’ve helped solve the problem. But I think it speaks to the fact that just because a business model seems adjacent doesn’t mean it’s the same and, actually, it’s very, very difficult to move over from column A to column B.**

**MN:** Yeah. Well, it’s funny, Ben, I think — I’m a very transparent person. One of the things I got most wrong from a media analyst standpoint was I didn’t think Netflix would scale outside the US as well as it did because, covering media before Netflix, the only international businesses we ever saw out of the US companies were in the UK or Australia. They didn’t really build businesses outside of English language secondary markets.

So I think if you’re [Former Time Warner CEO] Jeff Bewkes, [Former HBO CEO] Richard Plepler, anyone in media saying, “Okay, great, we’re going to have to go through France and Germany and Thailand and South Korea”, that’s not going to work, it hasn’t worked for us ever. So I think I give Netflix a ton of credit for actually finding opportunities in these markets that appeared to be disinterested in US content. And that was, I think, if you look at this from a classic media standpoint, that was something that we didn’t estimate properly in the business model.

**I completely agree, I think that that’s a good point. And I think it’s fairly understandable, I think you should stop being so mean to yourself and everybody else.**

#### The Logic of the Deal

**Well, to go back to this overall, I think if you zoom out, you were talking about the logic of the deal, I agree it’s fairly obvious. I think there’s a natural state of affairs for general entertainment that there’s only one or a limited number of points of distribution. That used to be the cable bundle, the cable bundle came about for[accidental geographic reasons](<https://stratechery.com/2022/cables-last-laugh/>), but it was a very logical way to organize content. Of course, everyone then blew it up, but the results were never going to be stable. You had all these entertainment companies who seemed to have no idea that content was not king, sustainable customer acquisition and retention was, and given that consolidation was inevitable, well, of course it’s going to end up at Netflix. Where else could it end up?**

**MN:** Right, exactly. But the funny thing is, Ben, when you go back to other businesses in the development cycle, there’s usually content acquisitions in the beginning of a new platform distribution model. Turner was building out TBS and TNT, [he merged with Warner Bros](<https://www.nytimes.com/1995/09/23/us/turner-to-merge-into-time-warner-a-7.5-billion-deal.html>), [he bought MGM](<https://www.nytimes.com/1985/08/08/business/turner-acquiring-mgm-movie-empire.html>). Usually you don’t see this acquisition from the leader to buy a library that’s late in their build out, they’ve won already, but the fact is they’re going to keep on winning by getting these assets under their control. The gap between them, well, it’s just going to widen going forward.

**I think as someone who’s[posited a Theory](<https://stratechery.com/2015/aggregation-theory/>) about this sort of virtuous cycle that results when you have the largest audience, you have the best customer acquisition strategy, your churn is mitigated. You get advantages in acquiring supply, which makes your offering that much more attractive, and you get a virtuous cycle going on. I think a lot of listeners going at me for years about whether Netflix is or is not an Aggregator, I guess you could argue they’re paying $80 billion, it’s not like they’re getting it for free.**

**MN:** Exactly. Yeah. They’re not YouTube in that sense, right?

**Right. But they also have $80 billion with an[unsecured bridge loan apparently](<https://www.ft.com/content/f82583ba-03ec-4cca-9560-a7a2e1d55574>), to make the purchase.**

**MN:** Exactly. Yeah, it’s crazy. So you were hitting on the things I wrote, my hot takes, the other thing is it’s basically, when I look at the world, so the media guys clearly lost, Netflix clearly won, but I think these fairy tales don’t always come true. Look at [Fox/Disney](<https://thewaltdisneycompany.com/disneys-acquisition-of-21st-century-fox-will-bring-an-unprecedented-collection-of-content-and-talent-to-consumers-around-the-world/>). That seemed like the same idea six, seven years ago and this way, I think you’ve learned as a media analyst now that you’ve become, there’s these relationships inside these industries that are not as normalized as you think. There’s egos and there’s agreements and it’s not that easy to get directors to want to work with you if they don’t think you’re going to put their movies in your theater or the back end’s going to disappear on a TV production company that’s not going to want to make shows for you, that’s going to take some time.

**Isn’t it all risk for Netflix that no one trusts them that they’re going to have theatrical releases or that they’re going to pay back ends?**

**MN:** Yeah, I know. And that’s to me what I’ve learned again, Robert Fishman, if he was on this podcast, we go back to our Disney/Fox model from the merger seven years ago, very little of what we assumed in that model actually played out. Fox was subsumed by Disney and all the Fox businesses that we thought we would model in terms of profit streams, we can’t find them anymore. It’s just been put into Disney and Disney Plus and Hulu and I think we’ll look back three or four years from now and say, “Wow, things didn’t work out as these Wall Street analysts predicted”, because relationships are very, very fluid in terms of how things get done.

**So is there a story here where this deal actually kind of goes sideways? We’ll get to the regulatory questions in a little bit, but assuming it goes through, actually Netflix does get a good library, but HBO’s not HBO anymore because it’s owned by Netflix quite literally.**

**MN:** Yeah, exactly. The guys who make the shows HBO could be picked off by another company. [Casey Bloys](<https://x.com/Caseybloys>), who runs HBO, consistently high hit rate, that’s a person that I would target to try to hire. The people who are on Warner Bros. TV, I’d try to hire them too. I’d make sure that the best directors are under my contract at Universal and not at Warner, I think that the industry itself is going to now go on attack mode and try to save what it can out of the status quo. If I was a competitor, I would pluck off whoever I can to come join me before this thing gets going.

**So how does this not become ultimately — how does Netflix get its full worth from this? Because the Warner Bros. revenue model includes selling stuff to TV, it includes theatrical releases. If they’re going to stick with their model and trim all this down, or is this to your point that you think they might adopt some aspect of these model? Isn’t their revenue destruction implicit in Netflix specifically buying this asset?**

**MN:** Yes. And let me ask you a question, Ben. Walt Disney’s cashflow in 2019, 2018 before it bought Fox, what year did Disney’s cashflow get back to where it was pre-Fox, pre-COVID? So 2018, call it $10 billion of cashflow. What year did it get back to where it was pre COVID, pre-Fox, pre-streaming?

**It hasn’t?**

**MN:** 2025\. It took seven years.

**Oh, I see. I was almost there.**

**MN:** You almost did. But I guess my point is that they’ll slow down their cost of content spend, they’ll raise prices, but that the content opportunity longer term will be interesting to watch because I think people will look to go someplace else as time goes on.

**You mean by the directors and then the content creators.**

**MN:** Yeah. The talent’s going to find — if I’m an agent, the agents have historically been very distrustful of Netflix, and they’ll try to find someplace else to go to get a back end.

**So what’s the other place to go?**

**MN:** Universal and what happens with Paramount or Sony. Sony always looks in this world as a hero because they avoided all this carnage and kept themselves very pure, I love that story.

**My personal heroes, I know.[I’ve been in Sony’s corner all along](<https://stratechery.com/2021/netflix-earnings-competition-and-buybacks-sony-signs-netflix-deal/>).**

**MN:** I know.

**Why do you think Warner Bros. chose Netflix instead of Paramount? Is this the moment of sanity in the industry we’ve all been waiting for? I mean, it kind of feels like Paramount is the last of the insane or do they still have company?**

**MN:** Yeah, Robert covers Paramount, but I would say to you, Paramount on their own scares me. They don’t have the girth to compete here, I think they went with Netflix because they thought it was a much better outcome in the long run for their company, and they didn’t trust these guys at Paramount to actually come in with the cash. I don’t know.

But the question I have, and I asked Robert, we’ll talk about this in our — it doesn’t feel like this is the end of the story. If I’m Paramount, why wouldn’t I come back? I would feel you’ve got to come back and just outbid Netflix because what’s their story? What’s their hand look like without this asset?

**Well, I don’t understand Paramount generally. I guess this is the question about David Ellison and what they’re doing. It feels like a rich tech kid with a lot of money who wants to build a media company, but they seem like the weakest of the streamers or I’m not sure what they actually have going on that gives them — yes, they and Peacock and whoever else and Time Warner should probably all be together, but is there any justification here for their sudden prominence in the industry other than you have a guy that’s willing to spend money?**

**MN:** I think you have CBS, you’ve got the top network in the country, you’ve got access to live sports that sports hand’s pretty good. Paramount Plus, even though people may not realize it, that [Taylor Sheridan content has done really well for them](<https://sharptext.net/2025/why-taylor-sheridan-is-worth-a-billion-dollars/>).

**[They just lost him](<https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/tv/tv-news/taylor-sheridan-leave-paramount-for-nbcuniversal-film-tv-1236410489/>).**

**MN:** They lost Taylor. I know, exactly. But I think you take CBS and sports and you go from there, their studio needs a lot of help. So under the Redstones, they’ve under-invested in studio, he’s going to try to fix that. If you want to be mean, I really ripped the Redstone family up too for terrible strategy for 20 years. But you know what? The people I’ve talked to about David Ellison really think he understands content creation and technology and he has a vision for what this industry needs to do and he’s got money behind him that’s smart money.

#### The Streaming Market

**Well, let’s think about the regulatory question. What is Netflix’s market?**

**MN:** Paid premium streaming. I don’t think you could say it’s YouTube, do you? Or it’s got to be paid premium streaming, it has to be. I would not say it’s all time spent in media, that would not be the right way to think about it.

**Why not?**

**MN:** Because at the end of the day, their combination of premium content, content library, better delivery system will lead to separation between them and everyone else. And this field’s got to accelerate their ability to take price in the market and I just think you’ve got free over here and free is becoming more and more short form or just AI-delivered and because it’s free, I don’t think it competes. Well, I’m backing up. I’m rethinking this as I answer the question because you are going to be, “Michael, they compete with a free product, what’s the big deal?”.

**Here’s the problem. There is one scarce resource in the world, that resource is[customer attention](<https://stratechery.com/2022/why-netflix-should-sell-ads/>). I mean, there’s a reason why Netflix doesn’t talk about any other streamers, they talk about YouTube.**

**MN:** YouTube and sleep.

**Yes. And I guess I could, okay fine — if you want to, now, I think it’s actually legitimate to say they compete with TikTok, but if you want to not go so far and say, okay, they compete for TV, it’s hard not to see how linear TV, YouTube, and these other services are in their market limiting it to the definition of their business model.
