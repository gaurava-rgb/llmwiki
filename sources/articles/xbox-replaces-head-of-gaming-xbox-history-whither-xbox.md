### Xbox Replaces Head of Gaming

From the [Wall Street Journal](<https://www.wsj.com/tech/microsofts-head-of-gaming-to-retire-after-38-years-at-company-7ab2459b>):

> Phil Spencer, Microsoft’s executive vice president and chief executive of gaming, is set to retire from the company after 38 years, a run during which he built the company up as a videogames powerhouse only to see it lose ground in recent years. Microsoft has promoted Asha Sharma, president of its CoreAI Product unit, to succeed Spencer as head of Microsoft Gaming, effective immediately, according to the company. Spencer, 58 years old, made the decision to retire last year, Chief Executive Satya Nadella told staff in an email viewed by The Wall Street Journal. He will stay with the company for the next several months through the transition.

There are conflicting reports as to whether Spencer’s decision was truly voluntary; meanwhile, there is a lot of drama around [this report in The Verge](<https://www.theverge.com/tech/883015/microsoft-xbox-new-ceo-shakeup-notepad>) about why Xbox President Sarah Bond wasn’t promoted to the top role:

> Instead of picking Bond for the role, Microsoft promoted Asha Sharma, a former Microsoft AI executive, to the top of Xbox. The decision to overlook Bond might have surprised many Xbox fans, but for the more than a dozen current and former Microsoft employees I’ve been speaking to, it’s felt inevitable in recent months…
>
> The pivot away from console, led by Bond, under Spencer’s direction, hasn’t gone well for Xbox. Microsoft’s Xbox hardware revenue has declined for three financial years in a row, and it looks like those declining revenues are going to continue throughout fiscal 2026. With Spencer’s retirement official, Microsoft is hitting the reset button on Bond’s Xbox strategy instead of embracing it further. Microsoft Gaming CEO Asha Sharma is now promising “the return of Xbox,” in a clear message to employees that the strategy over the past few years has not been working. “I want to return to the renegade spirit that built Xbox in the first place,” says Sharma.
>
> Xbox employees I’ve been speaking to have been concerned about the Sharma appointment, particularly because of her previous role as an AI executive at Microsoft. There is also concern about her lack of industry experience in entertainment and gaming. Sharma has been clear she’s not a gamer and has spent the weekend responding to people on X and even taking game recommendations. Some Xbox employees worry she’ll force AI into everything Xbox does, but Sharma was clearly ready for that reaction. “As monetization and AI evolve and influence this future, we will not chase short-term efficiency or flood our ecosystem with soulless AI slop,” said Sharma in her memo. “Games are and always will be art, crafted by humans, and created with the most innovative technology provided by us.”

Bond defenders have pointed out — correctly — that the “Xbox Everywhere” strategy predated Bond, and I’m sympathetic to claims that this article seemed to be throwing her under the bus. And, to be clear, it does appear that strategy hasn’t succeeded. The person who deserves blame, however, is not Bond, or even Spencer: the ultimate person responsible for the current state of Xbox is Satya Nadella, and before him, Bill Gates and Steve Ballmer.

### Xbox History

I have been a mostly consistent critic of the Xbox program, not for Xbox reasons, but for Microsoft reasons. From 2014’s [It’s Time to Kill Surface](<https://stratechery.com/2014/time-kill-surface/>):

> It is by focusing on the console world that one arrives at the conclusion that the Xbox, all things considered, is a success and something Microsoft can hang its hat on. In fact, that’s exactly what nearly all Microsoft employees, from executives to rank-and-file, do whenever questioned if Microsoft is innovative. “Look at the Xbox! Look at Kinect!” is their refrain.
>
> The problem is that winning at consoles is a small goal, one wholly different from the reason Xbox was created in the first place. For many years now Microsoft has been focused on “Three Screens and a Cloud,” the idea that they as a platform provider ought to have a presence on your desk, in your pocket, and in your living room, all tied together by the cloud. While that specific formulation arrived somewhere around 2009, that vein of thinking was central to Xbox’s creation; the console aspects were meant to be a trojan horse, giving people a reason-to-buy the Xbox (and not a Playstation); the more computer-type aspects would then be added over time until an Xbox was to living rooms what PCs were to every desk in every office and every home (running Microsoft software).
>
> It was this original goal that contributed to the current Xbox One disaster; Microsoft’s newest console is not only underpowered relative to the PS4, it’s also $100 more expensive (due to the mandatory Kinect), and launched with a terrible wave of publicity surrounding its always-on nature. The Kinect and connectedness were both included to help the Xbox One fulfill its goal of being the primary box in your entertainment system, controlling not just games but also live TV with your voice. Unfortunately, it doesn’t work that well for entertainment, even as it has hurt Microsoft’s ability to compete for console buyers. Thus, Microsoft has spent the last year walking back many of the main features of the console, including its DRM system, its connectedness, and, last week, offering the console without the supposedly essential Kinect to make it $100 cheaper. Now it’s the same price as a PS4, but still less powerful and with the same dark cloud.
>
> Here’s the bigger problem though: even if the Xbox One worked perfectly as an entertainment center, it would still cost $499. For those not good at math, that’s $400 more than an AppleTV, and completely unapproachable for anyone who does not care about gaming. In short, it is not enough to consider how the Xbox is doing relative to consoles; the Xbox must be evaluated based on how it is aligning with and contributing to Microsoft’s overall strategy, and in that light, it is an unmitigated disaster.

This is the Gates and Ballmer angle: Gates’ Xbox strategy was fundamentally flawed; Ballmer’s mistake was in not killing the platform when that flaw was clear. I understand why Ballmer didn’t, however: when I was at Microsoft from 2011–2013 it wasn’t just the Xbox division, but the entire company that held onto Xbox as the sole piece of evidence that the company could be cool and interesting to consumers; it had value as a morale booster, above and beyond the console business.

That meant it fell on Nadella to figure out what to do with the division that served no strategic purpose as merely a console. What is notable is that Nadella’s first big gaming move — buying Minecraft — not only didn’t have anything to do with Xbox, it actually seemed counter to the console concept. From [a 2014 Article](<https://stratechery.com/2014/microsofts-good-potentially-great-minecraft-acquisition/>):

> Minecraft, though, isn’t just a great financial decision; it’s a good strategic one as well that fits very nicely with Microsoft’s new vision as [outlined by Satya Nadella](<http://www.microsoft.com/en-us/news/ceo/index.html>):
>
>> At our core, Microsoft is the productivity and platform company for the mobile-first and cloud-first world. We will reinvent productivity to empower every person and every organization on the planet to do more and achieve more.
>
> At first glance, this statement seems to reference products like Office and Azure, but it also works very well for Minecraft. Minecraft is more than just a game: it’s a community, with a huge cloud component, developers, and, at its very essence, it’s about making things. What could be more productive than that?
>
> Moreover, like Office and Azure, Minecraft is truly cross-platform. It’s the best-selling paid-download game on both iOS and Android, and it also has a very popular PS3 version and a newly-released PS4 version, with a PS Vita port on the way. Xbox head Phil Spencer took care to note that this would remain the case:
>
>> Minecraft adds diversity to our game portfolio and helps us reach new gamers across multiple platforms. Gaming is the top activity across devices and we see great potential to continue to grow the Minecraft community and nurture the franchise. That is why we plan to continue to make Minecraft available across platforms – including iOS, Android and PlayStation, in addition to Xbox and PC.
>
> Here’s the thing, though: how much better would this acquisition look if Microsoft didn’t own Xbox at all?

Keep that thought in mind; I — and, I suspect Microsoft — will come back to it shortly. What was important over the last decade, however, is the idea that Microsoft’s strategy under Nadella would be about [software and services](<https://stratechery.com/2013/services-not-devices/>), which meant if Xbox were going to stick around, it had to fit in that paradigm; that’s where “Xbox Anywhere” came from. I wrote about the shift in [this 2021 Update](<https://stratechery.com/2021/starfield-an-xbox-exclusive-xbox-cloud-gaming-new-consumer-paradigms/>):

> Right there is the key terminology shift: you don’t buy an Xbox; rather, you become a member of Xbox. It follows, then, that the definition of an Xbox exclusive is no longer about needing a particular type of hardware, but rather a particular membership. The device is a commodity — at least in theory…
>
> What makes Microsoft unique is that the Xbox was a failure: the [original strategic goal](<https://stratechery.com/2014/microsoft-cloud-announcement-cordova-windows-rt-xbox-silliness/>) was to own the living room, not to simply be a games console, and [part of the recent press blitz](<https://www.nytimes.com/2021/06/10/technology/xbox-games.html>) is the revelation that Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella agreed: when he took over he wondered why the company shouldn’t just get out of the console business altogether. Xbox only made sense if it aligned with where Microsoft was going as a company, which was [away from device-centric exclusivity](<https://stratechery.com/2017/microsofts-monopoly-hangover/>) and towards over-arching subscription-based services.
>
> The end result is that Microsoft is uniquely positioned to pursue the cloud gaming opportunity: on one hand, they have genuine video game experience, including the ability to actually produce AAA games, but at the same time they aren’t beholden to the old business model. Quite the opposite, in fact: Xbox is very motivated to get to the new business model sooner rather than later.
>
> And even then it’s really hard. Microsoft still had to invest billions of dollars in the latest generation of consoles, even as it was inevitable that the company would be outsold by Sony; Microsoft still had to invest billions of dollars in buying Zenimax, a price that understates the cost given that making valuable IP exclusive would be short-term value destructive; and, of course, Microsoft actually has to make all of this stuff work across not just its own consoles and PCs (not there yet!) but also across mobile devices and TVs.
>
> I still like the strategy. Sony is going to win this generation, but looking 10 years out it seems likely that Xbox is going to be the default gaming service for some number of exclusives and cross-platform titles, with Sony slowly-but-surely reduced to a Nintendo-type position in the market: you’ll buy it for the exclusives, and perhaps hardware that is superior in specific ways, but it will be more of a second console, not your primary gaming device.

There is a part of me that regrets this last Update: I would be feeling pretty good right now if I could declare an unreserved “I told you so”; at the same time, my optimism about this new strategy was about the long-term — which hasn’t arrived yet — and that’s the biggest thing that has changed for Microsoft.

### Whither Xbox

If some of these excerpts look familiar, it’s because I used a few of them in [early 2024](<https://stratechery.com/2024/xbox-games-to-go-cross-platform-console-context-xboxs-final-strategy/>) when Xbox first announced that some of its games would be published on PlayStation; my take then was that the future of Xbox was as a game publisher and services provider, not as a console maker.

At that point, Microsoft seemed remarkably well-placed in AI: ChatGPT was dominant, and Microsoft had exclusive access to OpenAI models, putting Azure well ahead in the enterprise space. Fast forward two years and ChatGPT is still the biggest consumer AI service by a lot, and Microsoft still has exclusive access to OpenAI models, but those days are numbered; Azure, meanwhile, missed its numbers last quarter as Microsoft focused on its own products. [I thought that was the right decision](<https://stratechery.com/2026/microsoft-and-software-survival/>), but the broader takeaway is that Microsoft needs to devote all of its resources, from capex spending to engineering to executive attention, to winning in AI.

Given that, my reaction to this news was immediate and I thought obvious: Spencer was out because Xbox the console was out; a non-gaming executive was being brought in to lead the retreat to being the world’s largest gaming publisher, one that might eventually be spun off. Xbox Game Pass, meanwhile, would eventually be sunset, giving a direct path to recouping the cost of all of those acquisitions via the acquired publishers’ original business model: actually selling games for full price. After all, gaming isn’t growing, so Game Pass wasn’t going to make up for subsidizing whales through volume.

[This interview with Sharma and new Chief Content Officer Matt Booty](<https://www.windowscentral.com/gaming/xbox/exclusive-talking-to-new-xbox-ceo-asha-sharma-and-cco-matt-booty>) did give me some pause; Sharma said:

> Xbox players have thousands of dollars invested, in money and time too — it’s incredibly important for me to understand that and protect that…I am committed to ‘returning to Xbox,’ and that starts with console, that starts with hardware. You will hear more about that soon, we’ll have some announcements coming up. You will see us collectively investing here. We also know that there are a lot of players who aren’t on console or our hardware, and I want to deliver great games to them too. I need to learn more about what that can look like, what decisions were made, what we need to do going forward, and I want a little bit of time and space to do that.

Booty added:

> Our studio system is fully built around being first-party. We’re not built to just be a publisher…It is core to our partnership with the Microsoft platform, being involved in early hardware decisions — all the work we’ve done to get games like Gears of War running great on new devices like the Xbox Ally, and so on. It is embedded within our structure, we’re not backing away from that. We’re committed to being a first-party games publisher in partnership with our first-party platform team.

That sounds like a commitment to a console at first glance, but on a second read I think my initial instinct was right. Note that Sharma explicitly calls out players _not_ on an Xbox console; similarly, Booty’s hardware example is [the Xbox handheld made by Asus](<https://www.xbox.com/en-US/handhelds/rog-xbox-ally>), a third party, not a first-party hardware platform. This was framed in that article as a commitment to a console, but I don’t think it necessarily reads that way at all.

More importantly, however, a commitment to a future console simply doesn’t make sense for Microsoft. The original Xbox strategy stopped making sense a long time ago; the reboot didn’t work, or, if you want to be generous, hasn’t worked yet. Most importantly, Microsoft’s priorities are now elsewhere, and that means that Xbox the console isn’t going to get any more runway. The brand will live on in publishing, and there might be future consoles from 3rd-party OEMs; I am extremely skeptical, however, that Microsoft will make a 1st-party console ever again.
