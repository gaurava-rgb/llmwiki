Good morning,

Today’s (normally scheduled) Stratechery Interview is with Flighty founder and CEO [Ryan Jones](<https://x.com/rjonesy>). Jones builds my favorite iOS app: [Flighty](<https://flighty.com/>). The holidays are coming, and a lot of you will be traveling with winter conditions looming: the app you need is Flighty, which tracks your flights, gives updates before the airlines, and tracks your history.

More broadly, Jones is an App Store veteran. His first app, [Weather Line](<https://weatherlineapp.com/>), came out in the middle of the initial App Store boom, and shot to the top of the charts. He has a unique vantage point into how the App Store has evolved, and appreciates its strengths while critiquing its weaknesses.

In this interview, we discuss Jones’ background, his experience with Weather Line, and how App Store capabilities directly impact the viability of an app. Then we cover Flighty and its evolution, from data display to prediction, and the potential for a social network for flying and why Apple and AI are threats. The App Store and the app market are one of the oldest focus areas for Stratechery, long before it was cool, and I enjoyed getting Jones’ point of view on what it takes to succeed not just today, but how that compares to the last fifteen years.

What happened was they send everyone to the field for a year when you get hired. They’re like, “You go learn what actually happens on the ground, you can’t manage from here if you haven’t actually done those things”, I lived in the middle of East Texas for a year, did a ton of fracking, some drilling rigs, all the stuff you see in the movies, if you will. Because of the boom, basically on day one what they want you to do, what you’re supposed to do, is ride around in a truck with another guy for a month and then figure out what you want to do after that. They were basically, I showed up and they said, “There’s a frack job starting tomorrow, go run it”.

Which a frack job is, whatever, a million dollars a day being spent and 250 guys on the ground and $100 million worth of equipment, just a massive operation, I somehow got thrown into that right away and had no idea what I was doing, an insane amount of responsibility. Like a young guy, I would always work nights. I would work nights, being the leader of 50, 60, 40-year-old guys who had tons of expertise and they looked to me to answer questions. I just learned really fast to be like, “Well, what would you do, what do you think”, and yadda-yadda. After a little while, I got decent at it, but it was a ton of — I really learned quick to manage and to deal with high-risk/reward situations. Fracking is, to do a very short version of it, you’re pumping water and sand into the ground, and it takes around three minutes to get through the pipe and into the rock formation, and so you’re monitoring the pressure at the surface. You are making all of your decisions three minutes in advance, based on the pressure at the surface, about what you think is going to happen downhole three minutes later. If you get it wrong, bad things happen. You basically have to make a very expensive and potentially dangerous decision every 30 seconds, to keep going or not. I learned to deal with a lot of big decision-making with a lot on the line really early.

The other thing that was happening, so this was 2007, 2008, a little thing called the iPhone, at the exact same time this was happening. In between frack jobs, I would get on Twitter, read about, “Oh, my gosh, the software doesn’t just fall from the sky, there are humans out in California who write software and make these things”. It was just a total — I don’t want to say total, but it was, let’s just say, a total, “Oh, my gosh, people actually make software, someone actually has to do that”, it had never really crossed my mind before.

**This was very late. It wasn’t, “I got a computer when I was in sixth grade and typed out BASIC programs”, it was a very come-late-to-the-industry moment.**

**RJ:** 100%, yeah. I guess I can draw the line in hindsight, I was very engineering oriented and I liked to take things apart, figure out how things work, do things on my own. Then I minored in architecture, so I definitely have always had a little bit of taste or aesthetic to things. I don’t like just build it just to build it, I like it to look great and all those things and work great in the end, you’re absolutely right.

It just was like, “Wow, these things”. Following [Paul Haddad](<https://tapbots.social/@paul>) and [Loren Brichter](<https://x.com/lorenb>) and these people who made these original [Tweetie iPhone apps](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tweetie>) that were just — it never crossed my mind that that was possible.

**I can definitely relate. I grew up in small-town Wisconsin, and I was interested in technology, I was the only one around me that was, but it never occurred to me to go work in tech, it wasn’t even in the realm of possibility, that was something that happened out in San Francisco and California. Yeah, I totally know the perspective that you’re talking about, one that I think is very foreign to a lot of our listeners, so you and I may be commiserating between the two of us here, but it’s very hard to explain if you didn’t grow up just completely out of that world.**

**RJ:** Yeah, you feel like you’re watching it from afar. [Diggnation](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digg>) was a big part of me learning about tech, just anything that I could do to get involved in that world or try to understand what was going on on the ground.

**You’re working for the oil company, the iPhone is interesting — there’s a few twists and turns, I feel like that are coming in this story.**

**RJ:** That’s right. The good news about the oil industry was all the responsibility, and it’s a great industry to work in, they educate you, and they work what’s called a 9/80, which is kind of a funny variant of this [996 thing](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/996_working_hour_system>) that you hear in Silicon Valley now. They work nine days every two weeks for 80 hours total, which is a very dumb way of explaining you get every other Friday off. There’s just a lot of really cool cultural stuff, and they don’t — I gave that example to say you don’t grind your ass off.

Anyway, so it was hard to leave to one extent, but I had the distinct feeling that I’m going to look up 10 years from now and think what would’ve happened, I had to try, I couldn’t bear the thought of looking up and just staying in oil and never having tried starting something in tech. At that time, it was all apps.

#### Weather Line

**So is this when****[Weather Line](<https://weatherlineapp.com/>) got off the ground?**

**RJ:** You got it. Yeah, it was all apps. Weather apps were somehow kind of a battleground, if you believe it. This is very, very early. Things started slow, a little bit like they are with the AI stuff now, it was crawl, walk, run. I’ve tried multiple times in my life to code, not my thing, can’t do it, so I found a partner just remotely on Twitter, online, and built Weather Line.

The goal that I had set for myself was if I can get this app into the Top 100 in the App Store — which you’ll remember back in the day, it was like everyone’s watching the top charts — if I can get into the Top 100 in the App Store, that means I have an idea what I’m talking about. That means I can be a product guy, if you will, that’s the thing that I thought I would fit best into in Silicon Valley.

**Just to back up, Weather Line was actually an awesome app. The description’s in the title, you showed the weather as a line, so you could see the graph of temperature, of precipitation, of all those sorts of things.[John Gruber](<https://x.com/gruber>), I was just actually looking this up, [had a very effusive endorsement of Weather Line](<https://daringfireball.net/linked/2013/12/10/weather-line>), I’m looking at this back in 2013. But was that just sort of the take — “There’s a bunch of weather lines out there and they all are difficult to read, I just want to see where it’s going”?**

**RJ:** The idea was can I get it in the Top 100, and we launched and it hit No. 17 in the App Store, and Gruber was a big help there.

**Just for context, this is 2011, is that right? Or 2013, somewhere around then?**

**RJ:** That’s right.

**What does No. 17 in the App Store mean? What sort of numbers are we talking about?**

**RJ:** Oh, I mean, that’s like Instagram, but these were huge apps, I should look up a screenshot.

**Millions of downloads?**

**RJ:** I don’t know about millions. Hundreds of thousands.

**Hundreds of thousands, yeah.**

**RJ:** Yeah, per day.

**Yeah, that’s a lot. It’s so interesting, because I do want to compare and contrast when we’re going to get[to Flighty](<https://flighty.com/>) in a little bit, but that was, to your point, app mania. That was the thing to do, people were downloading apps left and right. Was this a thing where you just launched it and it took off? What were the drivers of getting to those kinds of numbers, hundreds of thousands a day?**

**RJ:** This is kind of where part of my life about Twitter being massive comes in. As someone who was away from the core of the scene, I was talking to people, trying to learn about things on Twitter, and basically was very good at covering the iPhone from a normal person point of view, a very, very lame version of Stratechery. I was talking about what is the strategy that they’re executing, what will they probably do next year, what’s going to change with iOS, and somehow got a following from that. Small, but lots of really cool people, like [[Marc] Andreessen](<https://x.com/pmarca>) and Gruber and you and [M.G. Siegler](<https://x.com/mgsiegler>), lots of, I thought, really cool people that started listening to what I was saying about iPhone stuff.

I really subscribed to the, “If you want something, just provide value to the other person and just see what happens, let it go”, my success rate of cold emailing people and saying, “Hey, I want to work for you”, or whatever is low, but if I do some work that I know you’re probably already doing and say, “Hey, I did this thing for you”, it’s worked very well for me in my career. I would send a note to people and say, “Here’s a 20-second video, it’s the most accurate data in the world”. At that time, it was the Dark Sky, Dark Sky had just come out, so it was a 60-minute rain prediction thing, and we had this unique take that shows everything you need to know about your weather in one view. People really loved it and gravitated towards it, so it just took off, literally organic.

**This is what we’re going to get to with Flighty, is data, finding good data sources. I mean, it’s almost like drilling for oil, if I could make an analogy. Is there an analogy there? What’s unique about that? How is it that that actually is the core of what you’ve — to be clear, you have two massive hits now, so you’ve repeated it. Why are they both about data?**

**RJ:** I think it just goes back to being super inquisitive, and just digging and digging and digging until I get to the bottom of something. I didn’t understand why no one was doing that visual representation on weather with that data, I got to the bottom of the Internet and I didn’t have an answer, so I did it. Same with Flighty. I didn’t understand why no one was doing a great flight tracking app. Every time I was taking a flight, I was digging around for where’s the most accurate data, what’s going on with the plane, all these things that I’m sure we’ll talk about. I eventually got to the bottom of the Internet and I had no answer. Just as an entrepreneur, it ate away at me enough where I said, finally, “I have to do this, the opportunity seems so big, I don’t understand it. If I’m wrong, I need to find out why nobody else cares about this”.

**Back in 2011, 2013,[Apple did not yet support subscriptions](<https://stratechery.com/2013/adobes-subscription-model-why-platform-owners-should-care/>). What was the business model for Weather Line?**

**RJ:** It oscillated between $3 and $5 upfront, which was terrible.

**Right** ,**because weather data is an ongoing expense, right?**

**RJ:** That’s right, it was really tough, it wasn’t a huge financial success. That’s how I accidentally ended up at Apple. I was in Austin, we were living in Houston at the time and I was in Austin and a big fan of Apple, and just looked up if there was any people in the alumni network who worked at Apple. A guy immediately replied, “Dude, we’re not hiring right now”, I was like, “No, you don’t understand, I finally left the oil industry, I have an app that people know of, I don’t want to get a job, I just want to come meet people”. Met with him, hung out, he a month later said, “Hey, will you apply for this job?”, I said, “No”, then he kept pestering me, and then I just got curious.

**I mean, this sounds like a real lesson. You had to go in and not want to get a job, is how you managed to get a job.**

**RJ:** It honestly is a recurring thing in my life, of I just kind of put myself out there, meet people, offer help and value when I can, I’m not very good at saying, “Hey, I want to come work with you”, and then just banging down the door until I get it done. I’m more of a spread helpfulness everywhere and watch what happens kind of person.

**Apple in Austin, especially back then, was mostly all operations stuff, right?**

**RJ:** That’s right. It was the year before they moved all the operations there, so at that time it was iPhone, iPad, and then while I was there, Watch was launched. I ended up staying for two years because I legitimately enjoyed it, worked on iPhone and iPad and Mac operations and then during that time, Weather Line, you’re not allowed to update it, you can’t have an app, which drove me nuts because you’re allowed to be a musician.

**Oh, as an Apple employee.**

**RJ:** Yeah. You’re allowed to be a musician and have something on iTunes back at the day, or you’re allowed to do basically anything, but you cannot have an app in the App Store. And so I couldn’t really update it, I kind of just had to let it go as it was and then I decided to switch jobs, and subscriptions happened is literally the truth, so that gave me the opening to do Weather Line 2 and to move it into subscriptions, to do the whole thing. Back 10 years ago, everyone was super panicking anytime one of their apps moved from paid upfront to a subscription, but that made it viable that I could work on it and make a living off of it more or less.

**Got it.[So Weather Line was acquired](<https://weatherlineapp.com/blog/weather-line-is-being-acquired>), you have not disclosed who acquired it, there’s rumors out there about big companies that launch an app shortly thereafter that looks a lot like yours, but people can go figure that out for themselves. But was this ultimately a happy ending or was it, “I’ll push this as far as I can”? What’s the mood on the end story here?**

**RJ:** Very happy ending. I was happy to get out of the weather game and it was kind of an unsolicited buyer who, like you said, had a strong need for it. So they were willing to pay more than I’d ever dreamed and I was happy to do it and to consult for them thereafter to make sure it was a good, clean takeover.

#### Flighty

**So I think even before it was acquired though, you started Flighty, right? Do I have the timeline right?**

**RJ:** You got it, you nailed it. Yeah.

**Give me the Flighty origin story.**

**RJ:** Okay.

**And again, I think it is my favorite app on my entire iPhone. Or it’s on the Mac too, which I actually use the Mac app a fair bit, but just I love it. So anyhow, give me the story.**

**RJ:** Yeah. I mean, I don’t want to pass over that, thanks so much. We’ll get to it, but it’s definitely been a dream come true. I can’t believe that people like you and people like whoever, Gruber, and whoever else talk about it being a favorite app. It’s crazy.

Origin story. So while at Apple and while at the product marketing company I’d worked at for two years after Apple, I was kind of an entrepreneur in residence there and also helping them get better at tech. I knew I was going to do another app and subscriptions had become a thing and the other couple things that kind of happened was there was an original flight tracking app called FlightTrack Pro that was an early App Store darling, they were on stage during WWDC, they were in commercials, Jobs mentioned it every once in a while. Back then it was, “Oh my God, you can look at a status of a flight in your pocket”, it’s just crazy. That app [ended up getting acquired](<https://www.expediagroup.com/media/media-details/2010/Expedia-to-Acquire-Mobile-Travel-Apps-Powerhouse-Mobiata/default.aspx>) by, funny enough, another one of your guests, there’s no way you would put this together.

**Expedia?**

**RJ:** [Yeah, Dara](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-uber-ceo-dara-khosrowshahi-about-aggregation-and-autonomy/>).

**Oh, there you go.** **Makes sense.**

**RJ:** So there’s a press release out there of Dara at Expedia talking about how great it is to bring this app on board.

**But after that, there was nothing.**

**RJ:** That sometimes happen, they drove it into the ground. It’s actually the only time I’m actually aware in my little small world of rolling out a new version and then they were like, “Oh, shit, this thing is not good”, and they re-rolled out the old version, [kind of like Sonos wishes they could do](<https://www.theverge.com/2025/1/13/24342282/sonos-app-redesign-controversy-full-story>), but they walked themselves down that path. So they basically cut their losses eventually.

It left a void and I was basically like, “I had a list of here’s 10 ideas that could be interesting and I’m just running through them in my head, what am I going to do?”, and I’m not going to lie, this was getting to the point in my life where I’d gotten married and we were talking about having kids and I knew all the stories of, “Oh man, you’ve got to do it before you have kids, it’s a ton of work”. So I had put a ton of pressure on myself of whatever the next thing is has to be the thing for me and Flighty, or the idea of flights was way up at the top of the list, probably the leading candidate.

So I just spent six months digging into every corner, like I said, dig to the bottom of the Internet, figure out why, “Who’s done this before?”, “Why has it failed?”, “What’s something I’m missing?”. Go talk to the person who started it before, [go talk to anyone](<https://x.com/rjonesy/status/834547482554204160?s=20>) and everyone and try to figure this out. And when you’re just so locked in on something like that, every conversation or podcast you listen to or YouTube thing you watch or whatever, there’s something that’s applicable and you just kind of — I was super honed in on it.

What happened was I was on New Year’s Day, 2018, I got stuck in the Fort Lauderdale Airport, and we were down the Keys for New Year’s Eve. So I drove my 88-year-old grandma up that long highway on the Keys where you’re like laser-focused so you’re not going in the water at any second, so I’m not doing my usual super nerd research of like, “Where’s the plane coming from? Is the airport backed up?”, all of these things. And we get there and it’s six hours delayed because the plane is still in Chicago and it’s January and so I was like, “That’s it, I can’t stand this anymore, that was so dumb”.

**“I could have been down in the beach for the last six hours!”**

**RJ:** I could have been at the beach, or at a minimum not sitting in the airport, I could have stayed nearby and not checked in the rental car and all that stuff. Plus my grandma is sitting in an airport Chili’s for six hours, as an 88-year-old, probably not very comfortable. So I literally tweeted, “F this, I’ll make it, who wants to make this with me?”. And the original team that’s still the team together today responded to that, there was about 40 responses. I talked to all the people that replied, had an Excel spreadsheet of like, “What could this guy bring?” “What could this girl bring?”, all that. [And that was how it started](<https://x.com/rjonesy/status/959475268976103425?s=20>).

**So right off the bat, you just sort of listed the key things, “Where’s the previous plane and what’s happening at the airport?”. Was that just the core of the idea? I mean, right now, you do all these amazing things with these flight predictions and then you have other ideas in the pipeline, you can see your friend’s flights, all these bits and pieces, but was it just really — was that it? That was the core of what you wanted to deliver?**

**RJ:** That’s the core, yeah. What I did was after I dug to the bottom of the Internet, I was like, “I have these kind of set of questions”, I wrote a Google Forms survey and I said, “If you’re interested in this, fill this out”, and put it on my own Twitter, and I went and put it [on FlyerTalk](<https://www.flyertalk.com/>) and other places and got a ton of replies, we got over a thousand people just filling it out, and it confirmed some hunches.

One was everyone was doing the manual, “Where’s my plane?”, thing that I thought. And for context for everybody listening, that’s the number one cause of delays is your inbound plane is late to your flight and back then, the joke I often say is websites or apps would have a button that says, “Where’s my plane?”, So you just open your flight and tap, “Where’s my plane? Where’s my plane?”, you’d have to tap it seven times to get to the actual one. That was one key thing that I thought, “There’s no reason this can’t be done by technology, this is crazy”.

The other, which was interesting to hear in the survey of people confirm was design. A lot of the similar products that were out at the moment were from pretty technically-minded people who weren’t great at design type stuff and this is such a big, hairy problem, and it’s really difficult to get it down into a clean interface that’s not just showing you a ton of data.

**There’s so much data that it becomes overwhelming to cogently design.**

**RJ:** Exactly. And then the last piece was after you take the flight, there were a couple websites out there, like a flight diary type thing where after the flight’s over, you go to a different place and put in your flight information so that you could see, “Here’s the places I’ve been”, and so the initial combination of insights was, we can do the automatic, “Where’s my plane?”, we can nail design and no one’s out there that’s existing is even close to us, and the users are actually saying that’s a critical thing, and then we’ll log it to a flight history type of view combining two or three services into one.

**Got it. So the flight history thing,[it delights everyone](<https://x.com/benthompson/status/1990227390497210564?s=20>) and so that was just something that you saw from the very beginning. Spotify does their annual year-end review sort of thing, Flighty does its year-end review. I think it’s launching tomorrow as we’re talking yesterday as you’re listening to this. Yeah, no, it’s a great insight.**

**RJ:** It is. My partners/co-founders give me a lot of grief for saying random percentages, so I’ll do that now. I thought it would be 5% or 10% of the draw of the app, it’s probably 30% or 35%.

**It’s still on my list to go back through all my old emails and add flights from before Flighty started, I think I was a very early user. So I think I go back to 2018 or 2019, somewhere around there and I want to go back to past years. It irks me that I know I did all this flying that’s not in there. The problem is I changed flights all the time and I have a lot of emails that are not valid flights, so I can’t just bulk import.**

**RJ:** Changing is tough. People who travel a lot and change flights is a real pain point.

**Yeah.**

**RJ:** To your past point comment, one thing that’s super interesting, I think we’ll get into this, but we’re super product-focused. That’s the thing that I think sets us apart and makes us is one of the reasons it succeeded so well in the market. And I have this kind of, like you, strategic, “How can I fit all the puzzle pieces on the playing field together to most benefit our users and our business at the same time?”. And one of them was this, “What can we do with the stats?”, with the, “Here’s your end of year flight statistics”, that people could share? And it took us, I think, three iterations to get it right.

The one you see now today, which will look obvious, is a passport. It’s basically a digital representation of an actual passport, and there’s a really cool little button you can press that makes it look like it’s under black light. We spent a ton of time designing it, so that looks incredible. **RJ:** To a certain degree, I have to play the game that’s on the field, I can’t just pretend like it’s 2010 over here with how we’re behaving. If you think about it actually from a point of view of like a society—

**What does that mean? What’s something you have to do because it’s just the way it’s done?**

**RJ:** Our pricing page, everybody does the, “What’s the annual price versus the monthly price?”, and the rules very clearly say you’re supposed to list it a certain way and everybody kind of gets as close to that line as you can and what’s considered a marketing push notification, people get as close to that line as you can. And I wish it wasn’t allowed because I wish my competitors wouldn’t do it and then therefore I couldn’t do it.

But at a certain point — I think we compete in a classy and fair way, we don’t go over the top with all these things, but at a certain point we have to play the game on the field. But if you think about it from a society point of view, imagine if there were laws and rules, but when you broke them, someone just told you like, “Hey, dude, you broke that rule, try again”, it doesn’t work like that. You have to have some sort of—

**Consequences.**

**RJ:** Consequence is exactly the word I was looking for. The consequence is the actual deterrent, the fear of the actual consequence happening and there’s ways that they could do this where it’s like de-ranked in search or your features are taken down. You don’t have to kick an app out of the App Store [Uber style](<https://www.nytimes.com/2017/04/23/technology/travis-kalanick-pushes-uber-and-himself-to-the-precipice.html>) for doing these things, but if there’s no actual punishment or consequence, without doing it explicitly, you’re kind of incentivizing people to push the rules.

**Because your competitor will do it.**

**RJ:** Correct, and so it’s an arms race.

**It’s funny because it’s really tough because I completely agree with you. Can Apple actually provide value to the App Store, please, if you’re going to control everything? But then that runs smack into the, “They have so much control”, “It’s unfair”, “Dictator of the world”, antitrust, etc., which I guess is sort of a takeaway in its own right.**

**RJ:** Yeah, to me, maybe I’m too simplistic, but it’s a very simple, as soon as the story went from iPhone growth to Services growth, this is what we all feared, and I think they’ve done a pretty decent job of restraining themselves. It could be a lot worse, but I wish it was dialed back a decent amount.

Another thing that drives developers nuts is refunds, they are very strict with allowing refunds, and a lot of times I want them to give a refund, I’m like, “Please give this user a refund”, but I don’t have any say in that. We have one little API we can communicate with where we can give them, I think, 200 characters of text and other than that, I have no say in it.

**Interesting. Usually, because you would think the problem would be someone uses it for a week and then asked for a refund, it’s very unfair, but actually you have the opposite problem.**

**RJ:** That’s right. Or people literally just don’t mean to download or they don’t mean to subscribe and they do, or they bought the family plan instead of the whatever plan, I don’t know the actual rules, no one really does, but you have around 14 days to ask for a refund and they’ll basically say yes, but there’s caveats to that. If they see a user that’s asking for a ton of refunds all the time, they don’t grant them as much. So there’s a lot of value they can bring to the ecosystem because they have that point of view, but they just kind of haven’t gone to the point where I can say, “I really want this person to have a refund unless there’s something you see on your end”, that’s one that drives us nuts.

I’ve actually had to Apple Pay users, because we have a really high lifetime price, we have a $300 lifetime plan for people that hate subscriptions or want to pay once with their business credit card or stuff like that, so when that purchase goes wrong, people are like, “Dude, this is not a $5 thing, you just charged me $300 and I’m not getting the value I thought I was going to get or it doesn’t work the way I thought it worked”, anything, and I’ve had to just Apple Pay people $250, it’s crazy.

**Wow, that’s amazing. Well, anything else on the list? You’re czar, you make refunds easier, you actually enforce the rules. All things considered, that’s not that many things. I really agree on the consequences one because that has really metastasized into just a horrible user experience in many respects, but is there anything else big picture? How can this be a better economy?**

**RJ:** I think I have the same view as most people about the anti-steering, that was insane. It seems like that’s getting fixed to a certain degree, I do wish that they would enable our external payments to be even easier and people like you and me know this, but not everyone may know that they’ve actually, in certain economy, in certain countries, in certain situations, you can do it a lot more now, but the numbers are turning out that it’s not that clear cut of a win. So it’s to their point that, “Hey, look, we’re providing almost 30% of value”, I think the numbers are a little lower than that where developers are able to basically do all the things that Apple provides and the funnel works out the way such that they’re getting, let’s say, whatever, 22, 25% is what they’re ending up paying. So Apple’s point is getting proven, they are providing that amount of value, but right now there’s no kind of outlet for people to find that out on their own, and so everyone’s just super aggravated.

#### Competition and Future Opportunities

**Would you ever do an Android app?**

**RJ:** That’s a great question, we think about it all the time, and I think this is one of the benefits of being a small team. We really have to do what do we think is the next best thing for our user base at any given time and we meet up every quarter, we all do a little dog fooding and we fly somewhere and the three of us, the original team, we meet up and strategize for what we want to do the next quarter, or we look further out than that.

And we always talk about Android. It’s always mentioned, and there’s always stuff that is higher value, higher upside for us and higher upside for the users that’s at the top of the list for the iOS.

**It feels like Android is not a viable enterprise for a small team, that’s a commitment for a larger company.**

**RJ:** That’s right. The way that we would have to do it if we did it would be get a small team, one or two people that more or less followed right behind us with iOS, so we wouldn’t be able to plan it and execute it simultaneously, we’d have to continue with what we’re doing today, and then once it works well on iOS, have a couple people apply it to Android.

I, of course, didn’t say this, but I heard recently at a conference, people say numbers like 50% of their effort goes to Android and 20% of the revenue comes from Android, and 50% of the effort goes to Android and 70 to 80% of the problems come from Android. So I think people who aren’t in the weeds may not realize the extent to which those markets are very different. A good example would be we probably also just aren’t a natural fit for Android, we’re $60 a year premium subscription, and it’s pretty commonly known-

**You don’t see a lot of Android phones in the front of the plane, put it that way.**

**RJ:** That’s another thing. And it’s pretty commonly accepted that you have to be more of a free model if you go to Androids, so it’s a little bit antithetical to who we are.

**Yeah. One of the things you always talk about is one of your biggest customer bases continues to be pilots and flight attendants and things like that. Is that still the case?**

**RJ:** Yeah, it is. It’s probably 25 to 30% of our users. We don’t have an amazing read on it because we’re super high on privacy, but based on all the indicators we have, customer support and places where you can tell us what you do, I think it’s 25 to 30%. And it’s actually a great word of mouth mechanism for us. If a crew member knows something about the plane that’s going to happen before the other crew members, you can bet your ass they’re going to tell the pilot and other crew members. And it spreads like wildfire through the crew, and then the crew will mention it at the front of the plane, like you said, and the people at the front of the plane will say, “Wait, how did you know that?”, and then it just spreads that way.

We have a lot of really cool stories too about someone sitting in 1B who mentions, “Oh shoot, we just got delayed”, and the crew and the pilot hear the comment and say, “No, we’re not”, and then five minutes later like, “How the heck did you know that?”. We’ve had a couple scenarios where a plane gets diverted and the person on the plane got the diverted alert and mentioned it in front of a crew member who had no idea yet, so there’s been some very cool things that come out of having the fastest thing.

**I’ve shown the app for crew members before, for sure, this is definitely something that’s happened.**

**Who’s your biggest threat? Is it Apple?****[They’re building in some of these features](<https://9to5mac.com/2025/09/02/ios-26-gives-boarding-passes-three-new-features-for-your-next-flight/>) as far as flight tracking. Is it the airline apps themselves? Who do you worry about, if anyone?**

**RJ:** Am I allowed to turn this question around on you? I would love to know what you think it is.

**Oh, I’d say it’s Apple. They’ll give a bare, basic, sufficient, they show my flights. The problem with the airlines is you have to download the airline app, and presume you’re flying multiple airlines. I guess the question I would have, I think I know the answer, but which airline has the best app?**

**RJ:** I think United’s is pretty good.

**Yeah. United’s is miles ahead of everyone. I made a claim a while ago that United is actually the best airline in the world—**

**RJ:** That’s one of those things you’re not allowed to say out loud, you can’t say that out loud.

**Because their tech stack is so much better than everybody else. Delta’s getting better but it’s still not even close, and the Asian airlines are a total disaster.**

**RJ:** I agree with you 100%, but if you say it out loud, people go ballistic, as you know.

**What’s next for you? You had a list of 10 apps. Are you going to do another one or are you a Flighty lifer?**

**RJ:** Close friends ask me that. Every once in a while, they’ll say, “What’s the next app?” — Flighty is the next app, it’s taken over, the potential and the product market fit is beyond what I had dreamed of, and I love working on it every day.

**Do you fear AI, that my AI is just going to tell me in my ear, “Hey, your flight’s delayed”, and I don’t need to look at a well-designed app anymore?**

**RJ:** It’s definitely something I think about, it’s at the top of the list of the fears, I do also believe in our ability to be the people that can execute on that, we have a ton of data. One of the reasons we’ve succeeded to the degree we have, I think, is we’re small and we all live this problem all day, every day, and we’re super focused on it, no other companies are doing that. So I think if anyone figures out how to apply that in the right way, there’s no reason why it can’t be us first, but it’s definitely a sea change that we are aware of and keeping tabs on. But I don’t think natural word of mouth growth with no marketing will happen forever, I’m not drawing a straight line through all of our metrics.

**That’ll be the next department is an actual marketing department.**

**RJ:** Yeah, so we’ll see when you can see things start to bend, the curves start to bend, and that’ll be an interesting time. But right now, it’s not a hold onto the rocket ship in terms of Silicon Valley exponential, but in terms of a bootstrap world, we’re doing everything we can to just keep shipping great features.

**Do you think anyone could build an app like Flighty today?**

**RJ:** Is your question more competition or could someone actually do it?

**No. Like, someone, “I want to be like Ryan, this is very inspiring, I want to build an app”.**

**RJ:** Oh, I don’t know, I don’t know, man. [CalAI is this small team](<https://apps.apple.com/us/app/cal-ai-calorie-tracker/id6480417616>) who’s had a huge breakout success story, it’s more or less you take a picture of a plate of food and it tells you how many calories it is. And I mean, they’re making, if the numbers they say are true, $40 million, big, big annual numbers for an app and that’s a 18-month-old app.

So to answer your question, the AI stuff is a complete unlock. Just like what’s happening at Facebook and OpenAI and Perplexity and all these huge AI companies, how do we actually apply that thing? That 100% is happening at the app level. There’s just things you’ve never thought of and things we’re all thinking of as toys that can be whole products and verticalized and specialized, so I think someone could do it. I hate to say it like this, but 90% chance if it’s not an AI-native, AI-first thing, you’re in an uphill battle. That’s the sea change that has unlocked avenues that weren’t possible before. When I started, it was subscriptions allowed you to monetize premium flyers, and I think AI has just changed the abilities, that’s the sea change that’s right now.

**Ryan Jones, maker of my favorite app, thanks for coming out Stratechery.**

**RJ:** Thanks for having me, Ben.
