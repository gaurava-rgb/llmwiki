Apple can ask Google to tweak aspects of how the Gemini model works, but otherwise Apple can finetune Gemini on its own so that it responds to queries the way Apple prefers, the person involved in the project said. (In the current prototype of Apple’s Gemini-based system, AI answers don’t include any branding related to Google or Gemini, this person said.)

One set of answers Apple hopes to improve with Gemini is the ones related to so-called world knowledge, or factual questions such as describing the population of a country or scientific information, this person said.

Previously, some answers simply included links to websites that might hold the answer. In the new Gemini-based regime, Apple will give a conversational answer and cite where the answers came from, similar to the way Google’s Gemini chatbot or **OpenAI's ChatGPT** answer certain questions, this person said.

Another common set of questions Siri has historically struggled with involved emotional support, such as when a customer tells the voice assistant it is feeling lonely or disheartened. In the Gemini-based version, Siri will give more thorough conversational responses the way ChatGPT and Gemini do, this person said.

Siri will also be able to handle other types of tasks, such as creating a **Notes** document with a cooking recipe or information about the top causes of drug addiction, for instance.

While certain common Siri tasks such as setting a timer, reminder or sending a specific text message to a phone contact will continue to be powered by technology stored on Apple devices, the new version of Siri would also be able to handle instances in which the customer’s question isn’t clearly understood.

For example, if someone asks Siri to send a text message to their mother or sister, but the customer doesn’t store their names that way in their contacts, the Gemini-based Siri would could search through their messages to figure out which of their contacts is most likely to be their mother or sister, this person said.

### A Downer For OpenAI

The Apple-Google AI partnership has been a long time coming. In July, we [reported](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/apple-losing-ground-ai-talent-war-just-money>) that Apple had been considering using models from Google, OpenAI and **Anthropic** to power an improved version of Siri, and in November, Bloomberg reported that Apple would pay about $1 billion annually to Google for the AI to power Siri.

Apple spokespeople didn’t comment about the figure but told us the new Google deal wouldn’t affect its agreement with OpenAI to tap ChatGPT for more complex queries that users might ask Siri.

However, from what we can tell, the new Google deal would thoroughly marginalize ChatGPT.

In the current OpenAI arrangement, Apple can route a Siri query to OpenAI’s models in cases where its own models are unable to answer a question. Siri can also display a prompt asking Apple device users if they want ChatGPT to answer a query.

But we [questioned whether the deal was remotely beneficial to OpenAI in a column last spring](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/good-apple-deal-openai>), and a person familiar with the deal said that, in fact, it didn’t drive much traffic to OpenAI.

That’s a letdown for OpenAI: in 2024, CEO **Sam Altman** and other OpenAI leaders [hoped the Apple partnership could drive billions of dollars in additional revenue to the startup](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-ceo-cements-control-as-he-secures-apple-deal?rc=c48ukx>) and they also hoped it would supplant the longstanding alliance Apple has had with Google in everything from search on Safari to Apple’s heavy use of Google cloud servers.

Instead, the Google-Apple alliance is even deeper now and the AI-related deal is more expansive than the earlier Apple-OpenAI arrangement.

Given that Apple’s AI answers aren’t expected to feature the Google Gemini branding, how much this deal will practically help Google (besides the alleged $1 billion annual payment, which isn’t bad in and of itself!) remains to be seen. Of course, even if Apple doesn’t share data with Google about how people use the new features, the deal symbolically cements Google’s place as a consumer-AI leader alongside OpenAI.

We imagine that if chatbots end up being a major advertising or commerce generator, Apple’s version of Gemini could incorporate such revenue-generating features and the company would effectively share revenue from them, similar to the way Google shares search-ad revenue with Apple for the privilege of powering search queries on Safari.

As for OpenAI, it’s [working hard on its own consumer devices](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-ramps-audio-ai-efforts-ahead-device>), so it’ll eventually get its own chance to get its AI in front of consumers without having to deal with the iPhone middleman. But that won’t be for a long time.

### 1X’s Humanoid World Model

**1X Technologies** , the company behind the **Neo** humanoid home robot, said Monday that it has developed a world model, which simulates real-world environments and aims to approximate the physics of how objects move and humans interact with their surroundings, to power its robots. When the robot receives an instruction, the world model can generate videos of different ways the robot could complete the task. The robot can then act based on those visualizations.

“The first thing that kind of blew my mind on this when I tested this model,” said CEO **Bernt Børnich** said in an interview, was that he “told the robot, ‘can you pick the post-it note there off the wall, and read what’s on it?’” And the robot did the job. That’s a task that the model would not have seen in its training data, Børnich said.

The world model is primarily trained on videos collected from the internet and replaces 1X’s previous AI system, which was based more on language than video. World models are a small but growing area of interest for robotic companies. While 1X is using the model to guide the robots decisions in real-time, world models can also be used as [simulated training environments](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/runway-luma-target-sales-robotics-companies>).

One of the drawbacks of using video models in robotics is that they can hallucinate actions that are physically unrealistic. “The classical video models cheat a lot,” said Børnich. For example, they might generate a video in which “you’re about to grab a cup, and the cup teleports into your hand.”

1X has addressed that issue by training the world model in part on physical data from sensors on the robots, such as tactile sensors in their hands, which has improved the model’s ability to generate realistic videos.

The model still struggles with some tasks, especially tasks that require a lot of dexterity, he said, because there is less available data about how the robot should perform them. But the world model can often help provide an initial attempt, which opens a path for the robot to improve its capabilities in the future.

Børnich said he expects video models of this sort to take over robotics in 2026. “It's a very clear engineering path from here to how you get to general intelligence in the physical realm,” he said. In fact, Skild AI, which develops AI models for robots, [detailed its own approach](<https://www.skild.ai/blogs/learning-by-watching>) to training on video data in a video released Monday.

1X is taking pre-orders for its robots, which it plans to start delivering to customers this year.— _Rocket Drew_
