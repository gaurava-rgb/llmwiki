An interview with long-time (retired) VC Bill Gurley about his new book about building a career you love, Uber, and the modern state of VC.

* * *

[Listen to this Update in your podcast player](<https://stratechery.passport.online/>)

* * *

This week’s Stratechery Interview is with [Bill Gurley](<https://x.com/bgurley>). Gurley was a longtime general partner at Benchmark, where he led investments in a host of successful companies, including GrubHub, Nextdoor, OpenTable, Zillow, and, most famously and controversially, Uber. Gurley also maintained a widely-read blog, [Above the Crowd](<https://abovethecrowd.com/>), that was a significant influence on Stratechery. Gurley retired from active investing in 2020, and recently wrote a book entitled [Runnin’ Down a Dream](<https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/769256/runnin-down-a-dream-by-bill-gurley/>), which went on sale this week.

_Runnin’ Down a Dream_ is not a memoir, but rather a guide to finding and building a career you love. In this interview we discuss the book, and use it as a lens to examine Gurley’s career, which ran from Compaq Computer to Wall Street to Benchmark. Topics include how the modern educational system is driving kids to burnout, why group chats are more important than you think, and how writing helped Gurley break through. Then, we discuss the Uber conflict between Gurley and founder Travis Kalanick, the changing nature of VC in the era of mega-funds, why the AI model companies are getting into products, and whether or not software is dead.

### An Interview with Bill Gurley About Runnin’ Down a Dream

_This interview is lightly edited for clarity._

**Topics:**

Runnin’ Down a Dream | Principles and Background | Above the Crowd | The Power of the Group Chat | The Uber Dilemma | The VC Barbell

#### Runnin’ Down a Dream

**Bill Gurley, welcome to Stratechery.**

**Bill Gurley:** Hey, thanks for having me on, Ben. It’s good to see you.

**Well, I have seen you before, most recently I saw you last fall at a basketball game, I thought you would be attending Florida–Texas today, but I hear you’re a working stiff now, having to do book promotion.**

**BG:** Yeah, I’m selling books 24-7, man. Part of it is I made it this far, this book took me about six years to turn into a book [from the presentation](<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xmYekD6-PZ8>), and I invested a lot of time. I don’t think I’ll ever do another one, so I have this internal motivator that’s saying, “You got to give it your all”, so I’m leaving it on the field.

**Well, you’re here for three reasons, which you hinted at. First you have retired as an active partner at Benchmark, which means you’re no longer covered by my somewhat permeable but fairly firm rule about not interviewing active VCs.**

**BG:** Fair enough.

**Second, you’ve published a new book,[Runnin’ Down A Dream](<https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/769256/runnin-down-a-dream-by-bill-gurley/>), just went on sale this week, we’ll have a link in the show notes, that’s going to be the primary topic that we talk about. And finally, I know you still have a lot of takes and opinions about tech. [You’ve gotten into the podcasting game recently](<https://www.bg2pod.com/>) and boy oh boy is it an interesting and challenging time to have takes and opinions about tech.**

**BG:** Unbelievable time. Unbelievable time.

**So with that as a framework, I always start these interviews with new subjects by talking about their background. And I assumed before I started reading the book that that would basically be the same thing, but it turns out you didn’t really get to yourself in the book until the epilogue, and even then it was shorter than I wanted. So let’s start with this specific angle on the book. What was your goal in writing it and why didn’t you want it to be about your own career and journey?**

**BG:** When I set out to write a book, a lot of, especially in the publishing industry, the agents and the publishers wanted me to write about myself or to write about Uber, or to write about venture capital or investing or tech. And of course with my blog posts over the years on [Above the Crowd](<https://abovethecrowd.com/>), I’ve mostly only written about those subjects. And I think even some of the people in the publishing industry, “What do you mean you’re not writing about those things?”, “How could you have the authority to write on something else?”, and it just so happens there’s been this passion project that started as a potential blog post.

When I was reading biographies a decade ago, I just really got into reading biographies, and I saw this through line that I felt was a methodology for achieving success in a career that was different than the philosophies that other people had taught, and I gave it as [a video presentation](<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xmYekD6-PZ8>) at my alma mater six years ago. A few people noticed, a few important people, like James Clear, who wrote [Atomic Habits](<https://jamesclear.com/atomic-habits>), which I’ve come to realize is maybe the best-selling personal development book of all time, and that gave me the confidence we could go do this. I hired a co-writer and co-researcher, and we ended up speaking to many of the great writers in this type of category: Adam Grant, Angela Duckworth, Daniel Pink, and James himself. We ended up studying a lot of the academic research, we did some new research of our own, and so there was a lot of intentionality that went into it.

The subject is “Runnin’ Down a Dream””, it’s a book that’s meant to inspire, give the permission to go chase something you love, give you the motivation to go do it and give you the methodology to go do it. It’s really about unlocking human potential. A few people saw that early video and have shared with me stories of how that video transformed their career, literally pivots into new directions and more fulfillment and I was telling someone, I’ve always been a closet American Idol fan, which some people are surprised by. But I love the rags to riches story that comes from something like that and I hope I can help lots and lots of people do that.

One last thing on this, somewhere in the past few years, I read this book, [From Strength to Strength](<https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/646134/from-strength-to-strength-by-arthur-c-brooks/>) from Arthur Brooks, it’s a book really about you’ve reached the end of your career or your working career, “What are you going to do now?”, and it was very motivational to me. I think this book, I think I already proved this based on how well the book’s doing in the past few days, has the potential to touch more, well, way more lives than if I’d written a book about how to be a better VC. So maybe I have the same rule that you do.

**Right, avoid the VC. Obviously from my perspective, my audience’s perspective, that’s the first thing they go to. But you mentioned you think you have a different take, and I’m going to go through the principles that you listed in here. But at a high level, how would you characterize what is different about that take and why is it not out there from your perspective?**

**BG:** I think part of it is that unfortunately the US academic system has evolved to a place that I think creates more stress and determinism than it does provide a place for people to develop curiosity, and I’m not the first person to say that. I think in [The Coddling of the American Mind](<https://www.thecoddling.com/>), there’s a chapter by Jonathan Haidt called The Decline of Play, and he talks about the resume arms race. The colleges in the US, everyone knows this, but they haven’t increased enrollment, yet the number of people that want to go to them has gone up and so they’ve all become really hard to get into, which puts even more stress on the children. It used to be that they wouldn’t let you pick a major until the end of your sophomore year and now they insist that you apply to a major before you ever visit the school.

It’s a real transformation about how people used to think about the journey, right? It is just so much more systematic and then the parents get caught up in this, and I think in a very well-intentioned way, they want their kid to be successful, but you start packing their school days to fill the resume. So kids today, and once again, I’m not the first person to say this, are over-scheduled. They’re scheduled like you and I were at the peak of our careers, they wake up and they’ve got lacrosse practice and chess lessons and Spanish lessons, it’s a lot. And anyway, I think they get to the end of that and they’re burnt out and they also feel a lot of sunk costs so they don’t explore a lot of possibilities.

I think that’s part of what has just put, there’s a Gallup survey that shows that 53% of people say they’re not engaged at work in the US, and that’s not ideal, obviously. Now add on that the anxiety of AI.

**Yeah, I was just going to say, now this technology is looming, that it threatens to blow up the entire system.**

**BG:** This wonderful person from Australia wrote this great review of my book, and he hinted at those are the people that are most likely, the people that don’t live a fascinated approach to their career are the ones that are probably most susceptible to the dislocation, and we could get into that more. But anyway, what people will find with my book is a much more, it’s like a how-to manual for a bespoke custom crafting of your career, which is not something a university could really do anyway, they can’t help everyone one by one.

We’re both friends with Mr. [Sam Hinkie](<https://x.com/samhinkie>), who is profiled in the book and when Sam read [Moneyball](<https://wwnorton.com/books/9780393324815>) by Michael Lewis, he was a consultant at Bain and he made a decision to go after that, and there’s this great thing in his story where he got accepted into Harvard and Stanford, so everybody doesn’t get that choice, but he visited Harvard before he said which one he said yes to and said, “I want to go be this sports analytics executive”, which didn’t really exist, and they didn’t have a specialty in that, they didn’t have a curriculum in that, he felt like they gave him the hand. But he went to Stanford and they said, “That’s awesome”, they were encouraging of it, they thought of five people they would introduce him to, and I want to help people like that. Someone that comes in with this deterministic mindset to go do something maybe that other people would tell you not to do. The books divided into profiles and principles, and there’s like nine profiles.

**We’re going to go through all them, I have a list of almost all of them.**

**BG:** I want to say one thing on this and I’ll stop. Almost all of them are in fields that your parents would tell you not to chase and that’s very intentional. There’s no VCs, there’s no doctors, there’s no lawyers, there’s nothing like that.

**Or if they’re consultants, they’re ex-consultants.**

**BG:** Exactly.

**Well, to this point, when you think about there’s a certain bit like, “Okay, I wrote a book, I want it to sell, I’m doing podcast after podcasts, I’m going to miss a college game with my buddies that I have courtside seats to to promote this book”, so there’s a competitive aspect, “I want a lot of people to read this”, but if you think about the addressable market and you go back to your career as a VC, markets can be way larger than you expect. You mentioned Uber on one of[your all-time blog post](<https://abovethecrowd.com/2014/07/11/how-to-miss-by-a-mile-an-alternative-look-at-ubers-potential-market-size/>) taking down the New York University finance professor who sized the opportunity by the taxi market — no, it’s market-expansionary. Is there a bit where your book is actually market-shrinking where you want a lot of people to read it, but actually the real success of this book will be measured by if there’s three people, or four people, who ended up doing extraordinary things like the people in this book because they got off the conveyor belt and did something special?**

**BG:** Look, I don’t think it’s for everybody and especially when you wade into the AI and employment space, some people come at me and go, “Well, you’re going to solve all of the employment dislocation from AI”, and that’s certainly not the calling that caused me to write this. But if people are out there that have this little inkling, this little spark that they love this other thing, but they’re doing this one thing, I want to talk to them.

And you’re right, I think the book will be measured more by the number of people that shift. But what I have found — the video’s been out there for six years — I’ve met people that watched that and found their way to a different place. There’s a gentleman who was a school teacher who saw that, and always wanted a career in football and never had given him self a chance to think he could. Today he has a podcast, he has an application you can download to organize and study offensive plays and he’s well known in the football diaspora by people that cover football, and he called me the other day when the book was coming out to thank me for the video. But this guy’s alive, he’s really alive and doing what he loves.

So yeah, the more people I can do that for, it will be quite fulfilling for me. It’ll make me happy and joyful if I help people find something that is more fulfilling for them.

**One more question before we get to the principles and profiles. All these people are amazing and they’re talented and you mentioned Sam, he was weighing offers between Harvard and Stanford. Is there a baseline level of talent or capability or whatever that is necessary to follow through on these principles you’re laying out?**

**BG:** I think what’s necessary is determination and so you need dogmatic determination and the best way I found to find that is people that do what they love, that stuff comes for free. Because they’re so fascinated by the topic that they read about stuff on their own and they consider it pleasurable. I suspect, I’m just going to go out on one guess here, [when Clawdbot happened](<https://openclaw.ai/>), Ben Thompson had to go read about it, just had to know about it.

**The example is when I was in Taiwan as an English teacher and I grew up very small town, blue collar, never even occurred to me to work in tech, which I think is a mindset — it’s very hard for people on the coast to even grasp or wrap their minds around. So I’m in Taiwan working as an English teacher, and my wife’s like, “All you do is read about tech and talk about tech all the time, why do you not work in tech?”, and I’m like, “That’s a good question, I should probably go do that” — and what do I do today for a living? I read about and talk about tech.**

**BG:** And smile while you’re doing it, which I think is an important thing. And I don’t go back to the Harvard/Stanford question. There are plenty of examples in the book of people starting with what way more meager means and at the very bottom of the ladder, the one that pops in my mind is [Jen Atkin](<https://www.instagram.com/jenatkinhair/>), who moved to LA with $200 and enrolled in a free cosmetology class offered by the city of Los Angeles. Which by the way, the program still exists, the woman that’s running it is the woman that was Jen’s teacher back when she went in.

**And Jen becomes the stylist for the Kardashians. Lots of great examples.**

**BG:** And not only that, and this is a common trait amongst people in the book, like [Danny Meyer](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Danny_Meyer>), they become so successful in the field they targeted that they start reading more broadly, they read business books and then they go do even more things. So Danny did Shake Shack, she went from being the best stylist to launching a haircare products company. It can become expansive because they teach themselves to learn. And once they’ve done that, what’s possible expands as they grow.

#### Principles and Background

**Well, let’s jump into the book. I had a couple more questions about your background I want to get to.**

**BG:** Go for it.

**I’ll circle back around to them. But the way the book is organized, you profile different successful people and then you discuss a principle that goes into achieving your dream career. So you just mentioned Danny Meyer, that’s your first example, and his obsession with restaurants. And the principle is, “Chase your curiosity”. To tie this back to you, what was the thing that you were curious about that this sort of resonates that made this principle come through?**

**BG:** It changed over time, and I think it’s important for young people to understand that they’re not locked or stuck. I think they get to the end of grinding after 16 years and they feel like, “Oh, I made myself a computer scientist, I have to be one”, and it’s not true.

So my first fascination was computers, I had a [Commodore VIC-20](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VIC-20>). I would type in programs out of the game, magazines and play games that I typed in, I’d have to debug them.

**This, by the way, is a story that’s come up by these interviews many times, a very common origin story.**

**BG:** I got to believe, I got to believe, yeah, exactly. I learned to program by debugging code that I typed in out of magazines, I learned backwards how to code, but fell in love with it, and would write, I would code in my free time. I remember when I wrote a solitaire game on a weekend. I didn’t have to do it, I kind of was fascinated with the idea of, “Could I do it?”, and so I got a computer engineering degree and I went to work as a computer scientist, and it was my fascination at the time.

While I was a computer scientist at Compaq Computer Corporation, which you’ll probably remember, but most people won’t, I bought a book called [One Up on Wall Street](<https://www.amazon.com/One-Up-Wall-Street-Already/dp/0743200403>) by Peter Lynch and started reading about stocks. And I bought this ValueLine notebook that had these one-pagers and I’m buying and selling stocks at night on the side. So my fascination shifted, and like you, it’s this thing you’re doing at home with your free time that may be a signal of where you’re headed.

So I went to business school, I was still fascinated with technology, that’s in the back of my mind. I go get a job on Wall Street as a sell-side stock analyst and I realized while I’m doing that that I like cutting edge technology and breakthrough young companies, and I don’t like following IBM or DAC or these old sleepy companies and that fascination led me to eventually want to get my way to Silicon Valley where I found my dream job, but I had two stops along the way.

**How intentional was this path? Is this something that someone could have written a book telling you what path to follow, or is that what you feel like you did is just keep chasing your curiosity even if it changed?**

**BG:** Yeah. Once again, we had a chance to talk to so many people that kind of studied this stuff for a living, and Angela Duckworth, I think she gave [a graduation speech](<https://penntoday.upenn.edu/news/oft-delayed-never-deterred-class-2020-and-2021-grads-celebrate-person-commencement>). She said, “Be like a paramecium and move towards warmth and nutrients and if you’re not there, just keep exploring around the boundary”, you might shift jobs within your own company. One of the [Acquired](<https://www.acquired.fm/>) founders had this thing where he would create a side hustle, every job he went to, he’d ask the employer, “Can I do something else in my free time to help the company?”, and the company thought of that as a very proactive thing to do, so he was rewarded, but he got to try two things instead of one. And that eventually led to him doing a podcast [at Madrona](<https://www.madrona.com/founded-and-founded-acquired-ben-gilbert-david-rosenthal-reflect-on-tom-alberg/>), and now he’s living his dream for sure.

And so I did have this thing I did, which I think is a pretty cool exercise, it’s not in the book, but in those first two stops, there was a day where I said to myself, “Do I see myself doing this 30 years from now?”, and the answer was no in both cases. And at that moment, I became very intentional about moving away from that, and it happened in both of those, and it never happened in venture. I never once said to myself, “Oh my God, do I see myself…” — I did it for 25 years, so I kind of answered my own question.

I think that’s similar to what, [Jeff] Bezos has this thing called the [Regret Minimization Framework](<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jwG_qR6XmDQ>), where he was trying to decide about starting Amazon or stay at D.E. Shaw where he was making tons of money and working with David Shaw — I mean, that’s a hard thing to walk away from. He asked himself, “What would my 80-year-old self tell me to do in this place?”, and young people, and you were young once, Ben, young people can’t see the end of time. It feels like they’ve got forever, but by the time you’re 40, that’s not true, and so if there’s any way to give yourself the wisdom you might have later in life to frame these earlier decisions, it might create more action and activity and impulsiveness than you would have on your own.

**I love the Regret Minimization Framework and I think the other thing for young people that I generally want to impart on them, and this was part of when I launched and did Stratechery, is you have more optionality than you think. There was a bit where by the time I went independent with Stratechery, it was already successful enough that I knew that I’d locked in another job if it didn’t work out and just by and large, it’s very easy to get hung up on worrying about if I leap, then what am I going to do? But you you’re creating more optionality for yourself than you might think.**

**BG:** Yes. And I think what you did is very, especially in today’s day and age of podcasts and YouTube videos and AI, you can go explore a new area for a very long time and get very knowledgeable before you leap, you can basically role play yourself almost in that role and learn.

**Yeah, and by the way, if you run out of energy to do it in your spare time, then you probably shouldn’t do it for a job.**

**BG:** Exactly, yeah, it’s a really cool balancing thing. So yeah, go study it, go map out people that are successful in that field, study them, listen to their stuff. You’ll start to have either confidence to move in the direction or awareness that you aren’t that interested in the first place, as you said.

#### Above the Crowd

**The second example is[Lorrie Bartlett](<https://variety.com/exec/lorrie-bartlett/>), a Hollywood agent, and the principal is, “Hone your craft”. For you, what do you think was the defining craft you honed in your career?**

**BG:** Certainly in venture, I studied investing writ large, that started with the Wall Street job. I felt quite differentiated in Silicon Valley with having read all of [Warren] Buffett and Howard Marks and [Benjamin] Graham and [David] Dodd, it gave me a different framing. I used to say that the job of a venture capitalist is to create a product that the buy-side wants to buy, and I had a very good understanding of what that was.

So it started there, but then I got to work with these four incredible founders at Benchmark, and I viewed the craft as an art and as an artisan pursuit and that’s a word that I don’t use much in the book, but as I’ve thought about it more, that I think is a really good way to frame it. I think the people that are great at what they do, regardless of the field, act like an artisan and think about what they’re doing in that way. And by an artisan, they study the history of the field, they know the pioneers, and they’re really into nuance.

**That certainly resonates, that is a good answer, it sounds right. I thought you might pick writing as sort of a unique skill. I obviously knew about you before we met via your blog, Above the Crowd, I didn’t realize that Above the Crowd was actually an old school fax newsletter back in the day.**

**BG:** It was.

**Tell me the story about that.**

**BG:** When I got to Wall Street — well, there’s two parts to the story. When I got to Wall Street, I luckily in business school had read a lot of personal development books and a lot of business success books, and some of those salesmen books talk about, “Listen to your customer”, and so I asked each of the salespeople to give me one client that I was going to be serving where I could start with a framework. And for each of those, they gave me one. And I said, “Give me 30 minutes, I’m only going to do this once, but what do you like? What do good sales side analysts do? What do you look for in them?” — I was just like, “I’m at the very beginning of my career, help me know what greatness is”, and they all appreciated me doing it, they all said yes and a bunch of them talked about this guy named Dave Korus.

Dave Korus was at Kidder Peabody, he was a competitor of mine, and he had a newsletter and it was once a week. It was distributed around the four walls of the institution, he was broadcasting it, which was very, very industrious and smart at the time, and he was a bit, more than a bit, he was almost disrespectful. He was willing to make a call and be outspoken, which a lot of people aren’t. So anyway, luckily, two months after I started in the role, he quit, and two weeks later, I launched a fax newsletter. I copied him and recognized that it would be this kind of vacuum and filled it. So that’s how it started.

**What was the payoff that you got from that? I mean, obviously you weren’t — I think you once told me years ago that I actually had your dream job because I got paid to write a newsletter, I don’t think you were charging for your newsletter.**

**BG:** No, I never did. The payoff is twofold. Well, one, and you mentioned writing, I think for anybody in any field, if they write about the edge of what’s happening in their field, for all the reasons Bezos makes people do the six-page letter and all the reason Buffett does the annual report, it really hones your thinking, because when you write something down and you do it all the time, there’s this inner desire to not be intellectually inconsistent and so you hold yourself actually to understanding things. Going back to that word nuance, you really get into the nuance because you really want it to hold together once you put something down on paper and there are plenty of people outside of ourselves that have studied this writ large, but it’s very well understood that writing is a great way to understand things to take it to a higher level.

In addition to that, it got my name known. It created this massive network of people who knew who I was, it would eventually become fly paper to attract founders to who I was and to reach out to me, which a powerful skill to have as a venture capitalist. Not all venture capitalists do that, but a lot of venture capitalists have some form of that.

**Should that have been a principle? Because you have bits about mentorship and peers and things like that, which we’re going to get to, but you had the networking. I got an MBA, they talk about networking and whatever it might be, and it’s like, “Be useful, be helpful”, I started out with 400 Twitter followers, and now I can reach out to basically anyone in tech and get a hold of them not because of networking but because of writing.**

**BG:** That’s powerful.

**Well, it’s because I put stuff out there and then that sort of, the fly paper sort of idea, should that be a principle for everyone or is this a unique skill that is maybe harder than it looks?**

**BG:** I don’t know if everyone’s capable of it or not. Certainly in the modern age, you talk about networking or finding peers or finding mentors, technology makes that 10 to a 100 times easier and changes the surface area of what that means. You can meet someone in a Reddit group — I was considering the fact that for people over 25, like over 50% meet their next life partner via some kind of online means. If that’s true, wouldn’t that also be true in business connections then? It probably is. So being skilled at how you connect to people through all these new mediums is really important.

I see it a little bit of a different way, which is for all of the principles in the book that are trying to teach people to do something, whether it’s AI or YouTube or podcast or LinkedIn or Reddit, it’s never been easier to do all of them. It’s never been easier to learn, to connect to mentors, to connect to peers, it’s all hyper-enabled by this technology. I don’t know if everybody can write and synthesize, I certainly think part of honing your craft and becoming a learn-it-all that understands nuance in your field is enhanced by writing if you do it.

If you choose to do it and one theme that runs through the book, is to not be concerned about thinking you have proprietary information that you shouldn’t share. You’ve got to get past that, you’ve got to get way past that, because a lot of stuff you can go accomplish is enhanced by having the opposite perspective.

**Right. Well, it’s kind of like a sister to the optionality point earlier, which you have to trust that if you’re generating and giving away insights, that’s going to lead to more insights. It’s not like you have a scarce number and then you’re done forever.**

**BG:** Yeah. And other people like to point to James Carse’s book about [Finite and Infinite Games](<https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Finite-and-Infinite-Games/James-Carse/9781476731711>), and I guess Simon Sinek developed that even more [with his book](<https://simonsinek.com/books/the-infinite-game/>). But we do borrow a lot of our strategies from finite games, from sports and from chess or from Monopoly or whatever, where there’s one winner and a bunch of losers. But the reality is, in most fields, lots of people win. If you have an attitude of sharing your ideas, a lot of the other stuff gets way easier, and the connectivity gets easier, the optionality gets easier, as you said, it’s mostly all good, it’s all goodness.

#### The Power of the Group Chat

**So your third example was Bob Dylan. And the principle was, “Develop Mentors in Your Field”, But then the next one, and I read this one, I’m like, “Yeah, okay, mentors”.**

**BG:** They’re not all one-to-one with the one that’s next to it.

**Well, the next one was my favorite one, because the next one was this group chat of Athletic Directors who started out as equipment managers and whatever it might be. And you sort of said in there, because I was reading the mentor chapter, and I’ll be honest, I was a little like, “Yeah, I’ve gotten the mentor, yes, find a mentor, be a mentor,” blah, blah, blah, blah, but then you get to the group chat like, yes, this is actually, I think, a huge thing that people miss. They’re so focused on trying to connect to important people, or senior people, missing the fact that the way to have close connections to important and senior people, is to have known them for years, because you came up together, and you look up one day, and these people you’re friends with are also very senior and important. And so, I thought these were good to be together, more because I think the second one is underrated and important and people can get too focused on the first one.**

**BG:** By far, my favorite of the principles is called, “Embrace Your Peers”, it’s for the reason you note, which is, it’s a less explored area of success than mentors and continuous learning, which other people have talked about.

What I am recommending to people is, try to find four to eight like-minded people on the journey that you’re on, I think hopefully outside of your corporation, we could get into why I think that’s better than someone inside your corporation. More diversity, less concern about competitive conflict, and start to share ideas and your network, and everything with them.

The example in the book’s just awesome, because they all started at the bottom rung in their each respective universities that they worked with. They met at a conference, which is a great place to meet this type of person that you might work with, or have this connection with. One day they woke up, and they were all Division 1 Athletic Directors at the top of their field.

**And at big schools, Texas, Alabama, Kentucky, we’re not talking about small fry here.**

**BG:** And by the way, so you learn together, you support each other on bad days. You give people peer pressure in a positive way that you don’t even know is happening. When you see someone you know rise, you believe you can rise, too. And so, shared learning, shared network, expanded network, emotional support, and this kind of peer pressure, positive peer pressure to succeed, those are all wonderful things. It’s interesting, this particular technique, I think, is hard for people to do for some reason, it’s not intuitive to them.

**Yep. I completely agree. What is your oldest group chat?**

**BG:** Probably there’s one called Gator Boys, that we have a mutual friend in, that is the guys I hung out in college with. In the epilogue, I point out that this was a group of drinking buddies that all did quite — you’d be shocked that a group of drinking buddies from the University of Florida accomplished the things that that group did. Part of that idea of maybe peer confidence, it’s really not peer pressure, but it’s peer possibility, maybe, is the right way to think about it. I think that’s really powerful.

**Yeah. Our mutual friend, you introduced me to him, he was in Taiwan, and now he’s a core part of another group chat of folks in Taiwan. How do you develop trust in group chats, though, if this is such an important thing? A lot of people, their only group chat is college buddies, or high school buddies.**

**BG:** Yeah. Oddly, and this isn’t necessarily the same thing as what I’m talking about in the book on a career, but I have group chats in a lot of variety of different topics.

**People are blown away when they see how many messages I send every day. It’s really nice.**

**BG:** And you think about the learning acceleration that happens through that, and the optionality that happens through that, it’s unbelievable. Trust is an interesting thing, there are sharp-elbowed people in the career space, especially ones that learned from being in athletics, or sports, they’re kind of winner-take-all types, they’re not going to do well in a group.

**I was going to ask you about the sports thing, I skipped that at the beginning, but you said you were interested in computers. You were a[D1 basketball player](<https://ufdc.ufl.edu/en/AA00047577/00001>), I don’t think you were just typing in front of the monitor all the time.**

**BG:** Yeah, but I wasn’t a very good one.

**You had to have been pretty good in high school.**

**BG:** I wonder how much I, watching two of the guys we played with at Florida, going to the NBA and being near them, and seeing them succeed kind of made me want to find a lane where I could do that. I don’t know if that had that kind of impact on me, but it’s possible.

**Was basketball a thing? You were known as the basketball guy, and then you go to college, you’re like, “I thought I was a basketball guy, but I’m not that much of a basketball guy”.**

**BG:** Yeah, I mean, I’m 6′ 8″, formerly 6′ 9″, so even if I never played, people would think of me a basketball guy. But most people, we push all these kids into athletics. But most of them, sometime between ninth grade and freshman year of college, they know it’s over, it’s a really weird thing that we do to these kids.

But anyway, that’s off the subject, you were talking about trust. Let’s go back to that, because I think it’s important. Trust. I think that there are certain people who you never feel like let down their guard and show some form of vulnerability, I always have a hard time getting close to those people. And so there’s this weird element of someone being willing to share something with you that shows a little bit of vulnerability, and that you obviously don’t take advantage of, and it’s bi-directional, I think that’s how trust gets built in these types of communities.

**Is there any trust in Silicon Valley?**

**BG:** I think so, yeah. And by the way, one thing I would tell you about Silicon Valley that relates to the book, it is a remarkably special place to reach out to people, and have them say yes, and give you some time. I don’t know what about it is in the water that way, but if you’re searching for mentors, or if you want 20 minutes of someone’s time, and you’re very authentic about how you reach out, I think the response rate in Silicon Valley is way higher than Hollywood, or Wall Street, or something like that. It may just be that there’s so much movement between companies and there’s so many people trying to figure things out, it may be that so many people were helped on the way up and feel the need to pay it forward, I don’t know, pay it back. But it is remarkable.

**This goes back to your Jen Atkin example, where the principle for her was, “Go Where the Action Is”.**

**BG:** Yes.

**Is this something you can only achieve in Silicon Valley?**

**BG:** I think it’s funny. The original video presentation didn’t have this chapter, and we added it for the book, it’s the one that I get the most feedback on. Which is, it says, “Go to the Epicenter”. The idea is, if you know what you’re aimed at, and you know this is the thing that you’re most curious about, and you want to dedicate your career to it, and there is a place, songwriting in Nashville, advertising in New York, you really should consider going to that place.

It feels overwhelming. It may be impossible for certain people, due to constraints in their lives. But if you can, you should. The reasons people don’t go, which I think are both wrong, one is, they think they can be a big fish in a small pond, which I think is dangerous thinking, for reasons I’ll explain in a second. And two, they think it’s going to be overly competitive and too hard. But the number of opportunities that exist in a place like that are so high, that you don’t feel the competition in that way.

And the other principles of the book, “Constant Learning”, “Finding Mentors”, “Finding Peers”, that stuff’s on steroids in these places. There’s so many people that are fascinated by it. In fact, you ever just go to LA and these people talk about TV shows and movies to a point where you’re like, “Is that all you guys think about?”.

**That’s the Silicon Valley story, for sure.**

**BG:** Yeah, exactly. And then on top of all that, in anyone’s career journey, you talk to a ton of successful people, you can find three or four times where they say, “Oh, and then this really lucky thing happened”, we want to say, “Oh well, that wouldn’t happen to most people”. But the thing is? The opportunity and optionality for luck goes up 10x to 100x if you’re in that place. The chance happening is everywhere.

**So have I made a mistake never living in San Francisco?**

**BG:** Maybe. I mean, you’ve been quite successful. I’m not saying that you can’t do it around the side. I’m just saying, if you’re at the beginning of your career and you want to maximize the chance of success? It’s super helpful to be in that place.

**My rationalization, justification, is that my actual home is the Internet, and I was forced to lean into that much more strongly by being remote, and that’s my habitat.**

**BG:** Fair enough.

**But I think there’s some rationalization.**

**BG:** No, but there are virtual epicenters. I think that’s something worth highlighting, like Reddit subgroups. There are places that if you want go deep on something online, you can, and as we already talked about, you can meet humans through those connections that can be very valuable.

#### The Uber Dilemma

**You mentioned earlier this concept of finite games versus infinite games, and that a career is an infinite game and people get trapped in thinking it’s finite. And again, even when you didn’t invoke that framework specifically, it came through in the discussion of peer networks, which you already mentioned.**

**What’s notable about this concept being permeated through the book, is that you didn’t talk too much about your VC career, it was like one paragraph, and you said, “Oh, every VC hopes for the big one”, and then you got that with Uber. That, needless to say, ended up being a complicated story.**

**But what’s interesting, you may remember this, I wrote an Article at the time called[The Uber Dilemma](<https://stratechery.com/2017/the-uber-dilemma/>) after the showdown you had with Travis Kalanick. My take is that VC and company building was an iterated game, that you know there’s going to be other startups, there’s going to be other companies. My take was, Uber was the first company that got massively large as a private company. I criticized that, because it transformed that into a finite game. Either this company was going to go out public very large, or it wasn’t, which led to a completely different stakes, in terms of decision-making, because it was for all the marbles. It made it into a finite game. Was that a fair characterization of what happened there?**

**BG:** Well, there’s one more piece to the puzzle that probably doesn’t exist in a lot of other examples that you might compare and contrast it with. Which is, we had, back then, I think it was 2017, so it’s a long time ago now, but we had fires going on in seven different parts of the company.

**Yep.**

**BG:** We had this cultural thing where Eric Holder’s running an investigation, we have a showdown with London, we have five different state’s Attorney Generals accusing us of breaking laws, we have a lawsuit with Google over this employment and idea theft, and we were losing market share.

**Not just that, but Lyft was about to go out of business, and suddenly they get millions of dollars in funding.**

**BG:** No, I’d say the opposite, we were losing market share to Lyft.

**That’s what I mean, it let Lyft come back, that was my point.**

**BG:** Yeah, yeah.

**You go back a year before, it looked like it was over, and suddenly Lyft has new life because of this.**

**BG:** Yes, we could see it in the numbers. The reason, especially in the coastal areas, the brand was taking heavy artillery fire, and people were switching to Lyft. By the way, that took eight years to get some people off of. They would ride Lyft, just because they hated the brand of Uber.

**It severely impaired Uber’s profitability, I would say, for about eight years.**

**BG:** And so not only did you have this thing that had a lot of stakeholders and consequences, and it had gotten so large and so valuable, all of my LPs, it was a meaningful return on their books in a paper state. And so there was a lot of responsibility in that moment, but not only was it big, it was at risk, if you will.

**Yeah. Was that sort of like, you got your big one, and the big one was bittersweet, because that simultaneously was sort of like, “Okay, I’m not sure how much I can do this anymore”?**

**BG:** Well, I wouldn’t want anyone to be in that situation that I found myself in. It’s been written about a lot, so I don’t know that I have any new information from it. I’ve said publicly, if I had done my job to perfection, we’d never get to that place, and so there’s some amount of mentorship or leadership that I didn’t provide that I wish I could have. I know a few of the efforts that were attempted that didn’t make it. But anyway, water under the bridge, we got to that place.

Now, I’m very thankful that Dara [Khosrowshahi] came in and did such an incredible job. He not only put out all of those fires that we talked about, but he got it public, took $200 billion in market cap and over $10 billion a year in free cash flow, which is very far away from where we were that day.

**Yeah, are you far enough away? Do you have any takes on Uber versus autonomous vehicles? Are they going to be okay?**

**BG:** Well, I will give you one take. I have been pushing the company for over 10 years to embrace open source and I think it would’ve been the best thing they could have possibly done. They took the assets they had at the time and sold them into, I can’t even remember the name of the company, and they gave that company money and so if that IP had instead kick-started an open source project.

**They’d have way more potential suppliers.**

**BG:** Yeah, and you commoditize that layer. Part of what we’re seeing in this AI war, and Google got really good at this not only with Android but Kubernetes, like you use open source to commoditize another layer so that your layer is protected as much as possible.

**One of your[all time great blog posts](<https://abovethecrowd.com/2011/03/24/freight-train-that-is-android/>), the Android moat or whatever it was around the Google castle, I think you could used evocative imagery than that.**

**BG:** Yeah, I said they burned like a 100-mile ring around the campus so no one could get to them. There’s some fascinating AI wars going on in that way, it’s not shocking to me that Nvidia is [releasing open source models](<https://blogs.nvidia.com/blog/open-models-data-tools-accelerate-ai/>), right? It’s not shocking.

**Should there be a principle in the book that relates to this that you have to be willing to make enemies?**

**BG:** I don’t think so. I think there’s a more human element that kind of rolls through the whole book that takes the opposite side.

**No, I know that’s sort of the — because you make these points again and again about being open and sharing and it’s not a finite game, but at some point is there a, “There will come a time…”?**

**BG:** I guess in any industry, there’s always — I named the concluding chapter, “It Ain’t Easy” and went through some of the hardships that a lot of these people had to face on the way up. I don’t think there’s any journey that’s free of conflict or error, and so you’ve got to be prepared for those things.

#### The VC Barbell

**One big change, and this aligned with your career, over the last few years is the shift in VC to massive funds investing everywhere from seed to growth stages. Benchmark, however held during your tenure in particular, held onto a more traditional model, smaller fund sizes. Is that still viable?**

**BG:** I don’t know. Candid question, the industrialization of venture capital is a real thing and the largest funds are now raising fund sizes and assets under management that rival Apollo and the PE funds, and they are doing things by encouraging companies to stay private forever that dramatically change the landscape of venture capital and maybe permanently, I don’t know. But to suggest that that’s not real would be crazy.

I do think that once you start behaving in that way and start writing checks at $500 million or $1 billion and having to raise money and manage money of that size your fidelity to care about the $5 million seed or Series A, it gets hard. I don’t think your best people in your organization can be aware of what that means because there’s all these other priorities and obligations now. So I think it’ll still be possible to compete primarily in the early stage and be successful, but you’re going to have to find a way to deal with the reality.

There is a crazy thing happening right now where the majority of rounds that are done in venture capital are proactive. So any successful company, these mega funds are demanding you take money. It’s almost like you have multiple [Softbank founder and CEO] Masa’s out there, and I lived this Masa thing in Uber/Lyft. I lived companies being overfunded intentionally and the things that causes from a company evolution standpoint and understanding unit economics, I think it’s mostly bad for the evolution of these companies. They’re forced to become real grown-ups and kings before — it reminds me of Game of Thrones where the 14-year-old has told they’re king, that’s what’s happening in almost every one of these fields. It’s not just Uber/Lyft now, it’s almost every successful venture company.

**The Uber/Lyft is a precursor of what’s going to happen.**

**BG:** Yeah, and $2 billion a year is not the biggest burn rate anymore, I thought that was crazy. Now, they’re bigger than that, so the field has definitely changed.

**I was going to ask about how much of a problem it is about fewer companies going public and you have this acquisition being frozen and this weird acquihire thing. You mentioned though that these large funds are almost predicated on keeping companies private, which one is it?**

**BG:** Oh, I think they’ve discovered a very clever — first of all, one thing your audience would fully understand and obviously, you would as well, part of what’s happened is there are things that we talk about. We use network effects, increasing returns, power laws, we use these words now and we see these Mag 7, and so part of what’s happened is everyone’s become educated at power laws and so if these large funds think this is one of those companies, they’re willing to pay a really, really high price relative to history because they’ve seen that that would still work out over time.

Now, there’s a question. If everyone believes in power laws, could that get priced into the market? Of course it could, and maybe that’s what we’re about to watch. But the amount of money they’re forcing into these companies creates a burn rate and an aggressiveness of growing the company that I don’t think is healthy. I really don’t, but it is where we are.

**Do any of these apply to the AI model companies?**

**BG:** Yes! And look, you don’t get to choose to be pragmatic like, “Oh, your competitor is going to raise $2 billion”, well, “You’re only going to hire five salespeople? No one’s going to know who you are!”, so sport of kings.

**Yeah. Are these AI model companies, do they have power laws? Are they going to be profitable or are like, what’s your prognosis?**

**BG:** You write about this more than I do. I think both OpenAI and Anthropic, most likely because of the success of the open source models with developers realized that just being a mid-layer model company who was risky, and you’ve seen this shift in Anthropic more than you see it OpenAI, but I think they both decided they had to connect directly with customers. And Anthropic’s last 12 months has been a real exercise in that even competing with some of their best customers.

**Well, that’s the question. Does AI mean VC is back, or does the fact that the model companies realize they have to go into the application layer mean that it’s going to be rough sledding?**

**BG:** We’re going to see, I think there’s going to be a lot of interesting experiments. I love to highlight something you know is true, which is in the beginning of the Internet, everyone started on Sun and Oracle and five years later, they were all on Linux and MySQL and I do think the optimization — in a period of a new breakthrough, everyone wants the best product. No one’s focused on unit economics, everyone’s focused on market share, but one day, and maybe you have to have a crash before this even happens, but I know some of it’s already happening. People start thinking about optimization and Benchmark’s an investor in Fireworks and Cerebras, we’re seeing people move towards optimization and then they experiment and try different things.

One of the companies, Anthropic’s obviously now targeting that, was a customer, is Cursor, and I can assure you Cursor’s playing around with other models because of what Anthropic’s doing to them. And so, you end up in the optimization phase doing things differently than you did, and I don’t think we know yet, I think we’re going to watch it play out.

**[Is software dead](<https://stratechery.com/2026/microsoft-and-software-survival/>)?**

**BG:** I doubt it. I think it’s really unlikely, and someone highlighted all the companies Anthropic’s actually paying seat licenses for, the notion that you would build your own ledger for financials is really stupid because the auditors have to come in and touch the thing and there needs to be systematic equivalents with other companies and there’d be no reason for everyone to be on their own bespoke version of that. It would make the auditor’s job ridiculously hard and so, I don’t think so.

**Is there a bit though where SaaS was so easy and so cookie cutter that — you mentioned you focused on the art of VC being an artisan.**

**BG:** Yeah.

**Did the whole industry almost lose that because it got so cookie cutter for an era and now that’s being blown up? Even if no one is ripping out these systems they’re maybe not worth the multiples that they had.**

**BG:** Look, that gets back into some classic learning you’d get if you studied the history of finance and Warren Buffett and this kind of thing. Like stocks, I’ve always believed this, most people in Silicon Valley don’t, are best valued using this kind of cashflow models, not PEs or these kind of things. If you do that, you recognize that if a company has a-

**Terminal values matter!**

**BG:** Yeah, it’s all in the terminal value. And so if all of a sudden, someone, whether it’s an Uber’s case with autonomous or with SaaS, if they think you might not be around in 20 or 30 years, you can see some aggressive multiple compression, really aggressive, and that’s what we’ve seen.

I do think that if you believe SaaS is going to be okay, you shouldn’t be out on Twitter saying that you’re upset or that people got it wrong. You should be raising money and buying stocks, be fearful when others are greedy and greedy when others are fearful.

**Is this actually one of the biggest PE opportunities of all time?**

**BG:** It might be.

**Because all the PEs that were doing SaaS before they bought too high, but now, you can walk in and buy all these companies at a fraction of what they were.**

**BG:** I love that idea and I think you want to look at pricing models.

**If you come in, by the way, how do you fix the stock-based compensation problem? You lay off a bunch of people, that’s going to fix the problem.**

**BG:** I love how you’re thinking. Man, I’m sure there’s PE people that are hearing this and they’re like, “That’s exactly what I’ve been saying internally”. That’s very clever, I think that’s right. I was going to make one point, there are clearly systems that are more wiped out by AI than others, depending on the exact set thing that you do, but you can apply that analysis and AI will help you do that analysis, which is funny.

**For sure, I actually think it’s a massive opportunity. I just don’t know for sure, if I want to be the guy going in and lay a bunch of people off.**

**Is Above the Crowd dead? You did post about your book, but the last post before that was in 2022.**

**BG:** Yeah, probably, but mainly just because, well, here’s where I would say, let’s go see. I do find the ability to use video and podcasting way more efficient to share ideas.

**For sure. Writing’s hard, believe me, I feel the pain.**

**BG:** Yeah. I don’t spend as much time preparing them maybe as I should, but I do find it more efficient and I’ve told a few people this, but after I birth this book and get it moving on its way, I’m going to go do a policy institute, it’s going to be called P3. It has a very specific objective function. But as part of that, I want to synthesize ideas and convey them, use that tool that I’ve learned over the years.

**You sure we’re not going to get a memoir? I’d still read the memoir.**

**BG:** Ah, no, the epilogue’s pretty good. You didn’t like the epilogue in the book?

**The entire VC part was one paragraph. I need more!**

**BG:** (laughing) It’s all good, man.

**BG:** Fair enough.
