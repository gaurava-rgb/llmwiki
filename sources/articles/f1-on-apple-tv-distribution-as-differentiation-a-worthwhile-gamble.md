### F1 on Apple TV

From the [Wall Street Journal](<https://www.wsj.com/business/media/apple-f1-us-streaming-deal-1dfb7724>):

> Apple has signed a five-year deal with Formula One for the U.S. rights to air its races, broadening its menu of live sporting events it will offer on its streaming platform. Apple TV will begin airing races in 2026. F1 TV Premium, F1’s own streaming service, will be available to subscribers in the U.S. through Apple TV. Terms of the deal weren’t disclosed. The Wall Street Journal previously reported Apple bid as much as $150 million a year for the rights to air the races.
>
> “We have a shared vision to bring this amazing sport to our fans in the U.S. and entice new fans through live broadcasts, engaging content, and a year-round approach to keep them hooked,” Stefano Domenicali, chief executive of F1. The deal was announced ahead of this weekend’s U.S. Grand Prix in Austin, Texas.
>
> Apple made a massive bet on viewers’ more general interest in the sport earlier this year with its Brad Pitt summer blockbuster “F1,” which the company said has grossed nearly $630 million globally. In 2022, Apple signed a 10-year broadcast deal with Major League Soccer worth at least $2.5 billion, its biggest foray yet into sports rights. But the company has said it isn’t interested in buying up slices of seasons, as is common for football and basketball rights deals. It also has a package of Major League Baseball games.
>
> “We’d like to own a sport, end to end,” Eddie Cue, Apple’s senior vice president of services, said on the podcast “The Town” earlier this week, citing the ease for sports fans to not have to navigate blackouts or search for where to watch which event. “Taking little rights here and there across all these different sports just doesn’t deliver that,” he said.
>
> F1’s broadcasting deal with Disney’s ESPN ends after the 2025 racing season. ESPN, which paid around $90 million a year, told F1 to explore other options around the time its exclusive bargaining window lapsed at the end of 2024.

I was pretty skeptical of this deal [in a July episode of Sharp Tech](<https://sharptech.fm/member/episode/apple-searches-for-an-ai-partner-a-second-fair-use-ruling-and-reckoning-with-reality-the-f-1-movie-and-related-matters>), and a bit more open to it in [a July Update](<https://stratechery.com/2025/netflix-earnings-apple-and-f1/>). The reason for skepticism is straightforward: F1 has been growing significantly in the U.S., and leaving basic cable for a niche streaming service may pay out more in the short-term — as much as $60 million a year more according to most reports, although veteren F1 reporter Joe Saward said the deal was actually worth $170 million a year in [his newsletter](<https://joesaward.wordpress.com/2025/10/20/jsbm-20-oct-2025-issue-25-40/>), which would be an $80 million a year premium — but stunt F1’s growth and long-term valuation.

The reason for optimism is that in today’s media world, basic cable is itself a niche product, particularly given F1‘s popularity amongst young people and women; that F1 is a premium event product that can actually drive subscribers, unlike MLS or Friday Night Baseball; and that if F1 isn’t going to maximize its broadcast rights, than what exactly is the point of giving away so much value to Netflix for _Drive to Survive_ anyways?

The actual details of this deal add to the optimism. First, F1 is included with the base Apple TV subscription; it’s not an add-on like the MLS deal. Moreover, in a real win for hardcore F1 fans, F1 TV is not going away in the U.S. as was rumored; rather, it’s a part of this deal: if you are a subscriber to Apple TV you will also get access to the full F1 TV product, including the F1 broadcast, various speciality views (like time sheets), and views from each driver’s car.

One note on F1 broadcasts: the actual feed is produced by F1 and is provided to worldwide rights holders, who add their own commentary, commercials, etc. This makes sense when you realize that F1 tracks are miles long, which makes it impractical for different broadcasts to have their own cameras, helicopters, drones, etc. That means I wouldn’t get too excited, at least not yet, about Apple doing any sort of innovation in terms of the actual visual product (and no, we’re not getting on-car immersive video, although I would be happy with an immersive lap around each track).

What is interesting is what Apple is going to do for commentary. ESPN really did put in the minimum effort possible into its F1 broadcasts, simply simulcasting the Sky TV feed from the U.K., complete with promotions of other Sky TV sporting content, the “red button on your remote control”, etc. At the same time, that means that most U.S. fans have an attachment to the Sky TV broadcast crew: is Apple going to stick with what people know, or are they going to invest in their own production?

I think that Apple needs to do the latter. I appreciate Crofty and Martin Brundle (and especially Nico Rosberg and Bernie Collins), but it’s impossible to make the broadcast feel like a premium product when the announcers work for another company entirely, and isn’t that kind of the point here? Of course Apple could also use the F1 announcers, which at least aren’t talking about channel features that the Apple TV broadcast won’t have. Then again, the reality is that this deal is not about innovation or presentation: it’s ultimately about distribution.

### Distribution as Differentiation

From [The Athletic](<https://www.nytimes.com/athletic/6723312/2025/10/17/f1-apple-tv-formula-one-streaming/>):

> Off the back of 2025’s summer success smash in the Apple-produced F1 movie starring Brad Pitt, F1 announced on Friday it has agreed a five-year deal for Apple to become its exclusive broadcast partner in the U.S., starting in 2026. It’s a significant choice for the sport, shifting it away from linear American broadcasting and banking on a streaming platform. F1 believes it will serve as a shot in the arm for its popularity and its relevancy in the U.S. “We know the power of Apple,” Domenicali told reporters ahead of the announcement at the 2025 U.S. Grand Prix in Austin. “It was just natural for us to sign this deal together”…
>
> Becoming part of Apple’s universe through “F1: The Movie” was a big boon for F1. Long before the June premiere, excitement built within the F1 organization about a future with Apple. F1, after all, recognized the enormous technological reach Apple enjoys across the world through its devices, as well as the potential integration into its famed products and branding. Domenicali referred to Apple as a “social movement” that could reach far beyond a traditional broadcaster through F1’s likely integration across the tech company’s products and platforms. “It will enable us to get into the minds of the American fans,” Domenicali said. “In the US, to be relevant, you need to be part of the culture. You wake up in American houses, you talk about sport, and you talk about events. That’s really the aim that we want to have with Apple”…
>
> Cue was clear that Apple would be looking to ensure there were plenty of avenues to watch F1 via its platforms. F1’s announcement statement mentioned apps such as Apple Maps, Apple Music, Apple Sports, and Apple Fitness being updated to promote F1 too. “Just like we did with the movie, you will not be able to not know that F1 is available and ready to be watched and that you should be watching,” Cue said. Domenicali added: “We don’t have a single doubt about it. We do believe that it’s the best amplifier of our presence in the U.S.”

I do think, that had the money been close, F1 would have still gone with ESPN; as I noted above, the sport does need to monetize the interest it has generated through things like _Drive to Survive_ , and money is ultimately the biggest things that Apple is bringing to the table.

Domenicali, however, is arguing that this deal is more than that, that Apple can actually grow the sport more than ESPN could, in part because of its brand, but especially because of its distribution advantages, which are going to reach to some pretty strange places. From [Apple’s press release](<https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2025/10/apple-is-the-exclusive-new-broadcast-partner-for-formula-1-in-the-us/>):

> Apple TV will deliver comprehensive coverage of Formula 1, with all practice, qualifying, Sprint sessions, and Grands Prix available to Apple TV subscribers. Select races and all practice sessions will also be available for free in the Apple TV app throughout the course of the season. In addition to broadcasting Formula 1 on Apple TV, Apple will amplify the sport across Apple News, Apple Maps, Apple Music, and Apple Fitness+. Apple Sports — the free app for iPhone — will feature live updates for every qualifying, Sprint, and race for each Grand Prix across the season, with real-time leaderboards, season driver and constructor standings, Live Activities to follow on the Lock Screen, and a designated widget for the iPhone Home Screen.

It’s going to be fascinating to see how far Apple goes in this regard. The company sent [a push notification to all iPhone users](<https://daringfireball.net/linked/2025/06/26/apple-wallet-sends-push-notification-ad-pushing-f1-the-movie>) with a Fandango offer for F1 movie tickets; are there going to be push notifications for every race for every iPhone user? Will I get an advertisement for F1 if I exceed the speed limit using Apple Maps?

What this does represent is another angle on Apple’s transition to being a services company. That revenue stream is, famously, primarily based on the company’s App Store fees and the Google Search deal; both are effectively taxes on distribution to iPhone users. In this case, Apple isn’t charging a tax, but the distribution they have is both (one of) the lures for F1, and the reason Apple can justify this deal (which, I should note, is about 3 hours of revenue for the company). To put it another way, Apple products entail earning users; Apple services entail capitalizing on the users they earned, which is exactly what F1 wants in on.

Apple TV, it should be noted, is not limited to Apple’s products: the not-a-box (but-also-a-box) and not-an-app (but-also-an-app) service is available on Smart TVs and Android and Fire Sticks and everything else that might show content; I suspect, however, that is table stakes from an F1 perspective. What Domenicali wants access to are iPhone users; that is increasingly the most important product that Apple is selling.

### A Worthwhile Gamble

Ultimately, I do approve of this deal for both sides. I really think that the amount of money was too much for F1 to turn down, this will be a good product for fans, and it is possible that Apple moves the needle in terms of growth amongst younger people in particular. Given the money, it’s worth the gamble. Apple, meanwhile, doesn’t have to spend that much in the grand scheme of things to prove out the worth of Apple TV once-and-for-all: there were legitimate reasons why Friday Night Baseball and the MLS deal didn’t move the needle, but now Apple has the entire package for a growing sport.

That, by extension, means this is a very important deal for sports rights in the long run. If Apple succeeds that is good for the sports leagues, because they will have legitimate alternatives to the traditional broadcasters, which will drive up prices in the long run; if they fail, then ESPN will regain some negotiating leverage, because their existing reach will prove to be worth a lot. I’ll be watching, both figuratively and literally (go Max).
