On to the Update:

### SpaceX Buys Spectrum

From the [Wall Street Journal](<https://www.wsj.com/business/telecom/spacex-agrees-to-buy-wireless-airwaves-in-17-billion-deal-5ab4ba55>):

> Elon Musk’s SpaceX is writing its biggest check ever to expand its foothold in the mobile-phone business. SpaceX said Monday it would pay $17 billion for the rights to use some of EchoStar’s valuable spectrum for cellphone service. The deal with EchoStar includes $8.5 billion in cash and up to $8.5 billion of SpaceX stock for two chunks of valuable U.S. wireless licenses and related domestic and international rights to beam signals from space. The rocket and satellite-internet company also agreed to pay about $2 billion of cash toward interest payments on EchoStar debt through November 2027.
>
> SpaceX is already a growing home broadband provider through its Starlink business and has dipped into the mobile-phone sector through a U.S. partnership with T-Mobile. However, the new spectrum rights would significantly improve Starlink’s ability to connect with cellphones in remote areas beyond the reach of cellphone towers. SpaceX said it would use the spectrum as a foundation for Starlink’s direct-to-cell business around the globe. It could also share spectrum with telecom partners. The most valuable piece of the deal covers two license blocks that allow a carrier to provide ground-based 5G cellphone and broadband service in the U.S.…
>
> Space and telecom executives have been debating the opportunity represented by connecting mobile phones and other devices through satellites over the past few years. Besides SpaceX, the idea has drawn in heavyweights, including Apple, as well as newer companies like AST SpaceMobile and Lynk Global.

Starlink is amazing. [I wrote about it](<https://stratechery.com/2023/starlink-solution-starlink-experience-starlink-implications/>) (via Starlink) in June 2023; the Starlink in that Update is now on my roof as a backup Internet connection, and I’ve acquired a Starlink Mini for use on fishing trips. The latter is really remarkable; it’s much smaller, and the router and Wifi access point are integrated into the dish itself, making it perfect for throwing on the roof of a fishing cabin:

![Starlink on top of a fishing cabin](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/starlink-2.png?resize=1275%2C1700&ssl=1)

Or sticking in the dash of your car in remote wilderness (you can power it with a cigarette lighter adapter):

![A Starlink Mini in an automobile's windshield](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/starlink-1.png?resize=1330%2C998&ssl=1)

This is an immensely valuable service, but the market is surely limited. There aren’t that many people going on road trips in the wilderness (and those that are are likely pausing their service when they’re not traveling — [which now costs $5/month after previously being free](<https://www.cnet.com/home/internet/starlink-users-will-now-have-to-pay-5-to-pause-service/>), albeit with the real benefit of maintaining sufficient access to restart service using your paused Starlink), nor that many people willing to pay for two Internet connections simultaneously (at the end of the day, even a crappy cable connection is still better than satellite). There are other applications of course, including residences without broadband (it’s nuts to me that the previous administration preferred spending billions laying wires that would never recoup their investment over simply handing out Starlinks), ships at sea, airplanes, etc., but ultimately Starlink has an addressable market ceiling on its value.

That ceiling gets a lot higher if Starlink’s market does not just include home broadband but also mobile broadband: not only does this mean there are vastly more devices that can connect, those are also the devices that people are actually taking on road trips where coverage is sparse, as opposed to sticking a dish in their windshield.

### Spectrum Specifics

From [SpaceX](<https://www.spacex.com/updates>):

> Through the agreement with EchoStar, SpaceX will purchase EchoStar’s 50 MHz S-band spectrum in the US (bands known as AWS-4 and PCS-H) as well as its global MSS spectrum licenses. Exclusive access to this spectrum, along with use of optimized 5G protocols designed for satellite connectivity, will enable a step change in performance for Starlink Direct to Cell.
>
> The next generation of Starlink Direct to Cell satellites will be designed to fully utilize this spectrum. Driven by custom SpaceX-designed silicon and phased array antennas, the satellites will support thousands of spatial beams and higher bandwidth capability, enabling around 20x the throughput capability as compared to a first-generation satellite. With the world’s most advanced phased arrays, the wider bandwidth operations enabled by this spectrum purchase, and optimized 5G protocols, the system will support an overall capacity increase of more than 100x the first generation Starlink Direct to Cell system. In most environments, this will enable full 5G cellular connectivity with a comparable experience to current terrestrial LTE service, which will be used in partnership with Mobile Network Operators to augment high capacity terrestrial 5G networks.
>
> Combined with new state-of-the-art satellite technologies, exclusive spectrum, and Starship’s unprecedented payload capacity to low-Earth orbit in a single launch, Starlink Direct to Cell’s next generation service will deliver unparalleled performance to standard, unmodified cell phones and IoT devices – helping to close coverage gaps and ultimately eliminate mobile dead zones around the world.

Starlink’s broadband service — which depends on customers actually owning Starlink dishes — uses high frequency spectrum (between 10–30 GHz); this provides excellent capacity, but requires line of sight and directional phased arrays. Both are no-gos for phones, which are used in a variety of situations and locations, and are limited to weak low-gain omni-directional antennas. That is why cellular spectrum is much lower frequency (sub-6 GHz, and the lower the better for broad and consistent coverage in challenging areas like cities).

This distinction is why Starlink’s initial direct-to-cell service launched in partnership with T-Mobile in the U.S., and other carriers around the world. Starlink needed cellphone-usable spectrum, which it had to get from T-Mobile — and it only got 10MHz at that (1910–1915 MHz for uplink, and 1990–1995 MHz for downlink). And, of course, the same limitations apply to mobile that apply to home broadband: having it is incredible when the alternative is nothing, but you will vastly prefer terrestrial connections when they are available, which means that any viable Starlink service needs a terrestrial partner.

This acquisition makes Starlink more independent in terms of the service it can provide. The most immediately useful bit of spectrum is the H-block downlink capacity, which operates at 1995–2000 MHz; that spectrum is supported by phones today, but it is still small. What is surprising about this deal, however, is that Starlink can’t actually use the rest of the spectrum it acquired:

  * H-block uplink capacity operates at 1915–1920 MHz, but there are not cellular bands that cover that range, which means that no phones support it.
  * AWS-4 has much more capacity: 2000–2020 MHz (which EchoStar designated as downlink; this could be reversed, but there is no ecosystem support), and 2180–2200 MHz (always designated as downlink), both supported by existing phones. However, AWS-4 has not been approved for use by satellite-to-handset communication.
  * Matching uplink capacity for AWS-4 is in the AWS-3 block (1695–1710 MHz); however, that spectrum is not in this deal.



In short, even with this massive investment, Starlink cannot offer service on its own (unless it actually sold its own phone with support for H-block uplink capacity), and the second reason for partnerships — service in areas where most people actually live — remains.

What this deal does give Starlink is tremendously more leverage in forming those partnerships: all of that spectrum is very useful to carriers, and can be traded for the spectrum Starlink needs for uplink in particular. The real unlock, however, will come from FCC approval for use of the AWS-4 spectrum for satellite-to-device communication, which will enable far greater downlink capacities. This approval isn’t trivial — in particular, AWS-4 spectrum is adjacent to spectrum reserved for federal space operations and telemetry — but one would imagine that SpaceX likes its chances under the current administration.

Even then, however, Starlink will be partnering for uplink capacity at a minimum. The big difference now is that instead of paying for that capacity, Starlink will be trading for it. That’s worth a lot; at the same time, if I’m Charlie Ergen, I’m pretty happy with $8.5 billion of cash and, even better, $8.5 billion of Starlink stock for spectrum that the FCC was threatening to force them to share.

### SpaceX’s Big Bet

From [The Information](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/apples-satellite-ambitions-threatened-elon-musk-internal-resistance>) in May:

> Three years ago, Elon Musk approached Apple with an eleventh-hour offer. Musk had heard that Apple was about to announce a feature for the upcoming iPhone 14 in partnership with satellite firm Globalstar that would allow iPhones to send text messages to emergency services in areas without cellular reception. He wanted Apple to instead use satellite internet service from Starlink, a Globalstar rival operated by Musk’s rocket company, SpaceX.
>
> His pitch: SpaceX would agree to exclusively provide satellite connectivity to iPhones for 18 months if Apple would pay it $5 billion up front, according to two people with direct knowledge of the deal. After that period of exclusivity ended, Musk proposed that Apple pay SpaceX $1 billion a year for Starlink service, the people said. Furthermore, if Apple couldn’t come to terms with SpaceX, Musk threatened to announce a similar satellite feature on his own that could work with iPhones, the people added. He gave Apple 72 hours to decide.
>
> Apple rejected the offer from Musk, who later made good on his threat. Two weeks before the iPhone 14 was announced, SpaceX in August 2022 announced a partnership with phone carrier T-Mobile, which allowed smartphone users to send and receive text messages in areas with no reception using Starlink.

That timing is particularly interesting given that this latest announcement seems to follow a similar pattern: I’m not sure it’s an accident that SpaceX announced this spectrum acquisition the day before Apple announced the iPhone 17.

However, that’s not the only development that may be undergirding this deal: it seems likely that SpaceX sees a unique opportunity at this specific moment in time to consolidate the entire market:

  * Apple is the primary customer and funder of Globalstar, which [just started building out its next-generation C-3 constellation](<https://investors.globalstar.com/news-releases/news-release-details/globalstar-kicks-global-expansion-first-c-3-tracking-antenna>); however, the first C-3 satellites aren’t scheduled for launch until 2027. There are ongoing upgrades to the current satellites, although [there are reports](<https://tmfassociates.com/blog/2025/09/08/spacex-disrupts-everyones-plans-again/>) that the price of Globalstar getting SpaceX launch slots was [the iPhone 13 supporting T-Mobile’s satellite capability](<https://www.pcmag.com/news/iphone-13-catches-up-with-satellite-support-in-ios-185-update>).
  * AT&T and Verizon are partnering with AST, which plans a larger-but-fewer array of satellites; AST, however, is struggling with [delays](<https://tmfassociates.com/blog/2025/08/12/delays-delays/>), including a missed deadline for launch of their first satellite at the end of August. AST is also depending on Blue Origin for much of their launch capacity.
  * EchoStar, meanwhile, which announced its own direct-to-device initiative, is obviously now out of the game.



In summary, right at the exact moment that all of the relevant players in satellite connectivity are facing go/no-go decisions in terms of future capabilities, and just as those future initiatives are suffering from delays and a lack of launch capacity, SpaceX is shoving its chips into the middle of the table: everyone knows that SpaceX has the most launch capacity, and now they have not just spectrum but a demonstrated willingness to arguably overpay to have the best service (or at least gain the bargaining capacity to trade for it). Facing this reality, why shouldn’t Apple or AT&T or Verizon blink, and simply use Starlink?

The reason, of course, is that Starlink is basically making a play to monopolize the space, which may be the only way to make money with satelite direct-to-device capabilities. Apple already set the market price at zero, and T-Mobile has followed suit, primarily using its satellite service to upsell customers to higher-tier plans. Both are useful strategies as long as they are differentiated, but once every device and every carrier has that capability the price premium will go to zero. If, however, that capability is table stakes, then the real winner will be the entity that sets the table. That’s exactly what Starlink is making a play to be.
