### Atlassian Buys The Browser Company

From [CNBC](<https://www.cnbc.com/2025/09/04/atlassian-the-browser-company-deal.html>):

> Atlassian said it has agreed to acquire The Browser Co., a startup that offers a web browser with artificial intelligence features, for $610 million in cash. The companies aim to close the deal in Atlassian’s fiscal second quarter, which ends in December.
>
> Established in 2019, The Browser Co. has gone up against some of the world’s largest companies, including Google, with Chrome, and Apple, which includes Safari on its computers running MacOS. The startup debuted Arc, a customizable browser with a built-in whiteboard and the ability to share groups of tabs, in 2022. The Dia browser, a simpler option that allows people to chat with an AI assistant about multiple browser tabs at once, became available in beta in June.\
>
> Only a small percentage of people who used The Browser Co.’s Arc adopted the program’s special features. “Our metrics were more like a highly specialized professional tool (like a video editor) than a mass-market consumer product, which we aspired to be closer to,” Josh Miller, The Browser Co.’s co-founder and CEO, said in a [newsletter update](<https://browsercompany.substack.com/p/letter-to-arc-members-2025>). The startup stopped building new features for Arc, leading to questions of whether it would release the browser under an open-source license.

[The Verge](<https://www.theverge.com/web/770947/browser-company-arc-dia-acquired-atlassian>) has a good story about the acquisition, and is clear that the focus is Dia:

> The acquisition is mostly about Dia, which launched in June. Dia is a mix of web browser and chatbot, with a built-in way to chat with your tabs but also do things across apps. Open up three spreadsheets in three tabs and Dia can move data between them; log into your Gmail and Dia can tell you what’s next on the calendar. Anything with a URL immediately becomes data available to Dia and its AI models. For a company like Atlassian, which makes a whole suite of work apps — the popular project-tracker Jira, the note-taking app Confluence, plus Trello, Loom, and more — a way to stitch them all together seems obviously compelling.
>
> Miller is clear, even forceful, that Dia is not about to become just a wrapper for Atlassian apps, or shift to thinking primarily about IT managers and enterprise features. Dia is still for individual users. It’s just that now, it’s primarily for individual users at work. Before, Miller says, “we talked a lot about shopping, making reservations, finding showtimes. That is going to go away in terms of our focus.” He says he sees everyone else, from ChatGPT to Claude to Gemini to Replika, competing to be a central new character in your personal life. He’s happy to build a work tool instead.

$610 million in cash for a company with basically no revenue — Dia, by definition, isn’t making much if any money from Google! — suggests that Miller is going to get his way, for a while anyways; that’s an amount you pay when you really, really, want to convince someone to join your company.

### Browser Wars

The [newsletter update](<https://browsercompany.substack.com/p/letter-to-arc-members-2025>) that the CNBC excerpt referenced is an interesting one; it’s from May, when Miller announced that the company was suspending work on Arc, their first browser, to focus on Dia. First, on why Miller thinks that browsers matter:

> At its core, we started The Browser Company with a simple belief: the browser is the most important software in your life — and it wasn’t getting the attention it deserved. Back in 2019, it was already clear to us that everything was moving into the browser…Even back then, it felt like the dominant operating system on desktop wasn’t Windows or macOS anymore — it was the browser. But Chrome and Safari still felt like the browsers we grew up with. They hadn’t evolved with the shift. And both of these trends have only accelerated since. Some companies only issue enterprise versions of Chrome with new employee laptops (their companies fully run on SaaS apps), and Chrome and Safari remain essentially unchanged.
>
> So that’s why we made Arc. We wanted to build something that felt like “your home on the internet” — for work projects, personal life, all the hours you spent in your browser every single day. Something that felt more like a product from Nintendo or Disney than from a browser vendor. Something with taste, care, feeling.

Miller is obviously correct, at least as far as the PC is concerned. Much of the industry’s attention over the last two decades has been on smartphones and apps, but as I’ve noted regularly, apps are the yin to the cloud’s yang — the user interface for accessing cloud services. The user interface on the computer, however, is the browser.

This, more than anything, is why you keep hearing about the return of the “browser wars”. The most important thing for AI workflows is getting proper context; this is why Google is so well-positioned to do something interesting with Android, and why Apple, despite all of their stumbles, is still in the game. There I go again, however, focusing on mobile, where the barrier to entry in terms of building a new operating system is astronomical: not only do you need to do all of the technical work to build an operating system, you also have to get all of the necessary apps, and, as someone who once worked on the Windows App Store, I can promise that isn’t happening!

In that context the PC seems much more approachable, because you don’t need to make an operating system: you can just make a browser and basically get all of the apps (look no further than Chromebooks, which takes the concept to its ultimate conclusion). And, to be clear, that is true: the platform network effect enjoyed by Android and iOS is all but insurmountable, whereas when it comes to the browser you only need to convince individual consumers to change one app.

The reality, however, is that while that is easier, it’s certainly not easy; this was the problem The Browser Company ran into with Arc, which simply wasn’t moving the needle. Back to Miller’s newsletter:

> After a couple of years of building and shipping Arc, we started running into something we called the “novelty tax” problem. A lot of people loved Arc — if you’re here you might just be one of them — and we’d benefitted from consistent, organic growth since basically Day One. But for most people, Arc was simply too different, with too many new things to learn, for too little reward.

The “too little reward” point is key; back in 2014 I wrote, [in the context of payments](<https://stratechery.com/2014/problem-payments/>), about how difficult it is to get consumers to change their habits:

> This is why I, for the foreseeable future, expect to see little if any progress in the United States. Note the caveat, though: “in the United States.” The problem with building a new payment system or service in the US is that credit cards, all things considered, aren’t that bad. Sure the fees are high, but the network problems are largely solved; rare is the location where you can’t use a credit card (and almost always by the merchant’s choice), and almost all consumers have one. Small wonder, too: the benefits of credit cards relative to checks and/or cash far outweighed the pain it took to roll them out:
>
> ![A drawing Comparing the Benefits and Pains of Payment Systems](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/04/photo-5.jpg?resize=1024%2C498&ssl=1)When the benefit (vertical distance) is greater than the pain (horizontal distance) of establishing a new network, a new technology can breakthrough. However, if the benefit is only incremental, then the status quo is likely to prevail.
>
> This is much less the case when it comes to alternative payment methods. The pain of establishing a new two-sided network remains just as significant, but the upside to the new payment systems relative to credit cards is much less than the benefits of credit cards relative to cash. That is why the most interesting places to think about when it comes to new payment systems are countries with low credit card penetration.

There is pain when it comes to switching to a new browser, even if that pain seems extremely minor (most browsers import your bookmarks, etc.); Arc ran into that reality, which is why The Browser Company shifted their resources to Dia: the promise of AI is that it is a paradigm shift that makes it possible to generate a massive delta in terms of user experience. Back to Miller:

> The part that was hard to admit, is that Arc — and even Arc Search — were too incremental. They were meaningful, yes. But ultimately not at the scale of improvements that we aspired to. Or that could breakout as a mass-market product. If we were serious about our original mission, we needed a technological unlock to build something truly new.
>
> In 2023, we started seeing it happen, across categories that felt just as old and cemented as browsers. ChatGPT and Perplexity were actually threatening Google. Cursor was reshaping the IDE. What’s fascinating about both — search engines and IDEs — is that their users had been doing things the same way for decades. And yet, they were suddenly open to change.
>
> This was the moment we were waiting for. This was a fundamental shift that could challenge user behavior and maybe lead to a true reimagining of the browser. Hopefully you can now see why Dia felt like a no-brainer. At least for us and our original aspirations.

The theory behind Dia — and many of the would-be contestants in the alleged “browser wars” — is that AI is the paradigm shift that will re-open competition in the browser market, but only if you build around AI from the beginning. And then, if you succeed, you have the potential to have an operating system-like position in terms of user’s interaction with all of the apps in their life, making your AI even better given the Google or Apple-level of context you can command.

### Why Atlassian

Atlassian was a fascinating company for their time. Just as Microsoft deserves credit for pioneering the subscription model for software long before software was available as a service, [Atlassian deserves credit](<https://stratechery.com/2015/atlassian-files-for-ipo-square-prices-ipo-below-last-round/>) for pioneering the bottoms-up sales motion, where individual users and teams brought the company’s software into their work, also long before that became a powerful go-to-market motion for SaaS.

Atlassian today, however, is very much a traditional SaaS company, complete with a sales force, although their most important product is the same as it ever was: Jira, which capitalized on the rise of Agile 25 years ago, is project management software primarily for software developers. That right there is a bit of a concern: Atlassian is selling a seat-based software product for the category that is probably the most impacted by AI. For that reason alone I can understand a major impetus for this deal: the company now has a product that is enabled by AI, instead of threatened by it.

Jira, meanwhile, is not exactly beloved for its design and ease-of-use. I suspect a lot of the frustration developers feel about Jira is downstream from just how much it does; there is a real tension between capabilities, all of which are used by someone, and a feeling of elegance and taste (see every Microsoft product ever, and Microsoft versus Apple historically). The Browser Company, to that end, does bring a real sense of design and thoughtfulness to a product category that can also suffer from featuritis; I suspect that is a mentality that Atlassian wants generally.

Ultimately, however, I can buy Atlassian’s reasoning that the best route to achieving the necessary distribution is via a more traditional enterprise channel. From [a post by CEO Mike Cannon-Brookes on the Atlassian blog](<https://www.atlassian.com/blog/announcements/atlassian-acquires-the-browser-company>):

> Knowledge workers need a browser designed for their specific needs, not one that’s been built for everyone on the planet. That’s what we will build with The Browser Company. Our vision is to make Dia the browser:
>
>   * Optimized for the SaaS apps where you spend your day…
>   * Packed with AI skills and your personal work memory to connect the dots between your apps, tabs, and tasks.
>   * Built with trust and security in mind, so you can bring it to the office. While 85% of enterprise workflows occur within web browsers, less than 10% of organizations have adopted a secure browser. Security, compliance, and admin controls will be baked into every aspect of Dia.
>


These are selling points that appeal to CTOs and CIOs, who Atlassian already sells to and has relationships with; it’s also a much smaller group who needs to be convinced of the value of an AI browser than the consumer market as a whole. Atlassian’s bet is that The Browser Company can actually deliver on the promise of AI as a paradigm shift for delivering a truly better browser, and that Atlassian can sell that paradigm shift. That this is a paradigm shift that Atlassian itself needs to navigate makes the proposition that much more attractive, and there is real upside here.

Still, despite the pitch, I’m skeptical. On one hand, I do think this is a smart decision by Miller and The Browser Company. First and foremost, they’re getting a great exit, but beyond that, I do think the enterprise opportunity is much more obtainable than the consumer one. On the other hand, the history of technology is about layering, not replacement — just look at the browser! Yes, Chromebooks exist, but by-and-large browsers are used on top of existing operating systems, not as wholesale replacements.

By extension, I think that the primary AI interfaces will be something that layers on top of browsers as well, and I think we’re already there: I’m still a chatbot believer! My bet is that chatbots like ChatGPT continue to expand their capabilities and integrations to slowly bleed away what used to be done in the browser, as opposed to AI-enabled browsers capturing new workflows. Yes, the fact that every SaaS app already works in the browser means that browsers have a leg up in terms of integration and context; user behavior and expectations, however, remains the most underrated moat in technology, and I think that new behavior and expectations are more likely to evolve from new paradigms than they are to transform old ones.
