Much has changed. OpenAI’s AI tech has evolved so quickly that CEO Sam Altman says OpenAI is on the verge of AGI. Microsoft leaders strongly disagree.

The AGI provision is now a point of contention between the companies and could derail the industry’s most important partnership. OpenAI is seeking Microsoft’s approval for a planned restructuring that would allow OpenAI’s for-profit unit, which runs ChatGPT, to eventually go public.

Microsoft, the for-profit unit’s biggest outside shareholder, hasn’t agreed, however: It wants OpenAI to remove the AGI clause that allows OpenAI to cut off Microsoft’s access to its AI. So far, OpenAI has refused.

In the past two weeks, tensions between the companies have boiled over as each side has privately threatened to take drastic action to bring the other party in line.

The idea behind the AGI contract provision is that Microsoft, as one of the world’s most powerful for-profit firms, shouldn’t get access to technology that might eventually help people colonize other planets or develop nuclear fusion. Doing so would go against OpenAI’s founding principle in 2015 to develop technology for the benefit of all of humanity, an idea that has roots in the beliefs of OpenAI founders including CEO Sam Altman and Tesla CEO Elon Musk, who wanted to ensure the most powerful technologies didn’t end up in the hands of for-profit firms.

But Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella and his colleagues see it differently. Accessing OpenAI’s latest technology is vital for Microsoft’s products as it competes with other tech giants like Google. Their concern over retaining access has grown over the past two years as it became clearer that OpenAI’s technology was improving and its business was becoming a runaway success, sometimes at Microsoft’s expense, said two people who have been involved in the discussions.

Over the past eight months, as the issue has stalled the restructuring discussions, OpenAI executives have at times suggested the company could soon declare it has achieved AGI, said one of the people involved in the discussions.

In January, Altman said publicly that OpenAI is “now confident we know how to build AGI,” and in February he said the technology, which he has defined as “a system that can tackle increasingly complex problems, at human level, in many fields,” was “coming into view.”

Nadella has said the opposite, arguing that companies wouldn’t have standing to claim AGI had arrived until the technology had substantially grown the global economy.

“This is where we get a little bit ahead of ourselves with all this AGI hype,” Nadella said in a February podcast interview with Dwarkesh Patel. “Us self-claiming some AGI milestone, that’s just nonsensical benchmark hacking to me. The real benchmark is the world [economy] growing at 10%” per year.

Microsoft believes OpenAI won’t be able to declare it has developed AGI before 2030, when the companies’ current deal is set to expire, according to a person who spoke to Nadella.

**Moving Goalposts**

There may be a solution to the impasse. The two companies have considered potentially replacing the AGI benchmark with another technology, colloquially known as artificial superintelligence, that has even bigger profit-making and world-changing potential, one of the people close to the talks said. That would move the goalpost further into the future while still ensuring Microsoft will eventually lose access to OpenAI’s most advanced technology.

In the meantime, Microsoft has balked at OpenAI’s request for a slew of concessions related to the restructuring, including giving up its right to take 20% of OpenAI’s revenue or allowing customers to access OpenAI models through cloud providers that compete with Microsoft, according to one of the people who spoke to executives in the deal talks.

Despite the sticking points, executives at the two companies continue to meet daily to discuss their contract, and Nadella and other Microsoft executives remain confident they can reach an agreement without a full breakdown in talks, according to one of the people who spoke to him.

The outcome of the stalled negotiations could affect a slew of other investors, including a who’s-who of Silicon Valley venture capitalists that own OpenAI shares as well as SoftBank, the Japanese conglomerate that has hitched its wagon to the AI developer. These investors expect OpenAI to raise the biggest initial public offering in history.

If the companies don’t reach a deal and OpenAI’s for-profit arm remains in its current state—with nontraditional shares that give shareholders a future cut of profits—an IPO would be impossible. Without the promise of an IPO, OpenAI may have a much harder time raising the capital it says it needs to hit its objectives. The company has projected it would burn $46 billion over the next four years as it spends heavily on servers to develop and run its models, as well as on other costs such as salaries, before turning a profit in 2029.

**Marriage of Convenience**

Years before OpenAI and Microsoft became locked in the high-stakes negotiations, working together looked like a slam-dunk win for both firms.

The companies’ initial courtship in 2019 and eventual shotgun marriage was born of necessity: OpenAI needed a lot of money to develop AI using specialized servers, which Microsoft possessed at its cloud-server rental business.

Microsoft, despite numerous in-house AI efforts, had failed to develop anything like what OpenAI was cooking up, so Nadella decided his company needed access to state-of-the-art technology.

By mid-2021, Microsoft had used OpenAI’s large language models to develop GitHub Copilot, an AI coding assistant that later helped Microsoft win broader deals with companies to rent its cloud servers.

Microsoft committed an additional $2 billion to OpenAI for servers to develop its technology, including collaborating to set up clusters of servers powered by Nvidia AI chips in Microsoft data centers near Des Moines, Iowa.

By the summer of 2022, the companies had started working on an even bigger investment deal after Altman successfully demonstrated to Microsoft founder Bill Gates that GPT-4, a model trained in Des Moines, could answer tough questions from an AP biology test and other sources that the model’s training data didn’t include, Gates later said in a podcast.

That culminated in a $10 billion commitment from Microsoft, announced in January 2023, which granted it the right to OpenAI’s intellectual property through 2030, and to taking 20% of its revenue.

**‘Sufficient AGI’ Declaration**

At that point, OpenAI and Microsoft added another AGI-related clause into the contract, a “declaration of sufficient AGI.” The activation of that clause would end Microsoft’s exclusive right to use OpenAI’s technology and allow OpenAI to strike deals with other cloud firms such as Google and Amazon to host and sell the startup’s models to their customers, the person involved in contract talks said.

The companies defined sufficient AGI as the point at which OpenAI’s nine-member nonprofit board of directors determined “in its reasonable discretion” that AGI “has been created that has the capability to generate” the maximum profits investors in the for-profit unit were entitled to, and that OpenAI “has the ability and authority to direct the AGI to generate” those profits, according to a document The Information viewed.

At the time, Microsoft had the right to get $92 billion in future profits, and six earlier investors, including Khosla Ventures and Reid Hoffman, were entitled to a combined $13.3 billion in profits. (Those figures have likely risen since then, as Microsoft and OpenAI agreed in 2023 that the maximum profits investors to which were entitled should go up 20% every year.)

Importantly, the AGI clause doesn’t say OpenAI had to actually generate those profits to declare that it had sufficient AGI.

However, Microsoft must also agree on the methodology used to determine that OpenAI’s AI is able to produce the required amounts of profits to end Microsoft’s exclusivity rights, according to a person who has seen the companies’ agreement. The contract also requires the companies to have outside financial experts weigh in, this person said.

The $10 billion deal between the companies quickly became mutually beneficial. Microsoft integrated OpenAI’s technology to bolster its own products, adding Copilot AI chatbot features to Bing search and Office 365 apps. The company also attracted more corporate customers to rent its Azure cloud servers so they could gain access to OpenAI models for developing their own AI applications.

And OpenAI quickly became Microsoft’s biggest Azure customer, though Microsoft was charging OpenAI favorable server rental rates compared to other customers.

Ironically, after striking the blockbuster deal, several top Microsoft executives during this period told colleagues they thought OpenAI’s business would eventually fail, even if its technology was good, according to a former manager who discussed it with them.

**Accidental Triumph**

What Microsoft and even OpenAI weren’t counting on was the accidental success of ChatGPT, which OpenAI launched in November 2022. OpenAI only launched the chatbot after employees heard that their archenemy—Anthropic, founded by former senior OpenAI researchers who disliked Altman—was testing a similar product, according to an employee at the time.

Despite the awkward name, ChatGPT became a sensation, garnering more attention and users than anything Microsoft was doing with OpenAI’s technology for its own products.

The companies’ honeymoon following the $10 billion deal was already starting to wind down. By March 2023, both were pitching nearly identical OpenAI-powered technology to the same corporate customers. Microsoft, leveraging its established enterprise sales channels, instructed salespeople to highlight Azure’s security and compliance features as advantages over licensing AI directly from OpenAI.

But OpenAI oftentimes beat Microsoft at its own game, inking deals with large customers, including Microsoft competitors like Salesforce and financial firms such as Jane Street.

Another problem emerged for Microsoft. OpenAI had promised to launch a significantly more efficient model, code-named Arrakis, that was supposed to save Microsoft money when it ran OpenAI models for products like its Bing chatbot.

But Arrakis failed, leaving Microsoft executives in a lurch and motivating the company to seek alternatives.

Microsoft’s AI researchers got busy leveraging their access to OpenAI technology to derive cheap models from OpenAI’s bigger, more expensive models. They hoped to convince businesses to use a family of cheaper models they had developed, Phi, instead of OpenAI’s, and to convince Microsoft’s product groups to use these models to power Copilot.

The Phi models are now used for a limited range of features in Office and Windows, but Microsoft’s AI products still heavily depend on OpenAI models.

Still, 2023 was a relatively smooth year in the relationship—that is, until November, when OpenAI’s board fired and rehired Altman in a spectacular failed coup.

Nadella, blindsided by the move, backed Altman’s return and even planned to hire him and any OpenAI employees who wanted to join Microsoft if Altman didn’t get his job back.

After Altman returned, OpenAI developed web search in ChatGPT, with the aid of technology from Microsoft Bing search. The feature, which launched in October 2024, has since taken a bite out of Google Search. (In contrast, Microsoft’s effort to use OpenAI tech to boost usage of the Bing app has largely failed.)

And OpenAI pushed boundaries by launching new reasoning models that perform better when they have more time to “think” or process a question.

After developing more-powerful supercomputers in Phoenix to train the reasoning model and other technology, the two companies even contemplated developing a $100 billion server cluster, which OpenAI referred to as Stargate.

**Apple of Altman’s Eye**

OpenAI’s businesses were soaring. By the middle of 2024, its sales of its AI models to businesses through an application programming interface had actually surpassed Microsoft’s sales of OpenAI models to its Azure cloud customers, despite the latter’s sales channel advantages.

Altman, a longtime fan of Apple, was also working on a deal with the iPhone maker that he hoped would bring ChatGPT millions more subscribers.

Two years earlier, Microsoft Chief Technology Officer Kevin Scott had arranged a meeting with Altman and Apple executives to discuss a potential deal involving the use of OpenAI models on Apple devices, according to two people briefed on the meeting. Some executives at Microsoft hoped Apple would opt to use OpenAI models through Microsoft’s Azure platform, one of the people said.

But by the spring of 2024, OpenAI had finalized a deal with Apple to use ChatGPT for providing answers to difficult queries iPhone customers were asking of Siri, with Apple accessing the models directly from OpenAI.

Altman later met with Nadella to discuss concerns about how the Apple pact could affect Microsoft’s own plans for launching AI products to consumers, according to people who spoke to the two executives. In the end, Nadella blessed the arrangement because he believed Microsoft would ultimately benefit from it, given that OpenAI’s models still ran on Azure, one of the people said. (It isn’t clear whether OpenAI has benefited much from the Apple deal.)

But the relationship began to sour by mid-2024, as Altman and other OpenAI leaders told their Microsoft counterparts they didn’t have enough servers to keep ChatGPT humming while developing new technology.

That resulted in OpenAI striking a big cloud deal with Oracle, a Microsoft rival, and pledging to raise debt to fund its own data centers in the future. Such moves would deprive Microsoft of cloud revenue it might otherwise get from OpenAI.

Other problems were brewing.

Microsoft that year had hedged its relationship with OpenAI by hiring Mustafa Suleyman, CEO of Inflection AI, a startup rival to OpenAI, to build an AI team capable of developing technology on par with OpenAI’s and helping Microsoft rely less on the company.

Some OpenAI leaders viewed the move as potentially threatening, said a person who spoke to them.

**Microsoft Pulls Away**

Suleyman quickly hit a wall, however, when he tried to replicate OpenAI’s reasoning models. Last fall, he claimed for weeks that OpenAI wasn’t fulfilling its end of the grand bargain between the companies, which gave Microsoft the right to access any intellectual property it wanted at OpenAI.

He even raised his voice at OpenAI’s then-CTO, Mira Murati, and its lawyers in an effort to get OpenAI to be more helpful. Around the same time, OpenAI hired several of Microsoft’s top AI researchers, some of whom had previously clashed with Suleyman over the company’s internal AI development.

The tensions eventually subsided as Suleyman’s team figured out some of the issues on their own, though similar conflicts around intellectual property access resurfaced this year.

The tensions took a toll, and Microsoft started to pull away. In January, when Altman announced OpenAI’s Stargate data center effort with SoftBank, Oracle and other partners, Microsoft was noticeably absent from the White House event promoting it.

Around that time, the companies were already in negotiations about OpenAI’s attempt to restructure itself.

At first, OpenAI wanted to completely separate its nonprofit arm from its for-profit business. Later, after state regulators and other critics objected to that idea, OpenAI watered down the proposal to focus on restructuring only the for-profit business so it could go public while still leaving the nonprofit board in control.

In either case, Microsoft has the right to veto the move. But doing so would make OpenAI an unattractive investment for public market investors.

**What Each Side Wants**

In early talks about the restructuring, OpenAI sought to significantly reduce the percentage of revenue it shares with Microsoft.

Some OpenAI leaders also want to exempt future products, such as AI performing at the level of a doctorate holder that costs $20,000 a month per customer, from the existing revenue-sharing agreement.

Microsoft has not readily agreed to these changes and is seeking its own concessions, such as extending the duration of its rights to use OpenAI’s intellectual property beyond the current 2030 timeframe.

The definition of AGI has also become a major point of contention. Altman and other OpenAI executives are more bullish than Microsoft is on the prospect of achieving AGI soon, and they have said privately that they expect the startup to declare the achievement of such a milestone before the current contract ends, according to someone who spoke to them.

Still, Microsoft executives have intimated that if the company doesn’t get more appealing terms from OpenAI, it will simply let negotiations stall and hold on to its existing rights to OpenAI’s revenue and future profits while scuttling its ability to restructure its business, according to someone who spoke to the Microsoft executives.

And Microsoft executives including Suleyman are still frustrated with the way OpenAI shares its intellectual property without necessarily providing detailed explanations of how its models actually work. The selective disclosures have made it harder for Microsoft’s internal AI teams to re-create OpenAI’s models, including understanding how they use a technique called “chain of thought reasoning” to solve complex problems, according to two employees who have worked on the effort.

**Turning to Google**

In late January, Altman posted a selfie with Nadella, saying that the next phase of their relationship was going to be “better than anyone is ready for.” The two men were also meeting to discuss Altman’s desire to lower Microsoft’s cut of OpenAI revenue, according to a person involved in negotiations. That cut could grow into something meaningful, as OpenAI has projected it will have $174 billion in revenue by 2030.

It isn’t clear if Nadella knew that during this time, OpenAI was [developing collaboration features for ChatGPT so multiple customers could communicate about documents they share in the app](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-quietly-designed-rival-google-workspace-microsoft-office?rc=c48ukx>). Such features could one day challenge parts of Microsoft’s Office productivity suite, though OpenAI hasn’t launched them yet.

Meanwhile, OpenAI continues to move closer to Microsoft’s rivals. After OpenAI launched an image-generating tool in ChatGPT in March that unexpectedly went viral, Microsoft ran out of servers to power the product, according to a person involved in the situation.

Around that time, OpenAI turned to Google Cloud to gain access to a cluster of specialized servers to handle surging traffic, this person said.

Despite competing directly in selling and developing AI models, OpenAI and Google have since brokered a broader cloud agreement, though that doesn’t give Google the ability to host and resell OpenAI models to its cloud customers—something managers at both companies say they would like to see happen.
