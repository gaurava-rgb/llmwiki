### The OpenAI Hype Cycle

It’s been quite a week, [all about OpenAI](<https://stratechery.com/2025/openais-windows-play/>), so forgive me for going to the well one more time. I just wanted to make a meta observation. Here’s a screengrab of OpenAI DevDay 2023, one year after ChatGPT’s release, and just a few weeks before that crazy November weekend where CEO Sam Altman was temporarily deposed:

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/openai-devday-1.png?resize=1330%2C666&ssl=1)

Here’s DevDay 2024 (which wasn’t streamed live, and didn’t include Altman):

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/openai-devday-2.png?resize=1330%2C660&ssl=1)

Here’s DevDay 2025:

![](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/10/openai-devday-3.png?resize=1330%2C662&ssl=1)

It’s 2024 that stands out; [I wrote at the time](<https://stratechery.com/2024/openai-devday-openais-wrenching-transition-lonely-at-the-top/>):

> That long introduction I posted above was titled [The OpenAI Keynote](<https://stratechery.com/2023/the-openai-keynote/>) and the occasion was the company’s first-ever “DevDay”. DevDay 2023 was a huge deal, with days of anticipation and preview articles, and tons of coverage and discussion afterwards.
>
> That little segment on Techmeme, meanwhile, is the only vestige of yesterday’s DevDay 2024. There isn’t even a keynote stream available of the closed event! The OpenAI of 2023, which seemed to bestride the world, has completely disappeared.

Now look again at that 2025 screengrab: OpenAI is very much astride the world once again, with [a trillion dollars worth of deals to match](<https://www.ft.com/content/5f6f78af-aed9-43a5-8e31-2df7851ceb67>). The company is the corporate embodiment of the [Gartner hype cycle](<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gartner_hype_cycle>):

[![A drawing of The Hype Cycle](https://i0.wp.com/stratechery.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/06/stratechery-Year-One-282.png?resize=1024%2C768&ssl=1)](<https://stratechery.com/2016/tv-advertisings-surprising-strength-and-inevitable-fall/>)

To go back to what I wrote after 2024:

> Consumer tech companies are the top of the stack in more ways than one: on the product side they are where customers go, and when it comes to their value chain they naturally end up in positions where other companies service them, not the other way around. It’s the highest prize in tech, but it’s a long and difficult journey to get there. And, when you reach the top, it’s pretty lonely; OpenAI and Altman are just speed-running the lonely part.

What we saw this week — and what I think Altman voiced [in my interview with him](<https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-openai-ceo-sam-altman-about-devday-and-the-ai-buildout/>) — is that OpenAI’s wrenching transition from research lab to consumer tech company is now complete. The next goal from here is world domination, and we’re all, for better or worse, along for the ride.

### Microsoft’s Game Pass Failure

From [The Verge](<https://www.theverge.com/news/789424/xbox-game-pass-premium-essentials-ultimate-price-increase-changes>):

> Microsoft is raising the price of its Xbox Game Pass Ultimate subscription again and rebranding some of its Xbox Game Pass plans. Starting today, Game Pass Ultimate will be priced at $29.99 per month instead of $19.99 — a 50 percent increase. Microsoft is attempting to justify the price hike with a variety of upgrades across all of its Xbox Game Pass tiers. Xbox Game Pass Core is being rebranded to Xbox Game Pass Essential, and the Standard plan is now Xbox Game Pass Premium.
>
> Xbox Cloud Gaming is also coming out of beta and getting 1440p resolution support and bitrate improvements for certain games on select devices. These cloud improvements are exclusive to Xbox Game Pass Ultimate subscribers…
>
> Game Pass Premium will be priced at the same $14.99 monthly price of the existing Standard subscription, but it will now have a bigger library of games to choose from…Game Pass Premium is also getting unlimited cloud access for the first time, meaning you no longer need a Game Pass Ultimate subscription to get access to Xbox Cloud Gaming. Premium subscribers will be able to stream titles from the Game Pass library, as well as select titles they own. While Game Pass Premium won’t get access to day one launch titles like Game Pass Ultimate does, Microsoft is now making a clear commitment to bring new Xbox-published games to this subscription tier “within a year of their launch.” This excludes Call of Duty titles, but every other Microsoft-owned Xbox game will be available.

That _Call of Duty_ tidbit is interesting; the first person shooter is the biggest game in the industry, and was the jewel of the $69 billion Activision acquisition. [Bloomberg is out with a notable albeit unsurprising story](<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-03/xbox-s-game-pass-hike-shows-cost-of-lost-call-of-duty-sales>) about just how much it is costing Microsoft to include the title in its subscription offering:

> The price hike and other changes to the Game Pass plans are a sign that Xbox’s big streaming push is still not generating the revenue it would like eight years after launch, according to interviews with seven current and former Xbox employees. The company is putting some of its top titles on the streaming service, but that’s cutting into sales of higher-margin games like Call of Duty, which came with Microsoft’s 2023 acquisition of Activision Blizzard Inc., said the people, who asked to not be identified discussing internal company matters. Xbox gave up more than $300 million in sales of Call of Duty on console and PCs last year, according to one of the former employees, who asked not to be identified discussing internal estimates…
>
> Putting Call of Duty on Game Pass was a great deal for subscribers, but not great for Xbox’s sales of the game, according to former employees with knowledge of the business. While the latest iteration of the shooting title, Call of Duty: Black Ops 6, was the top selling video game in the US last year, and the biggest ever for the franchise, that was largely due to Sony’s PlayStation, which accounted for 82% of sales, the trade publication IGN reported. Subscription revenue was up 16% industrywide, according to IGN, due in part to customers playing the new Call of Duty on Game Pass. Some may have paid for a month or two and canceled, unlike the industry’s old model where they would have paid $70 and owned the game…
>
> After a surge during the pandemic, Game Pass’s rate of growth has slowed significantly, from 80% between 2020 and 2021 to 36% between 2022 and 2024, according to numbers Xbox released. Subscribers were 34 million in February of 2024, the last time the company shared the data. Xbox has said Game Pass is profitable, with revenue reaching a record of nearly $5 billion for the fiscal year that ended in June.

“Profitable” is a funny term, particularly in this context, because the issue here is foregone revenue for _Call of Duty_ and other titles on Game Pass; that number isn’t a part of the Game Pass profit-and-loss calculation, because if it were, that profit would be a pretty significant loss.

I explained the argument for buying Activision when [Microsoft acquired Zenimax](<https://stratechery.com/2020/microsoft-and-zenimax-microsoft-ignite-microsoft-and-covid/>): 3rd-party publishers were not going to join Game Pass because it would cannibalize their sales, which means that Microsoft needed corporate control to expand Game Pass beyond their existing stable of games. I wrote in [Consoles and Competition](<https://stratechery.com/2022/consoles-and-competition/>):

> Microsoft is willing to cannibalize itself to build a new business model for video games, and it’s a business model that is pretty darn attractive for consumers. It’s also a business model that Activision wouldn’t pursue on its own, because it has its own profits to protect. Most importantly, though, it’s a business model that is anathema to Sony: making titles broadly available to consumers on a subscription basis is [the exact opposite of the company’s exclusive strategy](<https://stratechery.com/2020/ps5-and-xbox-sonys-console-microsofts-service/>), which is all about locking consumers into Sony’s platform.

The problem for Microsoft is that the way to overcome the cannibalization problem is to make it up in volume, and that volume just hasn’t materialized for Game Pass; the independent 3rd-party studios had it right! To that end, what this price increase represents is a capitulation: raising prices by 50% is a terrible way to grow the customer base, but if you have given up on user growth then the next best thing you can do is try to at least raise average revenue per user; if those users don’t like it and want to just buy games on an individual basis, well, at this point I suspect that Microsoft would be ok with it.

In short, it seems definitive at this point that Game Pass is a failure. Yes, it’s a product that exists, and yes, it’s a great deal for end users, but it didn’t accomplish Microsoft’s goals of significantly expanding their user base or stealing meaningful share from Sony. I respect the attempt, but wow, that was a lot of money paid for a business model shift that — if measured in opportunity cost — didn’t work, and worse, has to be supported indefinitely.

### Verizon’s Satellites

From [Space News](<https://spacenews.com/ast-spacemobile-gains-verizon-ally-amid-spacexs-direct-to-device-push/>):

> AST SpaceMobile’s shares closed up more than 8% Oct. 8 after Verizon joined AT&T in signing a definitive agreement to use its planned space-based cellular network, easing investor concerns about SpaceX’s aggressive push into the fledgling direct-to-device (D2D) market. The deal enables Verizon to provide D2D connectivity to its customers from some point in 2026, building on a strategic partnership announced in May 2024 that included plans for a $100 million investment in AST. AST signed a similar definitive revenue-sharing agreement with AT&T earlier that same month. Both carriers plan to contribute spectrum in the 850 megahertz band, which AST’s satellites would pool to directly connect to subscriber phones in the United States where their cell towers can’t reach.

I wanted to mention this story as it’s important follow-up to [an Update I wrote about SpaceX and spectrum last month](<https://stratechery.com/2025/spacex-buys-spectrum-spectrum-specifics-spacexs-big-bet/>). I noted at the time:

> Right at the exact moment that all of the relevant players in satellite connectivity are facing go/no-go decisions in terms of future capabilities, and just as those future initiatives are suffering from delays and a lack of launch capacity, SpaceX is shoving its chips into the middle of the table: everyone knows that SpaceX has the most launch capacity, and now they have not just spectrum but a demonstrated willingness to arguably overpay to have the best service (or at least gain the bargaining capacity to trade for it). Facing this reality, why shouldn’t Apple or AT&T or Verizon blink, and simply use Starlink?
>
> The reason, of course, is that Starlink is basically making a play to monopolize the space, which may be the only way to make money with satelite direct-to-device capabilities. Apple already set the market price at zero, and T-Mobile has followed suit, primarily using its satellite service to upsell customers to higher-tier plans. Both are useful strategies as long as they are differentiated, but once every device and every carrier has that capability the price premium will go to zero. If, however, that capability is table stakes, then the real winner will be the entity that sets the table. That’s exactly what Starlink is making a play to be.

Verizon decided that it didn’t want to play that game. The carrier giant is going to run the risk that its satellite service is delayed or potentially inferior to SpaceX’s offering, but at least it won’t be under the thumb of SpaceX.

This is, I would note, very much in character for Verizon. Just look at the iPhone, which Apple offered to Verizon first; the company declined, because they didn’t want to give Apple the total control of the experience the company wanted, and had to bleed subscribers for four years until finally getting the iPhone 4S in 2011.

The task for SpaceX, then, will be to make Verizon rue their decision, just like Apple did, but frankly, I think they have pretty long odds. Cellular from space is an incredible capability, but it’s also one that just isn’t that relevant to that many people, particularly since any meaningful coverage plan will require terrestrial-based coverage. If AST SpaceMobile continues to face delays in their next generation satellites, Verizon may suffer a bit; the company has decided that leading the industry into dependence on SpaceX would hurt much more.
