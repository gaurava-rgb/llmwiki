In June, the artificial intelligence startup Anthropic was finally getting close to hiring a candidate—a Republican—for its top government relations job after months of searching for someone. That decision marked a shift: Anthropic had hired several former Biden staffers earlier in the year, which had aggravated people in the Trump administration working on AI issues, according to two people familiar with the situation.

But after further discussion inside Anthropic, the company changed course. It called off the hire of the Republican candidate and instead promoted Sarah Heck, an existing staffer who had worked in the Obama administration.

In an interview last week, Anthropic cofounder Jack Clark said the company is now bringing on another government relations person who will report to Heck and be the company’s main point of contact in Washington D.C. Clark declined to identify the person or say whether they were a Republican or a Democrat.

The moves show how one of the most important AI startups is still figuring out how far it’s willing to go to play nice with the Trump administration. Anthropic’s rivals, including OpenAI, Meta Platforms and Google, have gone to extreme lengths to curry favor with the president and his allies by doing everything from donating to Trump’s inauguration, rolling back diversity initiatives and announcing huge investments in U.S. infrastructure projects. In July, OpenAI CEO Sam Altman [even forsook the Democratic party](<https://x.com/sama/status/1941151234775511328?lang=en>), his political home for most of his adult life.

Anthropic, in contrast, is taking a different approach. While the Trump administration has been focused on stripping away regulations to free up American AI companies, the San Francisco-based Anthropic hasn’t shied away from speaking out on the potential downsides of the technology, including risks to the safety of humanity. It even [screens new recruits](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/cult-anthropic?rc=ndc4g0>) partly on how they handle moral dilemmas.

Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei this year publicly opposed a draft provision in Trump’s “Big Beautiful Bill” to ban states from regulating AI, irritating the administration, according to two people familiar with the situation. This week, Anthropic publicly [endorsed](<https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-is-endorsing-sb-53>) an AI safety bill in California—the type of state legislation the proposed moratorium had been trying to block.

There are other conspicuous signs that Anthropic is keeping some distance in public from the administration—or vice versa. At a White House dinner last week, Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg, Altman and other tech CEOs took turns heaping praise on Trump. Representatives from Anthropic, meanwhile, were nowhere to be seen. Amodei was not invited to the dinner, said an Anthropic spokesperson, who added Heck had attended an AI education event hosted earlier in the day by First Lady Melania Trump.

It’s notable for a high-profile tech company—a relatively new one—to stake out such an independent position at this political moment. Jensen Huang, the CEO of Nvidia, the world’s most valuable company, frequently makes pilgrimages to Mar-a-Lago, Trump’s residence and private club in south Florida, to personally lobby the president. And Tim Cook, Apple’s CEO, gifted Trump a glass statue engraved with the president’s name and 24-karat-gold base at a White House event.

It’s unclear what fallout, if any, Anthropic could face as a result of any tensions with the administration. In the past, Trump has expressed a willingness to use his power to punish tech companies and leaders that displease him. He threatened to cancel the government contracts of SpaceX after a rupture in his relationship with the rocket company’s CEO, Elon Musk. And he publicly demanded the resignation of Intel’s CEO over his past connections to China, until the president reversed his position after the executive agreed to sell the U.S. government a 10% stake in the chip maker.

A former administration official said that while some staffers working on AI policy were frustrated by Anthropic, there were people in the government who were happy to work with them. “If they do more stuff to antagonize the federal government, that could change, but the government’s a big place,” the person said, noting that Anthropic already has several federal contracts and partnerships.

So far, Anthropic’s investors seem unbothered by the fact that its executives haven’t joined the Silicon Valley leaders seeking to ingratiate themselves with the president: A funding round Anthropic completed in September valued it at $183 billion, including the money raised—nearly three times its valuation six months before.

Still, Anthropic has recently been trying to become a more visible presence in Washington, and to highlight areas where it aligns with the administration. In August, Anthropic announced a new [national security advisory council](<https://www.anthropic.com/news/introducing-the-anthropic-national-security-and-public-sector-advisory-council>) to “support the U.S. government and closely allied democracies in building and maintaining enduring technological advantages in an era of strategic competition.” The company is also hosting events next week in Washington for a mix of policy professionals, academics, media and others. It also plans to double the number of people it employs in the city and graduate to an official office there from a co-working space, Axios [reported](<https://www.axios.com/2025/09/12/anthropic-ai-washington-dc-employee-congress>).

Asked about the dynamics with the Trump administration and how they compared to the Biden administration, Clark offered a careful response. “I’d say we have lots of areas of agreement on how to build the technology, and we’re all asking different questions about how you make it maximally secure and safe,” he said.

“Some of the questions that the admin is asking are different to the ones that we ourselves are asking—and it sometimes leads to you having different answers,” he said.

* * *

Anthropic was founded in 2021 by a group of ex-OpenAI employees, the most prominent being Amodei, a researcher known for his outspoken views and focus on AI safety.

The company, which has ties to the Effective Altruism movement, cultivated a distinct perspective and internal culture, even conducting “behavioral interviews” with candidates to make sure they were a fit.

In 2024, one year after launching its large language model, Claude, the company started doing some work with the U.S. government—a thorny area of business that had created internal tensions in the past at companies like Google.

To lay out its position, Anthropic published a set of policies outlining what it describes as “acceptable use” of its technology, including by governments. These prohibit people from using Anthropic’s AI to develop weapons, for example, a rapidly growing area of business in the AI and defense-tech industries.

This July, the Department of Defense awarded Anthropic a contract of up to $200 million—one of several such awards to AI companies—for national security purposes. Anthropic also recently began offering a version of Claude for government to federal agencies for $1 per year.

As its technology becomes more advanced, and it looks to bring in more money, there are signs the company could be reconsidering some of its policies.

When asked if Anthropic was exploring whether to allow its technology to be used for the development of weapons, for example, Clark said there was not a “direct discussion” about it, but he acknowledged that the company was considering changes to its usage policy.

“We’re discussing internally, is this the correct thing to have, or should we broaden it in certain ways,” he said of the usage policy. “The way that we’re sort of framing this to government partners and others is we’re saying, ‘Hey we’re taking a look at what we’re doing here, what are your thoughts?’ And then we’re going to sort through it and see what comes out the other side.”

There are some areas where Anthropic agrees with the administration, including on energy issues, a company spokesperson said. Amodei was among the many tech leaders who attended an energy event in Pittsburgh with Trump earlier this year, the spokesperson noted.

Anthropic has already made some notable concessions. The company recently agreed to pay $1.5 billion to settle a class-action lawsuit with a group of authors and publishers who had accused Anthropic of illegally using their copyrighted works to train its AI models.

In July, Amodei penned a memo to employees saying the company would seek investment from the United Arab Emirates and Qatar, a move that would likely enrich “dictators,” he said.

“Unfortunately, I think ‘No bad person should ever benefit from our success’ is a pretty difficult principle to run a business on,” Amodei wrote in the memo, which was viewed by The Information.

The frank wording was characteristic of Amodei, who is known for publishing essays and blog posts with his perspectives, sometimes earning him a “doomer” title—a reference to someone who is concerned that AI could lead to the end of humanity—from critics. (Amodei has rejected the label.)

Amodei has also been vocal about politics, writing on his personal Facebook page on election day in 2024 that “it’s quite clear to me that Kamala Harris scores better on” delivering thoughtful policy, and the “odds of things going well will be substantially improved if she is elected.”

His public opposition this year to an industry-backed proposal to ban states from regulating AI proved to be a particular lightning rod with the Trump administration.

In an op-ed in the _New York Times_ , titled “Don’t Let AI Companies off the Hook,” Amodei argued that AI technology was advancing “head-spinningly fast” and a move to ban states from regulating the technology over a 10-year period was “far too blunt an instrument.”

Administration staffers working on AI policy were frustrated by the company’s stance, which they learned of before Amodei’s op-ed published, two people familiar with the situation said. In phone calls with Amodei, staffers pressed him on the issue, wanting to better understand his thinking on it, one of the people said.

The talks did not change Anthropic’s position, and the issue remained a sore point with certain people in the administration. Ultimately, the provision never made it into law.
