During the past decade or so, music journalists have suffered all sorts of indignities. Corporate takeovers. Layoffs. Shrinking salaries. Stagnant freelance budgets. Publicists openly meddling in the editorial process. That’s just a partial list, and none of these developments have been good for journalists’ well-being—or the health of the field itself. But on a purely ego level, one pill has been particularly tough to swallow:

Artists don’t need us anymore.

That’s not news, or at least it shouldn’t be. The days of a Pitchfork review making or breaking someone’s career have been over for the better part of two decades now, and at this point, there’s not a single outlet with the power to significantly move the cultural needle. Music journalism no longer sets trends; it reacts to them, and even then, fewer and fewer people are interested in what the press has to say. Reviews are often ignored, and even if an artist lands on a magazine cover, the primary boost it provides will likely come not from the actual article, but from posting a photo of the magazine on their Instagram account. The written word simply doesn’t carry that much weight anymore, and while certain sectors of electronic and experimental music (i.e. the stuff I write about in First Floor) perhaps attract a more erudite—and, let’s face it, older—crowd, that crowd tends to put more stock in what Boomkat has to say than anything that gets published by the music press.

Good thing I’ve tied the bulk of my financial livelihood to the publishing of a long-winded, text-based newsletter, right? Sigh.

The point is, it’s now absolutely possible for artists to build a career, or at least some level of notoriety, while essentially ignoring the press altogether. Streaming and social media have a lot to do with that, as does the stubborn narrative that “gatekeeping is bad,” but the public has become accustomed to hearing directly from artists themselves—and most people honestly seem pretty happy about it. That arguably goes double in the DJ realm, where the success of even tastemaker-approved figures increasingly has far more to do with an artist’s public persona than whatever music they happen to make or play.

In the case of mad miran, however, her public persona to date has been largely nonexistent. An Afghan-Moroccan DJ born in the Netherlands and currently based in Barcelona, she rarely speaks to the press, and while she does have an Instagram account, it provides few windows into the ins and outs of her personal life. (Oddly enough, the most media attention she’s ever received was related not to her DJing, but the loss of her entire digital music collection when her bag was stolen earlier this year.) And yet, miran continues to get booked and actively tour around the world, her success seemingly fueled by little more than her reputation for being a sick DJ. That’s commendable, even enviable, but having witnessed her steady ascent, I nonetheless found myself wanting to know more, and asked if she’d be up for an interview.

miran by no means needed to talk to me, but as the conversation I published earlier this week clearly demonstrates, she actually has a whole lot to say—about music, about DJing, about growing up in the West as the daughter of refugees and about the paralyzing dissonance that comes with being expected to bring the party and constantly promote yourself while a genocide is being livestreamed to the world. It was a heavy, deeply personal talk, and you’ll find it below—and yes, the paywall is currently (albeit temporarily) down.

As for the rest of today’s First Floor digest, it’s packed with all of the usual goodies: news items, new release announcements, suggested reading links, track recommendations… you probably know the drill by now. As an added bonus, I’ve recruited veteran shapeshifter FaltyDL to pop in with a guest recommendation, so make sure to give that a look as well.

Let’s get started.

#  REAL QUICK

_A round-up of the most interesting electronic music news from the past week, plus links to interviews, articles and other things I think are worth sharing._

  * Within music circles, or at least certain music circles, the conversation about the entrance of private equity giant  **KKR** into the live music industry (via its purchase of festival conglomerate  **Superstruct** ) has been quite loud in recent months, sparking boycott movements targeting  **Boiler Room** ,  **Sónar** and a slew of other events around the globe. And during the past week or so, the outcry has spread to Australia. On the grassroots level, an  [ open letter ](<https://substack.com/redirect/3ac7eca5-a9de-4e1b-8ddf-210a2bb12246?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) —which has already been signed by more than 700 artists—has been circulating, and it finds signatories pledging to not perform at any event owned by KKR, including Sydney’s upcoming  **Field Day** festival and Boiler Room’s planned Australian tour. Yet what’s perhaps even more interesting is that the issue has also surfaced in the New South Wales Legislative Council, where Greens MP Cate Faehrmann publicly  [ questioned ](<https://substack.com/redirect/056a29aa-b1d5-4a52-92e1-de77cc89ef34?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) why government funds are being used to support festivals that are essentially owned by an American-owned firm with ties to weapons manufacturing, surveillance technology and the Israeli occupation of the West Bank.
  * With small- and medium-sized venues closing left and right, fans of independent and experimental music are struggling to come up with viable solutions, particularly as the costs associated with running these spaces continue to rise. In a new  _Black Sky Thinking_ [ essay ](<https://substack.com/redirect/7b0dcd34-d503-49b7-8c22-533caa4ed4db?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) for The Quietus, writer Dan McCarthy points to community ownership as a potential way forward. Although the piece is very UK-centric, its lessons could potentially be applied elsewhere, as McCarthy highlights the success of certain collectively owned working men’s clubs (WMCs) and the efforts of the Music Venue Trust to take more spaces out of the hands of landlords.
  * Resident Advisor’s long-running podcast series officially reached number 1000 this week, and the site has been going all-out to celebrate the milestone. Aside from rebooting the entire podcast archive via a new  [ microsite ](<https://substack.com/redirect/0fb9beee-9e0f-4d1d-b7cc-d679b7b6e313?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) , RA is currently in the midst of rolling out 10 exclusive DJ mixes from a line-up of heavy hitters that so far includes  **Theo Parrish** ,  **Helena Hauff** ,  **Bicep** ,  **Mark Ernestus** ,  **Tim Reaper** ,  **Sama' Abdulhadi** ,  **Jyoty** and  **DJ Sprinkles** .
  * **Valesuchi** ’s new  _Futuro Cercano_ LP is one of the year’s most delightful dance music full-lengths—more on that later in today’s newsletter—but the Chilean artist has also popped up in DJ Mag’s  _Recognise_ interview / mix series. Written by April Clare Welsh, the  [ article ](<https://substack.com/redirect/10e3b0b7-71da-442b-be4b-615d0734ef56?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) dives into Valesuchi’s background and how her 2017 move to Rio de Janeiro helped to sharpen her skills, and those skills are on full display in the accompanying  [ DJ mix ](<https://substack.com/redirect/f0f00294-dc02-437e-b4f1-ef373bfcce40?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) .
  * Is  **STROOM** the best curated label on the planet? It’s certainly in the running, and a new Bandcamp Daily  [ profile ](<https://substack.com/redirect/31a8e861-ba6c-4d1c-8977-acf22b5c5742?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) by Filipe Costa provides a small window into how the Belgian outpost operates, talking to founder  **Nosedrip** about his unabashedly personal approach and highlighting a selection of recent releases from the imprint’s impeccable catalog.
  * Ambient music has been seemingly everywhere in recent years, but even as the genre has gone global, Argentina’s ambient offerings still don’t seem to get much attention. Writer Maria Barros looks to change that with a new  [ feature ](<https://substack.com/redirect/cb3b2134-b621-4a56-bc04-55062641c4e5?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) for Bandcamp Daily, providing a brief overview of the country’s ambient past before recommending a selection of contemporary releases.

#  JUST ANNOUNCED

_A round-up of noteworthy new and upcoming releases announced during the past week._

  * Among a certain slice of punk and hardcore diehards, the band  **Black Eyes** has an almost mythic status. Within electronic music circles, however, the Washington DC outfit might be most notable for counting  **Daniel Martin-McCormick** (a.k.a  **Relaxer** ) and  **Jacob Long** (a.k.a.  **Earthen Sea** ) among its six-piece line-up. Following a nearly two-decade hiatus, the band first reformed for a series of reunion shows in 2023, and that eventually led to the creation of new music, a process which is now set to culminate in the release of a forthcoming full-length,  _Hostile Design_ . Produced by  **Fugazi** frontman and hardcore icon  **Ian MacKaye** , the LP is due to arrive on October 10 via the Dischord label, and will coincide with  [ Speaking in Tongues ](<https://substack.com/redirect/b737b1a9-3bf9-407c-8505-9061e0ddb05b?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) , a four-night festival in DC that was curated by Black Eyes themselves. Ahead of all that, one song from the new album,  [ “Pestilence,” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/ed8b598f-8cff-464e-817d-c56c0149fad3?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) has already been shared.
  * Released in May,  _From Where You Came_ was the first  **Kara-Lis Coverdale** album in eight years, but the Canadian composer won’t be waiting nearly as long to drop a follow-up. In fact, she has two more LPs slated to arrive on Smalltown Supersound before the year is finished. The first,  _A Series of Actions in a Sphere of Forever_ , is scheduled to surface on September 12, and it’s billed as “a return to acoustic fundamentals,” placing a heavy focus on the piano. It’ll be followed by  _Changes in Air_ on November 21. No further details about that album have been shared yet, but the label has already made available  [ “Turning Multitudes,” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/d025d0a8-fa6e-4aad-9674-e7867d3ba610?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) a song from  _A Series of Actions in a Sphere of Forever_ .
  * **Avalon Emerson** dropped what she described as “the final clank” of her  _Perpetual Emotion Machine_ series this week. Out now on Dead Oceans,  [ “E After Next” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/35622b9d-488e-435f-a873-bb1bceb1e7a1?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) is a actually a rework of  **Moby** ’s 1992 rave classic “Next Is the E.” (Full disclosure: Emerson and I work together on the Buy Music Club platform.)
  * Electro enigma  **Gesloten Cirkel** has a new EP on the way. Entitled  _Acid Puke_ , it will be issued on September 26 by Barcelona imprint Selvamancer, which promises both “dystopian rave energy and twisted melodic bliss.” To whet listeners’ appetites, closing track  [ “F-unkz3” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/1dadfad7-8ad7-4c74-aeed-6ce1ec9d7078?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) has already been shared.
  * Last we heard from  **Dylan Henner** , the mysterious UK artist was releasing a  [ tape ](<https://substack.com/redirect/1442b4c5-ff7d-48b7-9114-c8428c74245a?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) inspired by  _The X-Files_ . His next release resides in similarly fantastical territory, but it’s also far more personal, as it claims to be a recording of a radio broadcast that consisted entirely of memories from his own adolescence. As concepts go, this one is pretty elaborate, but  _Star Dream FM_ also promises more of the “choral-laced experimental ambient music” that Henner is known for. Phantom Limb will be issuing the album on October 17, but one song, the elaborately titled  [ “We Walked all the Way to the Lake and the Water Was So Still We Jumped in Naked” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/28995a89-c419-4111-86c4-29c889a6ffc8?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) is available now.
  * Over the past year, the first volume of  _Across the Horizon_ —a series billed as an “artist-curated exploration of instrumental music, bringing together pioneers from across ambient, folk and experimental worlds”—has brought 24 tracks into the world. That volume came to a close this week with the release of three songs curated by guitarist / composer  **William Tyler** . The first,  [ “Passport to Magonia” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/d3ef40f5-dce3-4339-a8c3-a714be71baf9?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) is a Tyler solo effort, while  [ “Covert Services” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/3e6e50f3-0e93-4798-87bf-04ed9d8cc6a0?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) is a collaboration—one intriguingly described as “a little bit Stereolab, a little bit Burial”—with  **claire rousay** and  [ “Drift of the Jenny” ](<https://substack.com/redirect/b74722bc-cb8f-4431-b3f3-a292fbcf85a9?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) features the vocals of  **Julianna Barwick** . A second volume of  _Across the Horizon_ is due to begin in January 2026, and is said to include contributions from  **Clarice Jensen** ,  **more eaze** ,  **Alan Sparhawk** ,  **Mary Lattimore** and others.

#  FALTYDL HAS BETTER TASTE THAN I DO

_First Floor is effectively a one-person operation, but every edition of the Thursday digest cedes a small portion of the spotlight to an artist, writer or other figure from the music world, inviting them to recommend a piece of music._

[ ![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VT92!,w_1100,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff32746f2-4a35-4aff-aff3-edb27ba7f014_3936x2624.jpeg) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/46dbc98f-2029-48d5-899f-8380fcb1a1b8?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Photo by Jennifer Medina

_Today’s recommendation comes from[ FaltyDL ](<https://substack.com/redirect/2e3438eb-7bd5-449c-83b1-006327a35230?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) , a Brooklyn-based producer who first emerged during the post-dubstep era, but long ago left behind both the confines of bass music and the usual needs of the dancefloor. Over the years, he’s dipped into house, techno, garage, IDM, electro, ambient, hip-hop and more, mostly on his own projects, but he’s also forged a deep working relationship with Mykki Blanco, having executive produced the rapper’s last two albums. FaltyDL’s musically omnivorous nature is also reflected in the curation of his own [ Blueberry ](<https://substack.com/redirect/73e80f7a-317d-41e9-895d-556c13d7a311?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) label, but even those accustomed to his stylistic switch-ups were likely surprised by his most recent album, _ [ Neurotica ](<https://substack.com/redirect/c96c9205-12d4-4b6d-ad34-66700cad4366?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) . _Released in June via Planet Mu, the LP delves headlong into a sugary strain of breakneck techno and EDM-adjacent mayhem that frequently approaches 200 bpm. His selection below, however, heads in the completely opposite direction, offering something that’s better suited to a wind-swept chaparral than the wilds of TikTok._

###  [ Cowboy Sadness “Second Rodeo” (People Teeth) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/85d728ed-848b-4edb-95a9-b8f3b35baff5?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Wide. Lonesome. Wind-chapped. Raw and honest. Cowboy Sadness deals in that kind of feeling. They come from Kingston, New York—a new breeding ground for artists—and this song is from their debut album, _Selected Jambient Works Vol. 1_ . Their touch is light. Their imagery is sharp. I don’t know much about them. And maybe that’s best. I don’t want to cloud the work. The music speaks on its own. Simple. Patient. Made with love.

#  LISTEN UP

_The following is a selection of my favorite tunes from releases that came out during the past week or so. Click the track titles to hear each song individually, or you can also just head over to this convenient[ Buy Music Club list ](<https://substack.com/redirect/57b29273-7614-499e-a339-23a536ddbaa4?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) if you prefer to listen to them all in one place. _

###  [ Valesuchi “G” (Discos Nutabe) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/67cc2b0f-33f3-4ac8-a271-cc7925f93251?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

###  [ Valesuchi “Amor” (Discos Nutabe) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/98d1c118-7f2e-4565-8562-56ce4ede5c31?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

It’s not uncommon for artists to display some level of growth as they move from one album to the next, but _Futuro Cercano_ , the sophomore full-length from Valesuchi is more than a mere progression—it’s a giant leap forward. Pulling liberally from house and techno, but refusing to conform to the traditional formulas of either genre, the album is lithe, playful and deeply rhythmic, in the sense that even the Chilean producer’s spritely synth programming has an almost percussive feel, albeit not a hypnotic one. Harnessing her experience as a live performer, Valesuchi rarely settles into grooves, preferring to let her frisky brand of machine music dart merrily to and fro. And while the sound palette is often sci-fi, the music itself is infused with a kind of campy irreverence, bounding along with the enthusiasm of a seven-year-old who’s just heard the ice cream truck pulling onto their street.

_Futuro Cercano_ ’s highlights are numerous, but “G” sounds like a space-age simulacrum of an ancient Middle Eastern palace. Moving with the speed and sensual precision of a belly dancer, its nervous drumlines excitedly click and clack as the song’s regal melodies gregariously wind and weave. Less grandiose, but no less energetic, is the standout “Amor,” a buzzing, bustling tune where Valesuchi offsets her twinkling keys with a boisterous synth line that honestly sounds like the barking of a hyperactive chihuahua. Evoking the energy of a yappy little dog—even unintentionally—is a risky move, but it speaks to _Futuro Cercano_ ’s willingness to throw out the dance music rulebook. Fun, not functionality, is the priority, but the LP nonetheless makes for a fantastic dance party.

###  [ JIALING “NINE OH NINE” (Sorry) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/eeadcdf9-b35e-4728-9062-9e59817bde15?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Anyone who’s done their _Homework_ will quickly recognize the sample at the heart of “NINE OH NINE,” but the boisterous track—which closes out the new _WUPS!_ EP—shouldn’t be mistaken for some slapped-together edit. Though she now resides in Brooklyn, JIALING hails from Baltimore, and true to the spirit of her hometown’s speaker-rattling club music, she not only has an obvious love of old-school breaks, but also displays a real knack for for elevating familiar source material into a zone that’s both joyous and rowdy. With its percolating rhythms and streetwise swagger, “NINE OH NINE” is perhaps more Windy City than Charm City, sounding at times like something that would have dropped on Dance Mania back in 1997, but irrespective of its exact musicological makeup, what matters most is that the song unquestionably slaps.

###  [ Alex Reece “Pulp Fiction (Benny L Remix)” (Metalheadz) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/f1e295f3-7f56-434e-a7db-c1cf938430d4?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Does “Pulp Fiction” need to be remixed? Probably not. First released in 1995, the track will forever be a drum & bass classic, but for anyone who ever found themselves wishing that the Alex Reece original had a bit less jazzy restraint and a bit more menacing bassweight, this rework from Benny L—which is being issued as part of the _30 Years of Metalheadz VIP_ series—will surely hit the spot. Those fearing a detour into tech-step tearouts need not not worry; though this “Pulp Fiction” refresh does feature corpulent basslines that are capable of vibrating every window in a five-block radius, Benny L has made sure that the song’s signature trumpet riff is still the star of the show.

###  [ Tony Quattro “En Mis Sueños” (WVWV) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/3c008853-8791-48a7-9674-e9610417e038?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Active since the early 2010s, Tony Quattro has long been something of a bass music polyamorist, more interested in trying his hand at whatever low-end-centric sound strikes his fancy at any given moment than he is in planting his flag in one particular corner of the hardcore continuum. A quick stroll through the East Coast producer’s catalog turns up forays into jungle, gqom, UK funky, rave and other styles too numerous to list, and on the new _En Mis Sueños_ EP—his debut for Mexico City’s WVWV imprint—he’s jumped headfirst into the rhythms of the Global South. He’s at his best on the record’s title track, which harnesses the crooked gallop of tribal guarachero—for the uninitiated, the genre is something like house music’s seasick Mexican cousin—as Quattro choreographs a serpentine procession of squealing synth notes and (sampled) ancient woodwinds.

###  [ Bodhi “T.O.P.” (Hotflush) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/299c84ec-8635-42d7-8bc5-6e0743e74dd1?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

2-step garage tends to be slinky and sexy—and that’s great—but “T.O.P.” demonstrates what sort of gut-rumbling magic can happen when the genre is taken in a more unruly direction. Taken from Bodhi’s new _Corrupta_ EP—a record that’s brimming with high-quality breaky and bassy hybrids—the song combines the shuffling snares of UK garage with buzzing synths and industrial-strength low-end, with a dash of dancehall toasting tossed in to further rev up the dance. Brawny enough to appease the gunfingers crowd and loose enough to ensure that they won’t be the only ones left on the dancefloor, “T.O.P.” more than ready for the big room.

###  [ WRACK “Ban” (N.A.A.F.I) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/4b4daf68-66a0-4ddb-9506-cd8793c845c5?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Japanese house and techno get a decent amount of attention from the Western dance music ecosystem, but Japanese bass music? That rarely seems to enter the conversation, even as an artist like WRACK not only repurposes traditional taiko drum sounds, but does so while blurring the lines between dancehall, reggaeton, gqom and other low-end-heavy sounds. In recent years, the Tokyo producer has dropped a string of releases on the N.A.A.F.I label, and he’s just returned to the Mexican outpost with the new _SUPE_ EP. Layering haunted-house synths atop a feverish drum attack and hulking bass blasts, “Ban” is the record’s strongest selection, and its polished-chrome aesthetic brings to mind both the glory days of Night Slugs and the IMAX-ready club sounds favored by the Fractal Fantasy crew.

###  [ Matt Karmil “Havin’ It” (Aus) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/b93008b0-a61d-4c8f-9aab-a9b7542c619b?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Underrated. Underappreciated. Cult favorite. Your favorite producer’s favorite producer. These kinds of descriptors have been attached to Matt Karmil for as long as I can remember—and yes, I’m as guilty of anyone for perpetuating that practice in my own writing—but while listening to his new _WNE_ EP, it struck me that all perhaps these niceties were in reality a form of damning the guy with faint praise. It’s not that the people who say these things aren’t genuinely fans of Karmil and / or his music; it’s that the subtext is some form of “this artist isn’t that popular” or “there’s a good chance you haven’t heard of this person.” To his credit, the British-born artist—who’s also a very in-demand mixing and mastering engineer—has never complained about any of this, at least not publicly. Nevertheless, a starry-eyed track like “Havin’ It,” which leads off the new record, deserves to be evaluated on its own merits, as its hazy atmosphere, twinkling keys and pulsing house groove are more than good enough to be presented without any well-meaning “this person is an underdog” caveats. In other words, it’s a great tune from someone who’s made a whole lot of them over the years. What else does anyone really need to know?

###  [ SEL.6 “UR MY DESIRE” (IMPACTO) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/5d0cfb92-e1e4-48c2-8977-7d9e4228a61e?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

As a general rule, referring to anything as “Floridacore” is not going to be a winning marketing strategy, but it’s honestly a perfect descriptor for the music of SEL.6. A dyed-in-the-wool Floridian who grew up just north of Miami in Broward County, she’s the kind of artist who can casually connect the dots between 69 Boyz, Pitbull and DJ Icey. As a producer, much of her past output has been heavily steeped in jungle and electro sounds, but the new _SECULAR MUSIC_ EP—her debut for Nick León and Jonny from Space’s IMPACTO imprint—takes a more borderless approach, comfortably employing both wiggily Aphex-isms (“AS RIGHT AS RAIN”) and swaying Latin house grooves (“TRIO 99’”). Yet it’s “UR MY DESIRE” that truly stands out, its electro beats bopping along as the song’s muted melodies gently sway back and forth, conjuring visions of the ocean calmly lapping against the Miami shoreline.

###  [ Heathered Pearls “Soft Lock” (The Ambient Institute) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/3d4e6f10-8e77-4bc4-98d2-c982d1a7c537?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Arriving with very little in the way of additional context, “Soft Lock” is apparently the first single from Heathered Pearls’ forthcoming—but as yet unannounced—new album. Weightless and elegant, the song is ambient but never static, its billowing, soft-focus melodies harkening back to the ’80s-era work of Tangerine Dream, or maybe even the ambient-ish portions of Sasha’s _Airdrawndagger_ album. More importantly, it’s practically oozing bliss, its delicate undulations radiating with the kind of warmth and intimacy that only comes with focused listening and a willingness to shut off (at least temporarily) the chaos of the outside world.

###  [ Carrier “The Fan Dance (Dub)” (Modern Love) ](<https://substack.com/redirect/3335e349-d26b-4d2b-82d5-2bb10b532fa5?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>)

Succinctly describing what Carrier’s music sounds like has proven endlessly vexing to journalists. Just this week, Philip Sherburne was [ struggling ](<https://open.substack.com/pub/futurismrestated/p/fr-129-rituals-and-patterns?r=1jar1&selection=0706be67-e24c-4fdf-aede-42ce6dc04157&aspectRatio=instagram&textColor=%23ffffff>) with the matter, and despite the fact that First Floor [ interviewed ](<https://substack.com/redirect/e6ebc656-464d-4e39-8b03-be2013de6f72?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) Carrier earlier this year, I have yet to come up with a neat little box—or even a collection of boxes—in which to place his productions. _The Fan Dance_ , which is part of Modern Love’s 45 series and exclusively available via [ Boomkat ](<https://substack.com/redirect/6e3b9fd8-a1a7-4b04-a20c-437b3df94575?j=eyJ1IjoiMzhqd3YifQ.5A9UDKbxtmTMpf48aHOFTZVvvXtwbwJ-khjaPLWh8tA>) , won’t make things any easier. The title track, a collaboration with Gavsborg, deals in a sort of dancehall minimalism, and while the Equiknoxx co-founder’s cooly detached flow is undeniably charming—the line “I’m sexy when I’m mad, I’m sexy when I’m moody” is absolutely the sort of thing that’ll get stuck in your head for days at a time—I’m more partial to the spacious expanse of the dub version. With its chunky, lethargic guitar tones, the song at times recalls the work of bands like Slint or …And You Will Know Us by the Trail of Dead, tapping into a morose strain of post-hardcore that works surprisingly well with the static-laced legacy of dub techno and autonomic drum & bass. Carrier’s sonic universe keeps on expanding, and rather than trying to map its every nook and cranny, perhaps it’s better—or at least more enjoyable—to simply go along for the ride.
