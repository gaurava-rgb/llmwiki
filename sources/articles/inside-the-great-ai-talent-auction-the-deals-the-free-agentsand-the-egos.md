Say you’re at the top of your field. Say you’ve been promoted to be a senior researcher at OpenAI, watching the paper value of your equity swell as your company’s valuation goes from less than $20 billion to $260 billion in just four years. Say Mark Zuckerberg—yes, Zuck, personally—comes texting and calling, offering you tens of millions of dollars to help salvage his struggling AI lab.

What do you do? Consider whether the move is actually worth it.

“A lot of the early OpenAI people have achieved financial independence already,” said one person who was in that situation. “At some point, people are seeing a $25 million offer, and they’re just like, ‘Well, after tax it’s half that.’” The person turned Zuckerberg down. “I remember 10 years ago, people would be like, ‘Wow, a $400,000 offer.’ But now it’s gotten so crazy.”

That’s something of an understatement. Hiring for AI talent has been hot since the moment after ChatGPT’s 2022 launch. As an astonishing amount of money poured into the industry, the recruiting drive intensified. A year or so ago, Elon Musk [called](<https://x.com/elonmusk/status/1775644544853221599?lang=en>) the environment “the craziest talent war I’ve ever seen.” It has only escalated since.

“AI researchers have become soccer players in the past 18 months: They go where they are paid the most,” said Victor Sanh, a former Hugging Face researcher who received his own series of “eye-watering offers” before deciding to do his own AI startup.

Circumstances have reached the point of outright frenzy in the last several weeks, driven by Zuckerberg’s blitzkrieg recruiting—a bid to bolster Meta Platforms’ ranks in an effort to catch up with its AI rivals.

The splashiest deal: Meta hired Scale CEO Alexandr Wang as part of a [$14.3 billion deal](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/meta-betting-nearly-15-billion-alexandr-wang?rc=sslhyj>) to take a 49% stake in Scale. It’s also in [advanced talks](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/meta-talks-hire-former-github-ceo-nat-friedman-daniel-gross-join-ai-efforts?rc=sslhyj>) to hire former GitHub CEO Nat Friedman and Safe Superintelligence CEO Daniel Gross and partially buy out their venture fund, which could cost more than $1 billion. Meanwhile, OpenAI’s Sam Altman has said Zuckerberg has offered as much as $100 million in signing bonuses to lure away OpenAI talent. (Meta CTO Andrew Bosworth has denied the company is offering $100 million in signing bonuses). And earlier this week, Zuckerberg managed to pry away three researchers from OpenAI’s Zurich office, who’d worked together previously at Google DeepMind.

![](https://dmxvlyap9srmn.cloudfront.net/article/pictures/2498a580-69cd-4498-a03d-ecaaaeab9664.jpg)Meta Platforms CEO Mark Zuckerberg.  _(Getty Images)_

As Zuckerberg’s recruiting push has unfolded, it has become a favorite Silicon Valley guessing game to think about who might be on his wish list. Even if someone has no intention of actually joining Meta, a Zuckerberg golden offer can constitute primo bragging rights and the chance to squeeze a counteroffer from their current employer. “I’ve had plenty of friends checking their messages—their WhatsApp messages—for a Zuckerberg text,” said one venture capitalist.

Meanwhile, the influx of highly paid new talent is causing grumbling among existing employees, who must now share an open plan office with co-workers making double or triple their salary—or more. At Meta, the new offers are breeding resentment as existing employees feel like Zuckerberg’s emphasis on the new crop of superstars is labeling them mediocre by comparison.

At Microsoft, managers are discussing whether to offer more equity to employees who transferred internally into the Microsoft AI team, to even out situations where external hires are making two or three times as much, according to a hiring manager. And at OpenAI, researchers debate how they could snag a retention bonus. One former OpenAI researcher said: “You absolutely hear people say, ‘Wow, should I throw a hissy fit?’”

The talent grab is shaping up as the most intense hiring period ever in Silicon Valley, as our conversations with more than a dozen researchers, founders, investors and executives within the industry make clear. The acquisitiveness is one with few parallels in the history of any industry: Never before has so much money been committed to a capitalistic endeavor so quickly. Meta, for instance, has said it plans to spend $64 billion to $72 billion in capital expenditures this year in large part as it builds data centers to support its AI efforts, while Google CEO Sundar Pichai reaffirmed in April that the company planned to put $75 billion into capital expenditures.

As the multimillion-dollar offers mount, the hiring boom presents a snapshot of the broader contest to develop and commercialize artificial intelligence, and offers a gauge of who’s up and who’s down in what has quickly become the most significant tech race in recent memory.

* * *

By far, the most lucrative jobs are the ones for AI researchers and AI research engineers, the people who work to develop the models that underpin much of the AI boom. An engineer starting in this field can expect a salary base around $100,000 to $120,000, with $150,000 or so in equity that vests over four years, according to one big tech hiring manager. The numbers could be bigger elsewhere: One founder of a billion-dollar AI startup said he might expect to pay $150,000 or more in salary plus generous equity.

A distinct pecking order has developed. At the very top are OpenAI, Anthropic and Google’s DeepMind outfit, which have the clout and money to get just about anyone. Elon Musk’s xAI has vaulted up quite close to them. Meta is sometimes talked about in this class, but its reputation has taken a hit since the disastrous launch of its Llama 4 model. (When asked about Meta, a researcher at a competitor responded with their own question: “Why would you go to a less advanced lab just for money?”)

A step further down is Amazon, Apple and Microsoft. Then it’s the billion-dollar startups, like Perplexity, Cohere and Mistral AI, followed by additional rungs, many filled with also-rans and never-will-bes. Of course, for cream-of-the-crop engineers, the easiest path to riches may be starting an AI business and getting acquired later, as Noam Shazeer showed last year.

Generally, companies can recruit freely from the rungs below them but find it challenging to reach for someone at rungs above them. Startups like to pick off Amazon’s perennially harried workforce and employees at Google and Meta working on uninteresting parts of AI, like, say, integrating it into Instagram. Meanwhile, the publicly traded companies can up the appeal to a candidate by emphasizing how their equity offers are much more liquid than any startup’s.

In some cases, the recruiting spans continents. Spirit AI, a Chinese humanoid robotic startup, has ruled out hiring Americans in light of geopolitical tensions. However, the company, backed by a co-founder of Chinese battery giant Contemporary Amperex Technology Ltd. and Saudi Arabia’s sovereign venture firms, Prosperity7 Ventures, plans to open an office in the Bay Area solely to recruit Chinese citizens working in America.

Spirit AI is actively talking to Chinese talent from companies like Google and OpenAI after the Trump administration’s recent hostilities against Chinese students and graduates in the U.S. provided an opening for attracting top staff in AI and robotics. “Some of these people indeed didn’t want to return to China previously,” said Spirit AI CEO Han Fengtao. “But now a lot of them are prepared to come back.”

* * *

News of the biggest offers tend to ping-pong around the entangled world of AI researchers, where everyone seems to have worked with everyone else or graduated from the same doctorate programs. One researcher reported that multiple friends called to express disbelief at the offers they were getting from Meta. On top of eight figures in equity per year, the social media company was dangling eight-figure cash signing bonuses. Such cash compensation tends to be rare at big technology companies, where stock compensation typically accounts for a significant portion of overall pay and helps with retention.

Even once a researcher has started a job, there’s a chance of a second payday if they threaten to leave. Employees threatening to leave Meta have been promised increased freedom, scope and pay, according to two people with direct knowledge. In a previously unreported move, Meta has offered researchers millions in additional equity to remain at the company, according to two people told about such offers. Some of those offers happened prior to the deal with Scale. When asked about Meta’s policy on counteroffers, a company spokesperson said the company does not give counteroffers. Rather, “we provide proactive grants to stay consistent with the market,” the spokesperson said. “Which is standard compensation practice.”

Another researcher said, with an air of bemusement, that OpenAI offered him hundreds of thousands in cash after rumors swirled that he might leave for another startup. A former OpenAI manager, told about that offer, said OpenAI had offered retention packages that far exceeded it.

From the recruiters’ perspective, one problem seems to be that there’s no clear criteria for knowing who the golden hire will be. Zuckerberg’s outreach has become more personalized—including specific references to recruits’ previous research—than [his last recruiting push](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/meta-joins-the-ai-talent-war-with-quick-offers-emails-from-zuckerberg>), where his emails to candidates were more generic, according to a person with direct knowledge, suggesting that Meta is trying to zero in on known quantities behind specific research breakthroughs.

![](https://dmxvlyap9srmn.cloudfront.net/article/pictures/e5953a52-7034-47df-9c5f-710dd43afe22.jpg)OpenAI CEO Sam Altman.  _(Getty Images)_

But a senior leader at another of the major AI labs said it was hard to know what research specialties actually mattered for improving AI models. AI is a field where researchers are designing such complicated systems that it is difficult to break up one aspect of the work from another, the leader said. Ultimately, they said, recruiting often comes down to word of mouth, a game of knowing a person or having worked with them before.

Even in a more typical hiring process, outside of trying to pluck out stars, knowing whether to hire a given AI researcher is difficult, according to a third person who has been involved with recruiting at major AI labs. Technical interviews by the likes of OpenAI, Google DeepMind and Anthropic often bear little relationship to the actual content of the person’s job, the person said.

However they decide on their candidates, AI companies are pulling out all the stops to close the deals. Zuckerberg and now Wang have been personally involved with recruiting. Hiring managers at Google DeepMind will pull in Google co-founder Sergey Brin to sweet-talk promising candidates, according to a person involved in the process.

At Microsoft AI, engineering candidates can often make more than double what they would in roles at equivalent levels on other Microsoft teams, according to a hiring manager at Microsoft AI. Hiring managers will tell engineering candidates they will match offers from OpenAI, Google, Anthropic or Meta, the person said. They have even given candidates an extra $50,000 cash signing bonus to stop all interviews and accept their offer immediately, in order to avoid the possibility that the candidate gets a much higher competing offer.

Jonathan Siddharth, founder of Turing, a billion-dollar AI startup, estimates he currently spends more than 30% of his time on recruiting, double the amount he devoted to it a few years ago. A couple months ago, he added a daily meeting to his calendar to discuss nothing but recruiting, deliberately scheduling it at 8 a.m., which he calls his peak performance window. “Right after my Starbucks triple latte,” he said.

Still, no matter how much time and money a company or a founder throws at a candidate, the frantic pace of AI is burning out some of the people within the industry, leaving them to ponder their fates in other parts of tech.

One leader in Meta’s AI department departed earlier this year, giving up total compensation that stretched well over $2 million, after the company emphasized “that last year was tough and this year is going to be just like that.” (Seeing the initial equity come in, “I had felt like I had won the lottery,” the person admitted.)

Some friends at a top-tier lab encouraged this person to consider joining them, even suggesting they could get a spot in one of the company’s newest initiatives. Eager for a break after the typical AI trajectory of earning an undergraduate degree, then multiple advanced degrees, working at a startup and joining Meta, the person has been mulling over their options and has thought quite a bit about joining one field they expect to be less demanding than AI: venture capital. “It’s gotta be less of a grind,” the person said.
