Public interest in **ChatGPT's** impact on society is rising fast, especially given recent high-profile lawsuits filed by family members of people who used the chatbot before taking their own life. On Monday, **OpenAI** opened the chabot's kimono a little bit, with a [new study](<https://cdn.openai.com/pdf/a253471f-8260-40c6-a2cc-aa93fe9f142e/economic-research-chatgpt-usage-paper.pdf>) showing exactly how users are using ChatGPT.

Notably, non-work usage of ChatGPT dominates; between last June and this June, non-work-related queries have exploded from 53% to 73% of all queries sent, according to the study. Most of these queries were people looking up information as a **Google Search** alternative.

That’s in stark comparison to **Anthropic** , which coincidentally published its own [usage study](<https://www.anthropic.com/research/anthropic-economic-index-september-2025-report>) on the same day and found that more than half of **Claude** users were using the chatbot for work-related tasks such as coding, business and financial operations and office and administrative support. (It’s worth noting that by every metric, the Claude chatbot’s usage is a fraction of ChatGPT’s.)

Surprisingly, only a small percentage of ChatGPT users use the chatbot for coding—4.2% compared to Claude’s 36.9%. It could be that users are more likely to use OpenAI’s models for coding through its coding assistant **Codex** or other coding apps like **Cursor** , which are powered by models from OpenAI and Anthropic, rather than through ChatGPT.

Even more surprising, though, is that a tiny percentage of ChatGPT messages are related to companionship or social or emotional issues, according to the report. Only 1.9% of ChatGPT messages are related to relationships and “personal reflection,” while 0.4% are related to games and roleplay. That number seems remarkably small given the flood of news reports about people developing relationships with their chatbots or hurting themselves after talking to their chatbot.

Another interesting tidbit is that 2.1% of ChatGPT messages came from users asking about “purchasable products.” That’s a small percentage today, but if it grows, it could represent a new source of revenue for OpenAI, which [expects to make a decent chunk of future sales from its non-paying users](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-says-business-will-burn-115-billion-2029>).

OpenAI and Anthropic usage differed in other ways. As we mentioned earlier, OpenAI found that most people are generally using ChatGPT to look up information, rather than instructing the AI to carry out tasks on their behalf, while Anthropic found the opposite is true for its products.

That discrepancy could have to do with the companies’ customers. OpenAI makes the bulk of its revenue from individual consumers who use ChatGPT, while Anthropic’s data includes consumer and enterprise customers of Claude, as well as corporate customers of its application programming interface.

Those findings make sense given the tracks that each company is pursuing: OpenAI has been [increasingly challenging search engines like Google](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-challenging-google-using-search-data?rc=geicgp>), while Anthropic’s growth is [mostly relying on business users](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/anthropic-projects-soaring-growth-to-34-5-billion-in-2027-revenue?rc=geicgp>) looking to automate workplace tasks like writing code. Still, it seems likely that both firms would like to dominate the market for automating tasks, as they’ve both raced to release more products [capable of taking over white collar work](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-preps-chatgpt-agents-challenge-microsoft-excel-powerpoint?rc=geicgp>).

### Big Tech’s AI Product Problem

Running an enterprise software startup is immensely difficult. But these days, it seems like it's easier to go down that path than to steer a legacy enterprise app business toward AI.

A couple recent examples: **Salesforce** CEO [Marc Benioff’s rude awakening](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/marc-benioff-said-ai-easy-crazy-team-salesforce-proved-wrong>) after erroneously telling customers that they’d be able to set up AI agents for customer service in minutes, using Salesforce’s Agentforce software. Meanwhile, some of his employees have been leaving to join **Sierra** , a smaller customer service-focused competitor started by former Salesforce co-CEO **Bret Taylor** whose valuation has rocketed to $10 billion. According to Kevin’s report, Benioff is annoyed that these defectors are bringing their customer relationships with them to Sierra.

**Microsoft** has similarly [struggled to sell Office 365 Copilot](<https://www.theinformation.com/articles/microsoft-hopes-hastened-ai-rollout-price-discounts-can-fuel-office-365-growth>) despite the flashy AI-powered features it offers. Internally, some Microsoft engineers who work on Office told us they were impressed by **Shortcut** , a startup that builds AI agents for spreadsheets, and its ability to create Excel spreadsheets, a task Microsoft has been trying to improve in its own product. (A Microsoft spokesperson said that the company’s strength is that it’s a “full-stack AI company” that can offer products across a variety of applications.)

Design software company **Adobe** , meanwhile, has seen its stock tank 33% over the last year as it faces growing competition from **Midjourney** , **OpenAI** and a flurry of other image and video generation startups.

Not every big tech company is struggling to release good AI products: I’m personally a fan of **Google’s Nano Banana** , which helps users easily edit their photos (and its **Gemini** app, which includes Nano Banana, did just rocket to the top of the App Store leaderboard for free apps).

But many of the AI product flops we’ve written about were a result of executives getting ahead of themselves in their marketing of these products and their capabilities. And the public market isn’t as forgiving as private market investors when it comes to overpromising and underdelivering.

Still, it doesn’t seem like we’ve learned our lesson. Microsoft CEO **Satya Nadella** has told deputies, for instance, that he wants its new-and-improved **Office 365 Copilot** to work as effectively as an entire “coworker” that companies could hire to carry out roles like HR manager and junior salesperson.

If we’re to take anything from Salesforce’s experience, it’s that that might be easier said than done. I’d recommend to Nadella to keep things simple for now and focus on getting Microsoft’s AI to read Excel spreadsheets accurately before we start talking about AI coworkers.

**Nvidia** agreed to purchase $6.3 billion of cloud computing capacity from **CoreWeave** , the latest example of the chip company striking a deal to rent its own chips from a third-party cloud business.

**Google** said it would spend 5 billion pounds in the U.K. over the next two years, investing in AI, research and development and related engineering, creating more than 8,000 jobs a year in the U.K.

**Workday** announced that it had acquired **Sana** , a developer of enterprise AI software, for $1.1 billion.

**Invisible Technologies** , a data labeling firm, is raising $100 million in funding at a $2 billion-plus valuation led by **Vanara Capital** , according to Bloomberg.

**SEON** , a fraud prevention and anti-money-laundering compliance company, raised $80 million in Series C funding led by **Sixth Street Growth**.

**Remedio** , which has developed an AI model that acts like a hacker, raised $65 million in funding from **Bessemer Venture Partners**.

**GreenLite** , a New York City-based startup that offers AI-powered permitting and plan review software, raised $49.5 million in Series B funding led by **Insight Partners**.

**Highbeam** , which provides AI-powered finance software for consumer brands, raised a $30 million Series A funding round led by **Acrew Capital**.

**Doctronic** , which lets people ask questions to an AI doctor, raised $20 million in Series A funding led by **Lightspeed Venture Partners**.

**Spara** , which provides AI agents to help companies with online sales, raised $15 million in seed funding led by **Radical Ventures** and **Inspired Capital**.

**Sophont** , which is developing AI models for medicine, raised $9.22 million in pre-seed and seed funding rounds led by **Kindred Ventures**.

**Rulebase** , which offers an AI agent for back-office finance tasks, raised a $2.1 million pre-seed round led by **Bowery Capital**.

**OpenAI** has hired **Mike Liberatore** , the former chief financial officer at **xAI** , to be its business finance officer, responsible for overseeing its infrastructure spending.

**OpenAI** said Monday that it's updating its AI coding agent, **Codex** , with a new model called **GPT-5-Codex** which spends its “thinking” time more dynamically than previous models.

**Google** on Tuesday announced a new open protocol for purchases made by AI agents called the **Agent Payments Protocol** , meant to provide a traceable paper trail for each transaction.

**Microsoft** said on Monday that its **Visual Studio Code** editor will automatically pick the best model for “optimal performance” and will primarily rely on **Anthropic's Claude Sonnet 4** for paid users.
